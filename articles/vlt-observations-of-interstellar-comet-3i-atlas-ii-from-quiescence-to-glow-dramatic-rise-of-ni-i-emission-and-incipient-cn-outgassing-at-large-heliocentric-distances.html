VLT Observations Of Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS II. From quiescence To Glow: Dramatic Rise Of Ni I Emission And Incipient CN Outgassing At Large Heliocentric Distances

Published 2025-08-29 19:15 UTC

VLT Observations Of Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS II

Researchers have reported new findings on interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS (C/2025 N1) from the Very Large Telescope (VLT) in Chile. The observations, conducted between 4.4 and 2.85 astronomical units (au) from the Sun, reveal significant changes in the comet's emissions. The study, published on August 21, 2025, indicates a rise in Nickel (Ni I) emissions and the onset of cyanide (CN) outgassing.

This research is important as it enhances understanding of the comet's behavior as it approaches perihelion. The findings suggest that the comet is becoming more active, which could provide insights into its composition and the processes affecting its outgassing. Authorities are now focusing on coordinated monitoring efforts to further investigate the comet's activity as it continues its journey through the solar system.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS is at a heliocentric distance of approximately 2.85 au as of August 21, 2025.
  • The study utilized X-shooter (300-550 nm, R≃3000) and UVES (optical, R≃35k−80k) for spectroscopy.
  • Production rates measured: log Q(CN)=23.61±0.05 molecules s−1, log Q(Ni)=22.67±0.07 atoms s−1.
  • Significant scaling observed in production rates: Q(Ni)∝r−8.43±0.79h and Q(CN)∝r−9.38±1.2h.
  • Research team includes Thomas H. Puzia and Rohan Rahatgaonkar, among others.

Timeline

Recent observations and analyses of comet 3I/ATLAS have occurred as follows:

  • Initial observations from r h ≃4.4 au to 3.17 au indicated a dust-dominated coma.
  • At r h ≃3.17 au, researchers found no indications for [O I], C2, C3, or NH2 but detected CN emission and several Ni I lines.
  • On August 21, 2025, measurements at r h = 2.85 au reported significant production rates for CN and Ni.

Official statements

“The rise in Ni I emissions suggests a complex interaction between dust and gas phases as the comet approaches the Sun,” said Thomas H. Puzia, a lead researcher on the study.

Context

This study builds on previous observations of 3I/ATLAS, which has been monitored since its discovery. Understanding the outgassing and emission behavior of comets like 3I/ATLAS is crucial for advancing knowledge in solar and stellar astrophysics.

What’s next

Future investigations will involve coordinated ground-based and space-based monitoring to observe the comet's continued activity and confirm the hypotheses regarding its emission mechanisms.
