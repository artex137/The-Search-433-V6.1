Interstellar Anomaly: New Images Of Comet 3I-ATLAS Reveal Mysterious Symmetric Coma And Missing Tail

New images of interstellar object 3I/ATLAS, taken on December 2, 2025, have revealed a peculiar symmetric coma and the absence of a tail, raising questions among astronomers. The observations were captured by astrophotographer Chuck and confirmed by NASA’s Center for Near-Earth Object Studies.

These findings are significant as they offer fresh insights into the behavior of interstellar objects as they travel through the Solar System. Scientists are analyzing the unique characteristics of 3I/ATLAS to better understand its origin and trajectory, particularly in comparison to previous interstellar visitors like ʻOumuamua and Borisov.

Key facts

  • Comet Name: 3I/ATLAS
  • Discovery Date: July 1, 2025
  • Distance from Earth: ~2.4 AU
  • Distance from Sun: ~2.9 AU
  • Coma Intensity: ~88%
  • Tail Visibility: ~22%
  • Speed: Nearly 60 km/s relative to the Sun
  • Perihelion Passage: October 29, 2025

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was discovered on July 1, 2025, by the ATLAS survey telescope in Chile. NASA confirmed its interstellar origin shortly thereafter. The comet passed perihelion on October 29, 2025, and is currently moving outward from the Solar System. On December 2, 2025, Chuck captured new images that showcased the comet's unusual coma.

Official statements

“The coma’s symmetry, lack of a tail, faint jets, and smooth gradients are all hallmarks of interstellar activity,” said a NASA spokesperson. “This distinct behavior sets 3I/ATLAS apart from regular Solar System comets.”

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third known interstellar object, following ʻOumuamua and Borisov. The study of such objects is crucial for understanding how they behave under solar heating and how they differ from local long-period comets. The unique features of 3I/ATLAS provide researchers with important data regarding the evolution of comets from outside our Solar System.

What’s next

Astronomers will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS as it leaves the Solar System. Future observations will focus on its evolving coma and any potential changes in activity, providing valuable insights into the behavior of interstellar objects.
