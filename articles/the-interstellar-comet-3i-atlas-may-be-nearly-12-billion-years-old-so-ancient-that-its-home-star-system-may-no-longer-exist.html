The interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS may be nearly 12 billion years old — so ancient that its home star system may no longer exist

The interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, which became notable in 2025, has been found to be between 10 and 12 billion years old, according to a recent study. The research indicates that the comet may have formed shortly after the Milky Way galaxy itself, suggesting its original star system may no longer exist. The study was led by Martin Cordiner of NASA’s Goddard Space Flight Center.

This discovery is significant as it provides insights into the early history of the Milky Way and the formation of planetary systems. The findings were based on isotopic analysis conducted using the James Webb Space Telescope, which revealed the comet's unique chemical composition.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS is estimated to be 10 to 12 billion years old.
  • It was discovered moving at a speed of 58 km/s, the fastest recorded for a comet.
  • The study utilized the Near-Infrared Spectrograph (NIRSpec) aboard the James Webb Space Telescope.
  • 3I/ATLAS contains significantly less ¹³C than known objects in the Solar System.
  • The comet's low abundance of ¹³C suggests it formed before the galaxy reached its current levels of this isotope.

Timeline

In 2025, comet 3I/ATLAS was discovered and quickly gained attention due to its high speed and interstellar origin. Initial estimates of its age ranged from 3 to 11 billion years. A recent study led by Martin Cordiner has revised this estimate, placing it firmly between 10 and 12 billion years.

The isotopic analysis conducted using the James Webb Space Telescope provided crucial data, allowing scientists to understand the comet's formation conditions and its implications for early planetary systems.

Official statements

“Cometary materials generally represent the building blocks of planets beyond the snow line,” said Martin Cordiner. “Interstellar comets provide a unique opportunity to glimpse what extrasolar planets were made of in very early epochs.”

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third confirmed interstellar object to visit the Solar System, following 1I/‘Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov. The study of such objects helps astronomers understand the formation and evolution of planetary systems beyond our own.

What’s next

Further investigations will likely focus on analyzing the chemical composition of 3I/ATLAS and other interstellar comets to enhance our understanding of their origins and the conditions of the early universe.
