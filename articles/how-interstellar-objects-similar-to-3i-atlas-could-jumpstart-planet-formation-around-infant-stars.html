How interstellar objects similar to 3I/ATLAS could jumpstart planet formation around infant stars

Interstellar objects like 3I/ATLAS could play a crucial role in the formation of giant planets around young stars, according to a study presented by Professor Susanne Pfalzner at the EPSC-DPS2025 Joint Meeting in Helsinki on September 11, 2025. These comet-like bodies, which wander through interstellar space after being ejected from their home systems, may facilitate the creation of planets by serving as seeds in planet-forming disks.

This research is significant as it addresses a long-standing challenge in astrophysics regarding how larger planetary bodies can form within the turbulent environment of a young star's accretion disk. Pfalzner’s findings suggest that capturing these interstellar objects could enhance the efficiency of planet formation, particularly around higher-mass stars.

Key facts

  • Event: Presentation of research findings
  • Date: September 11, 2025
  • Location: EPSC-DPS2025 Joint Meeting, Helsinki
  • Researcher: Professor Susanne Pfalzner, Forschungszentrum Jülich, Germany
  • Interstellar objects detected since: 2017
  • Notable interstellar objects: 1I/'Oumuamua, 2I/Borisov, 3I/ATLAS
  • Size of 1I/'Oumuamua: Approximately 100 meters

Timeline

Since 2017, astronomers have detected three interstellar objects passing through our solar system. These include:

  • 1I/'Oumuamua
  • 2I/Borisov
  • 3I/ATLAS, discovered in summer 2025

Pfalzner presented her findings on September 11, 2025, highlighting the potential of these objects to enhance planet formation around young stars.

Official statements

“Interstellar objects may be able to jump start planet formation, in particular around higher-mass stars,” said Pfalzner.
“Interstellar space would deliver ready-made seeds for the formation of the next generation of planets,” she added.

Context

Planet formation occurs in dusty disks around young stars, where smaller particles typically accrete to form larger bodies. However, theoretical models have struggled to explain how objects larger than a meter can form in such chaotic environments. Pfalzner's research proposes that interstellar objects could provide a solution to this problem by being gravitationally captured within these disks.

What’s next

Pfalzner plans to model the success rate of captured interstellar objects to determine how many can form planetary bodies and whether their distribution within a planet-forming disk is uniform or concentrated in specific regions.
