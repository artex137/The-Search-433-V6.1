3I/ATLAS Update: Harvard Expert Flags 'Symmetric' Triple Jets as Possible Tech Signature

3I/ATLAS has entered a controversial phase of scrutiny following the release of images in January 2026 that reveal a precise 'triple-jet' structure emerging from its core. Harvard astrophysicist Professor Avi Loeb has suggested that the interstellar comet's behaviour may not be entirely natural. NASA continues to classify 3I/ATLAS as a natural comet, but the new findings have intensified debate among astronomers.

The significance of these findings lies in their potential implications for our understanding of celestial bodies. As 3I/ATLAS approaches the Sun, its behaviour has become increasingly volatile, transforming it into one of the most closely monitored space phenomena of the decade.

Key facts

  • 3I/ATLAS was first detected on 1 July 2025 by the ATLAS telescope in Chile.
  • Images revealing the triple-jet structure were taken in January 2026.
  • The jets are separated by exactly 120 degrees, a geometry considered unusual for comets.
  • Professor Avi Loeb claims this structure challenges the understanding of cometary outgassing.
  • The object is expected to have a close encounter with Jupiter's moon Eupheme in March 2026.
  • Non-gravitational acceleration observed is significantly higher than previously modelled.
  • 3I/ATLAS has become the most studied object in space history.

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was detected on 1 July 2025 by the ATLAS telescope in Chile. Initially, it was considered a typical interstellar comet. However, as it neared the Sun, its behaviour became increasingly erratic. In January 2026, new images revealed a symmetrical triple-jet structure, prompting a reevaluation of its nature.

As the object continues its journey, astronomers are preparing for its close approach to Jupiter's moon Eupheme in March 2026, with telescopes worldwide focused on capturing this rare event.

Official statements

“The moment the data became clear was completely transformative,” said Dr Elena Vance, a key researcher involved in the imaging project.
Professor Loeb noted that the precise geometry of the jets poses a challenge to conventional explanations, calling it "puzzling" and difficult to explain by random ice pockets.

Context

3I/ATLAS has drawn comparisons to previous interstellar objects such as 'Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov. Unlike 'Oumuamua, which remained silent, and 2I/Borisov, which behaved like a traditional comet, 3I/ATLAS exhibits hybrid characteristics. The discovery of its triple-jet structure suggests a more complex internal structure than previously thought.

This phenomenon raises questions about the nature of objects in space and their potential geological activity, which may be more dynamic than scientists have understood.

What’s next

As 3I/ATLAS approaches Jupiter, astronomers will continue to monitor its behaviour closely. The ongoing discussion regarding its classification as a natural comet or a potential technosignature is likely to evolve as new data becomes available.
