Interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS is leaving our solar system for good

The interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, discovered in July 2025, is on a trajectory to exit the solar system permanently after its closest approach to Earth in December 2025. NASA has confirmed that the comet, which is not a threat to Earth, has been traveling at speeds of 137,000 mph and is now moving away from the sun.

This development is significant as 3I/ATLAS is only the third object identified as originating from another star system. NASA officials have emphasized that 3I/ATLAS is a comet, not an alien spaceship, despite widespread conspiracy theories suggesting otherwise. Observations from various space agencies have provided important insights into the comet's characteristics and trajectory.

Key facts

  • Discovered: July 2025
  • Closest approach to Earth: December 19, 2025
  • Distance from Earth at closest approach: approximately 170 million miles
  • Speed: 137,000 mph
  • Estimated nucleus size: between 1,400 feet and 3½ miles in diameter
  • Current trajectory: hyperbolic orbit, exiting the solar system
  • NASA's TESS spacecraft reobserved the comet: January 15-22, 2026

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first spotted by a telescope in Chile in July 2025. The comet made its closest approach to the sun at the end of October 2025, followed by its closest approach to Earth on December 19, 2025. Since then, it has been on a path to exit the solar system.

NASA's Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite (TESS) collected data on the comet from January 15-22, 2026, while the SPHEREx telescope observed it in December 2025.

Official statements

"No aliens. No threat to life here on Earth," said Sean Duffy, NASA's then-Acting Administrator, in a social media exchange in October 2025.
"We certainly haven't seen any technosignatures or anything from it that would lead us to believe it was anything other than a comet," stated Nicola Fox, associate administrator of NASA's Science Mission Directorate, during a news conference in November 2025.

Context

3I/ATLAS gained attention as the third comet discovered from outside the solar system, with its trajectory traced back to the direction of the constellation Sagittarius. The comet has sparked numerous conspiracy theories, particularly one suggesting it could be an alien spacecraft, which NASA officials have consistently refuted.

What’s next

As 3I/ATLAS continues its journey out of the solar system, it can still be observed until spring 2026 in the pre-dawn sky with small telescopes. NASA encourages the public to follow the comet's movements through its online simulation, Eyes on the Solar System, and on the Jet Propulsion Laboratory's website.
