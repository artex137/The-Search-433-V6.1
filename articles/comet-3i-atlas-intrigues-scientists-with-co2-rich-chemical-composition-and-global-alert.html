Comet 3I/ATLAS intrigues scientists with CO2-rich chemical composition and global alert

The interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, detected on July 1, 2025, by the ATLAS telescope in RÃ­o Hurtado, Chile, has sparked NASA's planetary defense protocols due to its unusual orbital behavior. The comet, traveling at over 210,000 km/h, poses no threat to Earth.

This discovery is significant as it marks the third confirmed interstellar object, following 'Oumuamua in 2017 and Borisov in 2019. Authorities have initiated a training exercise coordinated by the International Asteroid Warning Network (IAWN) from November 27, 2025, to January 27, 2026, to refine measurements of the comet's hyperbolic trajectory.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS detected on July 1, 2025.
  • Travels at over 210,000 km/h, reaching perihelion on October 29, 2025.
  • Closest approach to Earth: 270 million km.
  • CO2 concentration in the coma exceeds six times expected variations in solar comets.
  • IAWN training exercise runs from November 27, 2025, to January 27, 2026.
  • Estimated nucleus diameter: between 320 meters and 5.6 km.
  • Expected exit from the Solar System: March 2026.

Timeline

On July 1, 2025, the ATLAS telescope in Chile identified the comet near the constellation of Sagittarius. Preliminary observations confirmed its extrasolar origin due to its high speed of 61 km/s. On August 6, 2025, the James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) captured data revealing a coma dominated by carbon dioxide.

On October 21, 2025, data from the Harvard Minor Planet Center emphasized the need for international alerts as the comet approached solar conjunction, limiting visibility. The IAWN has organized a training exercise to improve methods for observing active comets during this period.

Official statements

"We face unique challenges in predicting variations in the comet's trajectory," said an IAWN representative. "The data we gather will enhance our understanding of planetary formation in distant systems."

Context

The detection of 3I/ATLAS continues a trend of increasing interest in interstellar objects, with prior encounters including 'Oumuamua and Borisov. These discoveries have prompted enhanced international cooperation and protocol development for monitoring and studying such entities.

What’s next

NASA and IAWN will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS as it approaches its perihelion. The training exercise aims to refine techniques for tracking similar objects in the future, ensuring improved readiness for planetary defense initiatives.
