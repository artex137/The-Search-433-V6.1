Horrifying simulation exposes devastating effects if 3I/ATLAS collided with Earth

A simulation has revealed the catastrophic consequences of a potential collision between the comet 3I/ATLAS and Earth. The simulation estimates that if the comet were to strike Washington D.C., approximately 1.2 million people could be vaporized instantly, with a further 19.3 million expected to die from the resulting fireball. The simulation was conducted ahead of the comet's closest approach to Earth on November 18, 2023.

This simulation matters as it highlights the potential risks posed by celestial bodies, even when current scientific assessments indicate that 3I/ATLAS will not collide with Earth. NASA's Hubble Space Telescope has identified the comet's trajectory, confirming it will pass safely behind the Sun, alleviating fears of an imminent impact.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS is estimated to be between 440 and 5,600 meters in diameter.
  • The comet is traveling at approximately 221,000 kilometers per hour.
  • If it struck Washington D.C. at a 45-degree angle, it would create an impact radius of 27 kilometers.
  • The resulting crater would extend around 792 meters into the ground.
  • 1.2 million people could be vaporized instantly in the impact.
  • 19.3 million people could perish from the fireball generated by the collision.

Timeline

NASA's Hubble Space Telescope has monitored 3I/ATLAS for several months, confirming its trajectory. As the comet approaches its closest point to Earth on November 18, 2023, scientists have conducted simulations to assess the potential impact of a collision.

Earlier this year, concerns arose regarding another asteroid, 2024 YR4, which had a brief possibility of hitting Earth, but those fears were ultimately alleviated. The recent simulation of 3I/ATLAS serves as a reminder of the ongoing need to study and prepare for potential threats from space.

Official statements

“The simulation shows the devastating impact a comet could have, but it’s important to note that 3I/ATLAS poses no threat to Earth,” said a NASA representative.

Context

This simulation follows a trend of increasing public interest in near-Earth objects and their potential hazards. Past incidents, such as the Chelyabinsk meteor in 2013, have underscored the importance of monitoring and understanding the risks associated with such celestial bodies.

What’s next

NASA and other space agencies will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS and other near-Earth objects to ensure public safety and preparedness against potential impacts.
