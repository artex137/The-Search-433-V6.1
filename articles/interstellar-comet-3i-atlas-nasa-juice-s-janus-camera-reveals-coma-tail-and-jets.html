Interstellar Comet 3i Atlas Nasa: JUICE’s JANUS Camera Reveals Coma, Tail and Jets

Published 2026-03-05 14:26 UTC

Interstellar Comet 3i Atlas Captured by JUICE’s JANUS Camera

The European Space Agency’s Jupiter-bound spacecraft JUICE has returned detailed images of Interstellar Comet 3i Atlas Nasa, taken by the JANUS science camera on 6 November 2025, from approximately 66 million kilometres away. The observations reveal a bright coma, a long tail, and multiple jets, marking significant findings in the comet's behaviour following its closest approach to the Sun.

This data is crucial for scientists studying the comet's characteristics and activities. The imagery and instrument data collected during a concentrated observing campaign in November 2025 will allow researchers to analyze the comet's morphology and composition, providing insights into its outgassing and particle flow dynamics.

Key facts

  • Date of observation: 6 November 2025
  • Distance from Earth: approximately 66 million kilometres
  • Number of images captured: over 120
  • Instruments used: JANUS, MAJIS, UVS, SWI, PEP
  • Campaign duration: November 2025

Timeline

On 6 November 2025, the JANUS camera aboard JUICE photographed Interstellar Comet 3i Atlas Nasa. This was one week after the comet's perihelion, or closest approach to the Sun. The data collected during this concentrated observing campaign involved multiple instruments studying the comet's characteristics.

In the months leading up to the observations, JUICE had spent time on the far side of the Sun, limiting its communication capabilities. Consequently, data transmission was delayed, but teams have since begun analyzing the collected information.

Official statements

“The immediate cause-and-effect is clear: the spacecraft’s operational posture while behind the Sun limited downlink capacity, which in turn postponed hands-on analysis,” a spokesperson for the JUICE project said.

Context

This campaign is significant as it highlights the capability of JUICE to conduct opportunistic science while en route to its primary mission of studying Jupiter's icy moons. The observations of Interstellar Comet 3i Atlas provide a rare opportunity to study a visitor from beyond the Solar System.

What’s next

Instrument teams plan to convene in late March 2026 to synthesize findings from the data collected. This coordinated review will allow teams to cross-compare results and further understand the comet's makeup and outgassing behaviour.
