3I/ATLAS Makes Its Closest Approach To Earth on December 19

The interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS will reach its closest approach to Earth on December 19, travelling at approximately 170 million miles (270 million kilometres) away from the planet. There are no reported injuries or fatalities associated with this event.

This approach has drawn significant attention from stargazers and social media, with some speculating about doomsday scenarios. However, scientists assure the public that the comet poses no threat to Earth. Space agencies and observatories are actively tracking 3I/ATLAS for its scientific value rather than any safety concerns.

Key facts

  • Closest approach of 3I/ATLAS is on December 19.
  • Distance from Earth at closest approach: approximately 170 million miles (270 million kilometres).
  • Speed of the comet: 130,000 miles per hour (210,000 kilometres per hour).
  • 3I/ATLAS is only the third interstellar object detected.
  • Monitoring will continue well beyond December 19.

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was spotted in late June, moving rapidly through the solar system. As it approaches Earth, astronomers have confirmed its stable trajectory and lack of threat to the planet.

On December 19, the comet will reach its closest point to Earth. Scientists will continue to monitor its behavior after this date, looking for any gas releases or minor outbursts that are typical for comets.

Official statements

“There is no risk of collision. The comet’s path will not suddenly change,” said astronomers at NOIRLab.
“Despite online speculation, 3I/ATLAS is entirely natural,” researchers have confirmed.

Context

3I/ATLAS is significant because it is only the third interstellar object detected, following 1I/‘Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov. Unlike typical comets, 3I/ATLAS originated from another star system, providing unique insights into cosmic conditions.

Its approach has sparked wild theories online, but experts stress that December 19 is a day for scientific observation, not fear.

What’s next

Post-December 19, scientists will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS for any unusual activity, contributing to the understanding of interstellar objects and their impacts on our solar system.
