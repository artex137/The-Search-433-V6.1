This Telescope Just Proved an Alien Comet Is Layered Like an Onion, and It Changes Everything

A new study using Japan’s Subaru Telescope has shown that the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, which passed close to the Sun in late 2025, has a layered structure with varying chemical compositions. The findings were published on April 24, 2026, by researchers from Kyoto Sangyo University, who reported significant changes in the comet's carbon dioxide content relative to water following its perihelion passage.

This discovery is significant as it provides insights into the internal structure of 3I/ATLAS, suggesting that its surface chemistry differs from that of its deeper layers. The study's results could reshape our understanding of how interstellar objects evolve as they travel through space and interact with solar radiation.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS was first detected on July 1, 2025.
  • Perihelion was reached on October 29, 2025.
  • Observations for the study were conducted on January 7, 2026.
  • 3I/ATLAS was 2.87 astronomical units from the Sun during this observation.
  • CO2-to-water ratios varied from 0.34 to 2.12 in the study.
  • The Subaru Telescope is located in Maunakea, Hawaii.

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first detected on July 1, 2025, by the Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System (ATLAS). The comet approached the Sun, reaching perihelion on October 29, 2025. Following perihelion, researchers began analyzing the comet's chemical composition. On January 7, 2026, observations were made using the Subaru Telescope, revealing a significant drop in the carbon dioxide to water ratio compared to earlier readings.

Prior to perihelion, on August 6, 2025, the James Webb Space Telescope recorded a CO2-to-water ratio of 7.6. After perihelion, different instruments reported varying ratios, indicating a complex change in the comet's chemistry.

Official statements

“This layered interpretation, while supported by current data, is not yet definitive,” said Yoshiharu Shinnaka, one of the researchers involved in the study. “Further observations are needed to confirm these findings and to understand the processes at play.”

Context

3I/ATLAS is only the third confirmed interstellar object ever observed. Previous findings from other interstellar comets, such as 2I/Borisov, have shown similar characteristics, but the layered structure of 3I/ATLAS introduces new questions about the formation and evolution of celestial bodies outside our solar system.

The study highlights the potential for future interstellar object discoveries to provide additional insights into the chemical evolution of comets formed around other stars.

What’s next

Researchers plan to conduct further observations of 3I/ATLAS and other interstellar objects to verify the layered structure hypothesis and explore whether this compositional evolution is common among similar bodies. Upcoming survey telescopes are expected to increase the number of detected interstellar objects, providing more data for analysis.
