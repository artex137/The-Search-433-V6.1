ISRO captures rare and close-up image of fast-moving interstellar comet 3I/Atlas

The Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO) has released a rare, close-up view of interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS from its Mount Abu telescope, taken between November 12 and 15, 2025. The images show a compact, near-circular coma consistent with typical comet characteristics, according to ISRO's Physical Research Laboratory.

This observation is significant as it helps scientists refine models of the comet's activity and trajectory. ISRO’s imaging and spectroscopy efforts complement data from NASA missions, allowing researchers to gain a more comprehensive understanding of the comet's behaviour as it passes through the inner solar system.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS was observed from November 12 to 15, 2025.
  • ISRO's Mount Abu telescope captured false-colour images and a dawn spectrum.
  • The comet is exhibiting a near-circular coma and classic molecular bands.
  • NASA has utilized twelve assets to track and analyze the comet.
  • The comet is expected to approach Earth at a safe distance of 170 million miles in mid-December 2025.

Timeline

On November 12-15, 2025, ISRO conducted observations of 3I/ATLAS, capturing images and spectra that reveal its coma characteristics. The agency released these findings on November 19, 2025, highlighting that the comet behaves like a typical active body. Meanwhile, NASA has been compiling data from multiple missions to track the comet's path and activity.

Official statements

“The images show a near-circular coma... The result shows prominent emission features commonly seen in Solar system comets - the CN, C2 and C3 bands in the shorter wavelength side of the spectrum,” ISRO stated in a post.
“We were quick to be able to say, ‘Yup, it definitely behaves like a comet’,” said NASA’s Nicky Fox during a November 19 briefing.
“This object is a comet. It looks and behaves like a comet,” said NASA Associate Administrator Amit Kshatriya.

Context

Prior observations of 3I/ATLAS have shown it to be chemically interesting, with a CO₂-heavy composition. NASA’s multi-mission approach has been crucial in tracking the comet through various geometries, particularly during times when Earth-based telescopes faced solar glare.

What’s next

ISRO will continue to collect ground data, while NASA will provide calibrated image sets. Scientists will compare coma profiles and dust-to-gas behaviour as the comet moves further into the solar system.
