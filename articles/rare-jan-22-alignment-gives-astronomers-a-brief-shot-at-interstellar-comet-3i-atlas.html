Rare Jan. 22 alignment gives astronomers a brief shot at interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS

CAMBRIDGE, Mass., Jan. 16, 2026, 05:23 (EST) — Astronomers have announced that interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS will align closely with the Earth-Sun axis on January 22, providing a unique opportunity for observations. NASA has stated that the comet poses no danger to Earth.

This alignment is significant because it allows researchers to analyze the dust emitted by the comet during a brief observational window. The alignment is expected to enhance the measurement of light-scattering effects, which can be difficult to assess from other angles. The goal is to determine the properties of the dust, including its reflectivity and density.

Key facts

  • Date of alignment: January 22, 2026
  • Time of near-opposition alignment: 13:00 UTC
  • Comet's rotation period: Approximately 7.1 hours
  • Distance from Earth: No closer than 1.8 astronomical units (about 170 million miles or 270 million km)
  • Brightness magnitude: Expected to be around 16.7

Timeline

On January 22, 2026, interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS will reach a near-opposition alignment, with Earth positioned almost directly between the comet and the Sun. This geometry is expected to last for about a week, allowing astronomers to observe the comet under optimal conditions.

Prior to this, a separate analysis indicated the comet's rotation period at approximately 7.1 hours, helping researchers distinguish between brightness changes due to rotation and those caused by the viewing angle.

Official statements

“The combined data supports a post-perihelion rotation period of about 7.1 hours,” said Harvard astronomer Abraham Loeb.
“Future dense and contemporaneous monitoring is required to reduce systematic uncertainties,” noted the authors of the draft paper regarding the observations.

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third confirmed interstellar object to enter the solar system, following 1I/‘Oumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019. Interstellar comets are of significant interest to scientists because they carry materials formed around other stars, which can provide insights into planetary formation.

What’s next

Astronomers will closely monitor the comet during the alignment to collect data on its brightness and polarization. This information will help separate the effects of the alignment from normal cometary activity, contributing to the understanding of interstellar objects.
