Astronomical alert: NASA confirms mysterious object is ‘interstellar’ visitor

A mysterious object identified as comet 3I/Atlas has been confirmed by NASA as an interstellar visitor, making it only the third such object ever detected. The comet, discovered by NASA's ATLAS (Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System) survey telescope in Chile, is currently moving through space and was first reported in July 2025.

This discovery is significant as it provides insights into objects originating outside our solar system. NASA has stated that 3I/Atlas poses no threat to Earth, as it will pass at a distance of approximately 150 million miles. Scientists are currently studying its size and composition, as it travels at an extraordinary speed of about 152,000 mph.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/Atlas is the third interstellar object ever identified.
  • Discovered by NASA's ATLAS telescope in July 2025.
  • The comet is traveling at approximately 152,000 mph.
  • It is currently about 150 million miles from Earth.
  • 3I/Atlas will be visible to ground-based telescopes through September 2025.
  • It will reappear on the other side of the sun by early December 2025.

Timeline

In July 2025, NASA's ATLAS telescope in Chile identified the comet 3I/Atlas. Following its discovery, NASA confirmed its classification as an interstellar object. The comet has since been observed traveling towards the sun at a remarkable speed.

Throughout September 2025, scientists have been studying 3I/Atlas to gather data on its size and composition. Observations are ongoing until the comet approaches the sun too closely for visibility.

Official statements

“3I/Atlas is a unique object that provides a rare opportunity to study materials from outside our solar system,” said a NASA spokesperson.

Context

The identification of 3I/Atlas follows the detection of two previous interstellar objects: Comet 2I/Borisov in 2019 and ‘Oumuamua in 2017. Both objects sparked significant interest in the scientific community due to their unusual characteristics and origins.

NASA has been closely monitoring these interstellar visitors as they offer insights into the formation and evolution of celestial bodies beyond our solar system.

What’s next

Scientists will continue to observe 3I/Atlas until it becomes too close to the sun for visibility. Following its passage, they will analyze data collected to understand more about its composition and origin.
