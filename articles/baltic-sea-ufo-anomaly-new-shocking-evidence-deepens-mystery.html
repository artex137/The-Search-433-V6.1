Baltic Sea UFO Anomaly: New Shocking Evidence Deepens Mystery

The Baltic Sea Anomaly, discovered in 2011 by Peter Lindberg, Dennis Asberg, and their OceanX diving team, has drawn renewed attention due to new evidence that challenges its origins. The anomaly, located in the Gulf of Bothnia, features an unusual circular shape with a central depression and stair-like ridges. No injuries or fatalities have been reported in connection with the anomaly.

This development is significant as it may alter the understanding of the structure's origins. Authorities and researchers continue to investigate the anomaly, which has sparked various theories ranging from UFOs to a sunken city. Recent findings suggest that the structure may not be of natural origin, prompting further examination by experts.

Key facts

  • Discovery year: 2011
  • Location: Gulf of Bothnia, Baltic Sea
  • Depth: 90 meters
  • Primary researchers: Peter Lindberg, Dennis Asberg
  • Materials found: Sandstone, granite, gneiss, basalt
  • Temperature around the object: Nearly 0°C
  • Reported tech malfunctions: GPS failures, electromagnetic interference

Timeline

In 2011, the OceanX team discovered the Baltic Sea Anomaly while searching the Gulf of Bothnia. Since then, various expeditions have taken place to study the site and its features. Recently, Dennis Åsberg revealed new evidence from sub-bottom profiling, indicating that the circular structure is detached from the seabed, which contradicts previous theories of a natural formation.

Lab tests conducted on samples retrieved from the anomaly have identified organic material and basalt, raising questions about its origins. Observations of unusual phenomena, including GPS failures and electromagnetic interference during dives, have also been reported.

Official statements

“In 15 years, this is the most important data related to the Baltic Sea Anomaly,” said Dennis Åsberg during a podcast interview with Jesse Michels.
“The geometry of the structure is not natural. It consists of straight walls, 90° corridors, and a hard, uniform surface,” Åsberg added.

Context

The Baltic Sea Anomaly has remained a topic of intrigue since its discovery, with various experts offering differing interpretations of its origins. While many have attributed the structure to natural geological processes, Åsberg's claims of its artificial characteristics have reignited interest in the possibility of extraterrestrial involvement.

Prior to the anomaly's discovery, Åsberg reported witnessing a cigar-shaped craft over a Swedish plutonium facility, further fueling speculation regarding unidentified flying objects in the region.

What’s next

Investigations into the Baltic Sea Anomaly are ongoing, with researchers planning further expeditions to gather more data. The scientific community continues to analyze the implications of the new findings, and future studies may provide additional insights into the structure's origins and nature.
