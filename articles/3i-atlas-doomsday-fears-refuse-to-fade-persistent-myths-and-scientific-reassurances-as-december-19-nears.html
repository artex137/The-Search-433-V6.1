3I/ATLAS Doomsday Fears Refuse to Fade: Persistent Myths and Scientific Reassurances as December 19 Nears

As the date approaches, online fears about the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS remain strong, despite scientific reassurances from NASA stating there is no risk to Earth. The comet, discovered in July 2025 by the ATLAS telescope network, will make its closest approach at 1.8 astronomical units—approximately 270 million kilometres—on December 19, 2025. Its high speed, exceeding 60 kilometres per second, and its origins from another star system have fueled speculation and concern.

This situation matters because it highlights the ongoing struggle between scientific facts and misinformation spread through social media. NASA and other agencies continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS closely, working to inform the public and dispel myths surrounding the comet.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS discovered in July 2025.
  • Closest approach to Earth set for December 19, 2025.
  • Distance at closest approach: 1.8 astronomical units (about 270 million kilometres).
  • Speed of 3I/ATLAS: over 60 kilometres per second.
  • Mass estimated at 44 million tonnes, with a diameter between 0.52 and 0.75 kilometres.
  • NASA has monitored the comet since its discovery.

Timeline

July 2025: 3I/ATLAS is discovered by the ATLAS telescope network.

December 14, 2025: NASA confirms non-gravitational tugs on the comet align with typical cometary behaviour.

December 19, 2025: 3I/ATLAS is set to make its closest approach to Earth.

Official statements

“There is no danger to Earth from this comet,” NASA has stated.
Avi Loeb, a Harvard astrophysicist, noted, “There is no doubt that we can learn something new from the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS, irrespective of whether it is an icy rock or a spacecraft.”

Context

The fears surrounding 3I/ATLAS reflect a broader trend of misinformation regarding celestial events. Many myths arise from digital platforms, where speculation often outpaces scientific understanding. Previous incidents of public panic over celestial phenomena have prompted agencies to enhance their communication efforts.

What’s next

NASA will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS and provide updates to the public as it approaches Earth. The agency’s efforts will focus on clarifying any misconceptions and sharing data collected during the comet's passage.
