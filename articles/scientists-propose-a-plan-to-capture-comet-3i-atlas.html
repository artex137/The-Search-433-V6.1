Scientists propose a plan to capture comet 3I/ATLAS

Scientists have proposed a plan to intercept comet 3I/ATLAS, which is currently speeding past Earth at over 60 km/s. The proposal comes from the Initiative for Interstellar Studies (i4is) and aims to launch a spacecraft in 2035 to catch up with the comet, which has already passed Jupiter’s orbit.

The mission is significant because it represents a rare opportunity to study an interstellar object that has traveled between stars. Researchers have outlined a two-phase mission that will utilize a gravitational assist from Jupiter and the Sun's gravity to propel the spacecraft at unprecedented speeds. The mission could provide insights into the formation of other planetary systems.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS is moving at over 60 km/s.
  • The proposed mission is set for 2035.
  • The spacecraft will use Jupiter’s gravitational assist and the “Oberth effect” to gain speed.
  • The journey to intercept the comet will take approximately 50 years.
  • The mission aims to study the composition of interstellar objects and their potential biology.
  • The project has been published in the Journal of the British Interplanetary Society.

Timeline

Comet 3I/ATLAS was detected after it had already passed Jupiter’s orbit, making traditional interception methods unfeasible. Researchers have since proposed a bold mission that leverages gravitational assists from Jupiter and the Sun to accelerate a spacecraft. This mission is planned for 2035, when celestial alignments will be optimal for travel.

Official statements

“The only solution is a fast flyby mission, but it requires a strategic detour through the depths of our system to gain a phenomenal boost,” said Adam Hibberd from the Initiative for Interstellar Studies.

Context

The detection of interstellar objects like 3I/ATLAS is rare but significant for understanding the chemistry and biology of distant star systems. Previous missions have focused on asteroids and comets within our solar system, while the study of interstellar objects could unlock new knowledge about the universe.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to prepare for the 2035 mission, focusing on the technical challenges of using the Sun and Jupiter for gravitational assists. Further studies and developments in propulsion technology will also be necessary to ensure the success of the mission.
