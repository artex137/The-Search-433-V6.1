3I ATLAS Update: Latest Images from JANUS Reveal Comet's Behaviour

The European Space Agency (ESA) has released new images of the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, captured by the Jupiter Icy Moons Explorer (JUICE) spacecraft's science camera, JANUS, in November 2025. The images were taken seven days after the comet's closest approach to the Sun, and no injuries or fatalities have been reported.

This update is significant as it provides insights into the comet's behaviour, which appears consistent with typical comets, despite its interstellar origin. ESA is currently analysing the data with multiple instrument teams to understand the comet's composition and activity better.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS was first detected in July 2025.
  • Images were captured by JANUS approximately 66 million kilometres from the comet.
  • The comet was travelling at speeds exceeding 150,000 miles per hour during perihelion.
  • Five scientific instruments aboard JUICE have observed the comet: JANUS, MAJIS, SWI, PEP, and UVS.
  • Data transmission was affected by the spacecraft's position relative to the Sun, resulting in delays.
  • Analysis of the collected data has only recently begun, with a coordination meeting planned for late March 2026.

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first detected in July 2025, marking its entry into the solar system as the third known interstellar object. The comet made its closest approach to the Sun in November 2025, during which the JANUS camera captured images showing its bright coma and tail. Following transmission delays, the data has reached Earth and is currently under analysis by various instrument teams.

Official statements

“While 3I/ATLAS is a visitor from interstellar space, travelling from outside the Solar System, its behaviour is completely in line with that expected from a ‘normal’ comet,” said ESA.

Context

Comet 3I/ATLAS represents a unique opportunity to study material that has travelled through interstellar space for potentially billions of years. Unlike previous interstellar objects, scientists have not found evidence of unusual behaviour, such as erratic jets or fragmentation, that would differentiate it from typical long-period comets formed within the solar system.

What’s next

Researchers from the instrument teams will convene in late March 2026 to compare findings and consolidate their conclusions regarding 3I/ATLAS. This meeting will play a crucial role in interpreting the comet's significance in understanding the chemistry and structure of distant star systems.
