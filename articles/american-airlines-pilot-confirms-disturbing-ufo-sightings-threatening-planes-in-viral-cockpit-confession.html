American Airlines Pilot Confirms UFO Sightings Threatening Planes

Captain Steve, an American Airlines pilot with 426,000 TikTok followers, has revealed unsettling details about unidentified flying objects (UFOs) and drones that pose risks to aviation safety. In a recent Q&A session, he discussed these threats while flying over various regions, particularly mentioning the Canadian Maritimes. No injuries or fatalities have been reported in connection with these incidents.

This information is significant as it highlights growing concerns about the safety of commercial air travel. The Federal Aviation Administration (FAA) has reported a rise in near-midair collisions involving drones, prompting regulatory agencies to implement strict penalties for violations.

Key facts

  • 64% of near-midair collisions in 2024 involved unmanned aerial systems.
  • The FAA receives approximately 100 safety reports monthly regarding drones near commercial aircraft.
  • A flight crew in November 2024 spotted a drone 300 feet from their cockpit.
  • A Newark pilot reported a drone within 50 feet of their aircraft earlier in 2024.
  • Fines for reckless drone use can reach £60,000 ($75,000) per violation.

Timeline

In November 2024, a flight crew approaching San Francisco encountered a drone just 300 feet from their cockpit. Earlier that year, a Newark pilot reported a similar close call with a drone flying within 50 feet of their wing. Captain Steve's revelations have surfaced amid increasing reports of drone-related incidents affecting aviation safety.

Official statements

“I’ve seen drones go by before. That’s disturbing,” Captain Steve said during his TikTok session. “The threat of drones is not merely hypothetical.”
“It causes them to light up and not light up. So sometimes it can look they’re moving around but they’re not. They’re actually stationary,” he explained, referring to Starlink satellites.

Context

UAP (Unidentified Aerial Phenomena) sightings have become common, with many reports attributed to low-orbit satellite constellations like Starlink. These satellites can create visual phenomena that confuse pilots. The Pentagon's All-domain Anomaly Resolution Office has confirmed that many recent sightings are misidentified satellites.

The challenges posed by drones and satellite phenomena underscore the complexities pilots face in maintaining safety in increasingly crowded airspace.

What’s next

Regulatory agencies continue to monitor drone activity closely and enforce strict penalties for violations. Investigations into specific incidents are ongoing, and further measures may be implemented to enhance aviation safety.
