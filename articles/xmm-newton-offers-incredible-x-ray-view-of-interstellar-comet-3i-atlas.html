XMM-Newton Offers Incredible X-ray View of Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS

Published 2025-12-12 21:41 UTC

XMM-Newton Offers Incredible X-ray View of Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS

Astronomers have captured an X-ray image of interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS using ESA’s XMM-Newton observatory on December 3, 2025. The comet was located approximately 282-285 million km from the spacecraft during the 20-hour observation period. No injuries or fatalities have been reported.

This observation is significant as it marks the third confirmed interstellar object detected passing through the Solar System, following 1I/ʻOumuamua and 2I/Borisov. The data collected will help scientists understand the composition of interstellar objects and the interactions between solar wind and cometary gases.

Key facts

  • Observation date: December 3, 2025
  • Distance from XMM-Newton: 282-285 million km
  • Observation duration: 20 hours
  • Instrument used: European Photon Imaging Camera (EPIC)-PN
  • Previous interstellar objects: 1I/ʻOumuamua, 2I/Borisov

Timeline

On December 3, 2025, the XMM-Newton observatory conducted a 20-hour observation of interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS. The comet was positioned approximately 282-285 million km from the spacecraft. The observatory utilized its EPIC-PN camera, the most sensitive X-ray camera available, to capture the comet's X-ray emissions.

The X-ray image revealed a bright red spot at the center, indicating low-energy X-rays emitted from the comet, with surrounding areas showing faint gradients of purple and blue. This color differentiation highlights the interaction of gas molecules from the comet with the solar wind.

Official statements

“This XMM-Newton image shows the comet glowing in low-energy X-rays: blue marks empty space with very few X-rays, while red highlights the comet’s X-ray glow,” members of the XMM-Newton team said in a statement.
“These X-rays can come from the interaction of the solar wind with gases like water vapor, carbon dioxide, or carbon monoxide — which telescopes such as the NASA/ESA/CSA James Webb Space Telescope and NASA’s SPHEREx have already detected,” they added.
“This makes X-ray observations a powerful tool. They allow scientists to detect and study gases that other instruments can’t easily spot,” the team noted.

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third interstellar object confirmed by astronomers, following 1I/ʻOumuamua and 2I/Borisov. The first detected interstellar object, 1I/ʻOumuamua, raised interest regarding its possible composition, with some scientists suggesting it may be made of exotic ice, like nitrogen or hydrogen. Observations of 3I/ATLAS provide a new opportunity to study interstellar material.

What’s next

Scientists plan to analyze the data collected from the X-ray observations of 3I/ATLAS to better understand its composition and characteristics. Further studies may complement this data, aiding in the understanding of interstellar objects.
