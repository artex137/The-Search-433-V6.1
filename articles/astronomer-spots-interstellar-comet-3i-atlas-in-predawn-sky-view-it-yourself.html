Astronomer Spots Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS in Predawn Sky – View It Yourself

Comet 3I/ATLAS has become visible again after a brief disappearance during its close approach to the sun on October 30, 2023. Discovered on July 1, 2023, it is the third interstellar object identified entering our solar system. The comet is believed to have originated from a distant star, prompting scientists to analyze its unique characteristics closely.

This visibility is significant for both scientists and amateur astronomers. Qicheng Zhang, a postdoctoral fellow at Lowell Observatory in Arizona, successfully imaged the comet on November 1, 2023, and encourages observers to take advantage of this rare opportunity to view or photograph the comet before it disappears again.

Key facts

  • Date of visibility: Early November 2023
  • Location: Constellation Virgo, eastern horizon
  • Best time: Just before dawn
  • Required equipment: Telescope or camera for observation
  • Current appearance: Fuzzy blob among nearby stars

Timeline

Comet 3I/ATLAS was discovered on July 1, 2023. It approached the sun on October 30, leading to a brief disappearance. Observations resumed on November 1, 2023, when Qicheng Zhang captured images of the comet.

Official statements

“If the comet fades quickly, it may reach its peak visibility in one to two weeks,” said Qicheng Zhang, emphasizing the unpredictable nature of comets.

Context

Comet 3I/ATLAS is significant as the third interstellar object identified entering our solar system. Its discovery and observation provide valuable data for scientists studying the origins of celestial bodies.

What’s next

Observers are encouraged to use telescopes or cameras capable of long exposures to capture images of Comet 3I/ATLAS before it moves further from the sun and becomes less visible.
