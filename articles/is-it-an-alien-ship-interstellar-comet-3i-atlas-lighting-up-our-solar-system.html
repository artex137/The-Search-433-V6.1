Is it an alien ship? Interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS lighting up our Solar System

The interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS has captured the attention of social media users worldwide after its discovery on July 1, 2025, by the ATLAS survey in Chile. The comet, which travels at over 60 km per second, is currently on a hyperbolic trajectory, indicating it is not bound by the Sun's gravity and is visiting from another star system. There are no reported injuries or fatalities associated with this event.

This discovery matters because 3I/ATLAS is only the third interstellar object detected in our Solar System, following 2017's Oumuamua and 2019's 2I/Borisov. As it approaches the Sun, astronomers are preparing to study this unique object closely, which offers a rare opportunity to learn about materials formed around another star billions of years ago.

Key facts

  • Discovered on July 1, 2025, by the ATLAS survey in Chile.
  • Travelling at a speed of over 60 km per second.
  • Closest approach to the Sun is expected on October 29-30, 2025, at approximately 1.36 AU away.
  • Will remain about 270 million km away from Earth.
  • High carbon dioxide content and faint dust tail noted by astronomers.

Timeline

July 1, 2025: 3I/ATLAS is discovered by the ATLAS survey in Chile.

October 29-30, 2025: The comet makes its closest approach to the Sun, providing a key opportunity for observation.

Throughout this period, telescopes on Earth and future space missions will monitor the comet closely.

Official statements

“We shouldn’t rule out the artificial,” said Professor Avi Loeb of Harvard University, regarding the unusual characteristics of the comet.

Context

3I/ATLAS joins a rare group of interstellar objects, including Oumuamua, which was noted for its peculiar acceleration, and 2I/Borisov, a more traditional icy comet. These discoveries challenge our understanding of the cosmos and raise questions about the potential for extraterrestrial life.

What’s next

Astronomers will continue to observe 3I/ATLAS as it approaches the Sun, with plans for monitoring by telescopes on Earth and upcoming space missions like NASA's SPHEREx. This event provides a fleeting opportunity to study materials from another star system.
