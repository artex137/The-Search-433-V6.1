The Mysterious Interstellar Object May Be Slamming on the Brakes, Scientist Says

Scientists have observed an unusual interstellar object, named 3I-ATLAS, which has been causing excitement in the astronomical community since its discovery in early July. The object, believed to be an interstellar comet, is currently hidden behind the Sun and will remain unobservable from Earth for the next month.

This discovery is significant as it could provide insights into the origins of interstellar objects. Harvard researcher Avi Loeb has suggested that the object may be an alien spacecraft, a claim that has been met with skepticism from many in the scientific community. The latest images from the Nordic Optical Telescope in the Canary Islands indicate that the object has developed an "anti-tail," leading to speculation about its behavior as it approaches the Sun.

Key facts

  • Object name: 3I-ATLAS
  • Discovery date: Early July
  • Current location: Behind the Sun
  • Next observable date: December 19
  • Key researcher: Avi Loeb, Harvard University
  • Telescope used: Nordic Optical Telescope, Canary Islands

Timeline

3I-ATLAS was first spotted in early July 2023. Since then, scientists have monitored its trajectory and behavior. The object is currently behind the Sun, making it unobservable from Earth. Recent images from the Nordic Optical Telescope have shown that the object has developed an anti-tail, a phenomenon linked to its approach to the Sun.

Official statements

“If the object is an alien spacecraft slowing down, and the anti-tail is braking thrust, then this change from anti-tail to tail would be entirely expected near perihelion,” Loeb stated in his latest blog.
“The transition would constitute a technosignature in the form of an unexpected phenomenon indicative of controlled maneuvering,” Loeb continued, referencing potential intentions for the object’s trajectory.

Context

The observation of interstellar objects has gained attention since the discovery of 'Oumuamua, the first known interstellar object, in 2017. The scientific community has since debated the origins and nature of such objects, with theories ranging from natural formations to artificial constructs.

What’s next

Scientists will continue to monitor 3I-ATLAS and expect to observe it again on December 19. At that point, researchers will determine if its trajectory has changed or if it remains a natural comet.
