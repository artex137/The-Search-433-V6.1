Space Revelation - NASA Scientists Discover the Mysterious Object 3I/ATLAS Is Far Larger Than Previously Estimated

NASA scientists have confirmed that the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS, detected earlier this year, is significantly larger than previously estimated. This discovery marks it as the third interstellar object ever identified, following ʻOumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019. The object, which has been traveling through our solar system, poses no threat to Earth at this time.

The findings are crucial as they expand our understanding of celestial objects from beyond our solar system. Researchers are closely monitoring 3I/ATLAS as it approaches Mars’ orbit, providing a unique opportunity for study. NASA’s HiRISE camera aboard the Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter will track the object in high resolution, offering insights into its composition and behavior.

Key facts

  • 3I/ATLAS is over 5 kilometers wide and weighs more than 33 billion tons.
  • It is currently passing near Jupiter, Venus, and Mars.
  • The object will come within 2.68 million kilometers of Mars’ orbit.
  • NASA's HiRISE camera will monitor 3I/ATLAS for further study.
  • Avi Loeb, a Harvard astrophysicist, has led the analysis of the object.

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first detected crossing into our solar system earlier this year. Initially thought to be a comet, further observations revealed its unusual behavior and mass loss patterns. Researchers have concluded that it is significantly larger than both ʻOumuamua and 2I/Borisov.

As it approaches Mars' orbit, scientists continue to analyze its trajectory and characteristics. The object’s green glow, attributed to the release of carbon-based gases, has also been noted, though it is not considered hazardous.

Official statements

Avi Loeb stated, “3I/ATLAS is a beast. It raises questions about whether it formed naturally or if it might be something else entirely.”

Context

This discovery follows the detection of two previous interstellar objects, which have provided valuable data on the nature of celestial bodies from other star systems. The study of 3I/ATLAS could further enhance our understanding of the formation and behavior of such objects.

What’s next

NASA and other astronomers will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS as it approaches Mars. Further observations will help clarify its composition and origin, contributing to the ongoing exploration of interstellar phenomena.
