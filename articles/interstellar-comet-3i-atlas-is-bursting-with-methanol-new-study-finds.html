Interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS is 'bursting with methanol,' new study finds

A new study has revealed that interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS is rich in methanol, according to researchers using the Atacama Large Millimeter/submillimeter Array (ALMA) in Chile. The findings were made as the comet approaches the sun, releasing gas and dust that form a glowing halo around its core.

This discovery is significant because it could provide insights into how planets and icy bodies form around stars in different solar systems. The chemical composition of 3I/ATLAS indicates that it likely formed in a planetary system with conditions distinct from those of our own solar system.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS was discovered in July 2025 by the Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System.
  • ALMA detected strong signals of methanol (CH3OH) in the comet's gas cloud.
  • 3I/ATLAS is the third confirmed interstellar object entering the solar system.
  • The first two were 'Oumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019.
  • The study has been submitted for publication and is available as a preprint on arXiv.

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was discovered in July 2025. As it approached the sun, sunlight warmed its icy surface, causing it to release gas and dust. ALMA began observing the comet shortly thereafter, identifying high levels of methanol in its coma.

Researchers have monitored 3I/ATLAS using various telescopes, including the Hubble Space Telescope and the James Webb Space Telescope, to analyze its behavior and composition in detail.

Official statements

"Observing 3I/ATLAS is like taking a fingerprint from another solar system," said Nathan Roth, lead author of the study and a professor at American University. "The details reveal what it's made of, and it's bursting with methanol in a way we just don't usually see in comets in our own solar system."

Context

The unusual chemical composition of 3I/ATLAS suggests it formed under different conditions than those seen in comets from our solar system. This adds to the understanding of how celestial bodies can vary in composition based on their origins.

Prior to this, the first confirmed interstellar objects, 'Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov, provided initial insights into the diversity of materials found in space, but 3I/ATLAS offers a unique chemical profile that may enhance this understanding.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to analyze data from 3I/ATLAS as it travels through the inner solar system, providing further opportunities to study its chemical makeup and behavior.
