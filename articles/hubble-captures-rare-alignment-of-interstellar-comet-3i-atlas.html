Hubble Captures Rare Alignment of Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS

Published 2026-01-29 04:45 UTC

Hubble Captures Rare Alignment of Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS

On January 22, 2026, the NASA/ESA Hubble Space Telescope captured images of the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS during a rare alignment with the Sun-Earth axis. The event allowed astronomers to observe intricate jet structures and an extended anti-tail of the comet, but no injuries or fatalities were reported.

This alignment is significant as it provides insights into the behavior of interstellar objects. Authorities, including astronomers from Harvard University and the INAF-Padova Observatory, have noted the importance of studying such objects to enhance our understanding of materials from other stellar systems.

Key facts

  • Date of observation: January 22, 2026
  • Time of first imaging: 13:10 UTC
  • Time of additional imaging: 13:40 UTC
  • Distance from Earth-Sun axis: 0.69 degrees
  • Astronomers involved: Professor Abraham Loeb and Dr. Mauro Barbieri
  • Location of observations: Hubble’s Wide Field Camera 3 (WFC3)

Timeline

On January 22, 2026, the Hubble Space Telescope began capturing images of 3I/ATLAS at 13:10 UTC, shortly followed by additional observations at 13:40 UTC. The comet's alignment with the Earth and Sun allowed for unprecedented observations of its structure.

During this period, Earth positioned itself nearly directly between the Sun and the comet, resulting in a notable increase in the comet's brightness. The processed images revealed a system of four jets emanating from 3I/ATLAS, including a prominent anti-tail.

Official statements

“3I/ATLAS provides a unique opportunity to study materials from other stellar systems,” said Professor Abraham Loeb.
“The alignment conditions observed may not be repeated for decades,” noted astronomer Man-To Hui.

Context

The study of interstellar comets is crucial for understanding the composition of celestial bodies beyond our solar system. Previous interstellar visitors, like 1I/Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov, lacked detectable gas or were only observable at greater phase angles, limiting their analysis.

What’s next

The findings from Hubble are expected to lead to more detailed studies of the composition and behavior of interstellar bodies, enhancing our understanding of celestial phenomena beyond our solar system.
