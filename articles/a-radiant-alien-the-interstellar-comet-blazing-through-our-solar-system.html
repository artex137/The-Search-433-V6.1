A Radiant Alien: The Interstellar Comet Blazing Through Our Solar System

The interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS has made its closest recorded approach to Earth on December 19, 2023, at a distance of approximately 270 million kilometres. There have been no reports of injuries or fatalities related to this astronomical event.

This close encounter provides a unique opportunity for scientists to study the comet and its emissions. The comet, only the third interstellar visitor detected in our solar system, has exhibited intense X-ray radiation, which has generated significant interest among astronomers. The European Space Agency's XMM-Newton observatory and the X-Ray Imaging and Spectroscopy Mission (XRISM) have been utilized to gather data on this rare object.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS approached Earth on December 19, 2023.
  • It reached a distance of approximately 270 million kilometres from Earth.
  • The comet emits X-ray radiation extending up to 400,000 kilometres.
  • Observations were conducted by ESA's XMM-Newton and NASA's XRISM.
  • Distinct spectral signatures of carbon, nitrogen, and oxygen have been identified.
  • The comet is expected to pass near Jupiter in the coming months.

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was discovered earlier in 2023 and has been under observation since its detection. The comet reached its perigee on December 19, 2023, allowing for detailed study of its emissions.

In early December, while the comet was approximately 285 million kilometres from XMM-Newton, observations confirmed the X-ray emissions detected by XRISM.

As the comet moves away from the inner solar system, it is scheduled to pass near Jupiter, where researchers plan to utilize NASA's Juno spacecraft for further investigation.

Official statements

“We are witnessing an unprecedented opportunity to study an interstellar comet in detail. The data we have gathered will enhance our understanding of these rare visitors from other star systems.” – ESA representative

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third interstellar object identified in our solar system, following 1I/ʻOumuamua and 2I/Borisov. Previous encounters with these objects provided limited data, making the current observations of 3I/ATLAS particularly significant for planetary science.

What’s next

Researchers plan to continue observing 3I/ATLAS as it approaches Jupiter. They aim to conduct a flyby study using NASA's Juno spacecraft, marking a novel approach to studying an interstellar object with a mission designed for planetary science.
