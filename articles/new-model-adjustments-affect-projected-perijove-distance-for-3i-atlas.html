New model adjustments affect projected perijove distance for 3I/ATLAS

The projected perijove distance of the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS has been updated following changes in its non-gravitational acceleration modeling. Harvard Professor Avi Loeb reported the updates on NASA’s JPL Horizons website, indicating new figures for the object’s trajectory as it approaches Jupiter on March 16, 2026.

This revision is significant because it alters the forecast of how close 3I/ATLAS will come to Jupiter, which has implications for future observations and studies of the comet's behavior and composition. Authorities at NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL) are continuing to monitor the object and update the model as new data becomes available.

Key facts

  • Initial radial acceleration component A1 on October 30, 2025: 1.6x10-6 au per day squared.
  • Revised A1 on November 24, 2025: 4x10-7 au per day squared.
  • Final A1 adjustment: 6.8x10-8 au per day squared.
  • New projected perijove distance: 53.587 (+/- 0.045) million kilometers.
  • Jupiter’s Hill radius at perijove time: 53.502 million kilometers.
  • Next encounter with Jupiter: March 16, 2026.

Timeline

On October 30, 2025, the initial radial acceleration component A1 for 3I/ATLAS was recorded at 1.6x10-6 au per day squared. By November 24, 2025, this coefficient was revised to 4x10-7 au per day squared. Further adjustments led to a final value of 6.8x10-8 au per day squared, implementing a new 1/r² model for the object's trajectory.

As a result of these adjustments, the projected perijove distance changed to 53.587 million kilometers, slightly beyond Jupiter's Hill radius. Historical forecasts indicated a minimum perijove distance of 53.445 million kilometers, aligning closely with Jupiter's gravitational influence.

Official statements

“This unexpected match between 3I/ATLAS’s perijove distance and Jupiter’s Hill radius raises interesting dynamics that we will monitor closely,” said Avi Loeb.

Context

Prior to the recent model adjustments, the perijove distance for 3I/ATLAS was forecasted to align closely with Jupiter’s Hill radius, indicating significant gravitational interactions. The previous model relied on a steeper radial dependence suitable for water ice, while the new model accounts for the sublimation of carbon dioxide ice within a heliocentric distance of 5 au.

What’s next

Future observations from spacecraft such as Juno, Juice, or Psyche will verify the actual perijove distance of 3I/ATLAS as it approaches Jupiter. JPL Horizons will continue to refine its model based on new astrometric data.
