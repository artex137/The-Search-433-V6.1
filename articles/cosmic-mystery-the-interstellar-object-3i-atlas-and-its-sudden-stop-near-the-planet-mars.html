Cosmic mystery: the interstellar object 3I/Atlas and its sudden stop near the planet Mars

The interstellar object 3I/Atlas has exhibited unprecedented behavior during its passage near Mars, remaining motionless for several days at the end of 2025. This anomaly has prompted intense scrutiny from the international astronomical community, as it contradicts established laws of celestial mechanics.

Scientists are now analyzing a vast amount of data collected during this event, which has raised questions about the forces governing celestial bodies. The temporary halt of 3I/Atlas has transformed it into a unique subject for research, leading to a reallocation of resources by space agencies for continuous monitoring.

Key facts

  • Object: 3I/Atlas
  • Location: Near Mars
  • Date of event: End of 2025
  • Size estimates: Between 320 meters and 5.6 kilometers in diameter
  • Age: Approximately 10 billion years old
  • Observing agencies: NASA, global network of observatories

Timeline

At the end of 2025, 3I/Atlas approached Mars and unexpectedly stopped moving, remaining motionless for several days. This behavior was documented by NASA and various observatories worldwide. Following the event, scientists began analyzing the data collected during its passage.

As the investigation progressed, two primary theories emerged regarding the object's immobility. The first theory suggests electromagnetic interactions, while the second considers an internal process involving gas emissions. Both hypotheses challenge existing models of celestial mechanics.

Official statements

“The pause turned 3I/Atlas into a unique natural laboratory for studying rare cosmic interactions,” said a NASA spokesperson.
“The need to incorporate complex non-gravitational interactions has become evident,” stated an astrophysicist involved in the research.

Context

The behavior of 3I/Atlas represents a significant challenge to existing celestial mechanics, which have been established through centuries of observation. Objects on hyperbolic trajectories are expected to maintain high speeds and not exhibit such drastic changes in motion. Previous interstellar visitors have not displayed similar anomalies.

What’s next

As 3I/Atlas continues its journey through the inner Solar System, it is expected to reach its perihelion at the end of the year. Scientists will closely monitor its trajectory, particularly as it approaches Venus in November and Jupiter in March of the following year. Ongoing analyses of the collected data may lead to new insights into the dynamics of celestial bodies.
