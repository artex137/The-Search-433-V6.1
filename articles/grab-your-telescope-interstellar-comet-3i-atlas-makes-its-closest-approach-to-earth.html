Grab Your Telescope! Interstellar Comet 3I/Atlas Makes Its Closest Approach To Earth

The interstellar comet 3I/Atlas will pass within 267 million kilometres of Earth on Friday, marking its closest approach during its journey through the solar system. There are no reports of injuries or fatalities associated with the comet's visit.

This event is significant as it provides a rare opportunity for astronomers and enthusiasts to observe a comet that originated from another star system. NASA is actively monitoring 3I/Atlas with its space telescopes, encouraging backyard astronomers to take advantage of this fleeting chance to view the comet before it fades from visibility.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/Atlas is estimated to be between 440 metres and 1,100 metres in size.
  • The comet will pass within 267 million kilometres of Earth on Friday.
  • It will come much closer to Jupiter in March, at a distance of 53 million kilometres.
  • 3I/Atlas is the third known interstellar object to enter our solar system.
  • The comet was discovered by NASA's Atlas telescope in Chile in July.
  • The comet is expected to reach interstellar space in the mid-2030s.

Timeline

Comet 3I/Atlas was discovered in July 2023 by NASA's Atlas telescope while searching for potentially hazardous asteroids. As it approaches its closest point to Earth, astronomers have focused their observations on this unique interstellar visitor.

In March 2024, 3I/Atlas will make a close pass by Jupiter, coming within 53 million kilometres of the gas giant.

After its close approach, the comet will continue on its trajectory into interstellar space, not returning to the solar system.

Official statements

“This comet may have originated in a star system much older than ours, making it a tantalizing target for study,” said Paul Chodas, director of NASA’s Center for Near Earth Object Studies.

Context

Interstellar comets, like 3I/Atlas, are distinct from comets originating within our solar system. The first confirmed interstellar visitor was discovered in 2017, followed by another in 2019. These comets provide valuable insights into the formation and evolution of star systems across the Milky Way.

What’s next

As 3I/Atlas continues its journey, NASA will maintain its observations until the comet fades from view. Astronomers hope to gather as much data as possible before it departs for interstellar space.
