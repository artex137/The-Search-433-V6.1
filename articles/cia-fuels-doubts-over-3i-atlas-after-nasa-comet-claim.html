CIA Fuels Doubts Over 3I/ATLAS After NASA Comet Claim

Nearly two weeks after the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS made its closest approach to Earth on December 19, 2025, a renewed debate has emerged regarding its true nature. The Central Intelligence Agency (CIA) has responded to a Freedom of Information Act (FOIA) request, raising questions about previous assessments by the National Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA).

This matter is significant as it highlights ongoing concerns in the scientific community about the classification of 3I/ATLAS. While NASA has asserted that the object is a natural comet with no ties to alien technology, some scientists, including Harvard astrophysicist Avi Loeb, continue to challenge this conclusion.

Key facts

  • 3I/ATLAS made its closest flyby of Earth on December 19, 2025.
  • The CIA responded to a FOIA request stating it could “neither confirm nor deny” the existence of relevant documents.
  • Nasa classified 3I/ATLAS as a natural comet.
  • Avi Loeb has raised concerns about anomalies in the object's behaviour.
  • Scientists expect 3I/ATLAS to leave the solar system entirely by 2030.

Timeline

On December 19, 2025, 3I/ATLAS made its closest approach to Earth. Following this event, NASA released findings classifying the object as a natural comet, asserting no links to unidentified flying objects. On December 31, 2025, the CIA responded to a FOIA request regarding 3I/ATLAS, stating it could neither confirm nor deny the existence of relevant documents, which has sparked further scrutiny.

Avi Loeb has since pointed out various anomalies in the object's behaviour, including an unusual anti-tail and unexpected course changes, which he argues do not align with typical comet behaviour. This ongoing debate continues to attract attention within the scientific community.

Official statements

Nasa has classified 3I/ATLAS as a natural comet and ruled out any links to unidentified flying objects.
The CIA's response to the FOIA request has fueled speculation about classified knowledge.

Context

The CIA's guarded language in its response is standard for intelligence agencies but often leads to public speculation. The scientific community remains divided, with some experts advocating for a more thorough investigation into the object's characteristics.

What’s next

As the debate continues, scientists will monitor 3I/ATLAS as it travels toward Jupiter, with expectations it will exit the solar system by 2030. Further inquiries may be pursued regarding the CIA's response and the object’s behaviour.
