Interstellar object could be a time capsule from an era before the Universe

The interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, estimated to be around 10 billion years old, offers scientists a unique opportunity to study the evolution of celestial objects from other stellar systems. The findings were published in the Astrophysical Journal and are available on Arxiv.

This discovery is significant as it may provide insights into the formation of the galaxy and the universe. Researchers from the Center for Research in Information and Communication Technologies have indicated that 3I/ATLAS could serve as a time capsule, revealing details about the oldest planetary systems.

Key facts

  • Object: 3I/ATLAS
  • Estimated age: 10 billion years
  • Nucleus size: approximately 5.6 kilometers
  • Potential mass: more than 33 billion tons
  • Significant encounters tracked: 62 at the 2σ level
  • Closest notable perturber: Gaia DR3 6863591389529611264 at 0.30 parsecs
  • Velocity change from closest encounter: |∆v| ≃ 5 × 10⁻⁴ km/s

Timeline

Researchers have tracked the trajectory of 3I/ATLAS over time. They identified a total of 93 nominal encounters, but only 62 of these encounters were deemed significant. None of the encounters resulted in substantial disturbances to the comet's path.

The closest stellar flybys within the last 10 million years have not provided an explanation for the object's current trajectory or its origin, according to the research team.

Official statements

“Each observation is like opening a window into the past of the Universe,” said Pérez-Couto, a researcher and team leader.
“Taken together, all the data indicate that although 3I/ATLAS follows an orbit in the thin disk in the solar neighborhood, it may be an ancient object,” the research team noted.

Context

The study of interstellar objects is a relatively new field, with 3I/ATLAS being one of the few known comets of its kind. This research adds to the understanding of ancient celestial bodies and their origins, contributing to theories about the early stages of planetary formation.

What’s next

The research team plans to continue observing 3I/ATLAS to gather more data and refine their understanding of its trajectory and origins.
