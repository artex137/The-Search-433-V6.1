Interstellar Comet 3I/Atlas Has Another Surprise: It’s Full of Alcohol

Observations from the ALMA telescope in Chile's Atacama Desert have revealed that interstellar comet 3I/Atlas is heavily enriched in methanol, a type of alcohol. The findings, published on arXiv, show that 3I/Atlas contains up to four times the typical amount of methanol found in comets, marking it as the second most methanol-rich comet ever measured.

This discovery is significant as it suggests that 3I/Atlas has an unusual chemical composition, potentially formed in a colder, more irradiated environment than that of typical solar system comets. Researchers are investigating the implications of these findings for our understanding of comet formation and the characteristics of interstellar objects.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/Atlas contains up to four times the typical amount of methanol.
  • It is the second most methanol-rich comet ever measured.
  • Other organic compounds detected include carbon dioxide, iron, and nitrogen.
  • 3I/Atlas is moving away from the solar system at 60 kilometers per second.
  • It is only the third confirmed interstellar object in history.

Timeline

Recent observations from the ALMA telescope have provided insights into the chemical composition of comet 3I/Atlas. The study indicated that the comet's coma is significantly enriched in methanol, reinforcing its classification as a unique celestial body.

Researchers have also identified high levels of other organic compounds, suggesting a complex formation history for the comet. The study posits that 3I/Atlas may belong to the category of hyperactive comets, which produce more water vapor than expected from their surface alone.

Official statements

“The combination of excess methanol and other atypical chemical ratios supports the hypothesis that 3I/Atlas formed in an environment that is colder, more irradiated, or chemically distinct,” the researchers stated.

Context

Prior studies of comets in the solar system have shown varying levels of methanol and other organic compounds. Comet C/2016 R2, discovered ten years ago, remains the only comet with a higher methanol concentration than 3I/Atlas.

The findings from 3I/Atlas contribute to ongoing research into the origins and characteristics of comets, particularly those that originate outside the solar system.

What’s next

Researchers plan to continue studying comet 3I/Atlas and its chemical makeup as it moves away from the solar system. Future observations with advanced instruments are expected to enhance understanding of interstellar objects and their formation processes.
