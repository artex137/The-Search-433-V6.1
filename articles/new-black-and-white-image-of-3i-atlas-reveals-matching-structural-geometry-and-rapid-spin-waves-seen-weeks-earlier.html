New Black-and-White Image of 3I/ATLAS Reveals Matching Structural Geometry and Rapid Spin Waves

A new black-and-white image of comet 3I/ATLAS has confirmed findings of rapid spin waves and a consistent geometric structure, according to Ray’s Astronomy. The image, taken recently, matches an earlier observation from October 21, 2025, captured by AstroPhotoG. No injuries or fatalities have been reported.

This discovery is significant as it challenges the understanding of cometary behavior. The findings suggest that 3I/ATLAS may not conform to characteristics typical of natural comets. Authorities, including NASA and ESA, are under increasing pressure for transparency regarding the true nature of this interstellar object.

Key facts

  • New image captured by Ray’s Astronomy, date not disclosed.
  • Previous image taken on October 21, 2025, by AstroPhotoG.
  • 3I/ATLAS shows rapid spin waves moving through its coma.
  • The object rotates on a timescale of minutes.
  • Nickel-vapor signature detected, lacking detectable iron.
  • Next high-resolution observations expected within days.

Timeline

On October 21, 2025, AstroPhotoG captured an image of 3I/ATLAS, revealing a bright central core and a structured envelope. This observation raised questions due to its geometric symmetry and unusual features. Weeks later, Ray’s Astronomy released a new image that confirmed the shape and structural details of the object, indicating it spins significantly faster than typical comets.

As data from both observations align, scientists are prompted to reconsider the classification of 3I/ATLAS. The new findings suggest a coherent shape beneath its coma, contrary to known comet behavior.

Official statements

“The persistence of its geometric signature and the extraordinary spin rate leave scientists with uncomfortable questions,” Ray’s Astronomy noted in a statement.

Context

Natural comets typically exhibit chaotic jets and do not maintain stable geometric shapes over time. Previous observations of 3I/ATLAS have shown anomalies, including a nickel-rich vapor signature without iron, which is uncommon for comets. The new data raises the possibility that 3I/ATLAS may be an engineered object or a remnant of exotic astrophysical chemistry.

What’s next

As 3I/ATLAS approaches its December visibility window, new datasets are expected to arrive. Scientists will continue to analyze the structural alignment between early and late-phase images, with upcoming observations likely to provide further clarity on the nature of this interstellar visitor.
