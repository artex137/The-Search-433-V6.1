Comet C/2025 K1 ATLAS fragmentation: 10 & 11 Nov. 2025 images.

Published 2025-11-11 10:31 UTC

Comet C/2025 K1 ATLAS fragmentation observed in images

Images of Comet C/2025 K1 ATLAS have shown a fragmentation event occurring on 10 and 11 November 2025, according to the Virtual Telescope Project in Manciano, Italy. The comet's "double" nucleus and a spike-like feature pointing towards the Sun have been captured in detailed photographs.

This fragmentation event is significant for astronomers, as it allows for a better understanding of cometary structures and behavior. The Virtual Telescope Project has utilized advanced robotic imaging to document and analyze the comet's changes in real-time.

Key facts

  • Event date: 10 and 11 November 2025
  • Location: Manciano, Italy
  • Imaging equipment: Celestron C14+Paramount ME+SBIG ST-10XME and ARTEC250+Paramount ME+C3Pro61000EC robotic units
  • Exposure time: Nine 120-second unfiltered exposures
  • Observational unit: Virtual Telescope Project

Timeline

On 10 November 2025, the first images of Comet C/2025 K1 ATLAS were taken following the announcement of its nucleus break-up. The images revealed a secondary nucleus and a spike-like feature. The following day, additional imaging confirmed the observations.

The images released show the comet's fragmented structure, with the secondary nucleus clearly visible. The data from these images has been processed to highlight the comet's features, enhancing the scientific understanding of its current state.

Official statements

“The double nucleus is well visible, as well a spike-like feature pointing in solar (SE) direction,” said Gianluca Masi, a representative of the Virtual Telescope Project.
“We could easily see the secondary nucleus, which was reported about 24 hours earlier,” Masi added.

Context

Fragmentation events in comets can provide insights into their composition and behavior. This is not the first time astronomers have observed such events, and they continue to study the implications for understanding cometary evolution.

The Virtual Telescope Project has previously documented significant astronomical events, contributing valuable data to the scientific community.

What’s next

Further analysis of the images is expected to continue, with astronomers examining the implications of the fragmentation for future studies of comets. No specific follow-up events or deadlines have been disclosed at this time.
