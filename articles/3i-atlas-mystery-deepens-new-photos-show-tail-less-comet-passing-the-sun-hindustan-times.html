3I/ATLAS mystery deepens; new photos show ‘tail-less’ comet passing the sun

The interstellar object 3I/ATLAS has puzzled scientists as recent images from the R. Naves Observatory in Spain, taken on November 5, show no visible tail, a typical feature of comets after they approach the Sun. There have been no reported injuries or fatalities related to this astronomical phenomenon.

This discovery raises questions about conventional comet models, as astrophysicists highlight the lack of a tail as a significant anomaly. Experts are currently analyzing the implications of these findings and monitoring 3I/ATLAS as it continues its journey through the solar system.

Key facts

  • Images taken on November 5, 2023, at R. Naves Observatory, Spain.
  • 3I/ATLAS has brightened fivefold after perihelion.
  • The object turned an unusual green hue and exhibited an “anti-tail” pointing toward the Sun.
  • 3I/ATLAS follows a hyperbolic trajectory, indicating it originates from outside the Solar System.
  • NASA's Mars Orbiter's HiRISE camera is expected to capture additional images, currently delayed due to a government shutdown.
  • 3I/ATLAS will pass near Jupiter on March 16, 2026, during which observations will be made by NASA and the European Space Agency.
  • Initial entry into the solar system occurred on June 14, 2025.

Timeline

On June 14, 2025, 3I/ATLAS made its initial entry into the solar system. Following its perihelion, the object brightened significantly and displayed unique characteristics, including a green hue and an anti-tail. The latest images taken on November 5, 2023, revealed the absence of a typical cometary tail.

Official statements

“If 3I/ATLAS continues to show no tail, it is likely not a naturally-occurring comet,” said Avi Loeb, an astrophysicist from Harvard, in a report on Medium.
“It exhibits non-gravitational acceleration, which requires massive evaporation of at least 13% of its mass, but preliminary post-perihelion images do not show evidence for it so far,” Loeb added.

Context

The discovery of 3I/ATLAS challenges established theories regarding comet formation and behavior. Astrophysicists have noted similar anomalies in the past, but none have presented such a stark deviation from expected cometary characteristics. The comparison with comet Lemmon illustrates these differences, as Lemmon exhibited a standard tail facing away from the Sun.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS as it approaches Jupiter in 2026. The release of new images from NASA’s Mars Orbiter will provide further insight into the object’s characteristics and behaviors.
