Alien interstellar comet 3I/Atlas invading our Solar System seen from Earth

A new image of interstellar comet 3IATLAS (C2025 N1 ATLAS) has been captured by the Virtual Telescope Project in Manciano, Italy, revealing significant changes in its appearance as it travels through the inner solar system. The image, taken on November 13, 2025, shows a bright coma and an extensive ion tail, which has become increasingly active during its journey.

This discovery is significant as 3IATLAS is only the third confirmed interstellar comet, following 1IOumuamua in 2017 and 2IBorisov in 2019. The comet's increasing visibility offers astronomers a unique opportunity to study the chemistry and dynamics of material from beyond our solar system. The Virtual Telescope Project has taken advantage of clear autumn skies to document the comet’s evolution.

Key facts

  • Comet Name: 3IATLAS (C2025 N1 ATLAS)
  • Date of Observation: November 13, 2025
  • Location: Manciano, Italy
  • Image Taken: Using 18 separate 120-second exposures
  • Altitude of Comet: 14 degrees above the eastern horizon
  • Moon Brightness: 61 percent illumination
  • Previous Interstellar Comets: 1IOumuamua (2017), 2IBorisov (2019)

Timeline

On November 13, 2025, astronomers from the Virtual Telescope Project captured a new image of the comet 3IATLAS. The image revealed a developed ion tail, showcasing the comet's increasing activity as it approaches perihelion, the point closest to the Sun.

Robotic telescopes in Manciano recorded the image under clear autumn skies, allowing for detailed observations despite the low altitude of the comet and the presence of a bright moon.

Official statements

“Exploiting the unusually good weather of this season, we imaged the interstellar comet 3IATLAS again, recording a much more developed ion tail,” said Gianluca Masi, astronomer and founder of the Virtual Telescope Project. “Looking at the picture, we see how the ion tail of 3IATLAS is clearly showing better and better.”

Context

3IATLAS is the third confirmed interstellar comet, providing a rare opportunity to study material originating from outside our solar system. Previous discoveries, such as 1IOumuamua and 2IBorisov, have sparked interest in the chemistry and dynamics of interstellar objects.

What’s next

As 3IATLAS approaches perihelion, astronomers expect its activity to increase further, potentially making it visible through small telescopes in the coming weeks. Researchers worldwide are monitoring the comet closely for new insights into interstellar material.
