Can You See 3I/ATLAS from Home? The Truth About Earth’s New Interstellar Visitor

On July 1, 2025, astronomers discovered a new comet designated as 3I/ATLAS in the night sky over Chile's Atacama Desert. The object, initially named A11pl3Z, was detected by the Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System (ATLAS) telescope in Río Hurtado. There have been no reported injuries or fatalities related to this event.

The arrival of 3I/ATLAS is significant as it represents the third confirmed interstellar object to visit our solar system, following 1I/ʻOumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019. As astronomers prepare for its closest approach to the Sun on October 29–30, 2025, data from various observatories, including Hubble and the James Webb Space Telescope (JWST), is being analyzed to understand its composition and trajectory.

Key facts

  • Discovery Date: July 1, 2025
  • Initial Designation: A11pl3Z
  • Current Designation: C/2025 N1 (ATLAS) or 3I/ATLAS
  • Closest Approach to Sun: October 29, 2025, at 11:47 UT
  • Peak Speed: 68.3 km/s near perihelion
  • Orbital Eccentricity: 6.1374
  • Observation Arc: 129 days (646 observations)

Timeline

3I/ATLAS has followed a clear trajectory since its discovery:

  • July 1, 2025: Discovered by ATLAS in Chile.
  • Late June 2025: Crossed Jupiter's orbit.
  • October 3, 2025: Passed Mars at a distance of 0.1935 AU, imaged by ESA's Mars Express.
  • November 3, 2025: Will approach Venus at a distance of 0.6491 AU.
  • December 19, 2025: Will depart from Earth at a distance of 1.797 AU.

Official statements

“This trajectory paints a dramatic arc: inbound from Sagittarius, it crossed Jupiter's orbit, brushed Mars, and will flirt with Venus before leaving our solar system,” said a spokesperson from NASA's JPL Horizons system.

Context

3I/ATLAS is part of a small group of interstellar visitors, with only two prior confirmed objects recorded in human history. The unique nature of such comets provides insight into the conditions of distant star systems and the formation of the early solar system.

What’s next

As 3I/ATLAS approaches its perihelion, astronomers will continue to gather and analyze data from various telescopes, including Hubble and JWST, to better understand its composition and behavior. Observations will continue through its flyby and into early 2026.
