Hubble’s new 3I/ATLAS data show twin jets as rare interstellar comet fades

NASA's Hubble Space Telescope has observed interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS on December 27, 2025, as it fades from view after a recent pass by Earth. The comet is currently on a hyperbolic trajectory, meaning it will not return to the solar system once it leaves.

This observation is significant as 3I/ATLAS is only the third confirmed interstellar object to be detected in the solar system. NASA officials state that these observations provide a rare opportunity to study material from another star system. Further observations are scheduled for early January.

Key facts

  • Date of observation: December 27, 2025
  • Next observed location: Past Jupiter in March 2026
  • Comet classification: Third confirmed interstellar visitor
  • Closest approach to the Sun: October 30, 2025
  • Scheduled follow-up observations: January 7, 2026, and onward

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first identified by the ATLAS survey telescope, which is part of NASA's planetary defense network. The comet made its closest approach to the Sun on October 30, 2025, just outside Mars' orbit. It passed Earth earlier in December before heading out of the solar system.

The latest data from Hubble reveals a double-jet structure in the comet, with an unusual sun-facing dust feature referred to as an “anti-tail.” This observation builds on earlier images taken on December 12 and contributes to ongoing studies of the comet.

Official statements

“Anti-tail is the term astronomers use when a comet shows a sun-facing dust feature — a rarer geometry than the familiar tail blown away from the Sun by light and the solar wind,” said Harvard astrophysicist Avi Loeb regarding the new findings.
“I can’t believe it’s taken this long to find three,” said NASA’s Paul Chodas, emphasizing the importance of improved observing capabilities for future discoveries.

Context

The first two confirmed interstellar objects, 1I/‘Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov, were discovered years apart and have provided valuable insight into the characteristics of objects originating from outside our solar system. With advancements in search technology, NASA officials anticipate more discoveries of interstellar visitors in the future.

What’s next

NASA plans to continue monitoring 3I/ATLAS with additional observations scheduled for January. This ongoing study aims to gather more data as the comet moves farther away from Earth.
