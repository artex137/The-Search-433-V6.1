Comet or alien probe? 3I/ATLAS just flew into our Solar System

3I/ATLAS, an interstellar object, has officially entered our solar system, moving at a speed of 209,000 km/h relative to the Sun. Discovered on July 1, 2025, by the Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System (ATLAS) telescope in Río Hurtado, Chile, the object has sparked debate among scientists regarding its nature and origin.

The significance of 3I/ATLAS lies in its potential to provide insights into planetary formation beyond our solar system. Authorities, including NASA and the European Space Agency (ESA), are actively monitoring its trajectory and characteristics using various telescopes and space probes.

Key facts

  • Object Name: 3I/ATLAS
  • Speed: 58 km/s (209,000 km/h)
  • Discovery Date: July 1, 2025
  • Closest Approach to Earth: 1.8 AU (170 million miles)
  • Perihelion Date: October 29-30, 2025
  • Estimated Size: Nucleus diameter of approximately 5.6 km, possibly up to 128 km
  • Monitoring Agencies: NASA, ESA

Timeline

On July 1, 2025, the ATLAS telescope first identified 3I/ATLAS, located about 420 million miles from the Sun. Initial observations showed no clear cometary features. However, follow-up observations on July 2 by several telescopes confirmed its cometary nature and interstellar origin.

The Minor Planet Center officially designated it on July 2, 2025, after global astronomers verified its extrasolar path. As it travels through our solar system, its closest approach to the Sun is expected in late October 2025.

Official statements

“Extraordinary anomalies require extraordinary explanations,” said Harvard astrophysicist Avi Loeb. “If we keep dismissing the possibility of non-natural origin simply because it’s ‘too wild,’ we risk missing out on the truth.”

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third confirmed interstellar object to visit our solar system, following 1I/ʻOumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019. Its unusual speed and hyperbolic trajectory have led to discussions regarding its classification as a comet or a potential alien probe.

What’s next

Nasa and ESA will continue to track 3I/ATLAS using various telescopes and space missions to gather data on its size, composition, and activity as it approaches perihelion in late October 2025.
