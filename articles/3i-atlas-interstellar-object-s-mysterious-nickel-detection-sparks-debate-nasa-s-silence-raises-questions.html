3I/ATLAS: Interstellar Object’s Mysterious Nickel Detection Sparks Debate, NASA’s Silence Raises Questions

The interstellar object 3I/ATLAS has raised eyebrows among astronomers after nickel vapor was detected in its emissions, a finding described as "highly unusual" and chemically inexplicable under known cosmic conditions. Detected in July 2025 by the Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System (ATLAS), 3I/ATLAS is the third confirmed interstellar object, following ‘Oumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019.

This discovery matters because it challenges existing knowledge about interstellar chemistry and the formation of comets. Although NASA has not issued detailed public statements, it is closely monitoring the object through the International Asteroid Warning Network (IAWN). Reports indicate that NASA activated its planetary defense systems when the object began to show unexpected acceleration and developed an anti-tail pointing toward the Sun.

Key facts

  • Detected: July 2025
  • Size: Roughly the size of Manhattan
  • Closest approach to the Sun: October 29, 2025
  • Distance at closest approach: 268 million kilometers from Earth
  • Estimated mass: 33 billion tons
  • Monitoring agencies: NASA, IAWN

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was detected in July 2025 by ATLAS. Following its discovery, NASA activated its planetary defense systems due to the object’s unexpected acceleration. Observations made from Chile’s Very Large Telescope (VLT) revealed the presence of nickel vapor, leading to widespread scientific debate about its origin.

In the months following its discovery, further analysis using the James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) confirmed abnormally high levels of carbon dioxide and minimal water vapor. Scientists continue to study the object’s unusual behavior as it approaches the Sun.

Official statements

“Its acceleration cannot be explained purely by gravitational forces,” said Harvard astrophysicist Avi Loeb. “It may be a Trojan horse that appears natural but hides advanced technology.”

Context

The findings from 3I/ATLAS are significant in the context of previous interstellar objects. Both ‘Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov have contributed to the understanding of interstellar matter, but 3I/ATLAS's nickel-without-iron signature and abnormal orbit challenge established models of comet formation. Researchers are comparing these objects to determine if building materials in distant star systems follow universal rules or vary across the galaxy.

What’s next

Ongoing research by teams from Chile, Italy, Canada, the U.K., the U.S., and New Zealand will continue via JWST and VLT. Scientists aim to uncover whether the nickel compounds result from low-temperature chemical reactions or indicate something unprecedented. As 3I/ATLAS departs the solar system in 2026, it may provide vital clues about alien environments and the potential for life beyond Earth.
