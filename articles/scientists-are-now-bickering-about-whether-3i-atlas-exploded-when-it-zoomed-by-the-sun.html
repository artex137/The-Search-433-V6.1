Scientists Are Now Bickering About Whether 3I/ATLAS Exploded When It Zoomed By The Sun

Published 2025-11-12 19:42 UTC

Scientists Debate the Fate of Comet 3I/ATLAS After Solar Encounter

Scientists are currently debating whether comet 3I/ATLAS exploded during its recent perihelion, the closest point to the Sun, which occurred on a date not disclosed. The discussions have intensified due to significant mass loss observed in the comet, leading to questions about its nature and origins.

The debate matters because it highlights ongoing discussions about the existence of interstellar objects and the potential for extraterrestrial technology. Avi Loeb, an astrophysicist, has suggested that the mass loss could indicate that 3I/ATLAS shattered into multiple pieces, while other scientists argue it remains a typical comet. The controversy has attracted media attention, and Loeb has sought support from political figures to pressure NASA for additional imaging data.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS made its closest approach to the Sun at perihelion; exact date not disclosed.
  • Avi Loeb has claimed that the object experienced extreme mass loss, potentially indicating an explosion.
  • Loeb speculates that if 3I/ATLAS is intact, it may suggest the presence of alien technology.
  • Other scientists, including a researcher from Lowell Observatory, describe 3I/ATLAS as a "fairly ordinary/healthy-looking comet."
  • Loeb has faced criticism from peers, with some asserting his theories lack credibility.

Timeline

In recent weeks, as 3I/ATLAS approached perihelion, astronomers noted a significant loss of mass. Loeb proposed that this loss could indicate an explosion, leading to the fragmentation of the comet into 16 pieces. However, if future observations reveal the object intact, alternative theories may emerge regarding its composition and origin.

Loeb has actively promoted his theories through various media, engaging with political representatives to advocate for NASA to release delayed imaging data. This outreach has amplified public interest and scrutiny over the object's nature.

Official statements

“Zero planetary scientists give Avi's claims any credence,” said Jason Wright, an astronomer at Penn State. “It's whether it's so anomalous that there's any reason to think it's anything other than a comet.”

Context

The discussions surrounding 3I/ATLAS are reminiscent of previous debates about interstellar objects, particularly 1I/ʻOumuamua, which also sparked speculation about alien technology. The scientific community remains divided on how to classify these objects, with some researchers advocating for caution and thorough investigation.

What’s next

As scientists continue to observe 3I/ATLAS, further imaging and analysis are expected. The outcome could influence the understanding of interstellar objects and their characteristics, as well as the ongoing debate about potential extraterrestrial technology.
