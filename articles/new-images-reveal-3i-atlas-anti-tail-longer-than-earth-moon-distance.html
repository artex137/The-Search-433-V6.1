New Images Reveal 3I/ATLAS Anti-Tail Longer Than Earth-Moon Distance

New observations of the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS have revealed an anti-tail extending over 500,000 kilometers, surpassing the average distance between Earth and the Moon. The discovery was made on December 14–15, 2025, as the object approached a perigee of 268.9 million kilometers on December 19, 2025. This unprecedented finding was captured by telescopes in Italy, California, and Thailand.

This discovery is significant as it represents the largest anti-tail ever observed for any comet or interstellar object, according to experts. Ongoing observations will continue to track the anti-tail’s evolution as 3I/ATLAS moves away from Earth.

Key facts

  • Date of discovery: December 14–15, 2025
  • Distance from Earth: approximately 270 million kilometers
  • Perigee on: December 19, 2025, at 268.9 million kilometers
  • Length of anti-tail: over 500,000 kilometers
  • Locations of observations: Calabria (Italy), June Lake (California), Rayong (Thailand)
  • Key observers: Toni Scarmato, Dan Bartlett, Teerasak Thaluang

Timeline

On December 13, 2025, a 0.26-meter telescope in Rayong, Thailand, confirmed the anti-tail feature using rotational-gradient brightness mapping techniques. The following day, a 14-inch telescope in June Lake, California, produced an integrated image that revealed a green-blue anti-tail spanning 4.24 million kilometers. On December 15, a 0.25-meter telescope in Calabria, Italy, captured a notable image highlighting the anti-tail's sunward extension.

The anti-tail was first recorded in a Hubble Space Telescope image on July 21, 2025, at a distance of 2.98 AU from Earth and remained visible in subsequent images, confirming its persistent nature.

Official statements

“This distance exceeds the average lunar distance of 384,400 kilometers and represents the largest anti-tail observed for any comet or interstellar object to date,” experts say.

Context

Anti-tails typically appear temporarily in solar system comets when Earth crosses the comet’s orbital plane. However, the anti-tail of 3I/ATLAS has shown persistence, indicating a more complex interaction with solar radiation and the solar wind.

What’s next

Ground-based and space-based observatories will continue to track 3I/ATLAS, with further observations expected near perigee on December 19, 2025. These studies aim to enhance understanding of interstellar objects and the processes that contribute to the formation of anti-tails.
