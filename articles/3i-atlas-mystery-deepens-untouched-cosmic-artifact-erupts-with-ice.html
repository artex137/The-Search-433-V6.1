3I/ATLAS Mystery Deepens: 'Untouched Cosmic Artifact' Erupts With Ice

Astronomers have discovered that Comet 3I/ATLAS, an interstellar object first detected in July 2025, may be erupting with active, icy cryovolcanoes, according to a new pre-print study. This comet, which is travelling at speeds peaking around 246,000 km/h, presents a unique opportunity to examine materials from which other star systems are built.

The findings challenge existing theories about comet formation and suggest that 3I/ATLAS may not be a simple ball of ice but a complex geological body. Researchers from the California Institute of Technology and the University of Maryland have conducted extensive observations to document the comet's unusual surface activity.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS is the second interstellar comet ever tracked.
  • It was first detected in July 2025 by the ATLAS telescope network.
  • The comet reaches speeds of approximately 246,000 km/h at its closest approach to the Sun.
  • It has a highly hyperbolic orbit with an eccentricity of about 6.1.
  • The study detailing its findings is currently awaiting peer review.

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first identified in July 2025. Since its discovery, astronomers have monitored its trajectory and surface activity. In recent months, researchers have recorded a significant increase in brightness, indicating a possible eruption across its water-ice surface layer as it approached the Sun.

The comet's brightness surged when it reached approximately 2.5 astronomical units from the Sun, suggesting cryovolcanism may be occurring.

Official statements

“Interstellar visitors like 3I/ATLAS continue to challenge and refine our understanding of planetary-system formation and the chemical evolution of small bodies,” the study's authors stated.

Context

3I/ATLAS follows the discovery of 'Oumuamua in 2017 and Comet 2I/Borisov in 2019, both of which were the first interstellar objects identified. The pristine nature of 3I/ATLAS, having spent billions of years in the interstellar medium, offers a unique perspective on cometary science.

What’s next

As the study awaits peer review, researchers will continue to investigate the implications of cryovolcanism on 3I/ATLAS and its potential to reshape existing models of comet formation.
