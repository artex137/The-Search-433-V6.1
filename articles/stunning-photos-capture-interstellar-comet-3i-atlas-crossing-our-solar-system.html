Stunning Photos Capture Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS Crossing Our Solar System

NASA’s Hubble Space Telescope has tracked the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, capturing images from a distance of approximately 178 million miles from Earth. The comet is on a trajectory that will soon take it out of the solar system.

This discovery is significant as it provides insights into the characteristics of interstellar comets. NASA and the European Space Agency (ESA) have collaborated on this mission, with plans for further data collection over the coming months.

Key facts

  • Distance from Earth during Hubble’s observations: 178 million miles
  • Close approach recorded by ESA’s Juice: November 4, at a distance of around 41 million miles
  • 3I/ATLAS may have two distinct tails: a plasma tail and a dust tail
  • Further data retrieval from Juice’s instruments is expected in February

Timeline

NASA released images of 3I/ATLAS captured by the Hubble Space Telescope, showcasing the comet as a bright point of light against the backdrop of stars. The observations occurred at a distance of about 178 million miles from Earth.

On November 4, the ESA’s Jupiter Icy Moons Explorer, known as Juice, recorded data about 3I/ATLAS just before its close approach.

In February, ESA plans to retrieve further data from Juice’s instruments, which will enhance understanding of the comet’s structure and behavior.

Official statements

“The images from Hubble and Juice provide a unique opportunity to study the characteristics of an interstellar comet,” said a NASA spokesperson.

Context

The observation of interstellar comets like 3I/ATLAS is rare. Such comets originate from outside our solar system and can offer valuable information about the formation of planetary systems. Previous studies have highlighted the importance of international collaboration in space exploration.

What’s next

NASA and ESA will continue data collection and analysis of 3I/ATLAS, with insights expected to emerge from Juice's instruments in February, enhancing our knowledge of interstellar objects.
