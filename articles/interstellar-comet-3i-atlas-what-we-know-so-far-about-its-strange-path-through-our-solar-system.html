Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS: What We Know So Far About Its Strange Path Through Our Solar System

Comet 3I/ATLAS is currently traversing the solar system, having been first discovered in July 2025 by an ATLAS telescope in Chile. The comet is intriguing both astronomers and the general public as it represents a rare opportunity to study an object from outside our solar neighborhood.

The significance of 3I/ATLAS lies in its unique characteristics. Authorities and astronomers worldwide are closely monitoring its approach, as it is the third known interstellar object to enter our solar system. The comet is on a hyperbolic trajectory, meaning it will not return after this visit, offering a fleeting chance to gather data on its composition and origins.

Key facts

  • Discovered in July 2025 by an ATLAS telescope in Chile.
  • Estimated size of approximately 12 miles (20 kilometers) in width.
  • Travelling at a speed of about 58 km/s (over 200,000 km/h).
  • Currently located 170 million miles from Earth.
  • Has an orbit eccentricity of 6.1, indicating a highly unusual path.

Timeline

Comet 3I/ATLAS was initially identified in July 2025 by astronomers using an ATLAS telescope in Chile. Since its discovery, it has been monitored as it approaches the Sun on a hyperbolic trajectory. The comet's speed and orbit suggest it was likely ejected by a large planet in its original star system.

As it nears the Sun, its bright coma has become more pronounced, allowing for detailed observations of its composition. Astronomers have noted that this comet may be one of the oldest discovered, potentially predating the formation of the solar system.

Official statements

“The data we collect from this rarified opportunity will shed more light on materials that exist in distant planetary systems and how planets in other regions of the galaxy form,” said an astronomer involved in the study of 3I/ATLAS.

Context

Comet 3I/ATLAS is only the third known interstellar object to pass through our solar system, following previous notable visitors. Its hyperbolic orbit sets it apart from typical comets, which are usually bound by the Sun's gravity. The unique characteristics of 3I/ATLAS provide scientists with valuable insights into the nature of celestial bodies from other star systems.

What’s next

As 3I/ATLAS continues its approach, astronomers will conduct ongoing observations to gather data. Future findings may provide updated insights into the comet's behavior and composition before it exits the solar system.
