New 3I/ATLAS Image Sparks Outrage And Awe As 'Dead' Interstellar Object Roars Back To Life

A previously 'vanished' interstellar comet, 3I/ATLAS, has reappeared with a longer and brighter tail, stunning astronomers worldwide. The comet was captured in a composite image on 10 November 2025, revealing a strikingly luminous ion tail and complex dust structures, just weeks after its tail seemed to disappear entirely.

This renewed activity has reignited curiosity about the comet's composition and behaviour. Researchers and amateur astronomers are now scrambling to understand the mechanisms driving this transformation. The latest high-resolution image, compiled from 18 long-exposure frames, was released by the Virtual Telescope Project in Italy.

Key facts

  • Date of image capture: 10 November 2025
  • Location: Virtual Telescope Project, Italy
  • Height above horizon: 14 degrees
  • Moon illumination: 61 percent
  • Distance of ion tail: Nearly 0.7 degrees
  • Projected encounter: Jupiter in March 2026

Timeline

3I/ATLAS, the third confirmed interstellar object, was first identified in 2019. In the months leading up to November 2025, its tail appeared to vanish, puzzling astronomers. On 9 November 2025, independent observers, including amateur astronomers Michael Buechner and Frank Niebling, produced images that revealed large-scale structures in the comet's dust environment. The significant image on 10 November 2025 showed the comet's renewed activity.

Official statements

“We imaged the interstellar comet again, recording a much more developed ion tail. Each observation shows it better and better,” said Dr Gianluca Masi, head of the Virtual Telescope Project.
“If confirmed, it will be the third known interstellar object, providing more evidence that such interstellar wanderers are relatively common in our galaxy,” noted Mark Norris, professor at the University of Central Lancashire.

Context

The sudden disappearance of 3I/ATLAS's tail earlier this year led to speculation about the comet's material properties. Harvard astrophysicist Avi Loeb remarked that “nearly 13 percent of the comet's mass should appear as a tail,” yet no such feature was initially visible. The reappearance of the tail challenges previous assumptions about the behaviour of interstellar objects as they enter a solar system.

What’s next

Coordinated observations across professional and amateur observatories are underway to measure the nucleus size, rotation, and outgassing patterns of 3I/ATLAS. The comet's behaviour will continue to be monitored as it approaches Jupiter in March 2026, providing scientists with valuable data on interstellar bodies.
