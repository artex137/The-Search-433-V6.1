What is the Nature of 3I/ATLAS?

Avi Loeb, an astrophysicist at Harvard University, has raised questions about the nature of comet 3I/ATLAS, an interstellar object currently traversing the inner solar system. His observations suggest that 3I/ATLAS may possess unusual characteristics that challenge conventional scientific understanding.

Loeb's concerns are significant as they could reshape our understanding of celestial objects and their origins. He has called for more open-minded research into the anomalies surrounding 3I/ATLAS, emphasizing the need to explore whether it is of natural or technological origin.

Key facts

  • 3I/ATLAS was imaged on July 21, 2025, by the Hubble Space Telescope.
  • It has been noted to shed materials composed of nickel alloys without iron.
  • The object follows a path aligned with the ecliptic plane of the planets.
  • Loeb has co-authored a study on the object's anomalously high mass, which is currently blocked from public view by arXiv moderators.
  • The HiRISE camera on the Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter is expected to capture a high-resolution image of 3I/ATLAS on October 3, 2025.
  • The object will be approximately 29 million kilometers from the HiRISE camera during the observation.

Timeline

On July 21, 2025, Hubble Space Telescope captured the first high-resolution image of 3I/ATLAS. Following this, Loeb published a paper discussing the anomalies associated with the object, including its mass and material composition. However, this paper has faced delays in publication.

On October 3, 2025, the HiRISE camera is set to achieve a pixel resolution of 30 kilometers, which will provide further insights into 3I/ATLAS's characteristics.

Official statements

“The most pressing question about the nature of 3I/ATLAS is whether it is natural or technological in origin,” said Loeb.
“Anomalies are ignored, and attempts to explain them using known physics are sidelined,” he added, criticizing current academic attitudes.

Context

This inquiry into 3I/ATLAS follows the discovery of the first recognized interstellar object, 1I/`Oumuamua, which did not exhibit a tail. The discourse surrounding these objects has become contentious, with some scientists insisting on traditional classifications that may overlook significant anomalies.

Loeb argues that the scientific community's reluctance to explore unconventional ideas is detrimental. He believes that such a mindset could prevent the discovery of important insights about our cosmic environment.

What’s next

Loeb is advocating for further research into 3I/ATLAS and aims to obtain clearer images that could provide additional data on its nature. He stresses the importance of remaining open to new interpretations of unusual celestial phenomena.
