Interstellar object 3I/ATLAS passed Mars last night

The interstellar object 3I/ATLAS made its closest approach to Mars at 4:00 UTC on October 3, 2025, which corresponds to 11:00 p.m. CDT on October 2. At that time, the comet was approximately 18 million miles (29 million kilometers) from Mars. There have been no reports of injuries or fatalities related to this event.

This close approach marks the object’s nearest encounter with any planet during its journey through the solar system. Multiple space agencies, including NASA and the European Space Agency (ESA), are currently coordinating observations using various spacecraft and orbiters to gather data on this interstellar visitor.

Key facts

  • Closest approach to Mars: October 3, 2025, at 4:00 UTC
  • Distance from Mars at closest approach: 18 million miles (29 million kilometers)
  • Instruments observing 3I/ATLAS: ESA’s Mars Express, ExoMars Trace Gas Orbiter, NASA’s Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter
  • Estimated size of nucleus: 3.5 miles (5.6 km)
  • Perihelion date: October 29, 2025
  • Perihelion distance: approximately 1.36 astronomical units from the sun

Timeline

On October 2, 2025, the Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter began observations of 3I/ATLAS using its HiRISE camera between 1:00 a.m. and 4:00 a.m. UTC. ESA’s Mars Express and ExoMars Trace Gas Orbiter aimed their cameras at the comet on October 3, 2025. The comet is expected to reach its closest point to the sun, or perihelion, on October 29, 2025.

Official statements

“During the Mars close approach, the Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter will observe 3I with HiRISE,” said Marshall Eubanks of Space Initiatives.
“Both of the European Space Agency’s satellites around Mars are already aiming their cameras at the comet,” reported Marcia Dunn for the Associated Press.

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third known interstellar object to pass through our solar system. Its trajectory has been traced back approximately 10 million years, but researchers have not identified a specific star as its origin. Studies suggest that the object likely originated from the Milky Way’s thin disk, which contains younger celestial bodies compared to the thick disk.

What’s next

NASA and ESA will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS as it approaches perihelion on October 29, 2025. Observations from the ESA’s Jupiter Icy Moons Explorer (Juice) between November 2 and November 25, 2025, are expected to provide additional insights into the comet's characteristics.
