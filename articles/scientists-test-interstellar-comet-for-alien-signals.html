Scientists test interstellar comet for alien signals

A team of astronomers has found that 3I/ATLAS, a comet that entered the solar system from interstellar space, is not emitting any radio signals that would indicate it is an alien spacecraft. This finding was announced following a directed radio observation on December 18, 2025, using the Green Bank Telescope in West Virginia.

This research is significant as it sets a clear standard for how scientists will examine future objects from beyond our solar system. The investigation aimed to close the possibility of misinterpreting natural signals as signs of extraterrestrial technology.

Key facts

  • Comet: 3I/ATLAS
  • Observation date: December 18, 2025
  • Location: Green Bank Telescope, West Virginia
  • Lead researcher: Benjamin Jacobson-Bell, Breakthrough Listen
  • Frequency range tested: 1 to 12 gigahertz
  • Detection limit: down to 0.1 watt

Timeline

On December 18, 2025, astronomers at the Green Bank Telescope conducted a directed radio observation of 3I/ATLAS. The team, led by Benjamin Jacobson-Bell, sought to determine if the comet emitted any technosignatures, or signs of technology from another civilization. The following day, December 19, marked the comet's closest approach to Earth.

After analyzing the data, the researchers concluded that 3I/ATLAS did not produce any signals that would suggest it is an artificial object. This result confirmed initial expectations that the comet was a natural body.

Official statements

“The evidence was against 3I/ATLAS being one such probe, but we would have been remiss not to check,” said Jacobson-Bell.
“We all would have been thrilled to find technosignatures coming from 3I/ATLAS, but they’re just not there,” he added.

Context

The search for technosignatures from comets and other celestial bodies has gained interest as robotic spacecraft could provide a less costly alternative for exploring distant worlds. Historical observations of comets have generally indicated natural phenomena rather than signs of extraterrestrial life.

With the upcoming Legacy Survey of Space and Time (LSST) project, astronomers will have improved tools to identify and track fast-moving objects in the southern sky. This will allow for more timely radio observations of new interstellar visitors.

What’s next

As new interstellar objects are detected, astronomers plan to conduct similar radio searches quickly to verify whether any emit signals. The findings from 3I/ATLAS will help refine the standards for identifying potential technosignatures in future observations.
