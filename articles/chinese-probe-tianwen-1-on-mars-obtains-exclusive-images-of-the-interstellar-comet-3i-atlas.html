Chinese probe Tianwen-1 on Mars obtains exclusive images of the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS

The Chinese probe Tianwen-1 has successfully captured images of the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS from its orbit around Mars, as confirmed by the Administração Espacial Nacional of China (CNSA) in October 2026. The comet was photographed from a distance of approximately 30 million kilometers.

This achievement marks a significant milestone in space exploration, providing unique perspectives on celestial bodies originating outside our Solar System. The data collected from Mars offers valuable information to scientists studying the composition and dynamics of interstellar comets. The operation showcases China's advanced capabilities in space technology and reinforces Mars's role as a strategic platform for astronomical research.

Key facts

  • Event: Tianwen-1 captures images of comet 3I/ATLAS
  • Date: October 2026
  • Distance: Approximately 30 million kilometers from the comet
  • Comet diameter: Estimated at 5.6 kilometers
  • Speed: About 58 kilometers per second
  • Agencies involved: CNSA, ESA, NASA

Timeline

In October 2026, the CNSA confirmed the successful capture of images of comet 3I/ATLAS by the Tianwen-1 probe. The operation involved meticulous planning and the use of advanced technology to track the small, fast-moving comet. The images were collected using the High-Resolution Imaging Camera (HiRIC) aboard the probe.

Following the capture, the raw data was transmitted to the mission control center in Beijing, where it underwent advanced processing to produce detailed images of the comet's structure, including its nucleus and coma.

Official statements

“This achievement not only validates the advanced capabilities of the Chinese space program but also reinforces the role of Mars as a strategic platform for future astronomy,” a CNSA spokesperson stated.

Context

Comet 3I/ATLAS is the third interstellar object confirmed to enter our Solar System, following ‘Oumuamua in 2017 and comet 2I/Borisov in 2019. Its trajectory confirms its extrasolar origin, as it follows a hyperbolic orbit that prevents it from being captured by the Sun's gravity.

The successful imaging of 3I/ATLAS enhances our understanding of interstellar objects and the processes of planet formation in other systems. It highlights the growing capabilities of space agencies to conduct high-impact science and collaborate internationally.

What’s next

Following the successful capture of images, international collaboration will continue to analyze the data collected from the comet. Future studies will focus on the chemical and physical composition of 3I/ATLAS to further understand its origins and implications for planetary science.
