Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS May Have Formed Around An Old, Low-Metallicity Star In The Milky Way's Outer Disk

A new study has suggested that interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS may have formed around an old, low-metallicity star in the Milky Way's outer disk. The findings come after the comet's closest approach to the Sun, which occurred on July 1, 2022. The study was led by Dr. Cyrielle Opitom from the Institute for Astronomy at the University of Edinburgh.

This research is significant as it enhances understanding of planetary formation processes in different stellar environments. Astronomers have utilized this brief opportunity to study 3I/ATLAS, as it will soon exit the Solar System and head towards the constellation of Gemini. Observations indicate that the comet recently passed Jupiter at a distance of 0.35832 astronomical units (AU).

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS detected on July 1, 2022.
  • Closest encounter with Jupiter at 0.35832 AU.
  • Study led by Dr. Cyrielle Opitom, Institute for Astronomy, University of Edinburgh.
  • High nitrogen and carbon isotopic ratios measured.
  • Study posted to preprint server arXiv.

Timeline

On July 1, 2022, the Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System detected 3I/ATLAS as it entered the Solar System. The comet made its closest approach to the Sun, allowing astronomers to study its characteristics. In December, observations from the Very Large Telescope confirmed high nitrogen and carbon isotopic ratios, supporting the theory of an origin in the Milky Way's outer disk.

Official statements

“Interstellar objects provide a unique opportunity to further our understanding of the planetary formation process by studying in detail material formed around another star,” Dr. Opitom stated.
“We measure a high 14 N/ 15 N isotopic ratio in the interstellar comet 3I, a factor of two higher than what is measured in solar system comets,” the research team explained.

Context

The study builds on previous findings regarding interstellar visitors, such as 1I/ʻOumuamua and 2I/Borisov, which were too small for detailed observation. 3I/ATLAS's larger size and proximity have allowed for more comprehensive analysis of its isotopic composition.

What’s next

Further studies are expected as astronomers analyze the final observations of 3I/ATLAS to confirm its origins and implications for understanding interstellar objects.
