See gorgeous snaps of emerald 3I/ATLAS over Egypt as interstellar anomaly rockets toward Earth

Published 2025-12-11 14:29 UTC

Emerald Comet 3I/ATLAS Approaches Earth

Interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS is set to fly by Earth on December 19, 2025. An amateur photographer, Osama Fathi, has captured a stunning image of the comet glowing green over the Egyptian desert on November 29, 2025. There have been no reported injuries or fatalities related to the comet's approach.

The close-up photograph has garnered attention from astronomers and space enthusiasts, as 3I/ATLAS is a rare interstellar visitor. Authorities and researchers are preparing for its closest approach to Earth, which will occur at a distance of 170 million miles.

Key facts

  • Comet Name: 3I/ATLAS
  • Photographer: Osama Fathi
  • Photo Taken: November 29, 2025
  • Closest Approach to Earth: December 19, 2025
  • Distance at Closest Approach: 170 million miles
  • NASA's Hubble Telescope captured images on: November 30, 2025

Timeline

On November 29, 2025, amateur photographer Osama Fathi captured an image of comet 3I/ATLAS in the Egyptian Black Desert. The photo shows the comet glowing green against a dark sky, enhanced by the absence of urban light pollution.

The following day, November 30, NASA's Hubble Space Telescope took additional images of the comet using its Wide Field Camera 3, revealing a blue glow as it traveled through the solar system.

3I/ATLAS is expected to make its closest approach to Earth on December 19, 2025, at a distance of 170 million miles.

Official statements

“From the heart of Egypt’s Black Desert, where volcanic hills rise like silent sentinels... an object from another star system drifts through our sky: interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS,” said Osama Fathi in a statement to the BBC.
“These long integrations allowed the comet’s delicate coma and motion against the background stars to emerge clearly, even under the extremely dark Saharan skies,” Fathi explained regarding his photographic technique.

Context

3I/ATLAS is one of the few known interstellar comets, providing scientists with a unique opportunity to study material from outside our solar system. Prior to this, astronomers have observed other interstellar objects, but 3I/ATLAS's approach offers a chance for closer observation and analysis.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS as it approaches Earth. Additional observations are planned to capture more data and images of the comet during its closest flyby.
