New Concept for Intercepting Interstellar Object 3I/ATLAS Using a Solar Oberth Maneuver

The Initiative for Interstellar Studies (i4is) has proposed a new mission concept to intercept the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS, with a launch planned for 2035. The proposal aims to study the object up close as it moves through our Solar System, which has generated significant interest among scientists.

This mission is significant because 3I/ATLAS is only the third known interstellar object detected, and direct observation could yield groundbreaking insights into celestial bodies beyond our Solar System. Current propulsion technologies have presented challenges for mission planning, prompting researchers to explore innovative strategies to achieve close encounters with these distant objects.

Key facts

  • Object: 3I/ATLAS
  • Proposing Agency: Initiative for Interstellar Studies (i4is)
  • Proposed Launch Year: 2035
  • Study Published In: Journal of the British Interplanetary Society
  • Key Researchers: Adam Hibberd, T. Marshall Eubanks, Andreas Hein
  • Projected Flight Duration: Approximately 50 years

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was detected as it traveled inside Jupiter's orbit, moving at speeds exceeding 60 km/s. This detection occurred after the optimal launch window for a direct intercept mission had passed, complicating potential mission planning.

In response to these challenges, researchers have developed a new approach using a Solar Oberth maneuver, which can be executed with a launch in 2035. This maneuver leverages the gravitational influence of the Sun to increase the spacecraft's speed, allowing it to intercept the object more effectively.

Official statements

"For a direct mission, the object 3I/ATLAS was detected too late, having already traveled inside Jupiter's orbit with a velocity exceeding 60 km/s," said Adam Hibberd. "The Solar Oberth option is designed for when an interstellar object has passed its perihelion and is rapidly moving away from the Sun."

Context

Prior mission proposals to intercept interstellar objects have primarily relied on chemical rockets launched from Earth, such as NASA's Janus mission and the European Space Agency's Comet Interceptor. The challenges in intercepting 3I/ATLAS highlight the limitations of current technologies and the need for innovative solutions in space exploration.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to refine their mission design and prepare for the proposed 2035 launch. Further studies will assess the feasibility of the Solar Oberth maneuver and its potential to achieve the scientific objectives of the mission.
