Why 3I/ATLAS Triggered A CIA Non-Answer Despite NASA’s Certainty

On December 31, 2025, the Central Intelligence Agency (CIA) issued a response to a Freedom of Information Act (FOIA) request regarding the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS, stating it could “neither confirm nor deny the existence or nonexistence of records” related to the object. This response has raised questions about the intelligence community's stance on the comet, despite NASA’s recent classification of it as a natural comet.

This situation highlights a significant discrepancy between scientific consensus and intelligence responses. NASA officials characterized 3I/ATLAS as a natural comet on November 19, 2025, dismissing any extraordinary claims about its nature. However, the CIA's use of a Glomar denial suggests a level of sensitivity surrounding the object that contradicts NASA's public certainty.

Key facts

  • Date of CIA response: December 31, 2025
  • Date of NASA announcement: November 19, 2025
  • Requester: John Greenewald Jr., researcher and transparency advocate
  • Type of CIA response: Glomar denial
  • NASA's classification: Natural comet

Timeline

On November 19, 2025, NASA officials publicly confirmed that 3I/ATLAS is a natural comet, indicating that it does not pose extraordinary questions within the realm of known cometary physics. This announcement effectively closed the door on further institutional debate regarding the object.

Following this, on December 31, 2025, the CIA responded to a FOIA request from John Greenewald Jr. with a Glomar denial, indicating that they could not confirm or deny the existence of any records related to 3I/ATLAS. This response has raised concerns about the implications of the CIA's stance on the object.

Official statements

“The CIA stated it could ‘neither confirm nor deny the existence or nonexistence of records’ related to the object.”

Context

The CIA's Glomar denial is a rare response, often reserved for sensitive information. This approach suggests that even low-probability, high-impact risks are taken seriously by intelligence agencies, despite NASA's public assurance regarding the nature of 3I/ATLAS.

What’s next

There are no current investigations or additional responses planned from either NASA or the CIA regarding 3I/ATLAS at this time. The situation remains under observation as the implications of the intelligence community's response continue to unfold.
