Largest interstellar traveller ever observed: Hubble reveals 3I/ATLAS centre up to 2.6 km in diameter

Astronomers have identified 3I/ATLAS as the largest interstellar comet on record, with a solid centre measuring up to 2.6 kilometres in diameter, according to observations from the Hubble Space Telescope. The comet, which is currently leaving our solar system, was first spotted on July 1, 2025, by the NASA-funded ATLAS survey telescope in Chile.

This discovery is significant as 3I/ATLAS is the third confirmed interstellar visitor to our solar system, following 1I/’Oumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019. The size of 3I/ATLAS, much larger than its predecessors, offers new insights into the formation of distant planetary systems.

Key facts

  • Comet Name: 3I/ATLAS (also designated C/2025 N1)
  • Diameter: Up to 2.6 km
  • First Observed: July 1, 2025
  • Origin: Interstellar
  • Previous Interstellar Objects: 1I/’Oumuamua (2017) and 2I/Borisov (2019)
  • Monitoring Instruments: Hubble Space Telescope, NASA’s Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite (TESS), James Webb Space Telescope
  • Observation Dates: TESS tracked the comet from January 15 to January 22, 2026

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first detected on July 1, 2025, by the ATLAS survey telescope in Chile. Following its discovery, astronomers confirmed its hyperbolic trajectory and high speed, indicating its interstellar origin. The Hubble Space Telescope later provided detailed observations, revealing the size of the comet's nucleus.

From January 15 to January 22, 2026, NASA’s TESS tracked the comet, capturing data on its brightness variations and activity. As it continues on its unbound path, multiple NASA assets, including the James Webb Space Telescope, are monitoring 3I/ATLAS.

Official statements

“The size, mass, and composition of 3I/ATLAS challenge existing models of interstellar object formation,” said a lead astronomer involved in the observations.

Context

Prior to 3I/ATLAS, the largest known interstellar objects, 1I/’Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov, had nuclei estimated at under 1 km and around 1 km, respectively. The discovery of 3I/ATLAS raises questions about the prevalence of large comets in other star systems.

What’s next

As 3I/ATLAS exits the solar system, researchers will continue to analyse its properties and trajectory. Future observations may clarify whether such massive comets are common elsewhere or represent a rare occurrence in our solar system.
