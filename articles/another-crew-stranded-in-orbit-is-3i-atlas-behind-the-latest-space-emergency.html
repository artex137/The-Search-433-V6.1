Another crew stranded in orbit: Is 3I/ATLAS behind the latest space emergency?

China's Shenzhou 20 crew is currently stranded aboard the Tiangong space station following unexpected damage to their return craft. The incident has raised concerns about the impact of the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, which has recently been shedding material and producing unusual jet activity.

This situation is significant as it highlights the increasing risks associated with space travel, particularly during periods of heightened activity in the near-Earth environment. The Chinese space program has six astronauts aboard the Tiangong station, with officials stating that the crew remains safe. However, they have not disclosed key details about the damage to the Shenzhou 20 spacecraft.

Key facts

  • Incident occurred on October 10, 2023.
  • Six astronauts are currently aboard the Tiangong space station.
  • Details on the damage to Shenzhou 20 have not been disclosed.
  • China plans to use backup capsule Shenzhou 21 for crew return.
  • 3I/ATLAS is approaching a test window near Earth on December 19, 2023.
  • China has not indicated whether it will seek external assistance for the situation.

Timeline

On October 10, 2023, the Shenzhou 20 spacecraft sustained damage, prompting the crew to take shelter aboard the Tiangong space station. Following this incident, officials confirmed that the astronauts were safe but did not provide details about the nature of the damage. Analysts began to speculate about the potential involvement of 3I/ATLAS and other orbital debris.

In the days following the incident, China announced plans to utilize the backup Shenzhou 21 capsule for the crew's return. Engineers have started evaluating the backup spacecraft to determine its readiness for the mission.

Official statements

“All work on the station is proceeding normally,” a Chinese official stated, despite concerns regarding the crew's safety and the unidentified object that caused the damage.

Context

This incident follows a similar crisis faced by the United States earlier this year when astronauts aboard the International Space Station dealt with a coolant leak. NASA's response included real-time modelling of the situation, contrasting with the limited information provided by Chinese officials following the Shenzhou 20 incident.

The spaceflight community has expressed unease about the lack of transparency regarding the damage, especially as the object responsible remains unidentified. The increasing activity of 3I/ATLAS has intensified scrutiny of the risks associated with space travel.

What’s next

China is expected to confirm its plan for the return operation in the coming days. Engineers will assess whether Shenzhou 21 can safely carry out the mission or if an additional launch will be necessary. Meanwhile, agencies and research institutions will continue to monitor for further anomalies as 3I/ATLAS approaches Earth.
