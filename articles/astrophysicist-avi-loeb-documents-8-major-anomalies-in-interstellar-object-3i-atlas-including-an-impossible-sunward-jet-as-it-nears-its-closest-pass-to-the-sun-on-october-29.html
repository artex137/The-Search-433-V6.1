Astrophysicist Avi Loeb Documents 8-Major Anomalies In Interstellar Object 3I/ATLAS, Including An Impossible Sunward Jet, As It Nears Its Closest Pass to the Sun On October 29

Published 2025-10-18 18:33 UTC

Astrophysicist Avi Loeb Documents 8 Major Anomalies In Interstellar Object 3I/ATLAS

Harvard astrophysicist Avi Loeb has reported eight significant anomalies in the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS, which is set to make its closest approach to the Sun on October 29, 2025. Among these anomalies is an unusual jet directed toward the Sun, known as an "anti-tail."

This discovery raises questions about the nature of 3I/ATLAS, which Loeb argues does not conform to typical comet behavior. He suggests that the object may require explanations beyond natural origins. Authorities in the field are closely monitoring its trajectory and characteristics as it approaches perihelion.

Key facts

  • Object Name: 3I/ATLAS (also designated C/2025 N1)
  • Discovered: July 1, 2025, by the ATLAS survey telescope in Chile
  • Closest Approach: October 29, 2025
  • Orbital Characteristics: Hyperbolic path, retrograde motion, aligned within ~5° of the solar system's ecliptic
  • Nickel-to-Iron Ratio: High ratio detected in gas plume
  • Anti-tail: Jet pointing toward the Sun, captured by Hubble on July 21, 2025
  • Jet Length: ~6,000 km from the nucleus, observed by the Two-meter Twin Telescope on August 2, 2025

Timeline

The following events outline key observations related to 3I/ATLAS:

  • July 1, 2025: 3I/ATLAS is discovered by the ATLAS survey telescope in Chile.
  • July 21, 2025: Hubble Space Telescope captures imaging showing an anti-tail directed toward the Sun.
  • August 2, 2025: The Two-meter Twin Telescope at Teide Observatory in the Canary Islands observes a faint jet extending ~6,000 km from the nucleus of the object.

Official statements

“The jet oriented sunward implies a strong directed outflow,” said Loeb, emphasizing the object's unique characteristics.
“The odds of this alignment occurring purely by chance are under 0.2%,” Loeb noted, highlighting the improbability of a natural interloper.

Context

3I/ATLAS is only the third confirmed interstellar visitor, following ʻOumuamua and Borisov. Its unusual orbital plane and retrograde motion have prompted discussions about the potential for non-natural origins in such interstellar objects.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS as it approaches perihelion, with a focus on understanding its trajectory and the influence of solar phenomena on its path.
