New Interstellar Visitor 3I/ATLAS Reveals Key Differences from 2I/Borisov

NASA's Perseverance rover has captured high-resolution images of the new interstellar object, Comet 3I/ATLAS, which differs significantly from the first confirmed interstellar visitor, Comet 2I/Borisov, according to a report by USA Herald. The images show 3I/ATLAS moving through the Martian sky at nearly 130,000 mph, highlighting its larger size and irregular density compared to Borisov.

This discovery is significant as it challenges existing theories about interstellar objects. Scientists are now re-evaluating the physical properties of these celestial bodies. The differences between 3I/ATLAS and 2I/Borisov provide insights into the diversity of planetary system formation and the materials that can be found in different star systems.

Key facts

  • 3I/ATLAS speed: approximately 130,000 mph
  • 2I/Borisov speed: approximately 110,000 mph
  • 3I/ATLAS size: multi-kilometer object
  • 2I/Borisov size: 1 km nucleus
  • 3I/ATLAS composition: possibly metallic or silicate-based
  • 2I/Borisov composition: icy, volatile
  • 3I/ATLAS origin: hot inner-disk ejection from another star system
  • 2I/Borisov origin: cold outer disk

Timeline

In 2019, Comet 2I/Borisov was discovered as the first confirmed interstellar visitor, traveling at about 110,000 mph. In 2025, the Perseverance rover captured images of 3I/ATLAS, which has been observed moving faster and exhibiting different characteristics. The advanced detection capabilities of the Vera C. Rubin Observatory, operational since early 2023, have enabled regular tracking of interstellar objects like 3I/ATLAS.

Official statements

“This isn’t an isolated discovery,” said Amir Siraj, an astrophysicist at Harvard. “The Rubin Observatory finally allows us to track these objects systematically, giving a real sense of the galactic debris flowing through our solar system.”

Context

The comparison between 3I/ATLAS and 2I/Borisov illustrates the potential variety of interstellar objects that can enter our solar system. Previous studies have suggested that our solar system could host multiple interstellar travelers from different star-forming environments, as noted by Harvard astrophysicists Amir Siraj and Avi Loeb in a 2021 study.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to study 3I/ATLAS using advanced telescopes to better understand its composition and origin. Ongoing observations may provide further insights into the dynamics of planetary system formation beyond our solar system.
