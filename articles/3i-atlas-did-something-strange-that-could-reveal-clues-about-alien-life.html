3I/ATLAS did something strange that could reveal clues about alien life

Published 2025-10-30 05:57 UTC

3I/ATLAS Comet Reveals Clues About Potential Alien Life

The interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS has been observed releasing significant amounts of water, suggesting that the building blocks of life may exist in other star systems. Astronomers detected this phenomenon using NASA’s Neil Gehrels Swift Observatory, marking a potential breakthrough in the study of extraterrestrial life.

This discovery is important because it indicates that water may sustain life beyond our solar system. According to researchers at Auburn University, the comet exhibited unusual behavior by emitting hydroxyl (OH) gas at a distance from the Sun where comets are typically inactive. This research could reshape our understanding of comet behavior and the chemistry of planetary systems outside of our own.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS observed releasing water at approximately 40 kg per second.
  • Detected hydroxyl gas, a chemical indicator of water.
  • Comet was nearly three times farther from the Sun than Earth when the observations were made.
  • Comet reached solar conjunction on October 21 and perihelion on October 29.
  • Research conducted by astronomers at Auburn University, Alabama.
  • NASA's Neil Gehrels Swift Observatory utilized for detection.

Timeline

On October 21, 3I/ATLAS reached solar conjunction, becoming difficult to observe due to the Sun's glare. It then hit perihelion on October 29, a point at which it was closest to the Sun. Observations of the comet's water release and hydroxyl gas emissions occurred during this period.

Following perihelion, scientists expect to see changes in the comet’s behavior as it reemerges from the Sun's glare in the coming days.

Official statements

“When we detect water – or even its faint ultraviolet echo, OH – from an interstellar comet, we’re reading a note from another planetary system,” said Dennis Bodewits, professor of physics at Auburn. “It tells us that the ingredients for life’s chemistry are not unique to our own.”

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third known interstellar visitor to our solar system, following Oumuamua and Borisov. While Oumuamua was dry and Borisov was rich in carbon monoxide, 3I/ATLAS has been observed releasing water at a distance where it typically should not. This variation in composition among interstellar comets provides insights into the diversity of materials found in the cosmos.

What’s next

Scientists are currently waiting for 3I/ATLAS to reappear from the Sun's glare to conduct further observations. They anticipate that the comet's close encounter with the Sun may have altered its composition and behavior, providing additional data for study.
