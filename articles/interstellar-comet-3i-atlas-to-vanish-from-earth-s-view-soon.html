Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS to Vanish from Earth’s View Soon

The interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS is set to disappear from Earth’s view as it approaches the Sun, according to astronomers. Discovered by the NSF-DOE Vera C. Rubin Observatory in Chile, this comet is the third interstellar visitor documented in our solar system.

This event matters as it presents a unique opportunity for astronomers to study the comet's characteristics before it becomes too close to the Sun for observations. Ground-based observatories will monitor 3I/ATLAS until the end of September, after which visibility will cease until early December.

Key facts

  • Discovered: July 1, 2023, by NSF-DOE Vera C. Rubin Observatory in Chile
  • Closest approach to Sun: October 30, 2023, approximately 130 million miles away
  • Weight: Estimated at around 33 billion tons
  • Research study: Involved over 4,000 observations, co-authored by Avi Loeb from Harvard University
  • Visibility resumes: Early December 2023, after passing behind the Sun

Timeline

On July 1, 2023, the NASA-funded ATLAS survey telescope first identified comet 3I/ATLAS. Since then, extensive research has been conducted to understand its characteristics. Ground-based observatories will continue to monitor the comet until the end of September 2023.

Following its closest approach to the Sun on October 30, 2023, observations will resume in early December 2023, post its passage behind the Sun.

Official statements

Avi Loeb, co-author of the study, stated that “the object could potentially be more than just a typical comet, raising questions about its origin and composition.”

Context

Comet 3I/ATLAS is noted for being significantly larger than previous interstellar comets, 1I/`Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov. Preliminary studies suggest it has an unusual chemical composition, which could lead to new insights into the nature of such celestial objects.

What’s next

NASA and the European Space Agency (ESA) are set to continue tracking comet 3I/ATLAS using spacecraft. Future observations may involve NASA’s HiRISE camera on the Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter in October 2023 and NASA’s Juno spacecraft in March 2026.
