MANHATTAN-SIZED SPACE ROCK TURNS BLUE, SPEEDS UP - HARVARD PROF SAYS "MAYBE ALIENS" Interstellar object 3I/ATLAS just did something comets aren't supposed to do: it accelerated without gravitational assist and turned "bluer than the Sun

Published 2025-11-02 00:08 UTC

Manhattan-sized space rock turns blue, speeds up

The interstellar object 3I/ATLAS has exhibited unusual behaviour as it accelerates without gravitational assist while passing the Sun. NASA recorded this non-gravitational acceleration this week, and the object has changed colour from red to green and now to blue.

This phenomenon has drawn attention from scientists, particularly Harvard astrophysicist Avi Loeb, who has suggested it could indicate a technological signature of an internal propulsion system. The object is expected to make its closest approach to Earth on December 19, at a distance of 267 million kilometres.

Key facts

  • Object: 3I/ATLAS
  • Closest approach to Earth: December 19, 2023
  • Distance at closest approach: 267 million kilometres
  • Current observed colour: Blue
  • Previous colours: Red, then green
  • Recorded by: NASA and US Naval Research Lab

Timeline

3I/ATLAS has displayed a series of unusual changes in its behaviour:

  • The object was first identified as red, then shifted to green, and most recently to blue.
  • NAVAR has observed non-gravitational acceleration, which is atypical for comets.
  • The object made a flyby of Mars in October 2023, where close-up images were captured.

Official statements

Avi Loeb, Harvard astrophysicist, stated, "If we do not observe a massive cloud of gas around 3I/ATLAS in December, it could indicate a propulsion system."

Context

This is not the first time an interstellar object has raised eyebrows. Previous observations have led to speculation regarding their nature and origins. The unusual behaviour of 3I/ATLAS has prompted discussions about whether it could be a comet or something more technologically advanced.

What’s next

Researchers will closely monitor 3I/ATLAS during its approach in December for signs of a comet tail or other features that could clarify its nature. The upcoming observations will be crucial in determining whether it is a natural object or something engineered.
