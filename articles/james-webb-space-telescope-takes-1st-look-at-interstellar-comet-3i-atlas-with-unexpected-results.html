James Webb Space Telescope takes first look at interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS with unexpected results

The James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) has observed the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS for the first time on August 6, 2025. Discovered on July 1 by the ATLAS (Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System) survey telescope, 3I/ATLAS is the third interstellar object detected in the solar system.

This observation is significant as it allows scientists to analyze the chemical composition of the comet's halo and provides insights into the conditions of the early universe. The findings may help researchers understand the formation of celestial bodies in the solar system and beyond.

Key facts

  • Telescope: James Webb Space Telescope
  • Observation Date: August 6, 2025
  • Discovery Date: July 1, 2025
  • Other Interstellar Objects: 1I/'Oumuamua (2017), 2I/Borisov (2019)
  • Instrument Used: Near-Infrared Spectrograph (NIRSpec)

Timeline

On July 1, 2025, the ATLAS survey telescope discovered the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS. The JWST observed the comet on August 6, 2025, using its NIRSpec instrument to analyze the comet's chemical makeup.

The study of this comet is part of ongoing research to understand the conditions in other star systems and compare them to the early solar system, which formed approximately 4.6 billion years ago.

Official statements

“Studying comets like this from other star systems helps to study what conditions were like in those systems as they were forming,” said a team of astronomers involved in the investigation.

Context

3I/ATLAS is the oldest known comet, estimated to be around 7 billion years old, which is about 3 billion years older than the solar system. This age suggests it originates from the Milky Way's "thick disk" of stars, a region much older than the "thin disk" where our solar system formed.

What’s next

The study of 3I/ATLAS will continue as the comet approaches the sun, with the JWST playing a crucial role in uncovering its secrets before it returns to interstellar space.
