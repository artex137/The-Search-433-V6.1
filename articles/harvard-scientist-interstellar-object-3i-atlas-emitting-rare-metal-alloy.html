Harvard Scientist: Interstellar Object 3I/ATLAS Emitting Rare Metal Alloy

Recent observations have shown that the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS emits a unique nickel alloy, according to Harvard astrophysicist Dr. Avi Loeb. The findings were made using images from the Keck II telescope in Hawaii, taken in August. There have been no reported injuries or fatalities associated with this discovery.

This discovery is significant as it marks the first time nickel tetracarbonyl has been found in an astronomical context. Authorities, including the research team from the Keck Observatory, are analyzing the unusual characteristics of 3I/ATLAS to understand the processes behind its emissions.

Key facts

  • Emission Rate: 4 grams of nickel per second
  • Iron Presence: None detected
  • Emission Stream: 330 pounds per second
  • Composition: Primarily carbon dioxide and water, with traces of cyanide and nickel
  • First Observation: July 21
  • Distance from Mars: Passed within 12 million miles

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first captured by the Hubble Space Telescope on July 21. Observations continued, with significant findings reported in August from the Keck II telescope. Upcoming images from the Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter, taken between October 4 and 7, are anticipated to provide further insights into the object as it passed near Mars.

Official statements

“Why are we seeing a jet pointed at the Sun?”

— Dr. Avi Loeb, on the unusual emissions from 3I/ATLAS.

Context

Typically, comets exhibit both nickel and iron in similar ratios. The unusual characteristics of 3I/ATLAS, including its lack of a typical cometary tail, have made it a subject of intense study within the scientific community. The refined compounds observed are often used in coatings for metals, particularly in aerospace applications.

What’s next

NASA is expected to release data from the Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter once the government shutdown is resolved. This data will be crucial for understanding the nature of 3I/ATLAS and its emissions.
