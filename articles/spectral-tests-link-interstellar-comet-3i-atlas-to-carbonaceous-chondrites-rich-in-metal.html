Spectral tests link interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS to carbonaceous chondrites rich in metal

Researchers have conducted spectral tests on the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, revealing its composition is similar to carbonaceous chondrites. These tests took place on 2 December 2025, in New York, using meteorite samples curated by NASA from Antarctica. No injuries or fatalities have been reported in connection with this research.

This discovery is significant because it suggests that 3I/ATLAS may share characteristics with ancient solar system bodies, potentially offering insights into the origins of life on Earth. Authorities, including NASA and the Natural History Museum in London, have recognized the implications of these findings for understanding planetary formation and the evolution of life.

Key facts

  • Research conducted on 2 December 2025.
  • Comet 3I/ATLAS estimated to have a diameter between 440 metres and 5.6 kilometres.
  • Estimated mass exceeds 600 million metric tonnes.
  • Comet first observed travelling at approximately 221,000 kilometres per hour.
  • Carbonaceous chondrites believed to have contributed to the emergence of life on Earth.

Timeline

On 2 December 2025, researchers announced their findings from spectral comparisons between comet 3I/ATLAS and carbonaceous chondrites. The tests showed close similarities in composition, suggesting a link to ancient solar system bodies. The Hubble Space Telescope provided estimates of the comet's size and mass, further supporting the study's conclusions.

Official statements

“The emerging picture is that 3I/ATLAS shares key characteristics with these meteorites despite its interstellar provenance,” said a spokesperson from NASA.
“Carbonaceous chondrites are widely believed to have contributed to the emergence of life on Earth,” noted a representative from the Natural History Museum in London.

Context

This research builds on previous studies of interstellar objects and their potential impact on our understanding of the solar system's formation. The identification of 3I/ATLAS as compositionally similar to carbonaceous chondrites aligns with ongoing scientific inquiries into the origins of life and planetary development.

What’s next

Researchers plan to continue studying comet 3I/ATLAS to further understand its properties and implications for planetary science. Additional observations and analyses will help clarify its origin and behavior as it approaches the Sun.
