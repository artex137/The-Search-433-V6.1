Scientists want to catch interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS before it leaves the solar system forever: Here's how

Scientists have proposed a mission to intercept interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, which is currently moving away from the solar system at more than 60 kilometres per second. The mission, set for a potential launch in 2035, aims to study the comet before it leaves the solar system permanently.

This mission is significant because it represents a unique opportunity to gather data from an object originating from another star system. Researchers believe that the close-up study of 3I/ATLAS could provide insights into planetary formation and the composition of distant planetary systems. The Initiative for Interstellar Studies (i4is) is leading the effort to develop the necessary technology and strategies for this ambitious project.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS is moving at over 60 km/s away from the Sun.
  • The proposed mission launch is set for 2035.
  • The mission could take up to 50 years to complete.
  • The Solar Oberth manoeuvre will be used to accelerate the spacecraft.
  • The i4is team includes Adam Hibberd, Andreas Hein, and T. Marshall Eubanks.

Timeline

Comet 3I/ATLAS was detected after it had already passed Jupiter's orbit, missing the optimal launch window. The i4is team has since explored alternative strategies for intercepting the comet.

In 2035, the team plans to launch a spacecraft that will leverage the Solar Oberth manoeuvre, which uses the Sun's gravity to increase speed during an engine burn.

The mission aims to reach 3I/ATLAS despite its high velocity and the considerable distance involved, with the potential for groundbreaking scientific discoveries along the way.

Official statements

“This timing is crucial: it reduces the demands on the launch vehicle, limits the spacecraft's fuel requirements, and shortens the mission's flight time,” said Adam Hibberd, a software and research engineer with i4is.
“Studying 3I/ATLAS up close would allow scientists to analyse its chemical makeup, structure, and dust emissions,” Hibberd added. “These findings could reshape our understanding of planetary formation across the galaxy.”

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third interstellar object detected in the solar system, following 'Oumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019. Unlike comets originating from within our solar system, 3I/ATLAS carries material that has not been influenced by our Sun.

Current technology is sufficient for the planned Solar Oberth approach, while alternative propulsion methods, such as directed-energy propulsion, remain years away from practical application.

What’s next

The i4is team will continue developing the mission plan and technologies required for the intercept. If successful, the mission will provide unprecedented insights into the composition and formation of planetary systems beyond our own.
