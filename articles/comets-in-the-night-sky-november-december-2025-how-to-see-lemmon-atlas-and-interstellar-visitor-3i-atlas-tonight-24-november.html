Comets in the Night Sky November–December 2025: How to See Lemmon, ATLAS and Interstellar Visitor 3I/ATLAS Tonight

On 24 November 2025, skywatchers can observe several comets, including the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, which has been prominently featured in recent NASA images captured by spacecraft orbiting Mars. The visibility of these comets varies based on geographical location and local weather conditions.

This event is significant as it offers a rare opportunity to view multiple comets simultaneously, including the naked-eye comet C/2025 A6 (Lemmon) and the fragmented C/2025 K1 (ATLAS). Astronomers are actively monitoring these celestial bodies to report on their conditions and visibility.

Key facts

  • Date: 24 November 2025
  • Visible Comets: 3I/ATLAS, C/2025 A6 (Lemmon), C/2025 K1 (ATLAS), C/2025 R2 (SWAN)
  • Magnitude: 3I/ATLAS ~10, C/2025 A6 (Lemmon) ~5.2, C/2025 K1 (ATLAS) ~10–11
  • Best Viewing Times: C/2025 A6 (Lemmon) after sunset, C/2025 K1 (ATLAS) before dawn
  • Location for Lemmon: Low in the south-south-west, constellation Ophiuchus
  • Location for K1 (ATLAS): High in the northern sky, constellation Ursa Major
  • Distance from Earth: 3I/ATLAS approximately 0.40 AU

Timeline

On 8 October 2025, C/2025 K1 (ATLAS) made a close approach to the Sun, leading to its notable transformation into a golden comet. Following this, on 11–12 November, astronomers observed the comet breaking into multiple fragments. By 24 November, 3I/ATLAS has been identified as a key target for astronomers and is positioned in the pre-dawn sky in the constellation Virgo.

Official statements

“The visibility of these comets offers a unique chance for amateur astronomers and enthusiasts to engage with celestial events,” said a NASA spokesperson.

Context

Comet 3I/ATLAS is part of a larger trend of increased visibility for interstellar comets, which are rare and provide valuable data for scientific research. The fragmentation of C/2025 K1 (ATLAS) is particularly noteworthy as it highlights the dynamic nature of cometary bodies.

What’s next

Observers are encouraged to continue tracking these comets into December, when moonless nights may enhance visibility, especially for C/2025 K1 (ATLAS) as it fades but remains circumpolar in the northern hemisphere.
