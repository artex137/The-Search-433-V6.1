Google Maps users found a ‘flying saucer’ in Antarctica

Google Maps users have discovered a round object resembling a UFO in Antarctica, specifically in the area of Banger Hills, at coordinates 66°16’24.5″ S 100°59’03.5″ E. The finding has sparked interest among ufologists and internet users, but no injuries or fatalities have been reported.

This discovery has captured the attention of both enthusiasts and skeptics. Authorities, including scientists, have responded to the claims, with some providing explanations for the object. The debate highlights the ongoing intrigue surrounding Antarctica, a region known for its mysteries.

Key facts

  • Discovery made by Google Maps users in Antarctica.
  • Object located at 66°16’24.5″ S 100°59’03.5″ E.
  • Identified as a rounded object resembling a ‘flying saucer.’
  • Professor Bethan Davies from the University of Newcastle has provided a scientific explanation.
  • Object described as a small lake in a rocky basin, not a UFO.

Timeline

Google Maps users have recently identified a strange round object in Antarctica, drawing immediate comparisons to UFOs. The object has been examined by experts who have offered alternative explanations.

Onlookers have noted that the object features a silvery line along its edge and dark stripes surrounding it. This has led to various interpretations, with some insisting on the possibility of an alien craft crash, while others suggest it is a natural formation.

Official statements

“This is not a UFO at all, but a small lake in a rocky basin,” said Professor Bethan Davies, a glaciologist from the University of Newcastle.

Context

The interest in this object reflects a broader fascination with Antarctica, which is often seen as a land of mystery. Prior incidents involving UFO sightings and claims have fueled public imagination about extraterrestrial life in unexplored regions.

What’s next

Investigations into the object’s nature may continue, but no formal inquiries have been announced. The debate over its identity is expected to persist among both scientists and enthusiasts.
