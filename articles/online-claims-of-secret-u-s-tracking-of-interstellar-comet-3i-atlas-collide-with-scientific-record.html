Online Claims of Secret U.S. Tracking of Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS Collide With Scientific Record

The interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS entered the inner Solar System in late 2025, prompting extensive scientific observation. Simultaneously, online claims emerged suggesting that U.S. authorities have monitored the comet for decades through a secret program called CASSANDRA. No U.S. agency has confirmed the existence of this program or any prior tracking of the comet, and scientists assert that the evidence presented online fails to hold up under scrutiny.

This situation underscores the ongoing tension between scientific discovery and misinformation. Authorities have not issued statements regarding any classified tracking program, and scientists continue to emphasize the importance of evidence-based research in understanding celestial objects.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS arrived in the inner Solar System in late 2025.
  • No U.S. agency has confirmed the existence of a program named CASSANDRA.
  • Scientists assert that online evidence of tracking lacks credibility.
  • Claims of prior monitoring have not been substantiated.

Timeline

In late 2025, the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS was observed entering the inner Solar System. Following its arrival, various online sources began to circulate claims about U.S. authorities tracking the comet through a classified program.

As scientists analyzed the comet, they found no credible evidence supporting the claims of prior monitoring. U.S. agencies have not responded to these assertions, and the scientific community has focused on empirical research and observation.

Official statements

“The evidence cited online does not withstand scrutiny,” scientists have stated, reinforcing the need for rigorous scientific methods in the study of celestial phenomena.

Context

The claims regarding 3I/ATLAS are part of a broader trend of misinformation surrounding space exploration and government programs. Historically, similar assertions have emerged during significant astronomical events, often lacking substantiation from credible sources.

What’s next

Scientists will continue to study comet 3I/ATLAS and gather data, while the conversation around misinformation in scientific discourse is expected to persist.
