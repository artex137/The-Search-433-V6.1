700 AU Catch: How to Intercept 3I/ATLAS

A new mission plan targeting the comet 3I/ATLAS has been proposed, aiming to launch a spacecraft over 700 astronomical units (AU) from the Sun. The mission, which involves a significant rocket burn at the closest approach to the Sun, could potentially lead to a flyby of the comet by 2085. No injuries or fatalities have been reported in relation to this mission.

This mission is significant as it seeks to employ the Oberth effect, a technique that could propel the spacecraft to unprecedented speeds. The proposal highlights the challenges and innovations required to navigate extreme conditions close to the Sun. The mission's success hinges on advanced engineering and trajectory adjustments through gravity assists.

Key facts

  • Mission targets comet 3I/ATLAS, currently moving away from the Sun at over 61 km/s.
  • Proposed spacecraft will perform a solar Oberth manoeuvre at 0.015 AU from the Sun.
  • Projected journey time to intercept the comet is between 30 to 50 years.
  • Flyby of 3I/ATLAS is targeted for the year 2085.
  • Delta-V required for the mission is approximately 2.3 km/s.

Timeline

The mission begins with a transit to Jupiter, where the spacecraft will use the planet's gravitational force to decelerate. This step is crucial for achieving the necessary trajectory for a close solar flyby. Following this, the spacecraft will execute a solar Oberth manoeuvre, which is key to gaining the velocity needed to intercept 3I/ATLAS.

The spacecraft will then embark on a long-term journey that could span several decades. With the right conditions, the transit time could be reduced significantly, enhancing the feasibility of the mission.

Official statements

“Pretty much every launch uses the Oberth effect,” explained T. Marshall Eubanks, a former NASA scientist. “However, I haven’t encountered any documented instances of a direct Oberth manoeuvre of the type we’re proposing.”
“For future interstellar objects, a solar Oberth manoeuvre should be avoided if possible,” cautioned Adam Hibberd, a leading authority in this field.

Context

The mission to 3I/ATLAS represents a pioneering approach in space exploration, particularly in the context of interstellar objects. Previous missions have not utilized the Oberth effect in such a direct manner, making this proposal a potential landmark in the field.

As technology advances, the challenges of such missions may become more manageable, potentially leading to more frequent exploration of interstellar objects.

What’s next

Further research and engineering assessments are required to address the risks associated with the solar Oberth manoeuvre. The mission remains in the proposal stage, with ongoing discussions about its feasibility and potential modifications to improve safety and efficiency.
