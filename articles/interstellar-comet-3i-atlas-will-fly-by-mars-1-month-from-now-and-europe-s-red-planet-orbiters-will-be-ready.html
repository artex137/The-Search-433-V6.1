Interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS will fly by Mars 1 month from now — and Europe's Red Planet orbiters will be ready

The interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS will pass approximately 30 million kilometers from Mars on October 3, 2025, according to the European Space Agency (ESA). This approach is significantly closer than the comet's distance to Earth, which will be about 270 million kilometers.

This event presents a unique opportunity for scientists to study the comet using Mars-based spacecraft. ESA has confirmed that it will utilize both the Mars Express and the ExoMars Trace Gas Orbiter (TGO) to gather data during the comet's closest approach.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS will pass within 30 million kilometers of Mars on October 3, 2025.
  • It will be 270 million kilometers away from Earth at its closest approach.
  • ESA will use Mars Express and TGO to observe the comet.
  • The comet was discovered on July 1, 2025.
  • NASA’s MAVEN and Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter may also assist in observations.
  • The James Webb Space Telescope has already observed 3I/ATLAS.

Timeline

On July 1, 2025, astronomers discovered the interstellar object now known as comet 3I/ATLAS. Its passing near Mars was confirmed, prompting ESA to prepare for observations. As the date approaches, ESA has outlined plans to utilize its orbiters for data collection during the comet's flyby.

Official statements

“We can confirm that we are planning to observe 3I/Atlas around the time of its closest approach to Mars,” said Colin Frank Wilson, Project Scientist for ESA's Mars orbiters. “This is predicted to occur on 3 October 2025.”
“We will attempt to obtain images of the object using the High Resolution Stereo Camera (HRSC) onboard Mars Express,” Wilson added. “We will also attempt to measure the spectrum of the object, using the TGO's NOMAD and MEx's OMEGA and SPICAM spectrometers.”

Context

The discovery of 3I/ATLAS marks an important event in astronomy, as it is the third confirmed interstellar object to enter our solar system. Previous interstellar visitors include 1I/'Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov, which provided valuable insights into the nature of such objects.

What’s next

As October 3 approaches, ESA will finalize its observational strategies and prepare its spacecraft for data collection. The success of these efforts will depend on the brightness and activity of 3I/ATLAS during its flyby.
