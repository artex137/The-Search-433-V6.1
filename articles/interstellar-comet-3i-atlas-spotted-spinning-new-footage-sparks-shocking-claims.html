Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS Spotted Spinning? New Footage Sparks Shocking Claims

New videos and images have emerged online suggesting that interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS may be spinning as it travels through space. Amateur astronomers have shared footage showing unusual motion patterns, including rotating structures and changing "coma" around the comet. No leading space agencies have validated these claims so far.

This development is significant as it could alter our understanding of 3I/ATLAS's behavior in space. Authorities are investigating the claims and awaiting further data from space agencies, including NASA and the European Space Agency (ESA).

Key facts

  • Amateur astronomers have circulated videos and images of 3I/ATLAS.
  • The ESA's ExoMars Trace Gas Orbiter (TGO) captured images of the comet from around Mars.
  • The TGO reported a rotation period of approximately 16.16 hours.
  • NASA scientists have stated that 3I/ATLAS “behaves like a comet.”
  • New imagery from NASA is expected to be released in Fall 2023.

Timeline

On November 18, 2025, amateur astronomers shared footage of 3I/ATLAS, showing potential spinning and rotating patterns. The ESA's TGO captured images of the comet, highlighting its fuzzy dot appearance and coma. Following these observations, NASA scientists defended their analysis of the comet's behavior, indicating no evidence of alien technology.

As of November 21, 2025, social media users have circulated claims about dust rings or "detached shells" surrounding the nucleus of the comet at distances of approximately 150,000 km.

Official statements

“3I/ATLAS behaves like a comet,” said NASA scientists, clarifying that there are no technosignatures present.

Context

This is not the first time 3I/ATLAS has attracted attention. Its previous observations have generated interest among scientists and space watchers. The ongoing debate about the comet's behavior highlights the challenges in interpreting astronomical phenomena.

What’s next

NASA is preparing to release new imagery of 3I/ATLAS, which may clarify the spinning claims. ESA's JUICE spacecraft is also monitoring the comet for additional data. Observers on Earth are expected to begin viewing 3I/ATLAS again in November and December 2025.
