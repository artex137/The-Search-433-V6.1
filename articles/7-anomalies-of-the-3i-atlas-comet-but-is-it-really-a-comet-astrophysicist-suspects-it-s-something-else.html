7 anomalies of the 3I/ATLAS comet: But is it really a comet? Astrophysicist suspects it's something else

Astrophysicist Prof. Avi Loeb has identified seven anomalies associated with the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS, which entered our Solar System in July 2025. The findings have sparked debate about whether 3I/ATLAS is a comet or a technological artifact, as its features deviate from typical natural interstellar objects.

This analysis is significant as it challenges existing models of celestial objects and raises questions about the nature of 3I/ATLAS. Astronomers are closely monitoring the object, as its unique characteristics could offer new insights into the origins of interstellar visitors.

Key facts

  • 3I/ATLAS is the third confirmed interstellar object discovered on July 1, 2025.
  • Estimated diameter exceeds 5 km, potentially up to 20 km or more.
  • Orbital inclination is only 5 degrees relative to the ecliptic.
  • Discovery occurred at 4.5 AU from the Sun.
  • Timing allows for close passages by Venus, Mars, and Jupiter.
  • Alignment with the "Wow! Signal" is within 9 degrees.
  • Loeb's "Loeb Scale" ranks 3I/ATLAS at least 2 for technological potential.

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was discovered on July 1, 2025. It entered the Solar System from a distant star system, exhibiting several anomalies that differ from typical comets or asteroids.

As astronomers gathered data, they noted the unusual features of 3I/ATLAS, leading to increased scrutiny and analysis, particularly by Prof. Loeb.

Further observations are planned, including from Mars orbiters in October 2025, to gather more information about the object.

Official statements

“While natural explanations exist for each anomaly individually, explaining all seven simultaneously strains conventional models,” said Prof. Loeb. “Ongoing observations are crucial for resolution.”

Context

Prior to 3I/ATLAS, only two interstellar objects, 1I/'Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov, had been confirmed. Both were significantly smaller than 3I/ATLAS, with diameters of 0.2 km and 1 km, respectively. The rarity of large interstellar objects adds to the significance of this discovery.

The anomalies identified by Loeb challenge existing astrophysical theories and raise the possibility of technological origins, a notion that has garnered both interest and skepticism within the scientific community.

What’s next

Astronomers will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS, with observations planned from Mars orbiters in October 2025 to further investigate its characteristics and origins.
