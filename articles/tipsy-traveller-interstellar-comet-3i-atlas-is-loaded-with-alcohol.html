Tipsy traveller: Interstellar comet 3I/Atlas is loaded with alcohol

Astronomers have discovered that the interstellar comet 3IATLAS, the third confirmed interstellar visitor to our Solar System, is rich in methanol, a type of alcohol. The findings were announced on March 10, 2026, following observations made at the Atacama Large Millimeter/submillimeter Array (ALMA) in Chile.

This discovery is significant as it offers insights into the chemical composition of comets and the conditions under which planets may form in distant star systems. Researchers, led by Nathan Roth from American University, have noted that the high levels of methanol found in 3IATLAS are not typically observed in comets from our own Solar System.

Key facts

  • Comet 3IATLAS is the third confirmed interstellar comet detected.
  • High levels of methanol compared to hydrogen cyanide were measured.
  • Observations were conducted using ALMA in Chile in late 2025.
  • Methanol-to-hydrogen cyanide ratios were recorded at approximately 70 and 120.
  • Previous observations with the James Webb Space Telescope indicated large amounts of carbon dioxide in the comet's coma.

Timeline

In late 2025, astronomers used ALMA's Morita Array to observe comet 3IATLAS as it approached the Sun. They noted that sunlight heated the comet's icy surface, causing frozen materials to vaporize and form a coma. Multiple observations during this period revealed the comet's unusual chemical composition.

On two specific observation dates, scientists recorded the methanol-to-hydrogen cyanide ratios, confirming the comet's status as one of the most methanol-rich comets studied to date.

Official statements

“Observing 3IATLAS is like taking a fingerprint from another solar system,” said Nathan Roth, the study’s lead author. “The details reveal what it’s made of, and it’s bursting with methanol in a way we just don’t usually see in comets in our own Solar System.”

Context

Comet 3IATLAS follows the earlier detection of two other interstellar objects, 1I‘Oumuamua and 2IBorisov. These rare visitors provide valuable insights into the chemical conditions and processes occurring in planetary systems across the Milky Way galaxy.

What’s next

Further analysis of comet 3IATLAS is expected as astronomers continue to study its chemical makeup and the implications for understanding planetary formation in other star systems.
