The Non-Gravitational Acceleration of 3I/ATLAS is Not Directed Away from the Sun

A new analysis has found that the non-gravitational acceleration of comet 3I/ATLAS is not directed away from the Sun, contradicting earlier reports from NASA. The findings were published on March 3, 2026, and highlight significant differences in the understanding of the comet's movement based on its observed behavior.

This analysis matters because it challenges the existing models used by NASA to predict the trajectory of interstellar objects. The findings suggest that the dynamics of 3I/ATLAS are influenced by complex factors including localized mass loss and the presence of jets, which could have implications for future studies of similar celestial bodies. Researchers are now reviewing the new data to refine their models of comet behavior.

Key facts

  • Publication Date: March 3, 2026
  • Object: Comet 3I/ATLAS
  • NASA's previous report indicated a radial acceleration component five times larger than the tangential component.
  • The new analysis shows similar amplitudes for both radial and tangential components.
  • High-resolution images reveal the existence of collimated, high-latitude jets from the comet.

Timeline

In August 2026, observations of 3I/ATLAS approaching the Sun revealed collimated jets that exhibited a periodic wobble, suggesting a complex structure in the comet’s nucleus. This morphology indicated that mass loss was localized, rather than uniform, which could lead to significant non-radial acceleration.

Following these observations, researchers published a comprehensive analysis on March 3, 2026, that presented findings inconsistent with NASA's earlier conclusions. The new paper derived similar magnitudes for both radial and tangential acceleration components, suggesting a sideways push rather than a simple outward acceleration from the Sun.

Official statements

“Official statements from NASA can be wrong. Science is a learning experience.” – Avi Loeb, co-author of the new analysis.

Context

The analysis of 3I/ATLAS follows a trend in recent years where new observational technologies have led to revisions of existing models of celestial bodies. Earlier studies often relied on simplified models that did not account for the complexities of comet behavior, particularly in the case of interstellar objects.

This new understanding of 3I/ATLAS may prompt a reevaluation of other comets and their trajectories, especially those exhibiting similar non-gravitational behaviors.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to analyze the data from 3I/ATLAS to further understand the implications of its unique acceleration patterns. Future studies may focus on investigating the dynamics of its jets and their contribution to its overall movement in space.
