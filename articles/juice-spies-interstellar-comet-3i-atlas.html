Juice Spies Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS

The JANUS science camera aboard the European Space Agency’s (ESA) Jupiter Icy Moons Explorer (Juice) has captured new images of the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS on November 6, 2025. The images were taken seven days after the comet made its closest approach to the Sun, with Juice located approximately 66 million km from the comet.

This observation is significant as it marks a detailed study of an interstellar object that has entered the Solar System. The data collected will help scientists understand the comet's behavior and composition, contributing to knowledge about interstellar bodies. ESA has utilized five science instruments aboard Juice to gather information on 3I/ATLAS throughout November 2025.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS was first spotted on July 1, 2025, by the ATLAS survey telescope in Chile.
  • 3I/ATLAS reached perihelion on October 30, 2025, coming within 1.4 AU (210 million km) of the Sun.
  • The comet follows the most dynamically extreme orbit ever measured in the Solar System.
  • JANUS has captured over 120 images of 3I/ATLAS across a large wavelength range.
  • ESA is analyzing the data gathered to understand the comet's composition and behavior.

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first detected on July 1, 2025. It passed its closest point to the Sun on October 30, 2025. Following this, the Juice spacecraft conducted observations throughout November 2025, utilizing its science instruments to gather data on the comet.

On November 6, 2025, the JANUS camera captured images of the comet, which are now being analyzed by researchers. The data transmission from Juice to Earth was delayed due to the spacecraft's position on the opposite side of the Sun.

Official statements

“During the months that followed the observations, Juice was on the opposite side of the Sun to Earth,” said members of the Juice team. “It was using its main high-gain antenna as a heat shield, and its smaller medium-gain antenna to send back data to Earth at a lower rate.”
“This meant that we had to wait until last week to receive the data,” they explained. “We are now working hard to analyze them.”
“[The newly-released JANUS image] displays jets coming out of the nucleus of 3I/ATLAS opposite to the direction of the Sun,” said Harvard University’s Professor Avi Loeb. “This is surprising since pockets of ice on the surface of a rock are supposed to be warmed up by sunlight on the Sun-facing side, creating jets that are initially directed at the Sun.”

Context

3I/ATLAS is notable for being an interstellar comet, having entered the Solar System from the direction of the constellation Sagittarius. Its extreme orbital path highlights the unique characteristics of interstellar objects compared to typical Solar System comets.

The study of such comets is essential for understanding the formation and evolution of solar systems, as well as the materials that exist beyond our own.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to analyze the images and spectrometry data collected by Juice. They aim to uncover more about the composition and behavior of 3I/ATLAS, contributing to the broader understanding of interstellar objects.
