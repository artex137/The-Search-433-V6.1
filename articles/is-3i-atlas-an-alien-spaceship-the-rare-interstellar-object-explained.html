Is 3I/ATLAS an Alien Spaceship? The Rare Interstellar Object Explained

The interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS has been discovered, making its first appearance on July 1, 2025, by the ATLAS survey telescope in Chile. This marks only the third confirmed interstellar object ever found, after ‘Oumuamua and comet 2I/Borisov. While some theorists speculate it could be an alien probe, official sources maintain it is a natural comet.

This discovery matters because it offers insights into objects from beyond our Solar System. NASA, the European Space Agency (ESA), and most astronomers support the view that 3I/ATLAS is a typical comet, despite some ongoing debate regarding its unusual characteristics.

Key facts

  • Discovered: July 1, 2025
  • Location: Observed in the vicinity of Jupiter, Mars, and Venus
  • Size estimate: 0.6–5.6 km across
  • Brightness: Initially appeared too bright for its distance
  • Official position: Confirmed as a natural comet by NASA and ESA

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first identified on July 1, 2025, as part of ongoing observations of interstellar objects. The initial brightness led to speculation about its size and nature. Over time, further observations revealed it is behaving like a typical comet, with signs of a coma and tail.

In September 2025, 3I/ATLAS was noted to turn green, drawing additional public interest. Observations have confirmed the presence of carbon dioxide in its coma, supporting the classification of 3I/ATLAS as a comet.

Official statements

“The latest evidence overwhelmingly shows that 3I/ATLAS is a comet,” said Tom Statler, NASA’s lead scientist for the mission. “It’s growing a coma and tail, spewing out dust and gas, and brightening as the Sun’s heat wakes it up.”

Context

Prior to 3I/ATLAS, only two other interstellar objects had been confirmed: ‘Oumuamua and comet 2I/Borisov. These discoveries have heightened interest in the study of celestial objects from outside our Solar System. The debate surrounding 3I/ATLAS highlights the tension between mainstream scientific consensus and more speculative theories.

What’s next

As 3I/ATLAS continues its journey through the Solar System, astronomers will monitor its trajectory. It is expected to slip behind the Sun at the end of October 2025, temporarily disappearing from observation. Further studies will aim to gather more data on its composition and behavior as it approaches its closest point to the Sun.
