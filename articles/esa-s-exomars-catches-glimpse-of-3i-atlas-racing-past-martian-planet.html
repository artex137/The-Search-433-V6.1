ESA’s ExoMars catches glimpse of 3I/ATLAS racing past Martian planet

The European Space Agency (ESA) has released new images of the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS, taken by the ExoMars Trace Gas Orbiter (TGO), which is currently located 30 million kilometers from Mars. The images show 3I/ATLAS as a tiny, blurry white dot, with no reported injuries or fatalities.

This observation is significant as it provides insights into the characteristics of interstellar comets. ESA officials have stated that studying such objects can reveal information about the formation of worlds outside our solar system. The TGO's images follow earlier captures of the object by NASA's Perseverance Rover, which depicted it as a large, green-emitting cylindrical stripe.

Key facts

  • Object: 3I/ATLAS
  • Distance from Mars: 30 million kilometers
  • Nucleus diameter: 5 km to 46 km
  • Bright glow measured: 68 kilometers wide
  • First spotted: July 2023 by ATLAS telescope in Río Hurtado, Chile
  • Third interstellar object detected: Following 1I/Oumuamua (2017) and 2I/Borisov (2019)
  • No threat to Earth

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first detected in July 2023 by the Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System (ATLAS) telescope in Río Hurtado, Chile. Following this, NASA's Perseverance Rover captured images of the object, showing it as a large, green-emitting cylinder. Recently, ESA's TGO provided additional images, revealing the object as a tiny white dot at a distance of 30 million kilometers from Mars.

Official statements

“This was a very challenging observation for the instrument. The comet is around 10,000 to 100,000 times fainter than our usual target,” said CaSSIS Principal Investigator Nick Thomas.
“Every planet, moon, asteroid, comet and lifeform in our solar system share a common origin. But interstellar comets are true outsiders, carrying clues about the formation of worlds far beyond our own,” ESA officials stated.

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third interstellar object detected by astronomers, following 1I/Oumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019. The study of interstellar comets like 3I/ATLAS can provide valuable information about the early solar system and the origins of celestial bodies.

What’s next

ESA continues to analyze the data collected by the TGO as the object moves closer to other planets, including Venus, Mars, and Jupiter, while remaining at a safe distance from Earth.
