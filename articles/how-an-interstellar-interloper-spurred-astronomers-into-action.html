How an Interstellar Interloper Spurred Astronomers into Action

On 1 July 2025, astronomers detected a comet from deep space, designated as 3I/ATLAS, just inside Jupiter’s orbit. The object was moving at a speed four times faster than the New Horizons probe that passed Pluto. It was first identified by the Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System (ATLAS) in Chile, which is designed to detect fast-moving objects. There have been no reported injuries or fatalities related to this discovery.

This detection is significant as it marks the third confirmed interstellar object to enter our solar system. Scientists are currently mobilizing to study 3I/ATLAS before it escapes the Sun's gravitational pull in mid-2026. This has led to a surge of activity among researchers in the field of interstellar objects (ISOs), as they seek to gather as much data as possible on this unique visitor.

Key facts

  • Date of discovery: 1 July 2025
  • Location of discovery: Inside Jupiter’s orbit, detected by ATLAS in Chile
  • Speed of 3I/ATLAS: Four times faster than New Horizons
  • Time frame for escape from the Sun: Mid-2026
  • Number of research papers released: Nearly three dozen

Timeline

On 1 July 2025, ATLAS detected the interstellar object, which prompted immediate calculations of its orbit and trajectory by astronomers. Subsequent analysis revealed that 3I/ATLAS originated from a distant star system, not the Oort cloud, which is typical for most comets.

Researchers quickly mobilized, securing time on major telescopes worldwide and combing through archives for precovery images. By 2 July, teams began to observe the comet, with some researchers triggering their proposals just six hours after its confirmation as an ISO.

Official statements

“We knew we were going to get a 3I. We didn’t know when we were going to get a 3I,” said Michele Bannister, a researcher at the University of Canterbury in Aotearoa New Zealand.
“It was a very communal planning process, which I think for science often doesn’t happen so quick and on the fly,” said Teddy Kareta, a planetary scientist at Villanova University.

Context

The arrival of 3I/ATLAS follows the discoveries of two previous interstellar objects: 1I/’Oumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019. The detection of ‘Oumuamua surprised astronomers, who had limited time to gather data before it left the solar system. This experience led to improvements in the proposal process for telescope observations, allowing for more efficient responses to future discoveries.

With the detection of 3I/ATLAS, astronomers have implemented target of opportunity (TOO) proposals, enabling them to activate pre-approved observational programs at major telescopes as soon as an ISO is confirmed.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to observe 3I/ATLAS as it approaches its closest point to the Sun. They aim to gather as much data as possible before it escapes the solar system in mid-2026. Additional studies and analyses are expected to follow as more observations are made.
