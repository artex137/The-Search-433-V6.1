Rare Cosmic Flyby: Interstellar Comet 3I/Atlas to Zip Past Mars as Spacecraft Brace for Close-Up Study

Interstellar Comet 3I/Atlas will make a close approach to Mars on October 3, 2025, at a distance of about 30 million kilometers. The comet was first discovered on July 1, 2025, by the ATLAS survey telescope in Chile, and it is only the third known interstellar object observed in our solar system.

This flyby is significant as it provides an unprecedented opportunity for scientists to study the comet's composition and behavior. Both the European Space Agency (ESA) and NASA have mobilized their Mars orbiters and surface missions to gather data during this event.

Key facts

  • Discovery date: July 1, 2025
  • Comet speed: Approximately 219,000 km/h
  • Date of Mars flyby: October 3, 2025
  • Closest approach to Mars: ~30 million kilometers
  • Instruments involved: Mars Express, ExoMars Trace Gas Orbiter, Perseverance, Curiosity, Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter
  • Next major observation: ESA’s Jupiter Icy Moons Explorer (JUICE) between November 2–25, 2025
  • Closest approach to Earth: ~1.8 AU (about 169 million miles) in December 2025

Timeline

Comet 3I/Atlas was identified on July 1, 2025, marking its entry into the solar system. It will approach Mars on October 3, 2025, during which time multiple spacecraft will observe it closely. Following the Mars flyby, JUICE will observe the comet in November 2025, shortly after it reaches perihelion.

Official statements

“Data from this period will yield the most valuable data, revealing its true chemical makeup and activity at peak intensity,” ESA noted regarding the observations planned during the comet's perihelion.

Context

3I/Atlas is notable for being the third interstellar object ever observed, following ʻOumuamua and comet Borisov. Its composition differs significantly from typical solar system comets, primarily emitting carbon dioxide rather than water vapor. This raises questions about the chemical diversity of celestial bodies originating from different star systems.

What’s next

Following the flyby, astronomers will continue to observe 3I/Atlas as it moves through the solar system. Data collected will help reveal the comet's characteristics and behavior as it approaches perihelion later in October 2025 and its subsequent journey toward Jupiter.
