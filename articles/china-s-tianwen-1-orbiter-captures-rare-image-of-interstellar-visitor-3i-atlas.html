China’s Tianwen-1 Orbiter Captures Rare Image of Interstellar Visitor 3I/ATLAS

China's Tianwen-1 orbiter has successfully photographed the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS from approximately 30 million kilometres away on 3 October 2025. The China National Space Administration (CNSA) reported that the orbiter's high-resolution camera captured clear images of the object's nucleus and hazy coma, revealing its comet-like features.

This observation is significant as it marks one of the closest studies of 3I/ATLAS achieved by a probe. Discovered on 1 July 2025 by a telescope in Chile, researchers believe the object originated near ancient stars close to the galactic centre, making it between 3 and 11 billion years old. This insight could help scientists understand the composition and evolution of exoplanets and the early history of stars.

Key facts

  • Date of observation: 3 October 2025
  • Distance from Earth: Approximately 30 million kilometres
  • Speed of 3I/ATLAS: 58 kilometres per second
  • Relative speed to Tianwen-1: 86 kilometres per second
  • Size of 3I/ATLAS: 5.6 kilometres wide
  • Brightness: 10,000 to 100,000 times dimmer than the Martian surface
  • Discovery date: 1 July 2025

Timeline

The Tianwen-1 orbiter has been operational since it entered Mars’ orbit in February 2021. In early September 2025, the team began preparations for the observation of 3I/ATLAS, conducting multiple simulations and theoretical models to ensure precise imaging. On 3 October 2025, the orbiter successfully captured images of the interstellar object.

The captured images allowed scientists to produce an animated sequence using 30 seconds of images, aiding in the study of 3I/ATLAS's motion path.

Official statements

“This observation is highly significant as it opens a new window into understanding the origins of interstellar objects,” said a spokesperson from CNSA.

Context

The successful observation of 3I/ATLAS adds to China's growing achievements in space exploration. The Tianwen-1 mission has provided valuable data that enhances knowledge about celestial bodies beyond our solar system. Such missions contribute to the understanding of ancient cosmic phenomena and the evolution of our galaxy.

What’s next

The CNSA plans to use the experience gained from this observation to prepare for the upcoming Tianwen-2 asteroid exploration mission. The continued operation of the Tianwen-1 orbiter after four years and eight months in space demonstrates its reliability for future scientific endeavors.
