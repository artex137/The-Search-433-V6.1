Harvard Astrophysicist Flags 3I/ATLAS as Potential ‘Black Swan’ Threat to Earth’s Defenses

Cambridge, Massachusetts – October 27, 2025 – Harvard University astrophysicist Avi Loeb has warned about the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS, describing it as a potential "black swan event" that could pose existential risks to humanity. The object is set to reach its perihelion on October 29, 2025. Nasa's International Asteroid Warning Network (IAWN) has initiated an emergency observational probe to analyze its unusual trajectory and composition.

This warning is significant as 3I/ATLAS could be more than just a conventional comet. Discovered on July 1, 2025, by NASA's Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System (ATLAS) telescope in Chile, it is the third confirmed interstellar visitor to our solar system, following 'Oumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019. The object measures approximately 10 kilometers in diameter and travels at speeds exceeding 30 kilometers per second.

Key facts

  • Object name: 3I/ATLAS
  • Size: Approximately 10 kilometers in diameter
  • Discovered: July 1, 2025, by NASA's ATLAS telescope in Chile
  • Perihelion date: October 29, 2025
  • Speed: Over 30 kilometers per second
  • Minimum distance from Earth: Approximately 1.2 astronomical units on December 19, 2025
  • Emergency observational campaign dates: November 27, 2025, to January 27, 2026

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was discovered on July 1, 2025, and has been under observation since. As it approaches its perihelion on October 29, 2025, initial observations have revealed an anti-solar tail and emissions of nickel tetracarbonyl. NASA's IAWN has mobilized to monitor the object closely.

In September 2025, the object's tail reversed direction, raising questions about its nature. An emergency astrometry campaign is set to begin on November 27, 2025, to refine predictions regarding its trajectory.

Official statements

“While it poses no threat, 3I/ATLAS presents a great opportunity for the IAWN community to perform an observing exercise.” – Frank Budnik, ESA orbital specialist
“We should not miss an opportunity, because this is a gift from interstellar space.” – Avi Loeb

Context

3I/ATLAS follows two previous interstellar objects, 'Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov. Loeb's claims regarding its possible artificial origin have sparked debate within the scientific community, with some researchers expressing skepticism about the extraterrestrial hypothesis.

The object's size and unusual characteristics have led to discussions about planetary defense and the potential implications of encountering an engineered probe.

What’s next

The IAWN will conduct an emergency observational campaign from November 27, 2025, to January 27, 2026, to gather more data on 3I/ATLAS. Further observations are planned near NASA's Juno spacecraft at Jupiter on March 16, 2026.
