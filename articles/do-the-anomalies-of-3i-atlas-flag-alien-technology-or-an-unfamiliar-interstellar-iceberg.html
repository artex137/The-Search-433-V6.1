Do the Anomalies of 3I/ATLAS Flag Alien Technology or an Unfamiliar Interstellar Iceberg?

Avi Loeb has raised questions about the nature of comet 3I/ATLAS, which was discovered on July 1, 2025. Loeb's analysis suggests that this comet may not be a typical water-rich comet, but rather something more unusual, potentially an interstellar artifact. The discussion follows a recent interview on Fox News on November 16, 2025.

This matter is significant as it challenges conventional views on comet classification and suggests the need for a broader framework in understanding interstellar objects. Loeb argues that humanity's technological objects should be included in the dataset for comet studies, which could reshape our understanding of these celestial bodies.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS was discovered on July 1, 2025.
  • Loeb's interview aired on November 16, 2025.
  • The comet's nucleus is estimated to be a million times more massive than 1I/‘Oumuamua.
  • 3I/ATLAS has displayed unusual sunward jets, challenging typical comet behavior.
  • The comet's retrograde trajectory aligns with the ecliptic plane with a 0.2% probability.

Timeline

On July 1, 2025, astronomers discovered 3I/ATLAS. Initial assessments categorized it as a typical water-rich comet. However, as observations continued, anomalies became apparent.

In August and early November 2025, 3I/ATLAS displayed a sunward jet, prompting further investigation into its composition and behavior. On November 16, Loeb discussed these findings in an interview, emphasizing the need to reconsider the classification of interstellar objects.

Official statements

“The foundation of science is based on the humility to learn, not the arrogance of expertise,” said Loeb during his interview with Fox News.
“Would comet experts recognize these visitors as technological artifacts if their training data set includes only icy rocks? I do not think so,” Loeb added.

Context

Prior to 3I/ATLAS, the discovery of 1I/‘Oumuamua in 2017 raised similar questions about the nature of interstellar objects. Experts have debated the classification of such objects, often relying on historical data that primarily includes icy comets.

Loeb argues that the traditional dataset may limit our understanding of potential interstellar artifacts, which could include advanced technology from other civilizations.

What’s next

Further investigations into 3I/ATLAS are expected as astronomers continue to analyze its trajectory and behavior. Loeb advocates for a reassessment of comet classification to include technological artifacts in future studies.
