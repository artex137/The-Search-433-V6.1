'Interstellar messenger' 3I/ATLAS could be nearly as old as the universe itself, James Webb telescope reveals

Interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, discovered last year, may be up to 12 billion years old, according to new observations from the James Webb Space Telescope (JWST). The findings suggest the comet formed in a cold region of the early Milky Way, potentially making it nearly as old as the universe itself.

This discovery is significant as it provides insights into the early history of our galaxy and the conditions that existed when 3I/ATLAS formed. Researchers are now analyzing isotope measurements taken by JWST to better understand the comet's origins and composition.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS is estimated to be 10 to 12 billion years old.
  • The comet was first discovered in 2022 as it traveled through our solar system.
  • It reached its closest point to the Sun (perihelion) on October 29, 2025.
  • 3I/ATLAS made its closest approach to Earth on December 19, 2025, coming within about 270 million km.
  • The comet is between 440 meters and 5.6 kilometers wide.
  • The JWST conducted observations on December 22, 2025.

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was discovered in 2022 as it sped through the solar system. Its trajectory and speed indicated that it could be the oldest comet ever observed. The comet reached perihelion on October 29, 2025, before making its closest approach to Earth on December 19, 2025, at a distance of approximately 270 million km. JWST made key observations of the comet a few days later, on December 22, 2025.

Official statements

“If 3I/ATLAS is indeed as old as this study suggests, the large amounts of volatile molecules it contains indicate that rich prebiotic chemistry may already have been occurring in star-forming regions very early in the history of our Galaxy,” said Romain Maggiolo, a research scientist at the Royal Belgian Institute for Space Aeronomy.
“This manuscript exemplifies that interstellar comets are unique bodies that are able to sample remote regions of our Milky Way galaxy,” said Josep Trigo-Rodríguez, principal investigator of the Asteroids, Comets and Meteorites research group.

Context

Comet 3I/ATLAS is only the third interstellar object recorded in our solar system. Previous studies have indicated that it could be older than 3 billion years, but the new findings from JWST provide a more precise estimate of its age and origin. This research sheds light on the formation conditions of interstellar comets and their role in understanding the early Milky Way.

What’s next

The study of 3I/ATLAS is still in the preprint stage and undergoing peer review. Researchers will continue to analyze the data collected by JWST to further understand the comet's composition and the implications of its age.
