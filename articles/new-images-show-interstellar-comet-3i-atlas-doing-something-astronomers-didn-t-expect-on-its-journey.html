New images show interstellar comet 3I ATLAS doing something astronomers didn't expect on its journey

Astronomers have released new images of interstellar comet 3I ATLAS, showcasing its unexpected behavior during its journey through our solar system. The latest images were obtained from multiple observatories around the world and provide unprecedented detail about the comet, which is currently moving away from the Sun after its closest approach.

This discovery is significant because it enhances our understanding of interstellar objects and their impact on our solar system. The observations have involved a coordinated effort across seven major observatories, allowing scientists to gather comprehensive data on this unique visitor.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I ATLAS was first detected in 2017.
  • It is only the third confirmed interstellar object after ‘Oumuamua and comet 2I/Borisov.
  • The comet's nucleus measures approximately 1-2 kilometers across.
  • The coordinated observation campaign included observatories in Hawaii, Chile, and the Canary Islands.
  • A rotation period of about 7.3 hours has been observed based on brightness variations.
  • 3I ATLAS is releasing water vapor at rates inconsistent with its distance from the Sun.

Timeline

3I ATLAS was detected in 2017, marking the beginning of its journey through our solar system. Since then, astronomers have tracked its trajectory, confirming that it follows a hyperbolic path, indicating it originated from interstellar space. The latest images were captured recently, providing a clearer view of the comet's nucleus and coma.

The coordinated observation campaign took place across four continents, allowing for a multi-wavelength analysis of the comet's characteristics. This effort has revealed details about the comet's chemical composition and behavior that were previously unknown.

Official statements

“Every pixel in these images tells a story about materials that formed around a completely different star,” said Dr. Sarah Chen, lead researcher at the International Comet Tracking Consortium.
“The radio data from ALMA was particularly exciting,” noted Dr. Michael Rodriguez from the Chile-based observatory team. “We’re detecting molecular signatures that suggest this comet formed in a much colder, more carbon-rich environment than anything in our solar system.”

Context

Before the discovery of 3I ATLAS, interstellar visitors were thought to be rare. The detection of three confirmed interstellar objects in recent years has changed this perspective and highlighted the potential for more such encounters. The findings from 3I ATLAS may provide insights into the building blocks of life across the Milky Way.

What’s next

Researchers are preparing for the next interstellar visitor, estimating that roughly one detectable interstellar comet may be expected each year based on statistical analysis. The techniques developed for tracking 3I ATLAS will also aid in identifying potentially hazardous asteroids closer to Earth.
