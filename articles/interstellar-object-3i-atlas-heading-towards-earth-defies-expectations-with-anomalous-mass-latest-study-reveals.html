Interstellar object 3I/ATLAS heading towards Earth defies expectations with anomalous mass, latest study reveals

On September 25, 2025, researchers in Cambridge, Massachusetts, have reported that interstellar object 3I/ATLAS possesses an anomalous mass exceeding 33 billion tons, raising questions about our understanding of such celestial visitors. The study's findings, based on data from 227 observatories worldwide, indicate that the object has an unusually large nucleus, measuring over 5 kilometres in diameter.

This discovery is significant as it challenges existing theories about interstellar objects. Authorities in the astronomical community are now preparing for upcoming observations that could provide more clarity on 3I/ATLAS and its characteristics.

Key facts

  • Mass of 3I/ATLAS: 33 billion tons
  • Nucleus size: over 5 kilometres in diameter
  • Data collected from 227 observatories worldwide
  • Observation period: mid-May to late September 2025
  • Positional shift: less than 0.028 arcseconds
  • Next observation: HiRISE camera on Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter scheduled for October 3, 2025

Timeline

In mid-May 2025, astronomers began collecting positional data on 3I/ATLAS from various observatories. Over the following months, they amassed a total of 4,022 observations. By late September 2025, the analysis revealed the object’s mass and nucleus size, indicating minimal non-gravitational acceleration despite active outgassing.

On October 3, 2025, the HiRISE camera aboard the Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter is set to capture images of 3I/ATLAS, which may offer further insights into its structure and composition.

Official statements

“The observed outgassing has a negligible rocket effect, which means 3I/ATLAS must be extraordinarily massive,” said Professor Avi Loeb, co-author of the study.

Context

The findings present a statistical anomaly, as astronomers have previously identified only two other interstellar objects, 1I/Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov, which are significantly smaller. Theoretical models suggest that hundreds of thousands of smaller objects should exist within the interstellar population, making the discovery of 3I/ATLAS particularly puzzling.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS and analyze data from the upcoming HiRISE observations. The scientific community remains focused on understanding the origins and properties of this unusual interstellar object.
