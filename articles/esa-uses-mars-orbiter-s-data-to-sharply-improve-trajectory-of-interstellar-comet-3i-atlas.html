ESA uses Mars orbiter’s data to sharply improve trajectory of Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS

The European Space Agency (ESA) has significantly improved the trajectory predictions for comet 3I/ATLAS, which was discovered on 1 July 2025, by using data from the ExoMars Trace Gas Orbiter (TGO) that orbits Mars. The ESA announced this development following observations made between 1 and 7 October 2025, when the comet was approximately 29 million kilometers from Mars.

This advancement is important because it enhances the precision of tracking fast-moving interstellar objects like 3I/ATLAS. Although the comet does not threaten Earth, the successful integration of space-based and terrestrial observations demonstrates a method that could improve trajectory predictions for future space objects. ESA's Near-Earth Object Coordination Centre has incorporated the new data into its trajectory calculations.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS discovered on 1 July 2025.
  • Improved trajectory predictions by a factor of ten using TGO data.
  • Comet passed approximately 29 million kilometers from Mars on 3 October 2025.
  • Comet travels at speeds up to 250,000 kilometers per hour.
  • First astrometric measurements from a spacecraft orbiting another planet accepted into the Minor Planet Center database.

Timeline

On 1 July 2025, comet 3I/ATLAS was discovered. Until September 2025, predictions for its trajectory relied solely on Earth-based telescopes. Between 1 and 7 October 2025, ESA's ExoMars TGO observed the comet as it approached Mars. The observations allowed for a tenfold increase in trajectory accuracy compared to previous estimates.

The TGO's CaSSIS instrument, typically used for imaging Mars, was repurposed to track the comet against a background of stars. This collaboration involved various teams who integrated the spacecraft data into the trajectory calculations. The results were submitted to the Minor Planet Center, marking a significant milestone in interplanetary observation.

Official statements

“Triangulating data from different locations in space can improve the accuracy of orbit predictions for near-Earth objects,” ESA reported.
“These observations represent the first time astrometric measurements from a spacecraft orbiting another planet have been officially submitted and accepted into the Minor Planet Center database,” according to Space Daily.

Context

This exercise serves as a test case for planetary defence, demonstrating how data from multiple spacecraft can enhance tracking capabilities. Previously, observations of interstellar objects relied heavily on Earth-based telescopes, which limited the accuracy of trajectory predictions.

ESA is also preparing for future missions, including the Neomir mission to monitor regions obscured from Earth-based detection and the Comet Interceptor mission aimed at investigating a comet directly.

What’s next

ESA plans to continue monitoring 3I/ATLAS with the Jupiter Icy Moons Explorer (Juice) spacecraft, which will observe the comet following its closest approach to the Sun, with data expected by February 2026.
