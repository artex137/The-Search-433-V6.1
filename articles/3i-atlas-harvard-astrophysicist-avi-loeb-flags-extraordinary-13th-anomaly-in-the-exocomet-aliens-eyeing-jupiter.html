3I/ATLAS: Harvard Astrophysicist Avi Loeb Flags Extraordinary 13th Anomaly In The Exocomet; Aliens Eyeing Jupiter?

Harvard astrophysicist Avi Loeb has identified an extraordinary thirteenth anomaly related to the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS, which was discovered on July 1, 2025. Loeb’s findings suggest that the exocomet may exhibit non-comet-like behavior that indicates it could be a probe of extraterrestrial intelligence. There are no reported injuries or fatalities associated with this discovery.

This matter has garnered attention from both the scientific community and the public. Experts and agencies, including NASA, have downplayed claims of alien origins despite ongoing speculation. Recent releases of images from NASA have not quelled public curiosity, as some believe they lack clarity and context.

Key facts

  • Discovery Date: July 1, 2025
  • Next Close Approach to Jupiter: March 16, 2026
  • Analyzing Agency: NASA’s Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL)
  • Number of Observatories Contributing Data: Approximately 230

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first discovered on July 1, 2025. Since then, it has exhibited a series of twelve anomalies that have baffled scientists. In a recent blog, Loeb added a thirteenth anomaly, suggesting that the exocomet's trajectory may indicate it is a technological probe. He calculated the minimum distance of closest approach to Jupiter, based on data from NASA's JPL Horizons code.

Loeb noted that the non-gravitational acceleration of 3I/ATLAS during perihelion necessitated a small course correction, aligning its trajectory with Jupiter's gravitational influence, known as the Hill radius.

Official statements

“Let us suppose, hypothetically, that the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS is a mothership that was designed to seed Jupiter with technological devices,” Loeb stated in his blog.
“The distance of closest approach of 3I/ATLAS to Jupiter is calculated by NASA’s JPL Horizons code based on orbital data gathered by about 230 observatories,” he added.

Context

Prior to 3I/ATLAS, Loeb studied other interstellar objects, including a meteor that revealed new materials not found in our solar system. This ongoing research has raised questions about the potential for extraterrestrial life and technology in our cosmic neighborhood.

What’s next

Loeb anticipates that any new objects orbiting Jupiter after the passage of 3I/ATLAS could be identified by the Juno spacecraft or other orbiters. The implications of these findings may lead to further investigations into the nature of 3I/ATLAS and its potential connection to extraterrestrial civilizations.
