Interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS glows green in new image

A new image of the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS shows the object glowing green as it passes through our solar system. The image was captured on November 5 by astronomer Qicheng Zhang at the Lowell Observatory in Arizona.

This observation is significant because it reveals the comet’s reaction to solar ultraviolet radiation, which has triggered molecular changes in its coma. Astronomers aim to understand the comet's composition and origin as it moves away from the Sun.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS was discovered on July 1, 2025.
  • It made its closest approach to the Sun on October 29, 2025.
  • The green glow is due to fluorescent two-carbon molecules formed by ultraviolet light.
  • The image was taken using the Discovery Telescope at Lowell Observatory.
  • 3I/ATLAS is only the third known comet from outside our Solar System.

Timeline

On July 1, 2025, astronomers discovered comet 3I/ATLAS. It approached the Sun, reaching its closest point on October 29, 2025. Observations indicated that the comet exhibited unusual brightness and a blue hue prior to this date.

On November 5, astronomer Qicheng Zhang captured a new image of the comet, revealing its vivid green glow. This phenomenon is attributed to the breakdown of hydrocarbons in the comet's coma due to solar radiation.

Official statements

"It's sort of for the same reason that if we stay out in the sun too long without sunscreen, we get sunburnt," said Qicheng Zhang regarding the green glow of the comet.

Context

3I/ATLAS is notable for being only the third comet observed to originate beyond the Solar System. Previous studies of the comet indicated rapid gas release and behaviours not typically seen in solar system comets.

What’s next

Astronomers will continue to study 3I/ATLAS as it moves away from the Sun to gain further insights into its composition and the nature of its green glow.
