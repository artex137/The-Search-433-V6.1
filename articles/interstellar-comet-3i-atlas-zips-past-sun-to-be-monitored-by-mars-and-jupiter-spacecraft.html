Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS zips past Sun, to be monitored by Mars and Jupiter spacecraft

Interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS has passed close to the Sun, with its closest approach occurring on January 2, 2023. The comet is now being monitored by spacecraft around Mars and Jupiter.

This event is significant for astronomers as it provides a rare opportunity to study an interstellar object. NASA's Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter and the Juno spacecraft currently in orbit around Jupiter will gather data on the comet’s characteristics and trajectory.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS passed closest to the Sun on January 2, 2023.
  • NASA's Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter and Juno spacecraft are monitoring the comet.
  • The comet is classified as an interstellar object.
  • Observations will help scientists understand more about the composition of such comets.

Timeline

Comet 3I/ATLAS was first detected in 2019. It is believed to have originated from outside the Solar System. The comet made its closest approach to the Sun on January 2, 2023, and has since been tracked by various space agencies.

NASA has confirmed that both the Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter and Juno are collecting data as the comet moves through the inner Solar System.

Official statements

“The study of interstellar comets like 3I/ATLAS provides us with valuable insights into the building blocks of planets,” said a NASA spokesperson.

Context

Interstellar comets are rare visitors to the Solar System. The last known interstellar object, 'Oumuamua, was detected in 2017. Scientists are keen to study these objects to learn more about their origins and the conditions in other star systems.

What’s next

NASA will continue to monitor Comet 3I/ATLAS as it travels through the Solar System, with additional observations planned from both the Mars and Jupiter spacecraft.
