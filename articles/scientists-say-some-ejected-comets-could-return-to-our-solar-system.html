Scientists Say Some Ejected Comets Could Return to Our Solar System

Researchers have found that rare “quasi-interstellar objects,” which may be comets ejected from our solar system, could eventually return after drifting through the galaxy. This study was detailed in a preprint available on arXiv and reported by IFLScience.

This discovery is significant as it challenges the perception that once comets are ejected into interstellar space, they are lost forever. According to the study, about 95 percent of the comets and asteroids formed in the solar system have been expelled into space over billions of years, with estimates suggesting around 10,000 trillion objects scattered throughout the Milky Way. Researchers emphasize that while most of these objects may be gone for good, some could be reeled back in by the Sun’s gravity.

Key facts

  • Study focuses on “quasi-interstellar objects” ejected from the solar system.
  • Researchers estimate around 10,000 trillion comets and asteroids have been flung into space.
  • Fewer than one quasi-interstellar object enters the area inside Jupiter’s orbit each year.
  • Quasi-interstellar objects may return at much slower speeds than true interstellar objects.
  • The Oort Cloud, theorized to be a source of these comets, is difficult to observe due to its distance.

Timeline

The study indicates that our solar system has expelled a significant number of comets and asteroids over billions of years. Researchers have been analyzing the potential for these objects to return, observing their speed and trajectory as key factors in distinguishing them from true interstellar objects.

As technology improves, astronomers anticipate that observatories worldwide will identify numerous true interstellar objects in the coming decades. However, locating a returning quasi-interstellar object presents a unique challenge.

Official statements

“The Sun’s gravity never completely lets go. Sometimes, it might reel one of them back in,” researchers stated in the study.

Context

The concept of quasi-interstellar objects adds a new dimension to our understanding of cosmic bodies. While true interstellar objects, like 3I/ATLAS, originate from outside our solar system, quasi-interstellar objects represent a potential return of our own solar system's material after extensive journeys through space.

What’s next

Researchers plan to continue studying the dynamics of these ejected comets and improve observational techniques to identify potential returning quasi-interstellar objects in the future.
