Fact Check: Did comet 3I/Atlas really explode? Here’s what actually happened

In recent days, social media users have claimed that the interstellar comet 3I/Atlas exploded after passing near the Sun. Scientists have confirmed that these claims are false; the comet did not explode but temporarily went behind the Sun, making it difficult to observe.

This matter is significant as 3I/Atlas is only the third interstellar object observed by scientists, generating considerable public interest. Authorities and astronomers are now studying the comet to understand any changes it may have undergone during its perihelion, the point at which it was obscured by the Sun.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/Atlas temporarily went behind the Sun, obscuring it from view.
  • Claims of an explosion have been debunked by astronomers.
  • The comet has reappeared and continues its journey through the Solar System.
  • It is only the third interstellar object ever observed by scientists.
  • Early observations indicate it may be dimmer and missing part of its tail.

Timeline

Over the past few days, social media posts circulated claiming that 3I/Atlas had exploded. These claims arose after the comet's brightness diminished as it passed behind the Sun.

Astronomers confirmed that the comet did not break apart but was simply obscured during its perihelion. Recent observations have shown that 3I/Atlas has reemerged and continues to travel in its usual orbit.

Official statements

“There is no sign of an explosion. The comet is still moving in its usual orbit and has not broken apart,” said astronomers involved in the study of 3I/Atlas.

Context

Comet 3I/Atlas is notable for being one of only three interstellar objects identified by scientists. Its journey has sparked curiosity and speculation, particularly regarding its origins and composition.

Historically, other comets have been known to break apart when approaching the Sun, which contributed to the confusion surrounding 3I/Atlas.

What’s next

Astronomers will continue to study 3I/Atlas to gather more data about its composition and origins. They aim to determine how it may have changed after its close encounter with the Sun.
