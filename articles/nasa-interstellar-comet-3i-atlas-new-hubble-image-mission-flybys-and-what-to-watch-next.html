NASA interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS: new Hubble image, mission flybys, and what to watch next

The interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS has drawn renewed attention this week following the release of a new image captured by the Hubble Space Telescope on November 30, which confirms the comet's activity after its closest approach to the Sun, known as perihelion. This image is significant as it helps scientists understand the behavior and composition of this interstellar visitor.

3I/ATLAS is only the third confirmed interstellar object, providing a unique opportunity to study how "dirty snowballs" from other stars behave and how their chemistry compares to comets formed in our solar system. NASA and other space agencies are actively monitoring the comet's trajectory and characteristics as it travels through the inner solar system.

Key facts

  • Status: Interstellar comet (designation 3I/ATLAS, also known as C/2025 N1)
  • Perihelion: October 29–30, 2025 (about 1.4 AU from the Sun, just inside Mars’ orbit)
  • Distance now: Receding from the Sun; hundreds of millions of kilometers from Earth
  • Closest approach to Earth: Expected around December 19, at a safe distance near 270 million km
  • Threat assessment: None; 3I/ATLAS will remain far from Earth

Timeline

Significant events related to 3I/ATLAS include:

  • May–July 2025: Precovery and discovery images establish the object’s interstellar nature.
  • July 2025: Early Hubble imaging captures a compact coma pre-perihelion.
  • September 2025: NASA's Psyche spacecraft records multiple looks at the comet from deep space.
  • October 29–30, 2025: Perihelion occurs, with activity peaking around solar passage.
  • November 2025: Ground-based campaigns resume as the comet emerges into better morning geometry.
  • November 30, 2025: Hubble reobserves 3I/ATLAS post-perihelion, confirming sustained activity.
  • Mid–Late December 2025: Closest approach to Earth and best amateur observing window.

Official statements

“The new Hubble frame shows a compact nucleus embedded in a soft, asymmetric coma,” according to NASA. “The geometry and brightness confirm the comet’s activity remains robust even after its swing past the Sun.”

Context

3I/ATLAS is part of a growing interest in interstellar objects, following the discoveries of 1I/ʻOumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019. These objects provide insights into the materials and processes that shape planetary systems beyond our own.

What’s next

As 3I/ATLAS continues its journey, scientists will focus on composition mapping, jet physics, tail dynamics, and precision orbit work. These studies will help determine whether 3I/ATLAS is compositionally typical among comets and enhance our understanding of planetary formation across the galaxy.
