Newly Found Comet Sparks Buzz: Could It Be Visible In Daylight?

A newly discovered comet, C2026 A1 MAPS, has excited astronomers after it was spotted by a team of four amateur astronomers using a remotely operated telescope in the Atacama Desert on January 13. The comet is part of the Kreutz sungrazing family and is on a highly elongated orbit, set to pass within 120,000 km of the Sun in early April.

This discovery has generated significant interest due to the potential for the comet to become a spectacular sight, possibly even visible in daylight. Authorities are monitoring the comet's trajectory closely as it approaches the Sun, with hopes that it survives its perihelion passage.

Key facts

  • Comet Name: C2026 A1 MAPS
  • Date Discovered: January 13, 2026
  • Location of Discovery: Atacama Desert
  • Closest Approach to the Sun: Early April 2026
  • Distance at Closest Approach: 120,000 km

Timeline

On January 13, 2026, a team of amateur astronomers discovered Comet C2026 A1 MAPS. It was identified as a member of the Kreutz sungrazing comets shortly after its discovery.

As the comet approaches the Sun, astronomers are eager to observe its behavior, particularly during its perihelion in early April.

Official statements

“If the comet survives its closest approach to the Sun, it could put on a great show in early to mid-April,” said a spokesperson from the observing team.

Context

The Kreutz sungrazing family has a history of producing some of the brightest and most spectacular comets. Notable examples include the Great Comet of 1965, C1965 S1 Ikeya-Seki, which became bright enough to be visible during the day. The discovery of C2026 A1 MAPS marks a continuation of this tradition.

What’s next

As the comet approaches the Sun, astronomers will continue to monitor its brightness and trajectory. Observations from NASA's Solar and Heliospheric Observatory (SOHO) are expected to provide valuable images, particularly if the comet survives its close encounter with the Sun.
