Lanzarote Captures Comet 3I/Atlas: An Interstellar Traveler at 68 Kilometers Per Second

The Z39 Observatory in Lanzarote has captured images and videos of the interstellar comet 3I/Atlas, which is travelling at a speed of 68 kilometers per second. The observations occurred in the final days of this year, showcasing the comet's rapid movement against the night sky.

This discovery is significant as 3I/Atlas is only the third interstellar object confirmed to originate from outside our Solar System. The images taken by the Z39 Observatory provide valuable documentation of the comet's trajectory, contributing to ongoing global scientific studies. The observatory's accreditation by NASA's Minor Planet Center highlights the quality of these observations.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/Atlas moves at 68 km/s, equivalent to over 244,000 km/h.
  • The Z39 Observatory in Lanzarote captured images in late 2023.
  • 3I/Atlas is estimated to have a nucleus between 10 and 30 kilometers in diameter.
  • The comet poses no danger to Earth, maintaining a safe trajectory.
  • The Institute of Astrophysics of the Canary Islands has also monitored 3I/Atlas.

Timeline

In late December 2023, the Z39 Observatory in Lanzarote successfully captured images and videos of comet 3I/Atlas. The observatory's efforts coincide with an ongoing international observation campaign, which includes contributions from the Institute of Astrophysics of the Canary Islands.

The images reveal the comet's swift movement across the sky, emphasizing its status as an interstellar traveler. This sighting marks a notable achievement for local astronomers and the scientific community.

Official statements

“The capture of this interstellar comet from Lanzarote is not only a technical feat for local astronomy but also underscores the privileged position of the Canary Islands for observing the cosmos,” said an official from the Z39 Observatory.

Context

The capture of 3I/Atlas follows previous discoveries of interstellar objects, highlighting advancements in astronomical observation. The Canary Islands have become a focal point for astronomical research due to their clear skies and advanced observatories.

What’s next

Ongoing observations and studies of 3I/Atlas will continue as scientists work to determine its orbit and composition. Further analysis is expected to enhance understanding of interstellar objects and their properties.
