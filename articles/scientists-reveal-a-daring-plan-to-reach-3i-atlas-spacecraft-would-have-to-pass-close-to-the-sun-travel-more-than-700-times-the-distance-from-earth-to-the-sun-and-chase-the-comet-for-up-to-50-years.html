Scientists propose a daring mission to reach interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS

Scientists have proposed a space mission to intercept the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, which would require a spacecraft to travel more than 700 astronomical units and utilize gravitational maneuvers. The mission aims to explore 3I/ATLAS directly, as it moves away from the Sun at speeds exceeding 61 kilometers per second. The plan has garnered attention due to its complexity and the extreme conditions the spacecraft would face.

The proposed mission is significant because it represents one of the longest and most challenging journeys in space exploration history. The spacecraft would rely on a series of gravitational assists and a close approach to the Sun to achieve the necessary speed to pursue 3I/ATLAS. The mission, if realized, could not only advance our understanding of interstellar objects but also test new technologies in extreme environments.

Key facts

  • Distance to 3I/ATLAS: over 700 astronomical units
  • Speed of 3I/ATLAS: more than 61 kilometers per second
  • Mission duration: approximately 30 to 50 years
  • Closest approach to the Sun: 0.015 astronomical units
  • Temperature near the Sun: over 1,370 °C (about 2,500 °F)
  • Potential encounter with 3I/ATLAS: around the year 2085

Timeline

The mission concept begins with the launch of the spacecraft from Earth, followed by a journey to Jupiter. The spacecraft will use Jupiter’s gravity to adjust its speed and trajectory for a close solar approach. This maneuver is crucial to ensure the spacecraft can effectively utilize the Oberth effect, which enhances speed during a close flyby of a massive body.

Once the spacecraft approaches the Sun, it will execute a burn at the point of closest approach, known as periastron. This maneuver aims to dramatically increase its speed to pursue 3I/ATLAS. Following this, the spacecraft will embark on a decades-long journey through deep space to reach the comet.

Official statements

“The plan involves a risky but technically feasible approach to accelerate the spacecraft using gravitational assists and extreme maneuvers,” said T. Marshall Eubanks, a former NASA scientist and one of the study's authors.
“We have not seen an application of the Oberth effect as extreme as the one proposed in this mission,” Eubanks added.

Context

This mission is notable for its ambitious scope, as it seeks to explore an interstellar object for the first time. Previous missions have focused on objects within our solar system. The utilization of the Oberth effect and gravitational assists is common in space travel, but the scale of this proposed mission sets it apart.

Moreover, the mission underscores the ongoing interest in understanding interstellar objects, which can provide insights into the formation and evolution of the solar system.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to refine the mission plan and conduct further studies to address the technical challenges associated with surviving the extreme conditions near the Sun. The timeline for the mission remains tentative, with significant planning and development required over the coming decades.
