New Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS Captured By Very Large Telescope

The European Southern Observatory's Very Large Telescope (VLT) has captured images of the newly discovered interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS. The comet was identified by the Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System (ATLAS) survey telescope, and the capture occurred recently, exact timing not disclosed.

This discovery is significant as it marks another addition to the list of known interstellar objects, enhancing our understanding of celestial bodies entering the solar system. Authorities from the European Southern Observatory have stated that the observations will contribute to ongoing research in astronomy and astrophysics.

Key facts

  • Comet name: 3I/ATLAS
  • Discovered by: Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System (ATLAS)
  • Captured by: Very Large Telescope (VLT)
  • Location of capture: European Southern Observatory
  • Discovery date: not disclosed
  • Organizations involved: ESO, ATLAS, University of Ohio, NASA

Timeline

The Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System (ATLAS) discovered comet 3I/ATLAS. Following its discovery, the European Southern Observatory's Very Large Telescope captured images of the comet. The exact dates of these events have not been disclosed.

Official statements

“The observations of comet 3I/ATLAS will contribute to our understanding of celestial bodies entering our solar system,” a representative from the European Southern Observatory stated.

Context

Comet 3I/ATLAS is part of a growing list of interstellar objects that have been detected in recent years. These discoveries are crucial for understanding the origin and composition of such celestial bodies, as well as their potential impact on our solar system.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to study comet 3I/ATLAS to gather more data on its characteristics and trajectory, contributing to ongoing investigations in the field of astronomy.
