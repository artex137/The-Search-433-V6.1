A Q & A on 3I/ATLAS at Perihelion

Comet 3I/ATLAS made its closest approach to the Sun today, October 29, 2025, at 7:47 AM Eastern Time, according to the JPL Horizons trajectory data. No injuries or fatalities have been reported in connection with the event.

This perihelion is significant as it raises questions about the nature of 3I/ATLAS, including whether it is a natural comet or something else entirely. Authorities are currently monitoring the comet for any unusual activity, as its unique characteristics have sparked interest in potential technological origins.

Key facts

  • Perihelion time: October 29, 2025, at 7:47 AM Eastern Time
  • Distance from Earth at closest approach: 269 million kilometers on December 19, 2025
  • Diameter: Minimum of 5 kilometers
  • Unique characteristics: 8 anomalies ranked 4 on the Loeb scale
  • Monitoring by radio observers has been initiated
  • Congresswoman Anna Paulina Luna is facilitating the release of HiRISE images

Timeline

3I/ATLAS has been observed since its discovery, with significant observations occurring in July and August 2025, when it displayed a sunward jet. The perihelion event today marked the closest approach to the Sun, while further observations are planned as it continues its trajectory through the Solar System.

As of yesterday, radio observers have been asked to monitor 3I/ATLAS for any signs of technological activity. This follows the discovery of its unique properties that warrant further investigation.

Official statements

“If 3I/ATLAS maneuvers, releases mini-probes, or transmits radio signals, then we will know that it is technological in origin,” said Avi Loeb, head of the Galileo Project.
“The implication of alien technology would be huge and therefore we must take this possibility seriously,” Loeb added.

Context

Previous interstellar objects, such as 1I/`Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov, have prompted discussions about the nature of such visitors. 3I/ATLAS's anomalies place it in a unique category, raising the possibility of an artificial origin.

The ongoing analysis of these anomalies is crucial, as they could provide insights into the origins of interstellar objects and the potential for extraterrestrial technology.

What’s next

Scientists will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS for any signs of activity. Future observations may reveal more about its characteristics and origins, and the release of HiRISE images is expected to assist in this analysis.
