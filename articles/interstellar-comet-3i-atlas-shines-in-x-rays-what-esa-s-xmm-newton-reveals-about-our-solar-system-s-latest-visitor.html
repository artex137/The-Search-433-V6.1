Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS Shines in X-rays: What ESA’s XMM-Newton Reveals About Our Solar System’s Latest Visitor

Published 2025-12-13 15:27 UTC

Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS Shines in X-rays

In December 2025, the European Space Agency’s (ESA) XMM-Newton space observatory captured X-ray images of interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS from a distance of approximately 285 million kilometers. The 20-hour observation took place on December 3, revealing significant details about this rare cosmic visitor, which is only the third confirmed interstellar object in our solar system.

This discovery matters because it enhances our understanding of the composition of interstellar comets. The X-ray data collected by XMM-Newton provides insights into gases like hydrogen and nitrogen, which are difficult to detect using traditional optical telescopes. Researchers are collaborating with other observatories to analyze the comet further as it approaches Earth on December 19, 2025, at a distance of about 268 million kilometers.

Key facts

  • Comet Name: 3I/ATLAS
  • Date of X-ray Observation: December 3, 2025
  • Distance from Earth during Observation: 285 million kilometers
  • Closest Approach to Earth: December 19, 2025
  • Distance at Closest Approach: 1.79 astronomical units (268 million kilometers)
  • Previous Interstellar Objects: 1I/’Oumuamua (2017), 2I/Borisov (2019)

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first detected by the ATLAS observatory on July 1, 2025. Following its discovery, ESA’s XMM-Newton began tracking the comet. The significant X-ray observations were made on December 3, 2025, as the spacecraft utilized its European Photon Imaging Camera (EPIC)-pn to capture the comet’s interaction with solar wind.

The comet’s closest approach to Earth is scheduled for December 19, 2025, which will provide additional opportunities for observation from various global telescopes.

Official statements

“By capturing the X-ray signature of 3I/ATLAS, we provide a crucial missing piece to the puzzle,” said an ESA spokesperson, emphasizing the importance of this observation in understanding interstellar comets.

Context

The study of interstellar comets has gained traction since the arrival of 1I/’Oumuamua in 2017, which sparked debates about its unusual properties. While 2I/Borisov resembled more traditional comets, the differences raised questions about the variety of materials present in interstellar objects. 3I/ATLAS offers a new opportunity to examine these differences more closely.

What’s next

As 3I/ATLAS approaches Earth, observatories around the world are preparing to gather data across various wavelengths. Researchers will analyze the X-ray data alongside observations from the James Webb Space Telescope and NASA’s SPHEREx mission, aiming to further unravel the comet’s origins and composition.
