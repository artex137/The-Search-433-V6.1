Scientists baffled as interstellar visitor appears to reverse thrust before vanishing behind the sun

Astronomers have reported that the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS has performed a dramatic tail reversal, now pointing away from the sun, as it moves behind the sun, rendering ground-based observations impossible. The changes have been observed since July 2025, with new data confirming this shift in the comet's behavior.

This phenomenon is significant as it challenges previous understandings of comet behavior and raises questions about the nature of 3I/ATLAS. Researchers from the University of California and the University of Oslo are analyzing this unusual activity to better understand the comet's composition and trajectory.

Key facts

  • 3I/ATLAS has reversed its tail direction, now pointing away from the sun.
  • New observations confirm a transition from an 'anti-tail' to a classic tail.
  • The comet is shedding material at a rate of approximately 150 kilograms per second.
  • The mass lost from 3I/ATLAS amounts to about two million tons since July 2025.
  • NASA has detected the comet losing about 330 pounds (150 kilograms) of material per second, primarily carbon dioxide and carbon monoxide.
  • 3I/ATLAS measures approximately 5 kilometers (3.1 miles) in diameter.

Timeline

In July 2025, astronomers observed an 'anti-tail' from 3I/ATLAS, with dust particles scattering light towards the sun. By September 2025, this anti-tail transformed into a classic tail pointing away from the sun as the comet approached perihelion.

As of October 2025, 3I/ATLAS has moved behind the sun, making it invisible to ground-based telescopes.

Official statements

Avi Loeb, a Harvard professor, stated, "The total amount of mass lost from 3I/ATLAS during July through October 2025 amounts to about two million tons." He added, "Needless to say, we cannot infer the true nature of 3I/ATLAS from the skin layer that it has shed so far."

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third known interstellar object, following 1I/`Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov. Unlike 1I/`Oumuamua, which showed no signs of gas or dust, 3I/ATLAS exhibits unique features, including an anti-tail and significant color changes. Its behavior has drawn interest from scientists seeking to understand more about interstellar objects.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS as it becomes visible again after passing behind the sun. Further studies will aim to clarify the comet's composition and the implications of its unusual tail behavior.
