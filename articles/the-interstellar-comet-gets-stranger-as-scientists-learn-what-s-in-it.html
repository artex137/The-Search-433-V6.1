The Interstellar Comet Gets Stranger As Scientists Learn What's In It

Astronomers have studied the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS and detected unusual gases streaming off its icy surface. The findings were made using the Atacama Large Millimeter/submillimeter Array in Chile, according to the researchers. No injuries or fatalities have been reported.

This discovery is significant as it provides insights into the chemical composition of distant worlds. The research, led by Nathan Roth from American University, highlights how the unique chemistry of 3I/ATLAS may differ from comets formed in our solar system.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS contains high levels of methanol and hydrogen cyanide.
  • The study was published in The Astrophysical Journal Letters on March 6.
  • 3I/ATLAS is traveling at approximately 137,000 mph.
  • It passed just inside the orbit of Mars before returning to interstellar space.
  • Only two other interstellar objects have been observed: 'Oumuamua in 2017 and Comet 2I/Borisov in 2019.

Timeline

Comet 3I/ATLAS was ejected from another part of the galaxy, likely due to a gravitational jolt from a planet or star. After drifting through interstellar space for hundreds of millions of years, it entered our solar system. Astronomers tracked it for several months in 2025 as it approached the sun.

During this time, researchers utilized the Atacama Large Millimeter/submillimeter Array to analyze the gases emitted from the comet’s surface.

Official statements

“Observing 3I/ATLAS is like taking a fingerprint from another solar system,” said Nathan Roth. “The details reveal what it's made of, and it's bursting with methanol in a way we just don't usually see in comets in our own solar system.”

Context

The findings regarding 3I/ATLAS support the idea that different planetary systems can produce comets with varying chemical compositions. Earlier observations from NASA's James Webb Space Telescope indicated that 3I/ATLAS has an unusually high amount of carbon dioxide compared to water, adding to its unique characteristics.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to study the comet's composition and its implications for understanding the formation of other planetary systems. Further observations may reveal more about the conditions in which 3I/ATLAS formed.
