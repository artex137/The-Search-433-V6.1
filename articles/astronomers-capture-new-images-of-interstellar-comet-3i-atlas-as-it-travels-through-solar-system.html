Astronomers Capture New Images of Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS as It Travels Through Solar System

The interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, recently discovered, is currently traveling through our solar system, drawing considerable attention from astronomers. First identified on July 1 by the NASA-funded Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System (ATLAS) in Chile, this comet is only the third interstellar object documented in our solar system, following Oumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019.

This discovery is significant as researchers utilize advanced telescopes to track the comet's journey. The National Science Foundation's NOIRLab, which operates the Gemini South telescope, has reported increased activity in the comet's tail, providing new insights into its composition and behavior as it approaches the inner Solar System.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS was first identified on July 1, 2023.
  • It is the third interstellar object documented in our solar system.
  • New images were captured by the Gemini South telescope on August 27, 2023.
  • The tail of the comet is significantly larger than in earlier images.
  • NASA's James Webb Space Telescope detected high levels of carbon dioxide in the comet's coma.
  • The comet will dip behind the Sun later in September 2023 and is expected to re-emerge in November 2023.

Timeline

On July 1, 2023, the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS was first identified in Chile. Subsequent observations were made using various advanced telescopes. On August 27, the Gemini’s Multi-Object Spectrograph conducted multicolor imaging, revealing a prominent tail and insights into the comet's color and composition.

Earlier imagery from Gemini North in July displayed the comet’s compact coma. Recent data indicate that as the comet approaches the Sun, it is undergoing changes, particularly in the growth of its tail.

Official statements

“The primary goal of the recent observations was to analyze the comet's colors, which offer essential clues regarding the composition and size of the dust particles within the coma,” said Karen Meech from the University of Hawai‘i Institute for Astronomy.

Context

The discovery of 3I/ATLAS adds to the growing catalog of interstellar objects observed in our solar system. The previous interstellar comets, Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov, have provided valuable insights into the characteristics of such bodies. Ongoing research aims to understand the origins and compositions of these mysterious travelers.

What’s next

As 3I/ATLAS approaches the Sun, it will dip behind it later in September 2023. The comet is expected to re-emerge in November 2023, at which point further observations using the Gemini telescope are anticipated.
