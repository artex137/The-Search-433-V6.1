Spectroscopic mapping of 3I/ATLAS identifies unique chemical fingerprints from another solar system

Astronomers have discovered that comet 3I/ATLAS contains a significant amount of the organic molecule methanol, according to a study conducted using the Atacama Large Millimeter/submillimeter Array (ALMA). The findings were published on March 10, 2026.

This research is important as it sheds light on the composition of comets from other solar systems, enhancing our understanding of their origins and the materials they contain. Nathan Roth, the lead author and a professor at American University, emphasized the uniqueness of this discovery, stating that 3I/ATLAS provides a glimpse into the chemical makeup of distant celestial bodies.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS is rich in methanol (CH3OH).
  • Research conducted using the Atacama Large Millimeter/submillimeter Array (ALMA).
  • Study published on March 10, 2026.
  • Lead author is Nathan Roth, a professor at American University.

Timeline

The study on comet 3I/ATLAS has progressed as follows:

  • Initial observations of 3I/ATLAS were conducted with ALMA.
  • Molecular analysis revealed the presence of methanol and hydrogen cyanide (HCN).
  • Findings were compiled and published on March 10, 2026.

Official statements

“Observing 3I/ATLAS is like taking a fingerprint from another solar system,” said Nathan Roth. “The details reveal what it’s made of, and it’s bursting with methanol in a way we just don’t usually see in comets in our own solar system.”

Context

Prior studies of comets from our solar system have not shown such high concentrations of methanol. This discovery may challenge existing theories about the chemical processes in comets and their formation.

What’s next

Further investigations are expected to focus on the implications of these findings for our understanding of cometary chemistry and the potential for life-supporting materials in other solar systems.
