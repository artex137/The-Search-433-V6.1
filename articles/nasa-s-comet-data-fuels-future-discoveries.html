NASA's Comet Data Fuels Future Discoveries

The interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, discovered on July 1, 2025, by NASA's ATLAS survey telescope in Rio Hurtado, Chile, is set to leave the solar system, but its observations will remain in NASA's public data archives. This comet is only the third known object from interstellar space to visit our solar system.

This discovery is significant as it enhances understanding of interstellar comets and the differences in their chemical makeup compared to those formed within the solar system. NASA has utilized data from multiple missions to analyze 3I/ATLAS, which has revealed unique characteristics of the comet.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS discovered on July 1, 2025.
  • First observed by NASA's TESS satellite in May 2025.
  • Data collected from over a dozen NASA missions, including MAVEN, James Webb Space Telescope, and SPHEREx.
  • 3I/ATLAS is the third identified interstellar object visiting the solar system.
  • NASA's observations have revealed unusual production rates of water, carbon dioxide, and carbon monoxide.

Timeline

On May 2025, NASA's TESS satellite captured images of 3I/ATLAS, marking its first appearance on camera. This data was later used to track the comet's trajectory. On July 1, 2025, the comet was officially discovered by the ATLAS telescope in Chile.

NASA has since conducted observations with various spacecraft to gather data on 3I/ATLAS. The agency's commitment to open science has facilitated the sharing and analysis of this data across different missions.

Official statements

"NASA's scientific data archives are a gold mine of discoveries waiting to be made," said Kevin Murphy, chief science data officer at NASA Headquarters.
"Open science, as a set of principles, has been pushing us as research communities and NASA to make data more accessible," said Thomas Statler, lead scientist for Solar System Small Bodies at NASA Headquarters.

Context

3I/ATLAS is significant as it is one of the few comets observed by multiple spacecraft. Previous interstellar objects, such as 'Oumuamua, have been detected since 2017, but the frequency of these occurrences is expected to increase with advancements in telescope technology.

The data from 3I/ATLAS will serve as a reference point for future studies of interstellar comets, contributing to a deeper understanding of their characteristics and origins.

What’s next

Scientists will continue to analyze the data collected from 3I/ATLAS to further understand its structure and composition. The ongoing efforts in open science will ensure that this data remains accessible for future research.
