Nasa Confirms Interstellar Comet 3I Atlas Poses No Threat to Earth Amid Viral Rumors

NASA and the European Space Agency (ESA) have confirmed that interstellar comet 3I Atlas poses no threat to Earth, despite widespread social media panic. The comet, detected by NASA's Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System (ATLAS) telescope on July 1, 2025, is traveling through space at over 210,000 kilometers per hour (130,500 miles per hour).

This clarification is significant as numerous viral posts and conspiracy theories have circulated online, falsely claiming the comet could impact Earth or is an alien spacecraft. Authorities are actively working to dispel misinformation and reassure the public about the comet's actual trajectory.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I Atlas detected on July 1, 2025.
  • Closest approach to Earth occurred on July 21, 2025, at a distance of 270 million kilometers (168 million miles).
  • Speed of the comet is over 210,000 kilometers per hour (130,500 miles per hour).
  • Closest point to the Sun will occur on October 30, 2025, at approximately 210 million kilometers (130 million miles) from the Sun.
  • The comet is an interstellar object, the third of its kind ever recorded.

Timeline

NASA first detected interstellar comet 3I Atlas on July 1, 2025. The comet made its closest approach to Earth on July 21, 2025, passing at a safe distance of 270 million kilometers. Later this month, on October 30, 2025, it will reach its closest point to the Sun, remaining well beyond Mars’s orbit.

Official statements

“Comet 3I/ATLAS poses no risk to Earth. It is an interstellar object passing safely through our solar system and provides a rare opportunity for scientific observation,” NASA stated.
The ESA confirmed, “Atlas’s trajectory keeps it more than 2.5 times the distance between Earth and the Sun, making any chance of impact impossible.”

Context

Social media platforms have seen an influx of misinformation regarding 3I Atlas, with posts suggesting it is an alien spacecraft or a planet-threatening object. Fact-checkers from Al Jazeera SANAD and Reuters have debunked these claims, emphasizing that there is no credible evidence supporting such theories.

What’s next

NASA and ESA will continue to monitor the comet's trajectory and study its properties. They urge the public to rely on verified sources for updates and to be cautious of misinformation circulating on social media platforms.
