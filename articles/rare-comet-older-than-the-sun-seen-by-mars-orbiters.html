Rare Comet Older Than the Sun Seen by Mars Orbiters

ESA’s Mars orbiters have captured images of interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS as it passed close to Mars between October 1 and October 7, 2025. The comet, believed to be older than the Sun, displayed a bright, hazy coma during its closest approach on October 3, approximately 48 kilometres from the planet's surface. No injuries or fatalities have been reported.

The observations from ESA’s ExoMars Trace Gas Orbiter (TGO) and Mars Express spacecraft are significant as they provide insights into the composition and characteristics of a rare interstellar object. Researchers are now analyzing the data to better understand the comet’s origins and its implications for solar system formation.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS passed Mars between October 1 and October 7, 2025.
  • Closest approach occurred on October 3, at a distance of approximately 48 km.
  • ESA’s ExoMars TGO and Mars Express conducted observations.
  • The comet is believed to predate the formation of the Sun.
  • Images reveal a bright, hazy coma surrounding the comet.

Timeline

Between October 1 and October 7, 2025, ESA’s Mars orbiters were positioned to observe interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS. The closest approach occurred on October 3, allowing the spacecraft to capture detailed images of the comet’s coma.

These observations occurred as part of a planned investigation to study the comet's features and gather data on its composition.

Official statements

“This close pass of 3I/ATLAS provides a unique opportunity to study an object that has traveled from beyond our solar system,” said a spokesperson for the European Space Agency (ESA).

Context

Interstellar comets are rare occurrences, and 3I/ATLAS is one of the few observed since the discovery of 1I/2017 U1, the first confirmed interstellar object detected in our solar system. The study of such comets can enhance our understanding of the formation and evolution of planetary systems.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to analyze the data collected from the ESA orbiters to gain further insights into the characteristics of 3I/ATLAS. The findings may contribute to broader studies on the origins of comets and their role in the solar system's development.
