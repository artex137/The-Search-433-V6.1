NASA alert! Mysterious 3I/ATLAS interstellar comet set for close approach to Earth on December 19

The interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS will make its closest approach to Earth on December 19, coming within about 170 million miles (270 million kilometers) of the planet. There have been no injuries or fatalities reported related to the comet’s approach.

This event is significant for astronomers and space agencies. Telescopes around the world are currently monitoring the comet to refine predictions about its path and similar objects in the future. The United Nations’ International Asteroid Warning Network (IAWN) has completed nearly half of its observation campaign on 3I/ATLAS and plans to publish its findings in a peer-reviewed scientific journal next year.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS will approach Earth on December 19, 2023.
  • Distance at closest approach: approximately 170 million miles (270 million kilometers).
  • The United Nations' IAWN has nearly completed half of its observation campaign on the comet.
  • More than 80 observatories and citizen scientists are involved in tracking near-Earth objects.
  • IAWN is coordinated by NASA.

Timeline

The comet 3I/ATLAS has been observed by the Gemini North telescope, which revealed its bright green color as it travels through the solar system. Since its discovery, the IAWN has initiated an observation campaign to study its trajectory and characteristics. The campaign aims to enhance techniques for measuring positions of comets and asteroids, a process called astrometry.

James Bauer, a research professor at the University of Maryland, noted that 3I/ATLAS is the first interstellar object tracked by IAWN since the campaign began in 2017.

Official statements

“The main goal of these campaigns is to improve techniques for measuring the positions of asteroids and comets,” said James Bauer, principal investigator at IAWN’s Small Bodies Node.
“We will test a new tracking method on 3I/ATLAS, which could help plan future spacecraft missions to similar comets,” Bauer added.

Context

IAWN was established to monitor and study near-Earth objects, including comets and asteroids, with a focus on improving detection and tracking methods. Since its inception in 2017, the network has conducted various observation campaigns to enhance scientific understanding and preparedness regarding potential threats to Earth.

What’s next

IAWN will continue its observation campaign until its completion and plans to publish findings in a peer-reviewed journal next year. Scientists will also implement new tracking methods on 3I/ATLAS to aid in future missions targeting similar celestial bodies.
