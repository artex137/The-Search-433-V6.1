Comet 3I/ATLAS: NASA’s Latest Tracking and Origin Explained

NASA has tracked Comet 3I/ATLAS, a rare interstellar visitor discovered on July 1, 2025, by the Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System (ATLAS) in Chile. The comet travels at approximately 130,000 mph and will reach its closest point to the Sun inside the orbit of Mars on October 30, 2025, while remaining at a safe distance from Earth.

This discovery is significant as Comet 3I/ATLAS is only the third confirmed interstellar object observed, following ‘Oumuamua and Borisov. NASA is using advanced telescopes to monitor the comet's trajectory and chemical makeup, providing valuable insights into planetary system formation beyond our solar system.

Key facts

  • Discovery Date: July 1, 2025
  • Speed: 130,000 mph
  • Closest Approach to the Sun: October 30, 2025
  • Size Estimates: 0.2 to 3.5 miles for the nucleus
  • Observatories Involved: Hubble Space Telescope, James Webb Space Telescope, Neil Gehrels Swift Observatory
  • Interstellar Objects Detected: Three (including 3I/ATLAS)

Timeline

Comet 3I/ATLAS was first detected on July 1, 2025, by NASA's ATLAS survey telescope in Chile. Since its discovery, NASA has utilized the Hubble Space Telescope to capture detailed images of the comet, revealing its dusty tail, nucleus, and gaseous emissions. Observations from the James Webb Space Telescope and Neil Gehrels Swift Observatory have provided complementary data, aiding in the prediction of its path and the study of its unusual chemical composition.

The comet is currently observable from the Southern Hemisphere, with plans for it to move northwards in October 2025 as it approaches its perihelion.

Official statements

“Tracking Comet 3I/ATLAS is like spotting a rifle bullet for a fraction of a second,” said astronomer David Jewitt, highlighting the comet's fleeting nature.

Context

Comet 3I/ATLAS is notable as the third confirmed interstellar object detected, following the earlier discoveries of ‘Oumuamua and Borisov. Its hyperbolic trajectory indicates it is not bound to the Sun and has traveled from outside our solar system, likely ejected from another stellar system billions of years ago.

What’s next

NASA and astronomy centers will continue to provide updates and viewing tips as Comet 3I/ATLAS approaches its closest point to the Sun. Researchers will study the comet's composition and behavior to gain insights into distant planetary systems.
