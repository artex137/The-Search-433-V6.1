Interstellar Comet Closest Approach: Date and Viewing Guide

Published 2025-12-10 19:28 UTC

Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS Approaches Earth on December 19

The interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS (C/2025 N1) is set to make its closest approach to Earth on Friday, December 19, 2025. The comet will pass at a safe distance of approximately 270 million kilometers (167 million miles), posing no threat to the planet.

This event marks a significant moment in modern astronomy, as it represents the third confirmed observation of an interstellar object visiting our Solar System. Discovered on July 1, 2025, by the NASA-funded Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System (ATLAS) telescope in Río Hurtado, Chile, 3I/ATLAS has sparked scientific curiosity regarding its origins and chemical composition.

Key facts

  • Closest approach: December 19, 2025
  • Distance from Earth: Approximately 270 million kilometers (167 million miles)
  • Discovery: July 1, 2025
  • Initial observations: Hyperbolic trajectory, indicating a one-time visit
  • Perihelion: October 29, 2025, passed within 130 million miles of the Sun
  • Magnitude: Currently around 10 to 11.5, requiring a telescope with at least a 30 cm (12 inch) aperture to view
  • Location: Traversing the constellation Leo, near the star Regulus

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was discovered on July 1, 2025, by the ATLAS telescope. It entered the Solar System from the direction of the constellation Sagittarius and approached the Sun, reaching perihelion on October 29, where it survived intense solar heat without disintegrating. The comet is now on a hyperbolic trajectory, headed back into interstellar space.

Official statements

“We are looking at a time capsule from another star system. It’s telling us that the chemical recipe of the Milky Way is far more varied than we ever imagined,” said Dr. Elena Puzia, a lead researcher monitoring the comet’s trajectory.
“The high methanol count suggests this object formed in a protoplanetary disk rich in oxygen and carbon, likely in the frigid outer reaches of its home system,” explained Martin Cordiner of NASA’s Goddard Space Flight Center.

Context

3I/ATLAS joins an exclusive group of interstellar objects, following 1I/‘Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov. While ‘Oumuamua generated significant debate regarding its nature, Borisov confirmed the existence of interstellar comets with more traditional characteristics. 3I/ATLAS presents unique chemical properties that may challenge existing theories about the formation of comets and the origins of life.

What’s next

As 3I/ATLAS approaches its closest point to Earth, astronomers will conduct detailed spectroscopic analyses to study its chemical composition. The observations may provide insights into the conditions of its home system and the potential for life elsewhere in the universe.
