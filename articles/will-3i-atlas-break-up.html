Will 3I/ATLAS Break Up?

On October 29, 2025, the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS will reach perihelion at a distance of 203 million kilometers from the Sun. No small objects have been clearly associated with 3I/ATLAS in astronomical data sets, according to the European Space Agency (ESA).

This event is significant as it may provide insights into the nature of 3I/ATLAS, particularly its potential to disintegrate into fragments due to solar heating. Researchers are monitoring the object closely for any new activity or associated debris.

Key facts

  • Date of perihelion: October 29, 2025
  • Distance from the Sun: 203 million kilometers
  • Current observation status: No verified fragments detected
  • Monitoring agencies: European Space Agency (ESA), Galileo Project observatories

Timeline

3I/ATLAS is on a trajectory that brings it near the Sun on October 29, 2025. Prior to this, ESA has released images showing faint features around the object, but these are likely noise artifacts, not related to 3I/ATLAS.

As the object approaches perihelion, astronomers are particularly interested in monitoring for any signs of fragmentation or new activity that could indicate the presence of smaller pieces.

Official statements

“We should keep our eyes on this fuzzy ball of light and check whether it breaks up into independent smaller dots of light,” said Avi Loeb, an astrophysicist involved in the monitoring efforts.

Context

Comets can disintegrate primarily due to heating from the Sun, as well as gravitational tides and rotational stress from outgassing. Previous studies have shown that comets like 67P/Churyumov-Gerasimenko may have formed from smaller pieces, making them susceptible to breakups.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to observe 3I/ATLAS as it approaches the Sun, looking for any signs of fragmentation or related objects. Ongoing monitoring will involve data collection from both terrestrial telescopes and Mars orbiters.
