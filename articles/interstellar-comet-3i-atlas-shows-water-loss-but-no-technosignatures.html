Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS Shows Water Loss But No Technosignatures

Scientists studying interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS have reported significant water loss following its perihelion in late October 2025. Observations from the Solar and Heliospheric Observatory (SOHO) and a radio search conducted on December 18, 2025, revealed no technosignatures associated with the comet.

This research is significant as it provides insights into the behavior of interstellar objects as they travel through the solar system. Authorities from the Breakthrough Listen initiative and other scientific teams continue to analyze data from these observations to better understand the comet's characteristics.

Key facts

  • Perihelion date: Late October 2025
  • First SWAN hydrogen coma detection: November 6, 2025
  • Water production rate at 1.40 astronomical units: 3.17 x 1029 molecules per second
  • Water production rate by December 8, 2025: 1 x 1028 to 2 x 1028 molecules per second
  • Closest distance to Earth: About 270 million km (1.8 AU)
  • Speed relative to Sun: Up to 210,000 km/hour
  • Estimated nucleus size range: Roughly 440 m to 5.6 km

Timeline

Interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS passed its closest approach to the Sun in late October 2025. Following this, the SOHO's SWAN instrument first detected the comet's hydrogen coma on November 6, 2025, indicating significant water production. Subsequent observations on December 18, 2025, by the Green Bank Telescope revealed no signs of technosignatures, confirming that the comet behaved like a typical solar system comet as it moved away from the Sun.

Official statements

“We report a nondetection of candidate signals down to the 100 mW level,” said the Breakthrough Listen team led by Ben Jacobson-Bell at the University of California, Berkeley.
“Gas dynamics could still explain the structure, so the suggestion did not rule out normal cometary physics,” noted Avi Loeb, a Harvard astrophysicist.

Context

3I/ATLAS is only the third confirmed interstellar object observed entering the solar system. Previous interstellar objects have generated significant interest in their potential to display unique features compared to typical solar system comets. The current research adds to the understanding of how such objects behave as they interact with the Sun's gravitational pull and radiation.

What’s next

Research teams will continue to analyze data from the comet's observations, particularly looking into its water loss and structural characteristics. Further studies may provide more insights into the nature of interstellar bodies and their behavior in the solar system.
