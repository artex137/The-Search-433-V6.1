We could use a 'solar slingshot' to catch 3I/ATLAS, scientists say

Scientists have proposed using a solar slingshot to observe comet 3I/ATLAS, which was first spotted in July 2022 and is currently 167 million miles from Earth. The suggestion comes as a team from the Initiative for Interstellar Studies explores ways to send a spacecraft to this interstellar visitor.

This proposal is significant because 3I/ATLAS is only the third interstellar object discovered passing through our solar system. It could provide valuable insights into the formation of comets and planetary systems. The mission would involve a risky maneuver, using the sun's gravity to accelerate a spacecraft toward the comet, which is expected to reach Jupiter in about 20 days.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS was first detected in July 2022.
  • Current distance from Earth: 267 million kilometres.
  • Proposed launch date for the interceptor: 2035.
  • Estimated arrival at 3I/ATLAS: 2085, when it will be 68 million miles away.
  • Proposed speed boost: change in velocity of at least 5.1 miles per second.
  • Proposed spacecraft weight: 500 kg.

Timeline

Comet 3I/ATLAS was first spotted in July 2022. It has since generated debate among scientists regarding its classification as a comet or a UFO. The Initiative for Interstellar Studies has now proposed a solar slingshot maneuver to accelerate a spacecraft towards the comet. The spacecraft could harness the sun's gravity, launching in 2035 and arriving in 2085.

Official statements

“As a spacecraft is falling into the gravitational potential well, it fires its rockets, coming out of it with a greater kinetic energy,” said Dr Alfredo Carpineti, an astrophysicist not involved in the paper.
“The work doesn’t look at the feasibility of the mission but just the manoeuvre. Indeed, it’s possible to use this approach to catch up with the rocket,” Dr Carpineti added.

Context

3I/ATLAS, formerly known as A11pI3Z, is the third interstellar visitor discovered in our solar system. The first was Oumuamua in 2017, followed by Borisov in 2019. Both objects have contributed to the understanding of interstellar bodies and their origins.

What’s next

The next steps involve further analysis of the proposed solar slingshot maneuver and its feasibility for a mission to 3I/ATLAS. Researchers will continue to study the comet and prepare for the proposed launch in 2035.
