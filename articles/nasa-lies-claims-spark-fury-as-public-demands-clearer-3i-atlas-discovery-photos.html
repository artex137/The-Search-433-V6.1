NASA 'Lies' Claims Spark Fury as Public Demands Clearer 3I/ATLAS Discovery Photos

Published 2025-12-27 16:35 UTC

NASA 'Lies' Claims Spark Fury as Public Demands Clearer 3I/ATLAS Discovery Photos

A controversy has erupted over the comet 3I/ATLAS, discovered on July 1, 2025, by the ATLAS survey in Chile. The public has expressed outrage over NASA's release of only heavily processed images, leading to accusations of a cover-up regarding the comet's unique characteristics. No injuries or fatalities have been reported.

The demand for high-resolution images has intensified as scientists and the public seek to understand the comet's unusual dust emissions. NASA's planetary defense desks have faced criticism for not providing raw data from significant observations made by the James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) and the Parker Solar Probe. Critics argue that this lack of transparency hinders independent verification of the comet's behavior.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS discovered on July 1, 2025, in Chile.
  • NASA released processed imagery from December 2025.
  • Comet moving outbound post-perihelion at over 60 km/s.
  • Dust particles ejected at a rate of approximately 3.3 kilograms per second.
  • Comet's perihelion occurred on October 29-30, 2025.
  • Independent observations reported pulsing jets on December 19, 2025.

Timeline

On July 1, 2025, astronomers discovered comet 3I/ATLAS through the ATLAS survey in Chile. The comet reached perihelion on October 29-30, 2025. Following this event, NASA released processed images in December 2025, prompting public demands for clearer data. On December 19, 2025, independent observers at Tenerife's Teide Observatory detected pulsing jets from the comet.

Official statements

“The lack of raw data goes against the three main principles of scientific integrity: verification, replication, and falsification,” said a representative from the citizen science community.

Context

This controversy highlights ongoing tensions between NASA and the scientific community regarding data transparency. Previous incidents have seen similar frustrations over the release of observational data, with calls for greater openness in scientific research.

What’s next

As 3I/ATLAS approaches Jupiter in March 2026, demands for raw data are likely to grow. Authorities may need to address the public's concerns to maintain trust in scientific institutions.
