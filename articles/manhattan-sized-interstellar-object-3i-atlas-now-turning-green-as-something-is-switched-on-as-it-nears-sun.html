Manhattan-sized interstellar object 3I/ATLAS now turning green as something is ‘switched on’ as it nears Sun

The interstellar object 3I/ATLAS, measuring the size of Manhattan, is turning green as it approaches the Sun, according to scientists from the European Southern Observatory’s Very Large Telescope in Chile. The object has shown a significant increase in cyanide and nickel outgassing over the past two weeks, prompting experts to suggest that a process has been activated.

This development is significant as it marks a change in the object's physical characteristics, which have puzzled astronomers. Authorities are closely monitoring 3I/ATLAS as it travels through the inner solar system, providing a unique opportunity for observation.

Key facts

  • 3I/ATLAS is currently turning green due to increased outgassing.
  • The object is approximately the size of Manhattan.
  • Recent observations have shown a rise in cyanide and nickel emissions.
  • Experts describe the outgassing increase as “super-linear.”
  • 3I/ATLAS is the first interstellar object to pass through the inner solar system.
  • The likelihood of such a path is estimated at 0.02%, according to Dr. Avi Loeb.

Timeline

In recent weeks, scientists have observed a notable shift in the characteristics of 3I/ATLAS. Initially, the object exhibited no cometary tail, despite being categorized as a comet by researchers. Over the last two weeks, the outgassing of cyanide and nickel has increased dramatically, leading to its color change from red to green.

Dr. Avi Loeb, a Harvard astrophysicist tracking 3I/ATLAS, has provided insights into the process behind this change, suggesting a temperature-related activation that influences the object's surface and emissions.

Official statements

“What they mean is that there is a process with some minimum temperature in order for it to get activated,” said Dr. Avi Loeb. “Imagine that you want to buy a ticket to see a show. As long as you have a small amount of money in your pocket, you will not get in, but once you have more than some minimum, you will be able to see the show.”

Context

Prior observations of 3I/ATLAS did not indicate any typical cometary features, which has led to ongoing discussions among scientists regarding the nature of interstellar objects. The current observations provide a rare opportunity to study the behavior of such an object as it interacts with solar radiation.

What’s next

Scientists will continue to observe 3I/ATLAS as it nears the Sun. Further studies will aim to understand the mechanisms behind the observed changes and their implications for our knowledge of interstellar objects.
