Interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS displays an unprecedented red color for ESA probes passing close to Mars

Interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS has exhibited a rare reddish coma during its passage near Mars, as observed by two probes from the European Space Agency (ESA) on November 29, 2025. This unique observation provides valuable data about the comet's chemical composition and formation processes in a different cosmic environment.

The findings from the Solar Orbiter and BepiColombo probes represent a significant opportunity for the scientific community to analyze a celestial body formed outside our solar system. The data collected will enhance understanding of planet formation in other star systems and the distribution of essential chemical elements throughout the universe.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS is traveling at 57 km/s on a hyperbolic trajectory.
  • The reddish hue suggests a chemical composition rich in complex organic molecules.
  • The comet was detected by the Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System (ATLAS).
  • 3I/ATLAS is the second interstellar object formally recognized, after ‘Oumuamua.
  • The Solar Orbiter and BepiColombo probes collected detailed images and spectral data.

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was initially detected by the ATLAS astronomical survey system. Its hyperbolic trajectory confirmed its status as an interstellar object, indicating it was ejected from its home star system.

As the comet approached Mars, the Solar Orbiter and BepiColombo probes, which were not specifically designed for comet studies, redirected their instruments to capture data. This strategic positioning allowed for high-quality observations that terrestrial telescopes could not achieve.

The detailed observations took place during the comet's close passage to Mars, enabling scientists to analyze its coma and tail using advanced cameras and spectrometers.

Official statements

“The observations of 3I/ATLAS provide a unique opportunity to understand the chemical building blocks of other planetary systems,” said a spokesperson for the European Space Agency.

Context

Previous observations of comets from our solar system typically show comas in shades of green or blue due to the presence of molecules like cyanogen and diatomic carbon. The unusual red color of 3I/ATLAS marks a significant deviation from this trend.

Understanding the composition of comets like 3I/ATLAS can help refine theoretical models regarding the formation of planets across various galactic environments.

What’s next

The successful observation of 3I/ATLAS highlights the importance of ongoing sky monitoring systems like ATLAS. Future identification of interstellar objects will facilitate more detailed observations and may lead to missions designed to collect physical samples for laboratory analysis on Earth.
