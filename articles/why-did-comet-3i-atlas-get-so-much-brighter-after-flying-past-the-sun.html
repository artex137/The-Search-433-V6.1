Why Did Comet 3I/ATLAS Get So Much Brighter After Flying Past the Sun?

Comet 3I/ATLAS has exhibited a significant increase in brightness after its close flyby of the Sun, according to observations from NASA’s SPHEREx near-infrared observatory. The comet, which is exiting the solar system, has been venting material into space and is expected to fade from view soon.

This phenomenon matters to scientists as it offers a rare opportunity to study pristine interstellar material that has not been seen since before the formation of the solar system. Researchers are keen to understand the comet's unique behaviors and the implications of its interstellar origin.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS is the third confirmed interstellar object observed in our solar system.
  • NASA’s SPHEREx observatory has tracked its brightening after the solar flyby.
  • By December 2025, the comet was noted to be “full-on erupting,” according to astronomer Carey Lisse from Johns Hopkins University.
  • The comet has begun venting water vapor, carbon-rich compounds, and organic molecules into space.

Timeline

3I/ATLAS approached the Sun, which heated the comet and caused it to vent material into space. Observations recorded from NASA’s SPHEREx observatory indicate that this process began shortly after the flyby. By December 2025, the comet was exhibiting significant eruptive behavior.

Official statements

“This object spent eons drifting between stars, bombarded by cosmic radiation,” said Carey Lisse, highlighting the unique origin of 3I/ATLAS. “As solar heat penetrated beneath the crust, it warmed ancient ice locked deep inside.”

Context

3I/ATLAS is notable for its unusual behaviors, which have intrigued scientists since its discovery. Its interstellar origin suggests that it has been chemically altered over billions of years, making its study particularly valuable for understanding the composition of materials outside our solar system.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to monitor Comet 3I/ATLAS until it fades from view, aiming to gather as much data as possible about its composition and behavior before it exits the solar system.
