Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS Erupting Ice? Everything on the ‘Ice Volcano’ Claim, Hubble’s Odd Anti-Tail

Interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS has gained attention following new observations that have sparked scientific excitement and debate. NASA’s Hubble Space Telescope released a new image on November 30, 2025, showing an unusual anti-tail. Meanwhile, a European research team has suggested the comet may be exhibiting cryovolcanism, or “ice volcanoes,” on its surface.

This discovery is significant as it may enhance understanding of cometary behavior and the nature of interstellar objects. Researchers are closely monitoring 3I/ATLAS with upcoming observations from Hubble, the James Webb Space Telescope (JWST), and ESA’s JUICE spacecraft to confirm these findings.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS is approximately 286 million km from Earth as of December 5, 2025.
  • It was discovered in July 2025 by the ATLAS survey telescope in Chile.
  • The comet passed perihelion in late October 2025, at about 1.36 AU from the Sun.
  • Its incoming speed is approximately 58 km/s.
  • Hubble captured a new image of 3I/ATLAS showing a teardrop-shaped inner coma and an anti-tail extending about 60,000 km sunward.
  • The comet is currently at magnitude ~12, moving from the constellation Virgo towards Leo.

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was discovered in July 2025 by the ATLAS survey telescope. It passed perihelion in late October 2025 and has been observed by various telescopes since then.

On November 30, 2025, Hubble released a new image of the comet, revealing a teardrop-shaped inner coma and an unusual anti-tail. Following this, a European research team proposed that the comet may be exhibiting cryovolcanism, based on high-resolution images captured as it approached perihelion.

Official statements

“A large number of macroscopic, non-volatile objects may have separated from the comet due to non-gravitational acceleration,” said Avi Loeb, a Harvard astrophysicist, regarding the implications of Hubble’s new image.
“The jets that we observe could be explained by normal cometary activity, but upcoming spectroscopy will be crucial for testing our cryovolcanism hypothesis,” stated Josep M. Trigo-Rodríguez, a member of the European research team.

Context

3I/ATLAS is only the third confirmed interstellar object ever observed, following ʻOumuamua and Borisov. Its discovery has prompted increased interest in the study of interstellar objects and their characteristics.

Scientists have identified several components in the comet's atmosphere, including CO₂, water ice, carbon monoxide, carbonyl sulfide, cyanide, and nickel vapour.

What’s next

Researchers plan to conduct further spectroscopy using Hubble, JWST, and JUICE to investigate the nature of the jets observed on 3I/ATLAS and to confirm whether they are indicative of cryovolcanism or normal cometary activity.
