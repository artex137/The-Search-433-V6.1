Is 3I/ATLAS breaking apart? Astronomers detect bizarre twin tails after solar encounter

Astronomers have detected unusual brightening and twin tails from comet 3I/ATLAS after its closest approach to the Sun, which occurred recently. The findings raise questions about the comet's structure and behaviour in extreme solar conditions.

This event is significant as it presents a rare opportunity for scientists to study how interstellar objects react to solar heat. According to reports, 3I/ATLAS has shown signs of rapid heating and material loss, prompting experts to closely observe its behaviour. Astronomers, including Avi Loeb from Harvard, are particularly interested in the potential implications of this event for our understanding of comets and other celestial bodies.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS has formed an “anti-tail” pointing toward the Sun.
  • One tail extends approximately 10 lakh kilometres toward the Sun.
  • The second tail stretches nearly 30 lakh kilometres in the opposite direction.
  • Mass loss increased from 150 kilograms per second to nearly 20 lakh kilograms per second before closest approach.
  • Closest approach to Earth is on December 19; to Jupiter in March 2026.
  • Observations planned with the Hubble and James Webb Space Telescopes.

Timeline

Recent images taken by British astronomers Michael Buechner and Frank Niebling have shown the unusual characteristics of 3I/ATLAS. The comet brightened significantly during its closest approach to the Sun, indicating intense heating.

Avi Loeb noted that the comet may have fragmented into at least 16 pieces, which could explain the dramatic changes in its mass loss and tail formation.

Official statements

Avi Loeb stated, “This means 3I/ATLAS exploded near the Sun, and we are seeing the aftereffects.”
Loeb also cautioned, “If it didn’t disintegrate, 3I/ATLAS might not be a natural comet at all.”

Context

This unusual event is rare in the study of comets and could challenge existing theories about their formation and behaviour. If 3I/ATLAS proves to be a technological object, it could change the way scientists view interstellar objects and their interactions with the Sun.

What’s next

Astronomers will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS closely, with upcoming observations from the Hubble and James Webb Space Telescopes aimed at determining whether the comet has fragmented or remains intact.
