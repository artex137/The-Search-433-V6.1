Astronomer’s bombshell UFO funding call

A leading astronomer has called for increased funding for UFO research, stating that it could enhance Australia’s commitment to space research. The comments came after the release of a government statement on space by Industry Minister Tim Ayres on July 22, 2026, which outlines ambitious goals for research services, flights, agriculture, and exploration technologies.

Professor Brad Tucker from the Australian National University said the government’s statement lacks concrete funding commitments, comparing it to “buying a box of bandaids” without actually applying any. He emphasized that even minimal funding for tracking Unidentified Anomalous Phenomena (UAPs) could improve situational awareness regarding space debris. The Australian Space Agency has seen its budget cut by more than 50 per cent since 2024, which Dr Tucker described as a sign that space research is not a priority for the federal government.

Key facts

  • Professor Brad Tucker advocates for UFO research funding.
  • Industry Minister Tim Ayres launched a new government statement on space on July 22, 2026.
  • The Australian Space Agency's budget has been cut by over 50% since 2024.
  • Dr Tucker noted that space research is “quite far down the totem pole” for priority areas.
  • The government aims to expedite approval processes for space projects.
  • Australia intends to become a preferred location for rocket launches in the Indo-Pacific.

Timeline

On July 22, 2026, Industry Minister Tim Ayres released a government statement outlining future ambitions for Australia’s space research and technology sectors. Professor Brad Tucker responded to the statement, highlighting the lack of funding commitments and advocating for the inclusion of UFO research to enhance overall space situational awareness.

Dr Tucker pointed out the significant budget cuts to the Australian Space Agency since 2024, indicating a decline in the priority placed on space research by the federal government.

Official statements

“I’ll take whatever (money) we can get,” Dr Tucker said. “At least if you’re tracking UAPs (Unidentified Anomalous Phenomena) or UFOs, you’re also tracking space junk. That’s situational space awareness.”
“This focus is intended to support industry, government and partners to better understand where the Australian space industry can contribute to a more competitive, productive and resilient economy,” Ayres stated regarding the government's new strategy.

Context

Previous budget cuts have raised concerns about the future of Australia’s space capabilities. As other nations, including India and the United States, ramp up their space initiatives, Australia aims to enhance its position in the global space industry.

Dr Tucker’s comments reflect a growing frustration among researchers regarding the government’s prioritization of space research, especially in light of recent funding reductions.

What’s next

The government plans to implement a faster approval process for space projects as it seeks to bolster the Australian space industry. Further developments regarding funding allocations for UFO research and overall space initiatives remain to be seen.
