CT lawmaker wants state to study UFOs. ‘Crazy and wacky conspiracy theories’ not wanted.

Connecticut state Representative Joe Hoxha, R-Bristol, has introduced a bill that calls for the University of Connecticut to collaborate with various state agencies to study unidentified aerial phenomena (UAPs) in the state. The bill, H.B. 5422, aims to establish a dedicated program to record and analyze UAP sightings in Connecticut. Hoxha presented the proposal during a session of the Appropriations Committee, where he emphasized the need for a centralized approach to UAP data collection.

The proposal comes amid increased interest in UAPs, particularly following recent federal government actions. Hoxha, who has been a UFO enthusiast for years, stated that it is time for the state to take the subject seriously. The bill has gained bipartisan support, with Representative Aundre Bumgardner, D-Groton, as a co-sponsor. Bumgardner highlighted the importance of understanding UAPs, especially given his town's connection to U.S. Navy submarine production.

Key facts

  • Bill number: H.B. 5422
  • Proposed by: Rep. Joe Hoxha, R-Bristol
  • Co-sponsor: Rep. Aundre Bumgardner, D-Groton
  • Committee: Appropriations Committee
  • Testimonies heard from residents and experts, including Sri Tata, a PhD student at Yale
  • New Jersey passed a similar law to study UAPs, effective January 2026

Timeline

In May 2022, Congress held a hearing on UAPs for the first time in decades, increasing public interest in the topic.

In July 2022, the All-domain Anomaly Resolution Office was established by the Department of Defense to address UAP-related activities.

In March 2026, Rep. Joe Hoxha introduced H.B. 5422 to the Connecticut legislature, seeking to formalize the study of UAPs within the state.

Official statements

“The topic is finally starting to be taken seriously, starting with the federal government,” said Hoxha.
“We should know if it’s coming from one of our adversaries,” said Bumgardner, referring to the potential threats posed by UAPs.
“Establishing a scientific study for both public policy and scientific curiosity would be important,” stated Sri Tata.

Context

Recent years have seen a surge in reported UAP sightings, with individuals from various sectors, including military personnel and pilots, sharing their experiences. In 2024, a report from the All-domain Anomaly Resolution Office found no evidence of alien technology in UAP sightings, but interest in the subject continues to grow.

New Jersey's recent law to establish a state center for UAP studies indicates a shifting perspective on the topic, suggesting that more states may follow suit.

What’s next

The bill will proceed through the legislative process, with further discussions and potential amendments expected as lawmakers continue to evaluate the proposal.
