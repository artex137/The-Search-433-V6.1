3I/ATLAS Update: Harvard Expert Avi Loeb Warns Interstellar Objects Still Need to be Figured Out

Avi Loeb, a Harvard astrophysicist, has raised concerns about the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, which exhibits behaviors that challenge existing understanding of cosmic objects. The comet was first observed by the NASA-funded ATLAS survey in Chile on July 1, 2025, and made its closest approach to Earth on December 19, 2025, passing approximately 270 million kilometres away.

This study of 3I/ATLAS is significant as it may impact global planetary defense strategies, especially with asteroid 99942 Apophis set to approach Earth in 2029. Authorities are ramping up efforts to gather data on Apophis as it will pass within 32,000 kilometres of Earth, closer than some satellites.

Key facts

  • Object: 3I/ATLAS
  • First observed: July 1, 2025
  • Closest approach to Earth: December 19, 2025
  • Closest distance: 270 million kilometres
  • Speed: 58 kilometres per second
  • Next significant event: Apophis flyby on April 13, 2029
  • Distance during Apophis flyby: 32,000 kilometres

Timeline

On July 1, 2025, the NASA-funded ATLAS survey in Chile first detected the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS. Over the following months, astronomers tracked its motion and brightness changes using various instruments. On December 19, 2025, 3I/ATLAS made its closest approach to Earth, travelling at 58 kilometres per second. The comet is expected to leave the solar system by March 2026.

Official statements

Avi Loeb stated, "Interstellar objects are a new class of objects that still need to be figured out."
NASA's Planetary Defense Coordination Office confirmed, "Apophis poses no impact threat in 2029 based on current observations."

Context

The anomalies presented by 3I/ATLAS challenge established assumptions in planetary defense planning, particularly concerning the tracking of interstellar objects. Loeb has criticized delays in the release of important data from NASA's Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter, emphasizing the need for prioritizing scientific inquiry over bureaucratic processes.

What’s next

As agencies like NASA and the European Space Agency prepare for the Apophis flyby, they will focus on gathering radar data, thermal readings, and structural information. This will be crucial for refining models and improving simulations related to potential threats posed by future interstellar visitors.
