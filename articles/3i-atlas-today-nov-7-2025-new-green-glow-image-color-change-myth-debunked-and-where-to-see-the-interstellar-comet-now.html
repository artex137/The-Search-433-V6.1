3I/ATLAS Today (Nov. 7, 2025): New green-glow image, ‘color-change’ myth debunked, and where to see the interstellar comet now

On November 7, 2025, new images from the Lowell Discovery Telescope have shown interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS glowing green in the early predawn sky. The comet, which has been the subject of viral claims regarding a supposed color change, remains trackable with small telescopes in the eastern sky, according to scientists.

This development is significant as it helps clarify misconceptions about the comet's appearance. Authorities, including the International Asteroid Warning Network (IAWN), are mobilizing efforts to study and observe 3I/ATLAS. They have closed registration for an upcoming astrometry campaign aimed at refining tracking techniques for this rare interstellar visitor.

Key facts

  • Image captured on November 7, 2025, by the Lowell Discovery Telescope.
  • 3I/ATLAS is visible in the eastern sky, low in Virgo, during early predawn hours.
  • Closest approach to Earth is expected on December 19, 2025, at approximately 270 million km (1.8 AU).
  • IAWN has launched a campaign from November 27 to January 27 focused on the comet.
  • Comet reached perihelion around October 29-30, 2025, at about 1.4 AU from the Sun.

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was discovered on July 1, 2025, by the ATLAS survey in Chile. The comet reached its perihelion around October 29-30, 2025. As of early November, it has re-emerged in the predawn sky, becoming increasingly visible to amateur astronomers. The Lowell Discovery Telescope captured a new image on November 7, which confirmed its green glow.

On November 6, the Virtual Telescope Project released an image that also confirmed the comet's visible coma, further supporting the observations made by Lowell.

Official statements

“We don’t have any evidence for the gas coma changing colors,” said Qicheng Zhang, co-author of a recent study on the comet's appearance.

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third known interstellar object to visit our solar system, following 1I/ʻOumuamua and 2I/Borisov. The comet's hyperbolic trajectory confirms it as a passer-by rather than a long-term resident. Observations of its gas coma and tail have led to public fascination and some misconceptions about its appearance.

What’s next

The upcoming astrometry campaign, starting November 27, will help refine tracking techniques for 3I/ATLAS. The global observing community is expected to provide further insights into the comet's characteristics as it becomes more visible in the coming weeks.
