Breakthrough Listen completes search for technosignals on interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS without artificial detections

Scientists from the Breakthrough Listen project have reported that their detailed observations of the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, conducted using sensitive radio telescopes, have not detected any artificial signals that could indicate extraterrestrial technology. The observations took place after the comet passed close enough to Earth to allow for comprehensive scans, with results announced on January 5, 2026.

This finding is significant as it helps confirm the classification of 3I/ATLAS as a natural celestial body. Following these results, researchers indicate that no further immediate actions are necessary regarding the comet, but they will continue to study the data collected for insights into the nature of interstellar objects.

Key facts

  • Object: 3I/ATLAS, an interstellar comet
  • Observation date: January 5, 2026
  • Discovery date: July 2025
  • Distance from Earth at closest approach: approximately 1.8 astronomical units
  • Perihelion passage: October 2025, at about 1.4 astronomical units from the Sun
  • Instruments used: Green Bank Telescope, Allen Telescope Array, Hubble, and Vera C. Rubin telescopes

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first identified by the ATLAS system in July 2025 during routine sky scans aimed at monitoring asteroids. Astronomers quickly confirmed its interstellar nature based on its hyperbolic trajectory, indicating it originated from outside the Solar System. The comet passed through perihelion in October 2025, exhibiting typical cometary behavior such as outgassing.

Following its close approach, the Breakthrough Listen project conducted a thorough search for technosignals using advanced radio telescopes. This search focused on narrowband radio emissions, which are potential indicators of intelligent activity. On January 5, 2026, results were released, confirming no artificial signals were detected.

Official statements

“The lack of detection in 3I/ATLAS does not eliminate possibilities in other bodies,” said a representative from the Breakthrough Listen project. “Each visitor offers a unique window to test technosignal hypotheses.”

Context

To date, only three interstellar objects have been confirmed: 1I/’Oumuamua, discovered in 2017, and 2I/Borisov, identified in 2019. Each of these objects has contributed to understanding the nature of interstellar visitors, reinforcing the importance of ongoing monitoring efforts. The results from 3I/ATLAS add to the growing body of knowledge about these rare celestial bodies.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to analyze archived data from 3I/ATLAS to uncover more details about its composition and formation. Future studies may focus on the unique chemical signatures of interstellar materials that could shed light on the origins of life beyond our Solar System.
