New interstellar comet 3I Atlas detected, NASA prepares for unprecedented 2025 observation mission

Astronomers have confirmed the detection of a third interstellar comet, provisionally named 3I Atlas, identified by the ATLAS (Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System) survey. This discovery marks a significant milestone in astrophysics as NASA prepares for a comprehensive observation campaign throughout 2025 to study the comet's characteristics and origins.

The detection of 3I Atlas provides scientists with a unique opportunity to investigate matter from beyond our solar system. NASA plans to maximize data collection as the comet approaches its closest point to Earth and the Sun, potentially revealing insights into the formation of other stellar systems.

Key facts

  • Comet name: 3I Atlas
  • Detection method: ATLAS survey
  • Initial detection: Late 2024
  • NASA's observation campaign: Throughout 2025
  • Closest approach to Earth: 270 million kilometres
  • Instruments to be used: Hubble Space Telescope, James Webb Space Telescope

Timeline

The initial detection of 3I Atlas occurred in late 2024 through automated scans by the ATLAS telescope network. Its unusual orbital path indicated an interstellar origin, prompting further observations from multiple observatories worldwide. By early 2025, these observations confirmed its hyperbolic trajectory, validating its status as an interstellar object.

NASA has since mobilized a multifaceted observation strategy, including ground-based telescopes and space-borne assets, to gather data on the comet's composition and behavior as it approaches.

Official statements

“The study of 3I Atlas offers an unparalleled opportunity to sample material from another star system directly,” said a NASA spokesperson. “Each new interstellar visitor provides unique data points, building a more complete picture of the types of objects that traverse the Milky Way.”

Context

3I Atlas joins a select group of confirmed interstellar objects, following the detection of 1I/’Oumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019. These previous discoveries have each provided valuable insights into the characteristics of interstellar bodies, enhancing our understanding of planetary formation and the diversity of exoplanetary environments.

What’s next

Nasa and other astronomical organizations are planning public engagement initiatives throughout 2025, including online live streams, educational webinars, and updated observation guides for amateur astronomers. These efforts aim to share the excitement of this interstellar discovery with a global audience.
