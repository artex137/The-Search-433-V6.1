Dozen such coincidences - Harvard Professor Avi Loeb says 3I/ATLAS and gamma-ray burst alignments are statistically expected

Harvard Professor Avi Loeb has stated that reported alignments between interstellar object 3I/ATLAS and gamma-ray bursts are statistically expected, based on event rates and the object’s long transit time through the Solar System. His remarks came during a discussion about recent observations of the comet, which took place in 2025.

This matter is significant as it sheds light on the nature of astronomical coincidences and the physical conditions of 3I/ATLAS. Loeb’s findings provide a statistical framework for understanding these alignments, which could inform future research in astrophysics.

Key facts

  • 3I/ATLAS was discovered on July 1, 2025.
  • The longest-duration gamma-ray burst, GRB 250702b, was detected on July 2, 2025.
  • The angular separation between 3I/ATLAS and GRB 250702b was 17 degrees.
  • The probability of this alignment being a chance occurrence is 0.02.
  • 3I/ATLAS has been traveling through the Solar System for approximately 8,000 years.
  • In 2025, about six normal-duration gamma-ray bursts aligned within 17 degrees of 3I/ATLAS's arrival direction.

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was discovered on July 1, 2025. The following day, the longest-duration gamma-ray burst, GRB 250702b, was detected. Loeb observed that the angular separation between the two was 17 degrees.

On November 26, 2025, post-perihelion observations of 3I/ATLAS were conducted using the Gemini Multi-Object Spectrograph at the 8.1-meter Gemini North telescope in Hawaii.

The imaging results showed changes in the molecular composition of gas released by 3I/ATLAS as it passed near the Sun.

Official statements

“Altogether, there had been a dozen such coincidences while 3I/ATLAS was traveling inside the Solar System over the past 8,000 years and the latest one happened to occur this year,” said Loeb.

Context

Loeb referenced the gamma-ray burst GRB 111209A, detected on December 9, 2011, as an example of an unrelated transient event. He noted that it originated from an exploding star at a distance of 13.7 billion light years, establishing its astrophysical origin unrelated to Solar System objects.

These findings highlight the statistical nature of gamma-ray bursts and their distribution across the sky, which helps explain the observed alignments with 3I/ATLAS.

What’s next

Further research is expected to continue examining the alignments and characteristics of 3I/ATLAS and gamma-ray bursts, with additional observations planned to deepen understanding of these phenomena.
