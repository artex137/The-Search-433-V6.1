Mysterious Interstellar Object Showing Signs of "Non-Gravitational Acceleration"

In early July, astronomers discovered a mysterious object named 3I/ATLAS, believed to be the third interstellar visitor in our solar system, as it reached its closest point to the Sun last week. The object has displayed unexpected brightening and a change in color, raising questions about its nature and behavior.

This discovery is significant as it may indicate unusual physical processes affecting 3I/ATLAS. NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory navigation engineer Davide Farnocchia reported that the object is showing signs of "non-gravitational acceleration," suggesting it is losing mass. Harvard astronomer Avi Loeb has indicated that this could lead to observable changes in the object over the coming months.

Key facts

  • Object Name: 3I/ATLAS
  • Discovery Date: Early July 2023
  • Closest Approach to Sun: Last week
  • Reported Mass Loss: Up to one-tenth in a month
  • Expected Observable Changes: Large plume of gas in November and December 2025
  • Significant Brightening: Observed during perihelion

Timeline

In early July, astronomers first spotted 3I/ATLAS. Last week, the object reached its perihelion, or closest point to the Sun, and exhibited unexpected brightening alongside a color shift to a distinctly bluer hue. Recent reports from NASA and Harvard have indicated that the object is showing signs of non-gravitational acceleration, likely due to mass loss.

Avi Loeb has suggested that 3I/ATLAS could lose about a sixth of its mass, which may explain its unusual brightening. He also noted that observations from the Atacama Large Millimeter/submillimeter Array (ALMA) have detected deviations in the object's expected path.

Official statements

“Such a massive mass loss should be detectable in the form of a large plume of gas surrounding 3I/ATLAS during the upcoming months of November and December 2025,” said Avi Loeb in his blog.
“I think it’s inappropriate to withhold the scientific information from the community because we are planning future observations based on what we know about 3I/ATLAS,” Loeb told NBC.

Context

This incident marks the third confirmed interstellar object to enter the solar system, following previous discoveries. The ongoing investigation into 3I/ATLAS may provide insights into the characteristics and behaviors of such objects, as well as their potential implications for our understanding of space.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS and push for further observations, particularly from NASA's Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter. The potential mass loss and resulting plume will be closely observed in late 2025.
