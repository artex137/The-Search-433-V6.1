3I/ATLAS Interstellar Object to Become Super Active on October 29

Interstellar object 3I/ATLAS, the third interstellar visitor detected in our solar system, is set to enter a phase of heightened activity on October 29, 2025, as it approaches its perihelion, or closest point to the Sun. The object, first confirmed by NASA in early July, is currently hidden on the far side of the Sun and is expected to produce a spectacular display of gas and light as solar radiation intensifies its transformation.

This event is significant for scientists as it provides a rare opportunity to study an ancient celestial body that could reveal insights into the chemical makeup of other planetary systems in the Milky Way. Astronomers are preparing instruments worldwide to observe the comet's behavior as it nears the Sun and begins its exit from the inner solar system.

Key facts

  • 3I/ATLAS is the third interstellar object ever detected, after ‘Oumuamua in 2017 and Borisov in 2019.
  • The object will reach perihelion on October 29, 2025, at a distance of 1.4 astronomical units (about 210 million kilometers) from the Sun.
  • 3I/ATLAS is believed to be the largest, fastest-moving, and possibly the oldest interstellar object observed to date.
  • A breathtaking image of the object was captured on August 27, 2023, by the Gemini South telescope in Chile.
  • During its perihelion, it is expected to emit gas and dust at an accelerated rate, enhancing its visibility in early November.

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first confirmed by NASA in early July 2023. Since then, its trajectory has been monitored closely as it approaches the Sun. The object has been moving towards its perihelion, scheduled for October 29, 2025. Observations and data collection efforts have intensified in anticipation of its upcoming display.

On August 27, 2023, astronomers captured a significant image of 3I/ATLAS, showcasing a glowing plume of gas and dust around the comet’s nucleus. This image has contributed to the excitement surrounding its upcoming perihelion.

Official statements

“Every interstellar object offers a direct sample of material from beyond our solar system. It’s like receiving a message in a bottle from another star,” said an astronomer involved with NASA’s observation program.

Context

3I/ATLAS is part of a small group of interstellar objects, with only two others, ‘Oumuamua and Borisov, having been detected before it. Each of these objects provides a unique opportunity for scientists to study materials that originate from outside our solar system, enhancing our understanding of the universe.

What’s next

As 3I/ATLAS approaches its perihelion, scientists will continue to monitor the object closely. Observations are set to take place using ground-based telescopes and orbiting observatories, with the goal of gathering vital data on the comet's composition and behavior as it departs from the inner solar system.
