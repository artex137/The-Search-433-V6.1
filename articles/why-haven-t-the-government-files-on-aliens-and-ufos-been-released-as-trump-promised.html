Why haven’t the government files on aliens and UFOs been released as Trump promised?

A viral podcast moment last month has reignited speculation about extraterrestrials and led President Donald Trump to commit to releasing government files on unidentified anomalous phenomena (UAPs), commonly referred to as UFOs. However, as of now, no records or details regarding their release have emerged, highlighting the complexity of such disclosures.

This situation is significant because it reflects ongoing public interest in UAPs, a topic that has captivated both believers and skeptics for decades. Authorities, including the Pentagon's All-domain Anomaly Resolution Office (AARO), are currently coordinating efforts to consolidate existing UAP records and facilitate their release, according to a Department of Defense official.

Key facts

  • Trump's commitment to release UAP files follows comments made by former President Barack Obama on a podcast.
  • The Pentagon's AARO is tasked with investigating UAPs.
  • The U.S. government has investigated UAPs for almost 80 years.
  • Files related to UAPs often contain sensitive information regarding military capabilities.
  • The release process involves review by trained security officers to ensure national security is not compromised.

Timeline

In March 2026, President Trump announced his intention to expedite the release of UAP files following a podcast appearance by former President Obama, where he discussed the existence of extraterrestrial life. This announcement has led to a series of interagency meetings aimed at determining how to publicize classified information related to UAPs.

The Office of the Director of National Intelligence indicated that files concerning UAPs will be declassified “soon,” but no specific timeline has been provided. The process of declassification typically involves extensive review and could be subject to delays.

Official statements

“Files related to alien and extraterrestrial life, UAPs, and UFOs will soon be declassified,” the Office of the Director of National Intelligence stated on social media.
“I would try to temper expectations a bit. I think it’s going to be a fairly long, and probably a bit of a slow process,” said Christopher Mellon, former deputy assistant secretary of defense for intelligence.

Context

The U.S. government has a long history of investigating UAPs, dating back nearly 80 years. Past disclosures, such as those related to Project Blue Book in the 1970s, have involved the release of thousands of pages documenting sightings and investigations. These historical precedents suggest that the upcoming release may include similar types of documents, although experts caution that much of the content could be mundane.

What’s next

The AARO and other federal agencies will continue their efforts to identify and release UAP records, but the timeline remains unclear. The process will involve thorough reviews to ensure national security is not compromised, which may result in a slow release of heavily redacted files.
