NASA monitors comet 3I/ATLAS for atypical behavior and unique chemistry

NASA has intensified monitoring of interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, detected on July 1, 2025, by the ATLAS telescope in Chile. The object, which reached perihelion on October 29, 2025, is located 210 million kilometers from the Sun and displays unusual chemical properties.

This monitoring is significant due to the comet's hyperbolic orbit and its unique composition, which includes a coma with eight times more carbon dioxide than water. The International Asteroid Warning Network (IAWN) has organized a global training program from November 27, 2025, to January 27, 2026, to refine orbital measurements and improve tracking accuracy.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS detected on July 1, 2025.
  • Reached perihelion on October 29, 2025, at a distance of 210 million kilometers from the Sun.
  • Comet's speed exceeds 210,000 km/h, making predictions challenging.
  • Coma contains eight times more carbon dioxide (CO₂) than water.
  • Core diameter estimated between 320 meters and 5.6 kilometers.
  • Comet is over 7 billion years old, predating the Solar System.
  • IAWN coordinates telescopes in Hawaii, Chile, and Australia for monitoring.
  • The ESA's Juice probe will observe the comet from 64 million kilometers away in November 2025.

Timeline

On July 1, 2025, astronomers detected comet 3I/ATLAS using the ATLAS telescope in Chile. The comet reached perihelion on October 29, 2025, at a distance of 210 million kilometers from the Sun. It passed 28 million kilometers from Mars on October 3, 2025, and is expected to approach Jupiter in March 2026.

In November 2025, the IAWN will conduct a global training program to enhance tracking capabilities for interstellar objects like 3I/ATLAS.

Official statements

“The composition of 3I/ATLAS challenges traditional models of comets,” said a NASA spokesperson. “Its high carbon dioxide levels suggest formation in cold regions beyond the CO₂ frost line.”

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third confirmed interstellar comet, following 'Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov. Its unique chemical composition, especially the high levels of carbon dioxide and low presence of water, raises questions about the formation of planetary systems in the cosmos. The absence of iron associated with nickel further challenges existing models.

What’s next

Data collected until January 2026 will be crucial for understanding the formation of 3I/ATLAS and its implications for the early universe. Ongoing international collaboration aims to refine tracking and analysis of this unique interstellar object.
