Scientist Claims 3I ATLAS Is 'Artificial' and Not of This World—Internet Erupts in Shock

Dr. Aris Thorne, a materials scientist consulting for the Department of Homeland Security, has claimed that the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS is constructed from an "artificial composite material." The announcement has sparked widespread debate in the scientific community and beyond. 3I/ATLAS, first detected on July 1, 2025, by the ATLAS telescope in Hawaii, is the third interstellar object confirmed, following 1I/ʻOumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019.

The significance of this claim lies in its implications for humanity's understanding of life beyond Earth. Authorities are currently analyzing the object's unusual characteristics, which have led to theories suggesting it may not be a natural celestial body. The object is set to make its closest approach to the Sun on October 29, 2025, which could provide further evidence supporting or refuting these claims.

Key facts

  • 3I/ATLAS detected on July 1, 2025, in Hawaii
  • Estimated size: 5.6 kilometres wide
  • Closest approach to the Sun scheduled for October 29, 2025
  • First interstellar objects detected: 1I/ʻOumuamua (2017), 2I/Borisov (2019)
  • Claim of artificial composition made by Dr. Aris Thorne, materials scientist
  • Analysis indicates emission of nickel without iron

Timeline

On July 1, 2025, the ATLAS telescope in Hawaii detected 3I/ATLAS, marking it as the third interstellar object confirmed. Subsequent observations revealed its unusual size and trajectory. In recent analyses, scientists noted the object venting nickel but lacking iron, suggesting possible artificial origins. The object is expected to make its closest approach to the Sun on October 29, 2025.

Official statements

“The evidence points to one, universe-altering conclusion: humanity's first contact with an alien artifact may have already happened.”
“The refined compound being emitted, nickel tetracarbonyl, is known to humanity for one reason only: its use in industrial manufacturing processes.”

Context

The discovery of 3I/ATLAS adds to the ongoing discourse surrounding the existence of extraterrestrial life. Previous interstellar objects, 1I/ʻOumuamua and 2I/Borisov, have also prompted discussions about the nature of cosmic visitors. The current claims about 3I/ATLAS's artificial composition raise questions about NASA's transparency, particularly regarding the lack of high-resolution images of the object.

What’s next

As the scientific community continues to analyze 3I/ATLAS, the upcoming closest approach to the Sun on October 29, 2025, is anticipated to yield critical data. Observers are eager to see whether this encounter will confirm the official narrative or provide evidence supporting the claims of an artificial origin.
