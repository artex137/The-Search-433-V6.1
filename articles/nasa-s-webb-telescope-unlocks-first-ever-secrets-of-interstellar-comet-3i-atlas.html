NASA’s Webb Telescope unlocks first-ever secrets of interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS

The National Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA) has observed the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS for the first time using the James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) on August 6. The comet was discovered on July 1 by the ATLAS (Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System) telescope.

This observation is significant as it provides insights into the origins and characteristics of interstellar comets. NASA aims to explore the comet's size, chemical makeup, and physical properties, potentially expanding our understanding of the solar system and beyond.

Key facts

  • Comet Name: 3I/ATLAS
  • Discovery Date: July 1
  • Observation Date: August 6
  • Instrument Used: Near-infrared Spectrograph (NIRspec)
  • Detection of Compounds: CO2, water ice, carbon monoxide, water, carbonyl sulfide
  • Estimated Age: Approximately 7 billion years
  • Other Interstellar Comets: 1I/'Oumuamua (2017), 2I/Borisov (2019)

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first detected by the ATLAS telescope on July 1. NASA's James Webb Space Telescope conducted its first observation of the comet on August 6. This marked a significant milestone in the study of interstellar objects.

Official statements

“The close proximity of 3I/ATLAS near the Sun causes outgassing, turning frozen materials into gas,” NASA scientists stated regarding the comet's behavior.
Harvard professor Avi Loeb has suggested that 3I/ATLAS could be an alien probe, nuclear-powered and releasing its own energy.

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third known interstellar object to approach our solar system, following 1I/'Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov. Its unique characteristics and trajectory provide valuable data for understanding the composition and formation of celestial bodies beyond our solar system.

What’s next

NASA plans to continue studying 3I/ATLAS to further investigate its chemical composition and origins, potentially uncovering more about the nature of interstellar comets.
