Interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS approaches Earth in December without risk of collision

Comet 3I/ATLAS, the third interstellar object detected in the Solar System, will reach its closest point to Earth on December 19, 2025, passing approximately 270 million kilometers away. NASA and the European Space Agency (ESA) have confirmed that the comet's hyperbolic trajectory poses no risk of collision, allowing for continued scientific observations.

This event is significant as it offers astronomers a unique opportunity to study an interstellar comet and its chemical composition. Authorities are utilizing multiple space missions and ground-based observatories to gather data and improve understanding of the comet's origins.

Key facts

  • Closest approach: December 19, 2025, at approximately 270 million kilometers from Earth.
  • Discovery date: July 1, 2025, by the ATLAS telescope in Chile.
  • Speed: More than 210,000 kilometers per hour.
  • Perihelion: October 29, 210 million kilometers from the Sun.
  • Estimated core size: Between 440 meters and 5.6 kilometers in diameter.
  • Visible until: January 2026.

Timeline

The discovery and tracking of 3I/ATLAS unfolded as follows:

  • July 1, 2025: Discovered by ATLAS telescope.
  • July 21, 2025: Hubble Space Telescope captures images of the comet.
  • October 3, 2025: Comet passes 28 million kilometers from Mars.
  • November 3, 2025: Comet comes within 97 million kilometers of Venus.
  • December 19, 2025: Closest approach to Earth at 270 million kilometers.
  • March 16, 2026: Encounter with Jupiter at 54 million kilometers.

Official statements

“3I/ATLAS presents a remarkable opportunity to study the composition of an interstellar object and its formation under different stellar conditions,” said an ESA spokesperson.

Context

3I/ATLAS is classified as a natural comet, refuting earlier speculations about its technological origins. The object is estimated to be 7 billion years old, providing insights into galactic formation predating the Solar System.

What’s next

Space missions such as NASA's Europa Clipper and ESA's JUICE will continue to observe 3I/ATLAS, with data expected to improve understanding of its properties and trajectory. Observations will remain ongoing until the comet's departure from the Solar System in early 2026.
