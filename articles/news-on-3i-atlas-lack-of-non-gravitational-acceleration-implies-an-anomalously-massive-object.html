News on 3I/ATLAS: Lack of Non-Gravitational Acceleration Implies an Anomalously Massive Object

New findings about the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS have revealed it to be significantly more massive than previously thought. A study published on September 24, 2025, notes that the lack of non-gravitational acceleration indicates that the object has a mass greater than 33 billion tons and a diameter exceeding 5 kilometers.

This discovery is important as it challenges existing theories about interstellar objects. Researchers, including Avi Loeb, Richard Cloete, and Peter Veres, have analyzed data from the Minor Planet Center and the Webb Space Telescope to assess the characteristics of 3I/ATLAS. Their findings suggest that 3I/ATLAS is 3 to 5 orders of magnitude more massive than the previously identified interstellar objects, 1I/`Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov.

Key facts

  • Object: 3I/ATLAS
  • Mass: Greater than 33 billion tons
  • Diameter: Exceeds 5 kilometers
  • Observation period: May 15 to September 23, 2025
  • Data source: 4,022 observations from 227 observatories
  • Published: September 24, 2025

Timeline

Between May 15 and September 23, 2025, researchers collected data on 3I/ATLAS's motion. They found that the object's trajectory deviated minimally from a gravity-only path, with an upper limit on the deviation of 0.028 arcseconds.

On August 6, 2025, the Webb Space Telescope provided data that helped infer the mass loss rate and surface outflow speed of 3I/ATLAS. This information was critical in determining the object's mass and size.

Official statements

“The results indicate that 3I/ATLAS is a major anomaly among interstellar objects, given its unexpected mass and size,” said Avi Loeb, one of the study's authors.

Context

Prior to this study, only two interstellar objects had been detected: 1I/`Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov. The limited number of discoveries raises questions about the population of interstellar objects, particularly those of smaller sizes. The findings on 3I/ATLAS suggest a need for further investigation into the characteristics and origins of these celestial bodies.

What’s next

Researchers plan to continue studying 3I/ATLAS and its trajectory. Future observations may provide additional insights into its composition and the implications for understanding interstellar objects.
