In 1952, DC’s skies were littered with US fighter jets chasing UFOs. More than 70 years later, the mystery persists

Published 2025-12-28 11:23 UTC

U.S. Fighter Jets Intercept UFOs Over Washington D.C. in July 1952

On July 26, 1952, U.S. Air Force fighter jets scrambled to intercept unidentified flying objects over Washington D.C., marking a significant moment in the history of UFO sightings. Lt. William L. Patterson and Capt. John McHugo of the 142nd Fighter Interceptor Squadron responded to reports of unknown targets in restricted airspace around the White House and Pentagon.

This incident is part of a larger pattern of unexplained aerial phenomena, which has seen a recent surge in reports from pilots. According to Americans for Safe Aerospace, a nonprofit that provides confidential reporting channels for pilots, the number of unexplained encounters has increased, raising concerns reminiscent of those experienced during the Cold War.

Key facts

  • Date of incident: July 26, 1952
  • Location: Near Washington D.C., specifically New Castle Air Force Base, National Airport, and Andrews Air Force Base
  • Pilots involved: Lt. William L. Patterson and Capt. John McHugo
  • Speed of interceptor jets: Nearly 600 miles per hour
  • Duration of flight before return: Approximately one hour
  • Number of unidentified targets tracked: More than a dozen

Timeline

On July 19, 1952, radar operators at National Airport reported unidentified blips, which were confirmed by Andrews and a commercial pilot. Interceptor jets were dispatched but found no visual evidence, leading officials to attribute the phenomena to weather anomalies.

On July 26, radar screens lit up again with similar unidentified targets. Patterson and McHugo were directed to intercept these objects, which displayed unusual flight patterns. Patterson reported seeing four bright lights that maneuvered in ways that no known aircraft could.

After a tense engagement, the lights suddenly disappeared from radar, and Patterson returned to base low on fuel. The Air Force later sent investigators to assess the situation, confirming that operators tracked solid metal objects.

Official statements

“I have no idea what the Air Force is doing. In all probability it’s doing nothing,” said Capt. Edward Ruppelt, director of Project Blue Book, when asked about the sightings.
Operators insisted they were tracking unidentifiable, solid metal objects, contrary to the claims of weather-related phenomena.

Context

The July 1952 incidents occurred during a time of heightened public anxiety in the U.S., marked by the Red Scare and the Korean War. This period also saw a record number of UFO sightings, contributing to a national atmosphere of fear regarding aerial threats.

Prior to this, the Air Force had established Project Blue Book to investigate UFO sightings, but responses to such incidents often downplayed the seriousness of the reports, attributing them to natural phenomena or misidentifications.

What’s next

As investigations into the 1952 sightings continue, the Air Force faces renewed pressure to address modern-day reports of unexplained aerial phenomena. The increase in pilot accounts suggests that the mystery surrounding UFOs may not be resolved anytime soon.
