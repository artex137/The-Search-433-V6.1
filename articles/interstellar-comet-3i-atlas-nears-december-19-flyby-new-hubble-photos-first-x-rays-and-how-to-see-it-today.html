Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS Nears December 19 Flyby

On December 9, 2025, interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS is approximately 278 million km from Earth and moving at about 60 km/s, just 10 days away from its closest approach on December 19, 2025. No injuries or fatalities have been reported related to the comet.

This flyby is significant as 3I/ATLAS is one of the most studied comets in history. New images and data from various space agencies, including NASA and the European Space Agency (ESA), have provided insights into its composition and behavior. The International Asteroid Warning Network (IAWN) is using this opportunity to practice tracking active comets.

Key facts

  • Distance from Earth: 278 million km (173 million miles)
  • Speed: Approximately 60 km/s (over 215,000 km/h)
  • Magnitude: Around 10.9–11.5, too faint for naked-eye visibility
  • Closest approach: December 19, 2025, at about 1.8 astronomical units (AU)
  • First X-ray image captured by XRISM satellite from November 26–28, 2025
  • Comet discovered on July 1, 2025, by the ATLAS survey telescope in Río Hurtado, Chile

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first discovered on July 1, 2025. It has been observed using various instruments, including:

  • NASA's Hubble Space Telescope on November 30, 2025
  • ESA's JUICE spacecraft in early November 2025
  • Amateur telescopes in Namibia on December 2, 2025

On December 9, 2025, the comet is reported to be highly active, with jets feeding a large halo of gas and dust. The XRISM satellite's Xtend telescope captured the first X-ray image of an interstellar object during its observations.

Official statements

“This is a pioneering detection and a powerful new way to probe how interstellar ices behave under solar irradiation,” said astronomer Avi Loeb.

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third confirmed interstellar object observed in our solar system, following 1I/ʻOumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019. It follows a hyperbolic trajectory, indicating that it is not bound to the Sun and will not return.

What’s next

The IAWN's worldwide astrometry campaign for 3I/ATLAS will continue until January 27, 2026. This exercise aims to refine tracking methods that are critical for planetary defense against potential future threats.
