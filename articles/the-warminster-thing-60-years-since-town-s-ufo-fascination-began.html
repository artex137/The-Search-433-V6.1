The Warminster Thing: 60 years since town's UFO fascination began

Warminster has marked the 60th anniversary of its UFO phenomenon, known as "The Warminster Thing," with the unveiling of a large mural featuring aliens and a flying saucer. The mural, created by local artist Paul Boswell, was funded by the community in just five days, raising £1,500. The event took place in a car park in Warminster, with locals reflecting on decades of reported UFO sightings.

This anniversary is significant for the community, as it highlights the ongoing interest in unexplained phenomena that began in 1965. Local authorities and groups, including the Heart of Warminster social media group, have engaged residents to celebrate and maintain the mystery surrounding these sightings. The mural serves as a reminder of the cultural impact of these events on the town.

Key facts

  • Year of Origin: 1965
  • Mural Funding: £1,500 raised in five days
  • Artist: Paul Boswell
  • Key Locations: Cley Hill, Cradle Hill, Middle Hill
  • Community Group: Heart of Warminster
  • Mayor: Andrew Cooper

Timeline

In 1965, media reports began to circulate about unusual occurrences in Warminster, leading to a series of UFO sightings known as "The Warminster Thing." Over the years, local interest has persisted, resulting in multiple events and discussions surrounding the phenomena.

In 2023, as part of the anniversary celebrations, a mural was unveiled. The community rallied to fund the artwork, reflecting the ongoing fascination with UFOs. Local residents, including those who have lived in Warminster for decades, continue to share their experiences and sightings.

“Whatever you do or don't think about the folklore behind it, things like this bind a community,” said Warminster Mayor Andrew Cooper during the mural's unveiling.

Official statements

“It used to be a thing. We’d go up there and sleep out there all night expecting to be taken away by aliens,” said artist Paul Boswell, recalling his teenage years spent UFO spotting.
“I think it's a little bit like magic. If you really knew what was going on, it would take the interest out of it,” said Lesley Blain, a member of the Heart of Warminster group.
“What's vital is to record for posterity what people have seen. They exist, what they are and where they come from is another matter,” said former police officer John Hanson.

Context

The Warminster Thing began in the mid-1960s, when local residents reported various strange occurrences and UFO sightings. Over the years, this phenomenon has attracted attention from both local and national media, including the BBC. The Warminster Journal has documented many of these events, and individuals like Arthur Shuttlewood have contributed significantly to the narrative through interviews and publications.

Military activity in the area has often been speculated to influence reported sightings, with some locals attributing unusual lights and sounds to nearby exercises. Despite this, the mystery of "The Thing" has persisted, drawing interest from both skeptics and believers alike.

What’s next

The community plans to continue celebrating the legacy of "The Warminster Thing" with more events and discussions. Local groups, including Heart of Warminster, are expected to keep sharing stories and collecting reports of sightings as they arise.
