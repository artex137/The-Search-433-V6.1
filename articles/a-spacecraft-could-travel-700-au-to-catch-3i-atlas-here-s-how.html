A Spacecraft Could Travel 700 AU to Catch 3I/ATLAS: Here’s How

A new mission to intercept the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS has been proposed by space researchers, aiming to redefine space exploration through a risky maneuver close to the Sun. The mission could enable a spacecraft to travel over 700 astronomical units from the Sun, employing gravity-assist techniques to achieve unprecedented speeds. This ambitious plan could allow for a flyby of the comet by 2085.

The mission is significant as it represents a major advancement in the exploration of interstellar objects. Authorities are currently reviewing the technical aspects of the mission, which utilizes an advanced technique called the Oberth effect to gain velocity as the spacecraft approaches the Sun. Researchers believe that successfully intercepting 3I/ATLAS could pave the way for future missions to explore other interstellar objects.

Key facts

  • Mission aims to intercept comet 3I/ATLAS, currently moving away from the Sun at over 61 km/s.
  • Spacecraft could travel over 700 AU from the Sun using gravity assists.
  • Proposed solar Oberth maneuver requires the spacecraft to approach within 0.015 AU of the Sun.
  • The spacecraft's heat shield must withstand temperatures exceeding 1,370°C.
  • Arrival at the comet could occur as early as 30 years or as late as 50 years after launch, depending on velocity gains.

Timeline

The proposed mission involves several key steps:

  • The spacecraft will first travel to Jupiter to utilize its gravity for slowing down.
  • After the gravity assist, the spacecraft will approach the Sun for the Oberth maneuver.
  • Once the maneuver is completed, the spacecraft will embark on a long journey to reach 3I/ATLAS.

Official statements

“Pretty much every launch uses the Oberth effect,” said T. Marshall Eubanks, a former NASA scientist and one of the authors of the mission paper. “However, I cannot find a record of a straight-out Oberth maneuver of the type we propose, which is a major rocket burn at closest approach in a flyby.”
“For future interstellar objects, a solar Oberth maneuver should be avoided if possible,” said Adam Hibberd, a leading researcher in the field.
“I feel quite confident that when we develop the ability to reach these interstellar objects, there will be a strong desire to directly explore at least some of them,” Eubanks added.

Context

The exploration of interstellar objects has garnered significant attention in recent years, with scientists eager to learn more about these celestial bodies. Previous missions have utilized gravity assists, but the proposed solar Oberth maneuver represents a bold new application of this technique. As technology advances, missions to interstellar objects may become more common and efficient.

What’s next

Researchers plan to continue refining the mission’s technical details and will seek funding and support for the project. The success of this mission could lead to further exploration of other interstellar objects in the future.
