Astronomers confirm the presence of water on interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS

Astronomers at Auburn University have confirmed the presence of water on interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, which passed through our solar system this summer. The detection was made using NASA’s Neil Gehrels Swift Observatory, revealing that the comet releases approximately 1.36 × 10²⁷ water molecules per second, equivalent to about 40 kilograms per second.

This discovery is significant as it challenges previous understandings of comet behavior. The findings suggest that 3I/ATLAS is capable of releasing water even at a distance nearly three times farther from the Sun than Earth, a region typically too cold for comets to lose water. The data has prompted ongoing observations to monitor the comet's activity as it approaches the Sun again in mid-November.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS is the third known interstellar object.
  • Water emission rate is approximately 1.36 × 10²⁷ molecules per second.
  • Comet observed twice in summer 2025: late July and mid-August.
  • Hydroxyl signal nearly doubled between observations.
  • Surface area actively emitting vapor is over 8% of the comet's total surface.
  • 3I/ATLAS will return for observation after mid-November 2025.

Timeline

In late July 2025, astronomers used the Swift Observatory to observe 3I/ATLAS and detected faint emissions indicative of water release. This marked the first confirmation of water on this interstellar comet. By mid-August 2025, a follow-up observation showed that the hydroxyl signal had nearly doubled, indicating a significant increase in water production as the comet approached the Sun.

The observations utilized a technique called motion correction to capture clear images of the comet despite its movement. Scientists noted that the comet's unusual water activity occurred in a region typically too cold for such emissions.

Official statements

“When we see water—or even its faint ultraviolet echo—coming from an interstellar comet, we’re reading a message from outside our planetary system,” said Dennis Bodewits, Auburn physics professor and co-author of the study.
“Each interstellar comet that has come by so far has been a surprise,” stated Zexi Xing, the study's lead astrophysicist. “Each one is rewriting what we thought we knew about the formation of planets and comets around stars.”

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third interstellar comet observed, following 1I/’Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov. Unlike its predecessors, 3I/ATLAS shows significant water activity at unprecedented distances from the Sun, suggesting that comets formed in different stellar environments may exhibit diverse chemical properties. Previous comets have shown varying signs of gas activity, with 1I/’Oumuamua being dry and 2I/Borisov releasing both water and carbon monoxide.

What’s next

As 3I/ATLAS returns for observation after mid-November, astronomers will continue to study its water activity and other gases. Future observations may reveal whether the comet's emissions change as it approaches the Sun, furthering our understanding of interstellar bodies and the potential for life-sustaining materials in the universe.
