December 2025 skywatching highlights: 3I/ATLAS set for closest approach, meteors, and celestial buddies

As December 2025 unfolds, stargazers can look forward to a series of spectacular cosmic events, including the closest approach of interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS on December 19. NASA has announced that this comet, the third confirmed interstellar object detected in our solar system, will come within 170 million miles (274 million km) of Earth, providing a unique opportunity for observation.

This month’s celestial events matter because they allow both amateur and professional astronomers to engage with the universe. NASA has mobilized a fleet of spacecraft and ground-based telescopes to study 3I/ATLAS, aiming to gather data on its composition and origins. The agency has also provided practical viewing tips to enhance the experience for observers.

Key facts

  • December 7: Moon and Jupiter conjunction visible in the eastern sky.
  • December 13-14: Peak of the Geminid meteor shower.
  • December 19: Closest approach of comet 3I/ATLAS to Earth.
  • Distance: 3I/ATLAS will be 170 million miles (274 million km) from Earth.
  • Visibility: Best viewed in the early pre-dawn hours from now through late December.
  • Location: Look east-northeast low on the horizon, just below Regulus.
  • Equipment: A telescope with at least a 30-cm (12-inch) aperture is recommended for viewing 3I/ATLAS.

Timeline

December 1, 2025: The New Moon marks the beginning of optimal stargazing conditions for the month.

December 7, 2025: A conjunction between the crescent Moon and Jupiter occurs, visible in the eastern sky.

December 13-14, 2025: The Geminid meteor shower peaks, providing a stunning display for observers.

December 19, 2025: Comet 3I/ATLAS makes its closest approach to Earth, allowing for detailed observation.

Official statements

“This could be your best bet to see this interstellar interloper,” said a NASA representative, emphasizing the comet's fleeting visibility.

Context

3I/ATLAS is notable as the third interstellar object detected in our solar system, following 'Oumuamua in 2017 and Comet 2I/Borisov in 2019. Its discovery by NASA's ATLAS survey highlights the growing interest in studying objects from beyond our solar system.

What’s next

As the month progresses, NASA will continue to study 3I/ATLAS, aiming to gather valuable data on extrasolar chemistry and the formation of interstellar objects. Observers are encouraged to join local astronomy clubs for guided sessions to enhance their viewing experience.
