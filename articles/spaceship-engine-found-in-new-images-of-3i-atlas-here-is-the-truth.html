'Spaceship Engine' Found in New Images of 3I/ATLAS? - Here Is the Truth

A recent post on X has sparked speculation about Comet 3I/ATLAS, claiming that it may resemble a spaceship engine based on new X-ray images. The images were released between December 6 and 8, 2025, by the XRISM telescope, which is operated by the Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency.

This claim matters because it raises questions about the nature of 3I/ATLAS and the scientific understanding of cometary behavior. Authorities in the scientific community are currently analyzing the XRISM data to assess its validity and implications.

Key facts

  • X-ray images of Comet 3I/ATLAS were released by XRISM between December 6 and 8, 2025.
  • The images reportedly show a glow extending approximately 400,000 kilometres around the comet's nucleus.
  • NASA and ESA have confirmed that 3I/ATLAS behaves like a comet.
  • XRISM's findings may be influenced by instrumental effects, such as detector noise.
  • The scientific consensus is that 3I/ATLAS is a natural interstellar comet.

Timeline

On December 6, 2025, the XRISM telescope conducted a 'Target of Opportunity' observation of Comet 3I/ATLAS, capturing images that sparked viral theories about the comet’s nature. The images displayed a faint X-ray glow around the comet, leading to speculation about alien technology.

However, scientists quickly pointed out that the observed glow might be due to instrumental effects rather than a true representation of the comet's characteristics.

Following the release of the images, NASA and ESA reinforced the idea that 3I/ATLAS is consistent with known comet behavior, stating that they found no evidence suggesting it is anything other than a comet.

Official statements

“We certainly haven't seen any technosignatures or anything from it that would lead us to believe it was anything other than a comet.”
- NASA and ESA officials

Context

Since the discovery of cometary X-rays in 1996, scientists have understood that these emissions arise from the interaction of solar wind with the comet's neutral atoms and molecules. This process creates a diffuse glow rather than a focused engine-like plume.

Previous comets, such as Comet Hyakutake, exhibited similar X-ray behaviors, further supporting the notion that 3I/ATLAS aligns with established cometary characteristics.

What’s next

Scientists will continue to analyze the XRISM data to determine the nature of the X-ray emissions. The results will inform ongoing research into cometary behavior and may address public curiosity regarding potential extraterrestrial phenomena.
