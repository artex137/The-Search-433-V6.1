Not Aliens — But a Comet From Another Star Is Here

This week, an interstellar comet known as 3I/ATLAS is making its closest approach to Earth, reaching its peak visibility in the early morning hours. The comet, which scientists have confirmed formed around another star, is approximately 170 million miles away, posing no danger to the planet.

3I/ATLAS is significant because it is only the third interstellar object ever discovered passing through our solar system. NASA and astronomers worldwide have been tracking the comet for months to study its speed and trajectory. The comet travels at about 220,000 kilometers per hour and is on a one-way path, meaning it will eventually leave the solar system forever.

Key facts

  • Comet name: 3I/ATLAS
  • Distance from Earth: 170 million miles
  • Speed: 220,000 kilometers per hour
  • Peak visibility: 3:30 a.m. CST
  • Viewing location: Near the constellation Leo, below the star Regulus

Timeline

NASA and astronomers have tracked 3I/ATLAS for several months, studying its trajectory and speed.

Currently, the comet is approaching its closest point to Earth, providing skywatchers in Dallas–Fort Worth with a rare opportunity to observe it.

After its closest approach, 3I/ATLAS will continue on its trajectory, leaving the solar system for good.

Official statements

“This is not an alien spacecraft,” scientists have confirmed, addressing online rumors about the comet.

Context

3I/ATLAS is only the third interstellar object identified passing through our solar system, highlighting the rarity of such events. The comet has likely been traveling through space for billions of years prior to its visit.

What’s next

Skywatchers are encouraged to use binoculars or small telescopes to view the comet, with livestreams available from observatories for those unable to observe it in person.
