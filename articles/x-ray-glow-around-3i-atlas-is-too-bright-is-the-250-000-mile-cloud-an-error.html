X-Ray Glow Around 3I/ATLAS Is Too Bright: Is The 250,000-Mile Cloud An Error?

Scientists have discovered a massive X-ray cloud surrounding interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, which extends approximately 400,000 kilometres (about 250,000 miles) around its nucleus. The findings were made between November 26 and November 28, 2023, by the X-Ray Imaging and Spectroscopy Mission (XRISM), a project led by Japan's JAXA with contributions from NASA and ESA.

This discovery is significant because it challenges existing assumptions about cometary behaviour and formation. The unusual size and characteristics of the X-ray cloud may provide insights into the nature of interstellar objects and their interactions with solar winds. Researchers are now working to understand the implications of these findings.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS has an X-ray cloud measuring 400,000 kilometres (250,000 miles).
  • The discovery was made by XRISM between November 26 and November 28, 2023.
  • 3I/ATLAS is currently moving away from the Sun at over 210,000 kilometres per hour.
  • The comet was discovered on July 1, 2025, by the ATLAS telescope in Chile.
  • This is the third confirmed interstellar object, following 1I/'Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov.

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first identified on July 1, 2025, and has since been classified as a comet due to its active gas and dust emissions. Between November 26 and November 28, 2023, the XRISM mission detected an unprecedented X-ray glow surrounding the comet, prompting further investigation into its properties and implications.

The comet is now observable in the pre-dawn sky and is on a hyperbolic trajectory out of the solar system, necessitating quick data collection by scientists before it becomes unobservable.

Official statements

“The sheer size and structure of this ethereal, glowing halo are challenging every assumption astronomers have made about these cosmic wanderers,” said a representative from the XRISM team.
“Understanding the interaction between the comet's 'alien' gas and our local solar wind is vital,” they added.

Context

The detection of X-ray emissions from comets has been documented since 1996, with Comet Hyakutake being one of the earliest examples. However, 3I/ATLAS is unique as it originates from a distant star system, presenting a different chemical composition and interaction with solar winds than previously observed comets.

This observation offers a rare opportunity to study the formation of icy worlds beyond our solar neighbourhood, shedding light on the conditions in which these objects form.

What’s next

Researchers are currently processing the data obtained from XRISM to confirm the origins of the X-ray structure. They aim to distinguish the comet's actual behaviour from any potential instrument effects before 3I/ATLAS becomes too distant to study.
