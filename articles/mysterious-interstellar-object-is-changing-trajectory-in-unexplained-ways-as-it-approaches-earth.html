Mysterious interstellar object is changing trajectory in unexplained ways as it approaches Earth

The interstellar object known as 3I/ATLAS has exhibited unusual changes in shape and color as it approaches Earth, with the latest observations taken by Austrian astronomers on September 7 revealing a shift from a reddish glow to a green hue. No injuries or fatalities have been reported.

This phenomenon is significant as it challenges existing comet models and raises questions about the nature of 3I/ATLAS. The ATLAS telescope team has documented these changes, prompting scientists to investigate the object's composition and behavior further as it nears its closest approach to Earth on December 19, 2025.

Key facts

  • Object: 3I/ATLAS
  • Latest observation date: September 7, 2023
  • Closest approach to Earth: December 19, 2025
  • Distance at closest approach: 168 million miles (approximately 270 million kilometers)
  • Cyanide emission rate: 20 grams per second
  • Nickel emission rate: 5 grams per second

Timeline

On August 25, 2023, the Very Large Telescope detected that 3I/ATLAS was emitting cyanide at a rate of 20 grams per second. Following this, the Austrian astronomers captured images of the comet during a total lunar eclipse on September 7, revealing a blue-green coma and a tail.

On August 28, the team observed the tail becoming brighter, indicating the onset of comet activity. By early September, the object had reached a point where gas-rich comets typically become active.

Official statements

“During the total lunar eclipse, we captured a detailed image of Comet 3I/ATLAS from the dark skies of Namibia,” said astronomer Michael Jäger. “The most detailed images taken during the total lunar eclipse on September 7 already show a blue-green coma and a tail.”

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third known interstellar object, following 1I/ʻOumuamua and 2I/Borisov. Unlike its predecessors, 3I/ATLAS displays unique characteristics such as an anti-tail, extreme color changes, and a significant gas-rich coma.

The recent findings have prompted discussions about the potential technological origins of 3I/ATLAS, as suggested by Harvard physicist Avi Loeb. He noted the unusual nickel emissions, which differ from typical comet behavior.

What’s next

Scientists will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS as it approaches the Sun and Earth. Further studies are expected to explore its emissions and physical properties, which may provide additional insights into its nature.
