James Webb reveals unique composition of comet 3I/Atlas with high CO2 content

The James Webb Space Telescope has identified a unique chemical composition in the interstellar comet 3I/Atlas, located beyond the orbit of Mars, as of November 2025. The comet has a carbon dioxide to water ratio of 7.6, which is 4.5 times higher than that found in solar system comets. No injuries or fatalities have been reported related to this discovery.

This finding is significant as it enhances the understanding of cosmic chemistry and the long-term effects of cosmic rays on celestial bodies. Researchers have employed the NIRSpec instrument to analyze the comet's coma, with additional observations from NASA's SPHEREx mission. The results provide insight into the chemical transformations that have occurred over billions of years.

Key facts

  • CO2/H2O ratio: 7.6 ± 0.3
  • CO/H2O ratio: 1.65
  • Affected depth: up to 20 meters in the outer layer
  • Current position: Beyond the orbit of Mars
  • Minimum distance from Earth: 270 million kilometers on December 19, 2025
  • Next perihelion: March 2026, crossing the orbits of Jupiter

Timeline

Comet 3I/Atlas recently passed perihelion. Observatories in Arizona captured initial images in late October 2025. The comet's trajectory will cross the orbits of Jupiter in March 2026, coinciding with the Juice mission's observation window.

Researchers have confirmed the transformation of carbon monoxide into carbon dioxide due to prolonged exposure to galactic cosmic rays. Laboratory experiments have replicated these chemical changes, validating the findings from the James Webb telescope.

Official statements

“The outer crust of the comet forms a layer rich in CO2 and refractory material, while the interior remains preserved as a primitive core,” said a spokesperson from the research team.

Context

Comet 3I/Atlas differs significantly from other interstellar objects, such as 2I/Borisov, which has CO2 levels closer to solar comets, and 1I/'Oumuamua, which exhibited no visible coma. The study of these three visitors provides a comparative analysis of gas production rates and chemical compositions.

What’s next

Future observations with the James Webb Space Telescope are scheduled until March 2026. The MIRI instrument will analyze mid-infrared data, while the Vera Rubin Observatory will contribute optical data. The Juice mission will monitor the comet as it approaches Jupiter, aiming to measure variations in the rate of sublimation.
