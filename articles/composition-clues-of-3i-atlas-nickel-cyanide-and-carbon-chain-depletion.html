Composition clues of 3I/ATLAS: Nickel, Cyanide and Carbon-chain depletion

The detection of cyanide radicals around comet 3I/ATLAS has confirmed that its nucleus contains typical cometary volatiles, similar to those found in Solar System comets. The findings, reported recently, indicate that 3I/ATLAS formed in a cold outer region of its home star system, where nitrogen-rich ices can condense and survive for billions of years.

This research matters because it offers insights into the early chemical conditions of the system from which 3I/ATLAS originated. The presence of nickel-bearing species and the depletion of carbon-chain molecules suggest that the comet has experienced significant cosmic-ray processing over long periods, impacting its chemical makeup.

Key facts

  • Comet: 3I/ATLAS
  • Key findings: Detection of cyanide radicals and nickel-bearing species
  • Carbon-chain depletion observed, particularly in C₂ and C₃ molecules
  • Similarities noted with interstellar comet 2I/Borisov
  • Indicates formation in a metal-rich protoplanetary disk

Timeline

Recent spectroscopic observations have detected cyanide radicals around 3I/ATLAS. These findings confirm the presence of typical cometary volatiles similar to those in Solar System comets. The observations also reveal nickel-bearing species in the comet's coma, mirroring earlier findings in 2I/Borisov.

The research indicates that 3I/ATLAS likely formed in a cold outer region of its home star system, where nitrogen-rich ices can survive for billions of years. The depletion of carbon-chain molecules suggests that the comet has undergone significant cosmic-ray exposure.

Official statements

“The low abundance of C₂ and C₃ in 3I/ATLAS is consistent with extreme cosmic-ray processing, indicating the object has spent enormous spans of time in interstellar space, far from any protective star,” researchers stated.

Context

Prior research on interstellar comets, such as 2I/Borisov, has shown similar chemical signatures. The study of 3I/ATLAS further supports the understanding that comets can have distinct chemical compositions based on their formation environments.

The differences in composition between 3I/ATLAS and known Solar System comets strengthen the evidence that 3I/ATLAS is not a captured Oort Cloud object, but rather a genuine interstellar visitor.

What’s next

Further investigations are likely to focus on the chemical processes that have shaped 3I/ATLAS, as well as the implications for understanding the formation of comets in different stellar environments.
