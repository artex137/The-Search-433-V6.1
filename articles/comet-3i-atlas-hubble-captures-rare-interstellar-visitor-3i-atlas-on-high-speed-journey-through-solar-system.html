Comet 3I ATLAS: Hubble Captures Rare Interstellar Visitor

NASA’s Hubble Space Telescope has captured new images of the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS as it travels through the inner solar system. The observations occurred on November 30, 2025, when the comet was approximately 178 million miles from Earth. There are no injuries or fatalities reported.

The observations are significant for astronomers studying the composition and behaviour of this rare cosmic object. Hubble’s high-resolution images reveal details about the comet’s dust cocoon and its activity as it moves on a hyperbolic trajectory that will eventually eject it from the solar system. NASA has coordinated a global observation campaign involving multiple spacecraft to enhance understanding of the comet.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS is approximately 178 million miles from Earth.
  • Hubble captured images on November 30, 2025.
  • The comet reached its perihelion in late October 2025.
  • It is traveling at speeds exceeding 137,000 miles per hour.
  • 3I/ATLAS is the third confirmed interstellar object detected by humanity.
  • The observation campaign includes the James Webb Space Telescope, the Psyche mission, and STEREO spacecraft.

Timeline

On November 30, 2025, NASA’s Hubble Space Telescope obtained images of 3I/ATLAS, providing insights into its structure and behaviour. The comet reached its closest point to the Sun in late October 2025, moving at high speeds through the inner solar system. The observation campaign, which involves multiple spacecraft, aims to study the comet from various angles.

Official statements

“Each vantage point reveals unique details about the comet’s coma, tail, and nucleus,” NASA officials noted, emphasizing the multi-mission strategy's importance.

Context

3I/ATLAS follows the earlier discoveries of 'Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov, marking it as the third confirmed interstellar object detected. Scientific analysis has confirmed that 3I/ATLAS is a natural object, dispelling earlier speculation about its origins.

What’s next

The data collected during the observation of 3I/ATLAS will help astronomers understand the nature of objects forming in other planetary systems as the comet continues its journey into deep space.
