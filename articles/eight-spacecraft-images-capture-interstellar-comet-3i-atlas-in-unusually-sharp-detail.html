Eight Spacecraft Images Capture Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS in Unusually Sharp Detail

Eight spacecraft have captured detailed images of interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS as it travels through our solar system. The imaging campaign involved multiple missions and was conducted over the past month, providing unique insights into this rare celestial object.

This collaboration marks a significant achievement in space observation, as scientists aim to study the composition and behavior of 3I/ATLAS, which is not bound to the Sun like typical comets. The findings could enhance understanding of other planetary systems and inform future space missions. The comet is only the third confirmed interstellar object ever detected in our solar system, following ‘Oumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019.

Key facts

  • Comet Name: 3I/ATLAS
  • Confirmed Interstellar Objects: 3 (including ‘Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov)
  • Participating Spacecraft: SOHO, Parker Solar Probe, Juno, MAVEN, BepiColombo, Solar Orbiter, Venus Express, Akatsuki
  • Notable Findings: Unusual stability of the comet tail and lack of typical outgassing activity
  • Data Collection Period: Past month
  • Agencies Involved: NASA, ESA, JAXA

Timeline

The imaging campaign began in early September 2023, as the eight spacecraft coordinated their observations to capture 3I/ATLAS from various angles. The mission teams aligned observational windows and calibrated imaging sensors for optimal data collection. By the end of September, initial findings were analyzed, revealing the comet's unique characteristics.

Official statements

“The chemical signature is unlike what we see from native solar system comets,” said Dr. Elena Boucher, an astrophysicist at Johns Hopkins University Applied Physics Lab. “This visitor offers a rare opportunity to peer into matter formed around another star.”
“The spatial distribution of observational platforms essentially allowed us to scan the object like an interstellar CT machine,” said Prof. Joon Kim, Director of Planetary Imaging Systems at ESA.
“Every interstellar visitor is a time capsule—not just of its origin system, but of cosmic history itself,” stated Dr. Vanya Das, Solar System Dynamics Specialist at Caltech.

Context

Prior to 3I/ATLAS, only two interstellar objects have been confirmed: ‘Oumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019. The study of such objects is crucial for understanding the formation and evolution of planetary systems beyond our own. The unique characteristics of 3I/ATLAS, such as its stable coma, distinguish it from typical comets that exhibit prominent outgassing and tails.

What’s next

Researchers plan to continue analyzing the data from 3I/ATLAS to better understand its composition and origin. Discussions are underway regarding future missions, including NASA’s Comet Interceptor, which aims to intercept and study interstellar objects. The findings from this campaign will inform the design of trajectory systems and instrument arrays for upcoming missions.
