3I/ATLAS: SETI’s 5-Hour Long Scan Did Not Detect Alien Radio Signal; Avi Loeb Questions The Methodology

Harvard astrophysicist Avi Loeb has commented on the Search for Extraterrestrial Intelligence (SETI) Breakthrough Listen Project's failure to detect an anomalous radio signal from comet 3I/ATLAS during a five-hour scan on December 18, 2025. The project utilized the 100 m Robert C. Byrd Green Bank Telescope, scanning frequencies between 1-12 GHz. No signals were identified, leading to further examination of the methodologies used.

This development raises questions about the search for extraterrestrial life. Loeb, in a recent interview, stated that while the absence of a signal may suggest 3I/ATLAS is a natural object, it does not rule out the existence of intelligent life elsewhere in the universe. He emphasized the need for humility in the search for technosignatures.

Key facts

  • Date of closest approach of 3I/ATLAS: December 19, 2025
  • Date of SETI scan: December 18, 2025
  • Location of scan: 100 m Robert C. Byrd Green Bank Telescope
  • Frequency range scanned: 1-12 GHz
  • Signal detection limit: down to the 100 mW level
  • 3I/ATLAS is the third recorded interstellar object, following 1I/Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov

Timeline

On December 18, 2025, the Breakthrough Listen Project conducted a five-hour scan of comet 3I/ATLAS, seeking potential signs of extraterrestrial technology. The scan produced no detectable radio signals. The following day, December 19, 2025, 3I/ATLAS made its closest approach to Earth.

Official statements

“No-one would be more excited than us if 3I/ATLAS were a spaceship. But we've been looking at it since shortly after it was discovered, and there's no sign it's anything other than a natural (albeit interesting) object.”
— Avi Loeb
“It's not clear that there would be a transmission in our direction. It may be beamed in a completely different direction.”
— Avi Loeb

Context

3I/ATLAS follows two previous interstellar objects, 1I/Oumuamua, discovered in 2017, and 2I/Borisov, discovered in 2019. The ongoing search for extraterrestrial life has gained momentum in recent years, with various projects aiming to detect technosignatures from distant civilizations.

What’s next

Further analysis of the data gathered from the scan is expected. Researchers continue to explore other methods and technologies to enhance the search for signs of extraterrestrial intelligence in the universe.
