Harvard’s Avi Loeb: Interstellar Object 3I/ATLAS May Be Alien Tech

Harvard astrophysicist Avi Loeb has suggested that the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS, first observed on July 1, 2025, could be generating its own light, indicating potential artificial origins. The object was spotted as it travels through our solar system, sparking significant interest among astronomers and researchers worldwide.

This matter raises important questions about the nature of the object, its origins, and what it could mean for future space exploration. Authorities and researchers are closely monitoring 3I/ATLAS as it approaches its closest point to Earth in October 2025.

Key facts

  • Object designation: 3I/ATLAS
  • First observed: July 1, 2025
  • High-resolution image captured: July 21, 2025
  • Estimated size: up to 1 kilometer across
  • Speed: over 100,000 miles per hour
  • Closest approach to Earth: October 2025, near Mars

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first detected on July 1, 2025. A high-resolution image taken by the Hubble Space Telescope on July 21 revealed a fuzzy glow, resembling a comet’s coma. This glow has raised questions about the object’s composition and behavior.

Loeb has proposed that the glow may not be from natural sources, suggesting the possibility of artificial propulsion. Speculative discussions surrounding the object have gained traction on social media, with some attributing a "Loeb Scale" rating to its potential technological origins.

Official statements

“If the glow isn’t from a natural coma, it might stem from nuclear reactions or even artificial lighting, akin to a spacecraft’s headlights,” stated Loeb in his Medium article.

Context

The discussion surrounding 3I/ATLAS reflects a growing fascination with potential extraterrestrial phenomena. Previous interstellar visitors have prompted similar debates, but the current object’s unique characteristics have intensified scrutiny. Scientists are divided on its classification, with some attributing its glow to natural processes.

What’s next

As 3I/ATLAS approaches Earth, NASA’s Hubble and upcoming James Webb Space Telescope observations are expected to provide more data. Researchers are exploring rapid-response probes to study the object, emphasizing the need for international collaboration in the investigation.
