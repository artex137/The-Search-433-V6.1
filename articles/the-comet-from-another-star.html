The Comet From Another Star

Comet 3I/ATLAS, the third interstellar object ever detected, has passed through our Solar System, captivating astronomers worldwide after its discovery last summer. The comet, which travels at over 240,000 km/h, was first spotted by the ATLAS survey telescope in Chile.

This discovery is significant because it provides a unique opportunity to study an object that originated outside our Solar System. Scientists have observed the comet using various telescopes and instruments, gathering data that could yield insights into the formation of planetary systems.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS was discovered in June 2025.
  • It was traveling at over 240,000 km/h.
  • First observed by the ATLAS survey telescope in Chile.
  • NASA's Parker Solar Probe and ESA's JUICE spacecraft have observed the comet.
  • JUICE captured over 120 photographs of the comet from 66 million km away.

Timeline

In June 2025, 3I/ATLAS was discovered by the ATLAS survey telescope. Following its detection, astronomers from around the world began a series of observations. Notably, the Hubble Space Telescope captured images of the comet, while NASA's Parker Solar Probe monitored its trajectory as it approached the Sun.

On July 19, 2025, ESA's JUICE spacecraft took over 120 photographs of the comet, revealing its bright glow and long tail from a distance of 66 million kilometres.

Official statements

“This comet is behaving exactly like a perfectly ordinary comet, despite its extraordinary origins,” said a leading researcher involved in the study.
“In a very real sense, analysing this comet means reading a message from another planetary system, sent billions of years ago,” the researcher added.

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third interstellar object detected, following 1I/2017 U1 and 2I/Borisov. The study of such objects offers insights into the materials and conditions that existed in the early universe, enhancing our understanding of planetary formation.

The findings from 3I/ATLAS could reshape our knowledge of cometary science and interstellar materials, as they reveal similarities in comet formation across different systems.

What’s next

The full scientific results from the JUICE observations are still being processed. Instrument teams are expected to meet in late March to discuss their findings and further analyse the data collected from 3I/ATLAS.
