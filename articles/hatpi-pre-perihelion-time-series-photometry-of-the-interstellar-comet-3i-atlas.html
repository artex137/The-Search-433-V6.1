HATPI Pre-Perihelion Time-series Photometry of the Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS

HATPI, a new time-domain facility at Las Campanas Observatory in Chile, has successfully conducted time-series photometry of the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, first recovering it on July 2, 2025, just one day after its discovery. The comet displayed significant brightness changes, reaching a magnitude of G=14.071±0.073 by September 13, 2025, before becoming unobservable as it approached perihelion.

This research is significant as it provides detailed observations of 3I/ATLAS, which may enhance understanding of interstellar objects and their behavior as they approach the sun. The findings have been accepted for publication in the Astronomical Journal.

Key facts

  • Facility: HATPI at Las Campanas Observatory, Chile.
  • Comet 3I/ATLAS first observed: July 2, 2025.
  • Brightness at recovery: G=17.796±0.082 mag.
  • Maximum brightness observed: G=14.071±0.073 mag on September 13, 2025.
  • Last observable date: Not disclosed.
  • Brightness on August 6, 2025: G=16.396±0.029 mag.
  • Observation method: 45-second exposures; mosaic field-of-view of 7,100 square arcdegrees.

Timeline

On July 2, 2025, HATPI first recovered the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS after its discovery. Over the following weeks, the comet's brightness increased steadily.

By August 6, 2025, HATPI recorded a brightness of G=16.396 mag. The comet continued to brighten, reaching G=14.071 mag by September 13, 2025, after which it became unobservable as it neared perihelion.

Official statements

“The HATPI magnitudes have been well-fit as a power law function of heliocentric distance, providing key insights into the comet's behavior,” said Joel D. Hartman, one of the researchers involved in the study.

Context

The study of interstellar comets like 3I/ATLAS is crucial as they provide insights into the formation and evolution of our solar system. Previous observations of such comets have been limited, making HATPI's findings particularly valuable in contributing to the existing body of literature.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to analyze the data collected by HATPI to further understand the characteristics and behavior of 3I/ATLAS and other interstellar objects in the future.
