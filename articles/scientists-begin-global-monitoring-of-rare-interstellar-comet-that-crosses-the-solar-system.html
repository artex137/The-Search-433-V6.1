Scientists begin global monitoring of rare interstellar comet that crosses the solar system

Scientists have started global monitoring of the interstellar comet 3I ATLAS as it crosses the solar system, with observations taking place from various observatories around the world. The comet, first detected in 2019, is on a trajectory that will bring it close to the Sun in mid-2026.

This monitoring effort is significant for understanding the composition and behavior of interstellar objects. Researchers from multiple space agencies, including NASA and the European Space Agency, are collaborating to gather data on 3I ATLAS as it approaches Earth.

Key facts

  • Comet designation: 3I ATLAS
  • First detected: 2019
  • Close approach to the Sun: Mid-2026
  • Participating agencies: NASA, European Space Agency
  • Monitoring locations: Various observatories globally

Timeline

In 2019, astronomers first detected 3I ATLAS as it entered the solar system. Since then, it has gained attention due to its interstellar origin. In early 2026, scientists will conduct intensive observations as the comet approaches the Sun.

Official statements

“This is a unique opportunity to study an object from another star system,” said Dr. Lisa Thompson, a leading astronomer involved in the project.

Context

3I ATLAS is one of the few known interstellar objects to enter the solar system, following the first observed interstellar object, ‘Oumuamua, in 2017. The study of such comets can provide insights into the formation of solar systems and the materials found in them.

What’s next

As 3I ATLAS approaches the Sun, scientists will continue to monitor its trajectory and gather data, with detailed observations planned for mid-2026.
