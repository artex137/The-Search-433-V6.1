3I/ATLAS to Reveal Itself to Earth on October 29

The interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS will make its closest approach to Earth on October 29, 2025, passing at a safe distance of approximately 167 million miles. Authorities have confirmed that the comet poses no threat to the planet.

This event is significant as it offers scientists a rare opportunity to study an interstellar object. Researchers are closely monitoring 3I/ATLAS to learn about its composition, trajectory, and potential implications for understanding other star systems.

Key facts

  • Closest Approach: October 29, 2025
  • Distance from Earth: 1.8 astronomical units (167 million miles)
  • Threat Level: Zero - no impact risk
  • Nucleus Size: Under 1 km
  • Current Brightness: Between magnitude 12 and 14
  • Observation Requirement: Telescope of at least 8 inches (200mm) aperture

Timeline

3I/ATLAS first appeared in July 2025. Since its discovery, astronomers have observed its unique characteristics, including a massive jet of gas and dust emitted toward the Sun. The comet is noted for its hyperbolic trajectory, indicating it is a one-time visitor from another star system.

Images from Spain’s Teide Observatory in August 2025 showed the comet's tail flaring as it approached the Sun, demonstrating typical comet behavior.

Official statements

“3I/ATLAS is a mystery interstellar object moving along a hyperbolic path toward the Sun,” said theoretical physicist Michio Kaku.
“Its unusual speed and shape continue to puzzle researchers,” stated astrophysicist Miquel Serra-Ricart.

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third confirmed interstellar object to enter our Solar System, following ʻOumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019. Unlike regular comets that orbit the Sun, 3I/ATLAS will only pass through once before exiting into deep space.

This brief appearance allows researchers to study comet formation in different regions of the galaxy, providing insights into interstellar chemistry and evolution.

What’s next

As 3I/ATLAS approaches the Sun, scientists will continue to observe its behavior and emissions. The comet is expected to reappear in December 2025 for further tracking and study.
