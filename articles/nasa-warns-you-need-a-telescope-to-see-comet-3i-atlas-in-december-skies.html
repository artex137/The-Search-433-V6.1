NASA Warns: You Need a Telescope to See Comet 3I/ATLAS in December Skies

The Comet 3I/ATLAS will make its closest approach to Earth on December 19, 2023, at a distance of approximately 170 million miles (over 700 times the distance between Earth and the moon). This high-speed interstellar comet is the third object from outside our solar system to be discovered within our solar system.

The rarity of Comet 3I/ATLAS makes it a significant target for scientific study. NASA is actively observing the comet using various spacecraft and telescopes to gather data on its composition and trajectory. The comet is expected to reach a magnitude of 11 to 15 during its closest approach, making it too dim to see with the naked eye and requiring a telescope with a minimum aperture of 30 centimetres for observation.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS will approach Earth on December 19, 2023.
  • It will be about 170 million miles away during its closest approach.
  • Observers can look for it in the pre-dawn hours between 02:00 and 07:00 local time.
  • The comet's nucleus is estimated to be between 320 metres and 5.6 kilometres in diameter.
  • It is only the third interstellar object confirmed to enter our solar system.

Timeline

Comet 3I/ATLAS has been observed as it travels through our solar system. NASA has utilized various telescopes, including the Hubble Space Telescope, to study its characteristics and trajectory.

The comet's closest approach is scheduled for December 19, 2023, which is considered the best opportunity for observation before it exits our solar system.

Official statements

“Comet 3I/ATLAS poses absolutely no threat to Earth,” NASA has stated, emphasizing its safe distance from our planet.

Context

Comet 3I/ATLAS is a unique celestial object, providing a rare opportunity for scientists to study material from outside our solar system. This discovery highlights the importance of ongoing observation of interstellar objects.

What’s next

NASA will continue to monitor Comet 3I/ATLAS until it exits the solar system, gathering as much data as possible to enhance our understanding of interstellar objects.
