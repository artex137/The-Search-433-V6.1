Comet 3I/Atlas reaches 57 km/s and confirms hyperbolic trajectory in the Solar System

Interstellar comet 3I/Atlas has recorded a speed of 57 km/s as it approaches the Solar System, confirming its hyperbolic trajectory. The comet, which is on a path that allows it to escape the Sun's gravitational pull, is the third confirmed interstellar object following 'Oumuamua and Borisov.

This discovery is significant as it provides further evidence of interstellar objects entering our Solar System. Astronomers have confirmed the comet's origin from another star system through telescopic observations, which indicate its trajectory is not bound to the Sun.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/Atlas speed: 57 km/s
  • Comparison speeds: 'Oumuamua at 26 km/s, Borisov at 33 km/s
  • Third confirmed interstellar object
  • Speed exceeds necessary escape velocity
  • Observations track current path to deep space

Timeline

The detection of comet 3I/Atlas occurred recently, with astronomers utilizing telescopes to confirm its hyperbolic trajectory. The comet's speed has been recorded, and calculations have predicted its closest approach, which is expected to last a few weeks.

As the comet enters the Solar System, it undergoes a deviation in its path but does not form an orbit, allowing it to exit without being captured by the Sun's gravity.

Official statements

“The hyperbolic trajectory indicates speed greater than the local escape velocity at each point,” said a spokesperson from the astronomical observatory involved in the tracking of 3I/Atlas.

Context

Prior interstellar objects, such as 'Oumuamua, have shown unexpected acceleration during their passage past the Sun. The degassing of trapped hydrogen has been identified as a natural propulsion mechanism in those cases. The differences in speed and behavior of these objects highlight their external origins and the unique characteristics of interstellar comets.

What’s next

Observatories are continuing to monitor the trajectory of 3I/Atlas to refine real-time orbital data and analyze its chemical composition. This ongoing research aims to enhance our understanding of interstellar objects and their interactions with the Solar System.
