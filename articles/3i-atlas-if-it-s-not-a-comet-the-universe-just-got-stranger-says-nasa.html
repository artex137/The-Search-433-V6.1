3I/ATLAS: If It's Not A Comet, The Universe Just Got Stranger, Says NASA

NASA has confirmed that 3I/ATLAS is a comet, despite growing speculation among scientists that the interstellar object may be unlike anything previously observed. First detected on 1 July 2025 by the ATLAS survey telescope in Chile, 3I/ATLAS travels on a hyperbolic orbit at an extraordinary speed, indicating it originated outside the Solar System. Its unusual chemical composition and erratic brightness have prompted debate about its classification among known comets.

This matter is significant as astronomers are eager to decode the mysteries of 3I/ATLAS, which is only the third interstellar object ever recorded. The object’s CO₂-rich coma and peculiar behaviour challenge existing scientific models and offer insights into the formation of protoplanetary discs in distant star systems.

Key facts

  • Detected on 1 July 2025 by the ATLAS survey telescope in Chile.
  • Travels on a hyperbolic orbit, indicating an origin outside the Solar System.
  • NASA has classified it as a comet based on its behaviour and appearance.
  • James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) revealed an unusually CO₂-dominant chemical profile.
  • 3I/ATLAS is only the third interstellar object ever recorded, following 'Oumuamua and Borisov.

Timeline

On 1 July 2025, the ATLAS survey telescope in Chile first detected 3I/ATLAS. Following its discovery, astronomers observed its comet-like features, including a glowing coma and a dust-gas tail. The James Webb Space Telescope later provided crucial data, revealing its unique chemical composition.

As 3I/ATLAS approaches the end of its visible trajectory, astronomers are racing to complete final observations before it fades beyond the reach of telescopes.

Official statements

Nicky Fox, a NASA official, stated, "It looks and behaves like a comet. All evidence points to it being a comet."

Context

Prior to 3I/ATLAS, only two interstellar objects had been confirmed: 'Oumuamua, which lacked a visible tail, and Borisov, which displayed more traditional comet characteristics. The distinctive behaviour of 3I/ATLAS has sparked discussions among scientists regarding its classification and implications for our understanding of cosmic phenomena.

What’s next

Astronomers will continue to gather and analyze data from 3I/ATLAS as it moves away from the Sun. Future studies may further clarify its classification and contribute to our understanding of interstellar objects.
