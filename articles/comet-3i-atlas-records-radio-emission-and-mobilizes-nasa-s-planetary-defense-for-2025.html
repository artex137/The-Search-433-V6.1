Comet 3I/ATLAS records radio emission and mobilizes NASA’s planetary defense for 2025

An atypical radio signal from the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS has mobilized NASA’s planetary defense system in July 2025. The signal was detected by the ATLAS system in Chile and has generated significant interest among scientists. No injuries or fatalities have been reported.

This development highlights the unique behavior of comet 3I/ATLAS, which differs from previously observed interstellar objects. NASA has engaged its experts to ensure safety and enhance understanding of this celestial visitor.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS detected in July 2025 by ATLAS in Chile.
  • Estimated diameter of the comet's core between 320 meters and 5.6 kilometers.
  • Scheduled minimum approach to Earth on December 19, 2025, at a distance of 270 million kilometers.
  • Radio signal recorded by MeerKAT telescope in South Africa at 1.6 GHz on October 24, 2025.
  • NASA's planetary defense experts convened in August 2025 to assess the comet.
  • No risk of impact with Earth or any other celestial body, according to NASA and ESA.

Timeline

In July 2025, the ATLAS system in Chile detected a radio signal from comet 3I/ATLAS, prompting NASA to activate its planetary defense protocols. By August 2025, experts convened to analyze the comet's unusual characteristics. On October 24, 2025, the MeerKAT radio telescope in South Africa recorded a radio emission from the comet, confirming its active status.

Official statements

“The signal is of natural origin, and there is no external interference,” researchers confirmed regarding the radio emission from 3I/ATLAS.

Context

Comet 3I/ATLAS is classified as a fragment ejected from another star system millions of years ago. Its behavior differs from other interstellar comets like 2I/Borisov, which exhibited intense outgassing. Understanding the composition and dynamics of 3I/ATLAS may provide insights into the formation of planetary systems beyond our own.

What’s next

NASA and ESA will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS closely, analyzing its chemical spectrum and internal dynamics to gather data on its composition. Ongoing observations will enhance scientific knowledge of interstellar comets and their formation processes.
