NASA Unveils New Images of Comet 3I/ATLAS and Its Potential Origins

NASA has unveiled new images of the interstellar object designated 3I/ATLAS, which scientists have classified as a comet. The comet was first identified on July 1, 2025, by the Atlas survey telescope in Chile. The recent images were captured as 3I/ATLAS approached solar conjunction, positioning it directly on the opposite side of the Sun from Earth on October 21, 2025.

This discovery is significant as it provides insights into the origins of the comet. Research by the Royal Astronomical Society suggests that 3I/ATLAS may have formed from the Milky Way's thin disk layer, which contains primarily younger stars. However, there is a possibility that it originates from the thick disk, a region populated by much older stars. If confirmed, this could mean the comet is over seven billion years old, offering a rare chance to study material predating our solar system.

Key facts

  • Comet designated: 3I/ATLAS
  • First identified: July 1, 2025
  • Images taken: October 21, 2025
  • Location of identification: Chile
  • Potential age: Over seven billion years

Timeline

On July 1, 2025, NASA's Atlas survey telescope first identified the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS. The comet has since been studied as it neared its solar conjunction on October 21, 2025.

Recent research by the Royal Astronomical Society has shed light on its possible origins, indicating that it may have formed in the Milky Way's thin disk or thick disk.

Official statements

“These findings could deepen our understanding of the formation and evolution of our galaxy,” said a spokesperson for the Royal Astronomical Society.

Context

The study of comets provides critical insights into the early solar system and the formation of celestial bodies. Previous research has focused on the characteristics of various comets, but 3I/ATLAS may offer a unique perspective due to its potential age and origins.

What’s next

Scientists will continue to study 3I/ATLAS to confirm its origins and age, which could enhance our understanding of galactic formation and evolution.
