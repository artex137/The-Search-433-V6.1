Interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS is full of alcohol, ALMA observations suggest

Researchers have discovered that the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS contains a high concentration of methanol, according to observations made with the Atacama Large Millimeter/submillimeter Array (ALMA) in Chile. The findings have been posted on the arXiv preprint server. The comet approached the Sun in late 2025, leading to the release of gas and dust that formed its glowing coma.

This discovery is significant as it provides insights into the composition of 3I/ATLAS and the solar system from which it originated. The chemical signatures observed in the coma allow researchers to study the comet without the need for a physical mission to its home system. Nathan Roth, a professor at American University and lead author of the study, emphasized the importance of the findings, stating, “Observing 3I/ATLAS is like taking a fingerprint from another solar system.”

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS has been found to have more methanol than almost all known comets in the solar system.
  • Observations were conducted using ALMA in Chile.
  • Two specific molecules detected: methanol (CH₃OH) and hydrogen cyanide (HCN).
  • The methanol-to-HCN ratios were measured at 70 and 120 on two different occasions.
  • 3I/ATLAS is the third known interstellar object to enter the solar system.

Timeline

In late 2025, 3I/ATLAS approached the Sun, which warmed its icy surface and caused the release of gas and dust, forming a glowing coma. ALMA conducted observations that revealed the comet's chemical composition, focusing on methanol and hydrogen cyanide. These findings were later published on the arXiv preprint server.

In December 2025, NASA's SPHEREx mission also observed 3I/ATLAS, detecting methanol, cyanide, and methane. The study of the comet has provided valuable information about its outgassing behavior and composition.

Official statements

Nathan Roth, lead author of the study, stated, “The details reveal what it’s made of, and it’s bursting with methanol in a way we just don't usually see in comets in our own solar system.”

Context

The findings from 3I/ATLAS add to previous observations made by the James Webb Space Telescope (JWST), which indicated that the comet's coma was dominated by carbon dioxide when it was farther from the Sun. The study of interstellar comets like 3I/ATLAS is crucial for understanding the chemistry of distant planetary systems.

What’s next

Astronomers plan to continue studying interstellar objects to expand their understanding of their chemical compositions and the characteristics of distant solar systems.
