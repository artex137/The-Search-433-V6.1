Interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS will safely pass Earth this Friday

The interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS will make its closest approach to Earth on Friday, December 19, 2025, reaching a distance of 269 million kilometres. This distance is nearly twice the average distance between the Earth and the Sun. The European Space Agency (ESA) has confirmed that the comet poses no danger to Earth or any other planets.

The arrival of 3I/ATLAS offers astronomers a rare opportunity to observe an interstellar object. First spotted on July 1, 2023, by the Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System (ATLAS) telescope in Río Hurtado, Chile, the comet has generated interest due to its unique characteristics. It is the third confirmed interstellar object recorded and is expected to provide insights into the formation of comets and planets beyond our Solar System.

Key facts

  • Date: December 19, 2025
  • Closest distance to Earth: 269 million kilometres
  • First observed: July 1, 2023
  • Location of discovery: Río Hurtado, Chile
  • Observational period: Until spring 2026
  • Livestream start time: 5 am CET (0400 UTC) on December 19

Timeline

On July 1, 2023, astronomers first detected 3I/ATLAS using the ATLAS telescope in Chile. Since then, scientists have captured unprecedented images of the comet, including observations using ESA’s X-ray telescope XMM-Newton. These images revealed a diffuse X-ray glow around the comet nucleus.

As the comet approaches, it will be observable in the pre-dawn sky, even with a small telescope. The ESA provides an interactive map for real-time tracking of 3I/ATLAS, while the Virtual Telescope Project will host a free livestream of the event.

Official statements

“Interstellar comets are exceedingly rare, true outsiders to our galaxy that carry clues about how worlds form far beyond the reaches of our Solar System,” said an ESA representative.
“This is an exciting opportunity for astronomers to study the coma of 3I/ATLAS, offering new insight into how comets and planets form around other stars in the Universe,” the representative added.

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third confirmed interstellar object ever recorded, following previous discoveries that have sparked public interest and scientific inquiry. Observations of such objects are crucial for understanding the origins of our solar system and the formation of celestial bodies in different environments.

What’s next

As 3I/ATLAS approaches Earth, astronomers will continue to observe and study it. The comet is expected to remain observable until spring 2026, providing ongoing opportunities for research and education.
