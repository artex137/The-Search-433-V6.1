What’s Hidden Inside 3I/ATLAS? Astronomers Reveal Another Unexpected Discovery About Our Latest Interstellar Visitor

Astronomers have discovered that the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS contains an unusually high amount of methanol, a type of alcohol, according to new research from the Atacama Large Millimeter/submillimeter Array (ALMA). The findings were made as 3I/ATLAS approached its closest distance to the Sun in late 2022.

This discovery is significant because it suggests that 3I/ATLAS may hold more organic molecules than any other known comet in our solar system. Scientists are using this data to better understand the composition and formation of interstellar objects.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS was first discovered in summer 2022.
  • Observations were made using ALMA’s Atacama Compact Array in Chile.
  • Research led by Nathan Roth, a professor at American University.
  • Comet contains a methanol to hydrogen cyanide ratio of about 70 to 120.
  • Findings accepted for publication in The Astrophysical Journal Letters.

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was discovered in the summer of 2022. Subsequent observations were conducted in late 2022 as the comet approached the Sun. ALMA’s observations revealed the comet's unique chemical composition, including significant amounts of methanol.

During these observations, the comet's surface warmed, causing gas and dust to be released, which increased the size and brightness of its coma. The research team identified the chemical "fingerprints" of the comet's materials, providing insights into its composition.

Official statements

“The details reveal what it’s made of, and it’s bursting with methanol in a way we just don’t usually see in comets in our own solar system,” said Nathan Roth.

Context

Previous observations by NASA’s James Webb Space Telescope indicated that 3I/ATLAS had a coma primarily composed of carbon dioxide. The new findings from ALMA have introduced methanol into the equation, highlighting the differences between interstellar comets and those originating in our solar system.

These observations mark the first time such behaviors have been documented in relation to an interstellar object, expanding our understanding of how these celestial bodies behave as they approach the Sun.

What’s next

Astronomers plan to continue studying 3I/ATLAS and other interstellar objects to unravel the mysteries of their origins and formation processes. Further research could lead to more discoveries regarding the composition of distant planetary systems.
