Roswell, New Mexico, is rife with UFOs, scorpions and conspiracy theories — but few Jews

Published 2025-11-15 03:43 UTC

Roswell, New Mexico, UFOs and the Absence of Jewish Life

Roswell, New Mexico, is a town known for its connection to UFOs and conspiracy theories, yet it has very few Jewish residents. The situation has persisted since the closure of the last local synagogue, Congregation B’nai Israel, which moved to Albuquerque five years ago.

This matter is significant as it highlights the intersection of local culture and history, particularly the impact of the famous 1947 Roswell incident on the community. Authorities and residents have continued to engage with the legacy of this event, which has shaped Roswell’s identity as a UFO hotspot.

Key facts

  • Location: Roswell, New Mexico
  • Population: Approximately 47,000
  • Closure of Congregation B’nai Israel: Five years ago
  • Year of Roswell incident: 1947
  • Annual UFO Festival: Attracts thousands of tourists

Timeline

In 1947, local rancher W.W. “Mac” Brazen discovered unusual debris on his property, which he reported to Sheriff George Wilcox. On July 9, the Roswell Army Air Field announced the recovery of a “flying disc,” a claim later retracted as a mistake regarding a weather balloon.

In the 1970s, Stanton T. Friedman revisited the incident, advocating for the idea of extraterrestrial contact. His efforts sparked ongoing interest in the Roswell event, leading to numerous books and documentaries.

In 1997, the U.S. Air Force released a report stating that the recovered materials were part of a secret military program, not alien spacecraft. This report did not quell public fascination, as voiced by retired Air Force Col. Richard Weaver.

Official statements

“This has become a religion to many people. It’s almost a cult.”

- Retired Air Force Col. Richard Weaver, NBC’s Today show, 1997

Context

The Roswell incident has become a focal point for UFO enthusiasts and conspiracy theorists, contributing to a culture that often intertwines with antisemitism. The town’s identity is largely shaped by this event, despite the dwindling Jewish population.

Jewish residents, such as Leslie Lawner, have noted the absence of a supportive community for Jewish life. Lawner and her husband moved to Albuquerque when the synagogue closed, further highlighting the challenges faced by the local Jewish population.

What’s next

The town will continue to celebrate its UFO heritage with events such as the annual Roswell UFO Festival, which draws visitors from across the country, ensuring that the legacy of the 1947 incident remains a significant part of its identity.
