NASA and Gemini North Unveil Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS's Eerie Green Glow

Astronomers using the Gemini North telescope in Hawaii have released new images of Comet 3I/ATLAS, an interstellar object passing through the Solar System, on 26 November 2025. The comet displays a distinctive green hue, attributed to gases released from its surface interacting with solar radiation. No injuries or fatalities have been reported.

This discovery is significant as 3I/ATLAS is only the third known object identified as originating from beyond the Sun's gravitational influence. Scientists believe it may be the oldest comet ever observed, offering a unique opportunity to study material formed around another star system. The comet's transformation and its impending departure provide a rare glimpse into interstellar phenomena.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS discovered in 2025.
  • Images captured by Gemini North Observatory in Hawaii on 26 November 2025.
  • Comet is the third known interstellar object in the Solar System.
  • Comet exhibits a green coma due to diatomic carbon emissions.
  • Gemini telescopes will continue to monitor the comet until it is no longer visible.

Timeline

Comet 3I/ATLAS was first tracked using the Gemini South telescope in the Chilean Andes, appearing with a rusty red hue. In late 2025, as it reemerged from behind the Sun, its colour transformed to a vivid green, indicating significant changes in its surface composition due to solar heat.

On 26 November 2025, astronomers captured high-resolution images of the comet using the Gemini Multi-Object Spectrograph. The images required precise tracking due to the comet's high speed relative to background stars.

Official statements

“This initiative helps demystify the scientific and data collection process,” said Bryce Bolin, a research scientist from Eureka Scientific. “It offers the public a rare level of transparency into how we study these fleeting celestial events.”

Context

Comet 3I/ATLAS is unique as it is not bound by the Sun's gravity and is on a one-way trajectory out of the Solar System. Unlike familiar comets that return periodically, this interstellar object entered the Solar System from the interstellar medium and will not return.

What’s next

Astronomers will continue to monitor Comet 3I/ATLAS as it departs the Solar System. They caution that some comets experience delayed outbursts, where pockets of trapped gas may erupt as the comet cools down. The monitoring will persist until the comet is no longer visible.
