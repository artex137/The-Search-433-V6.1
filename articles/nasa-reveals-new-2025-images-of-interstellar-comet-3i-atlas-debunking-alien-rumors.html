NASA Reveals New 2025 Images of Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS, Debunking Alien Rumors

On November 20, 2025, NASA has unveiled a new collection of images and data of interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, dismissing online speculation that it may be an alien spacecraft. The release includes observations from various spacecraft around Mars, near the Sun, and deep space, as well as data from powerful telescopes worldwide.

This announcement is significant as 3I/ATLAS is only the third confirmed interstellar object ever observed in our solar system. NASA continues to monitor the comet’s trajectory and characteristics, which may offer insights into the formation of planetary systems.

Key facts

  • Discovery Date: July 1, 2025
  • Size: Nucleus estimated between 440 meters (1,400 feet) and 5.6 kilometers (3.5 miles) wide
  • Speed: Initially 137,000 mph (221,000 km/h), reaching 153,000 mph (246,000 km/h) near the Sun
  • Closest Approach: 170 million miles (270 million km) from Earth
  • Mars Observations: Captured by Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter on October 2, 2025
  • Comet Composition: Rich in carbon dioxide, water ice, and other compounds

Timeline

NASA-funded ATLAS survey telescopes in Chile first detected comet 3I/ATLAS on July 1, 2025. Follow-up observations confirmed it as an active comet on an interstellar trajectory. Observations from Mars spacecraft began in late September, with the Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter capturing images on October 2, 2025.

As 3I/ATLAS moved behind the Sun from Earth’s perspective in October, NASA's solar observatories continued monitoring the comet. Ground-based telescopes temporarily lost sight of it, but various missions provided critical data to track its path.

Official statements

“The flood of images and spectra from NASA, ESA and ground-based observatories is starting to turn 3I/ATLAS from a mysterious outsider into a well-characterized object,” a NASA spokesperson said.

Context

3I/ATLAS is only the third interstellar object confirmed to have passed through our solar system, following 1I/ʻOumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019. The comet's observations are crucial for understanding the chemical makeup of celestial bodies from other star systems.

Recent analyses suggest that 3I/ATLAS may have originated from the Milky Way's thick disk, indicating it could be over 7 billion years old, potentially predating the solar system itself.

What’s next

NASA and ESA will continue to analyze data from 3I/ATLAS, with additional insights expected from the Juice mission in February 2026. Ongoing observations will help refine understanding of its trajectory and composition.
