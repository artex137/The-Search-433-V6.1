3I/ATLAS Radio Signal Confirmed Natural; Scientists Rule Out Alien Origin

Scientists have confirmed that a radio signal detected from the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS has natural origins, according to a report released on November 12, 2025. The signal was captured using South Africa’s MeerKAT radio telescope earlier this year, putting to rest speculation about potential alien activity.

This discovery is significant as it enhances understanding of interstellar objects and their chemical properties. Researchers indicate that the signals are caused by hydroxyl molecules—fragments of water—present in the comet’s outer layer, not by any artificial source. The finding also highlights the need for ongoing research into the potential risks posed by interstellar objects to Earth.

Key facts

  • 3I/ATLAS was first detected on July 1, 2025.
  • MeerKAT radio telescope captured the radio emissions earlier in 2025.
  • The comet is expected to pass within 53 million kilometers of Jupiter on March 16, 2026.
  • NASA’s Juno spacecraft will observe the comet during its close encounter.
  • Low-latitude regions near the equator are identified as the highest risk zones for potential impacts from interstellar objects.

Timeline

The radio signal from 3I/ATLAS was first detected on July 1, 2025. Following its detection, scientists quickly began analyzing the data to determine the nature of the emissions. On November 12, 2025, researchers confirmed that the signals were produced by hydroxyl molecules due to sunlight breaking apart water vapor, confirming a natural origin.

As the comet continues its journey through the solar system, it is set to pass near Jupiter in March 2026, providing an opportunity for further study.

Official statements

“The ingredients for life’s chemistry are not unique to our solar system,” said Auburn University physicist Dennis Bodewits, commenting on the implications of detecting hydroxyl emissions from the comet.

Context

3I/ATLAS is only the third known interstellar object observed passing through the solar system. The comet's unusual trajectory and radio activity initially sparked public speculation about extraterrestrial technology, particularly following remarks from Harvard astrophysicist Avi Loeb regarding the potential for artificial origins of such objects.

Despite the excitement surrounding the comet, studies have indicated that regions near the equator, including South America, Central Africa, and Southern Asia, are at the highest risk for impacts from interstellar objects, underscoring the importance of planetary defense strategies.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS as it approaches Jupiter. Observations from NASA’s Juno spacecraft may provide further insights into the comet's structure and the environment from which it originated. Ongoing studies will also focus on assessing the risks associated with interstellar objects and developing detection and defense measures.
