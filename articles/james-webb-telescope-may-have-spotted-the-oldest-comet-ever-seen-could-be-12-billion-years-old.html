James Webb telescope may have spotted the oldest comet ever seen; Could be 12 billion years old

Interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS may be the oldest comet ever observed, according to new data from the James Webb Space Telescope (JWST). The findings indicate that the comet formed up to 12 billion years ago, suggesting a significant age for this celestial object.

This discovery matters because it provides insight into the early formation of our galaxy. Researchers believe that the unusual isotopic fingerprints of 3I/ATLAS indicate it originated in the cold, distant regions of the early Milky Way. The JWST continues to gather valuable data that could enhance our understanding of cosmic history.

Key facts

  • Comet: 3I/ATLAS
  • Estimated age: up to 12 billion years
  • Instrument: James Webb Space Telescope (JWST)
  • Location of origin: early Milky Way

Timeline

The James Webb Space Telescope has conducted observations of comet 3I/ATLAS, revealing data that suggests its ancient formation. The findings have emerged from ongoing research into the characteristics and origins of interstellar comets.

Initial observations of 3I/ATLAS have provided scientists with key isotopic data, leading to the conclusion about its significant age.

Official statements

“The unusual isotopic fingerprints suggest a birthplace in the cold, distant regions of the early Milky Way,”
said researchers involved in the JWST study.

Context

This discovery represents a growing interest in interstellar objects and their origins. The study of comets like 3I/ATLAS can reveal information about the conditions in the early universe and the processes that led to the formation of celestial bodies.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to analyze data from the JWST to deepen their understanding of 3I/ATLAS and its implications for the history of the Milky Way.
