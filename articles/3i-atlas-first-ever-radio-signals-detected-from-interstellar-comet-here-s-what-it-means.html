3I/ATLAS: First Ever Radio Signals Detected From Interstellar Comet

The interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS has emitted radio signals, which were captured by South Africa’s MeerKAT radio telescope on October 24, 2025. This marks the first known radio detection from an interstellar comet, contributing to the study of such celestial bodies.

This discovery is significant because it adds to the understanding of the behavior and characteristics of interstellar comets. Authorities and researchers are analyzing the signals to determine their implications for cometary science and the search for extraterrestrial intelligence.

Key facts

  • Event date: October 24, 2025
  • Location of detection: MeerKAT radio telescope, South Africa
  • Comet designation: 3I/ATLAS
  • Surface temperature of 3I/ATLAS: ~230 degrees Kelvin
  • Distance from the Sun at detection: 1.38 times the Earth-Sun separation
  • Thermal speed of OH molecules: ~0.8 kilometers per second

Timeline

On October 24, 2025, the MeerKAT radio telescope detected radio signals from the comet 3I/ATLAS. This event occurred shortly before the comet's perihelion, when it was closest to the Sun. The comet has been noted for its unusual behavior, which has intrigued scientists.

Prior to this detection, 3I/ATLAS was the third interstellar object recorded in the past decade, following 1I/Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov. The comet’s trajectory is notably within 9 degrees of the direction of the Wow! signal detected in 1977, which has led to further interest in its characteristics.

Official statements

“This absorption signal constitutes the first radio detection of 3I/ATLAS,” said Prof. Avi Loeb, a Harvard astrophysicist, in his blog. “It’s just a fingerprint of water molecules breaking down on the comet.”

Context

The detection of radio signals from 3I/ATLAS adds to a growing body of knowledge regarding interstellar objects. Previous interstellar comets have also exhibited unique behaviors, prompting ongoing research into their origins and compositions. The Wow! signal, detected in 1977, remains one of the most famous unexplained radio signals.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to analyze the data from the MeerKAT telescope to better understand the signals from 3I/ATLAS. Further studies may help clarify the comet's properties and the broader implications for the study of interstellar objects.
