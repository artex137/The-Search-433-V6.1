Exact trajectory of 3I/ATLAS object defies science by targeting Jupiter’s Hill radius

A celestial body named 3I/ATLAS has researchers intrigued due to its unusual trajectory, as it approaches Jupiter on March 16, 2026. Discovered in 2025, 3I/ATLAS exhibits characteristics that differ from known comets and asteroids in the solar system. There are no reported injuries or fatalities related to this astronomical event.

This event is significant because the predicted minimum distance of 3I/ATLAS to Jupiter almost perfectly aligns with the planet's Hill radius, a crucial gravitational limit. This precision raises questions about the object's nature and origin, prompting ongoing observations and studies by scientists worldwide.

Key facts

  • Discovery Date: 2025
  • Closest Approach to Jupiter: March 16, 2026
  • Predicted Distance at Perijove: 53.445 million km
  • Hill Radius of Jupiter: 53.502 million km
  • Error Margin: ±0.06 million km
  • Speed: 60 km/s
  • Mass: Substantially greater than previous interstellar objects
  • Observations: Continuous monitoring by global observatories

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was discovered in 2025, marking the beginning of a series of observations that challenge conventional astrophysical explanations. Between July and November 2025, scientists conducted critical observations that revealed a jet directed towards the Sun, indicating active forces operating on the object. The object is predicted to reach its closest approach to Jupiter in March 2026, with its trajectory fine-tuned by non-gravitational accelerations detected near perihelion.

Official statements

“The precision in the final trajectory, influenced by forces beyond gravity, is one of the most intriguing aspects that scientists seek to understand,” said researcher Avi Loeb.

Context

The identification of 3I/ATLAS has prompted discussions about the nature of interstellar objects. Previous interstellar objects, such as 1I/’Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov, have been less massive and displayed different characteristics. The unique features of 3I/ATLAS, including its retrograde orbit and high speed, have intensified the debate over its true nature and origin.

What’s next

Observatories will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS as it approaches Jupiter. New images and spectral data will be obtained and analyzed to uncover more about the object's composition and behavior.
