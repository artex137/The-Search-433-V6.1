3I/ATLAS comet is bursting with alcohol, surprising astronomers

Comet 3I/ATLAS, an interstellar object discovered in July 2025, has been found to be rich in methanol and hydrogen cyanide (HCN), according to observations made with the Atacama Large Millimeter/submillimeter Array (ALMA) in Chile. The comet has exhibited methanol-to-HCN ratios of approximately 70 and 120, making it one of the most methanol-rich comets ever recorded.

This discovery is significant because it challenges existing models of comet formation and behavior. Researchers, including astronomer Nathan Roth from American University, have spent months analyzing the comet to gain insights into its composition. The findings suggest that 3I/ATLAS originated in a very different environment compared to typical comets in our solar system.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS discovered in July 2025.
  • Located in deep space, currently returning after its closest approach to the sun.
  • Methanol-to-HCN ratios measured at around 70 and 120.
  • Observations conducted using the Atacama Large Millimeter/submillimeter Array (ALMA) in Chile.
  • 3I/ATLAS is the third known interstellar object to pass through our solar system.

Timeline

In July 2025, astronomers discovered comet 3I/ATLAS as it entered our solar system. Following its discovery, researchers began extensive observations of the comet. In late 2025, data collected from ALMA revealed the comet's high levels of methanol and HCN. The findings have provided new insights into the comet's unique formation environment.

Official statements

“Observing 3I/ATLAS is like taking a fingerprint from another solar system,” said Nathan Roth, an astronomer at American University.
“It’s bursting with methanol in a way we just don’t usually see in comets in our own solar system,” Roth added.

Context

3I/ATLAS is notable as only the third interstellar object identified in our solar system. Previous interstellar visitors have also provided valuable data, but the unique composition of 3I/ATLAS adds to our understanding of the diversity of materials found in other regions of the galaxy.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to analyze data from 3I/ATLAS as it journeys back into deep space. Further studies may reveal additional insights into its composition and the conditions under which it formed.
