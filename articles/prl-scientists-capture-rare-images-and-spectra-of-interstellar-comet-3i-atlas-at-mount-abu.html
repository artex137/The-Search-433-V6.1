PRL scientists capture rare images and spectra of interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS at Mount Abu

Scientists at the Physical Research Laboratory (PRL) in India have captured detailed imaging and spectroscopic data of the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS at Mount Abu between November 12 and 15, 2025. The comet is currently moving out of the inner Solar System following its closest approach to the Sun.

This research is significant as it reveals Solar System-like molecular features of the comet, providing insights into its composition and behaviour. The PRL team utilized their 1.2-meter telescope at the Mount Abu Observatory, a facility known for its astronomical research, to conduct these observations.

Key facts

  • Event: Imaging and spectroscopic observations of comet 3I/ATLAS
  • Date: November 12-15, 2025
  • Location: Mount Abu, India
  • Telescope: PRL’s 1.2-meter telescope
  • Altitude of Observatory: 1680 meters
  • Comet's emission rate: Near 1025 molecules per second

Timeline

Between November 12 and 15, 2025, PRL scientists conducted a series of observations of comet 3I/ATLAS using their 1.2-meter telescope. They employed wide-area imaging techniques to capture the comet's coma structure, which is the gas and dust surrounding the nucleus.

Before morning twilight, the scientists utilized the LISA spectrograph to obtain a flux-normalised spectrum of the comet. This analysis confirmed the presence of molecular features characteristic of Solar System comets.

Official statements

“The comet’s emission for the primary molecular bands was found to be near 1025 molecules per second, a sign consistent with ‘typical’ Solar System comets,” a PRL scientist stated.

Context

The observations of 3I/ATLAS contribute to a growing body of research on interstellar comets, which provide valuable information about the formation and evolution of the Solar System. The findings from this study align with previous observations of other comets, offering a comparative understanding of their activity levels and compositions.

What’s next

PRL scientists plan to continue their observations of 3I/ATLAS as it moves into darker night skies, aiming to gain further insights into this rare interstellar visitor.
