Interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS found releasing 40 kg of water per sec

Astronomers have detected water on interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, the third known visitor from another star system, observed near Mars by ESA’s ExoMars TGO and NASA’s Swift Observatory. The comet releases approximately 40 kg of water per second, despite being three times farther from the Sun than Earth.

This discovery is significant as it provides insights into how life’s building blocks may travel between stars. Scientists are currently analyzing the comet's icy, active surface to understand its composition and the implications for astrobiology.

Key facts

  • Comet: 3I/ATLAS
  • Location: Near Mars
  • Observation by: ESA’s ExoMars TGO and NASA’s Swift Observatory
  • Water release rate: Approximately 40 kg per second
  • Distance from Sun: Three times farther than Earth

Timeline

Astronomers have observed comet 3I/ATLAS using ESA’s ExoMars TGO and NASA’s Swift Observatory. The detection of water on the comet has led to ongoing studies regarding its surface composition and potential implications for the understanding of life in the universe.

Official statements

“The icy, active surface of 3I/ATLAS hints at how life’s building blocks may travel between stars,” a representative from ESA stated.

Context

3I/ATLAS is only the third interstellar comet identified, following previous discoveries that have raised questions about the origins of such celestial bodies. The study of comets has become increasingly important in understanding the potential for life beyond Earth.

What’s next

Scientists will continue to analyze data from ESA and NASA to gain further insights into the comet's properties and the broader implications for astrobiology.
