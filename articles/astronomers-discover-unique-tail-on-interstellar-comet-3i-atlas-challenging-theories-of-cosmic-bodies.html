Astronomers Discover Unique Tail on Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS, Challenging Theories of Cosmic Bodies

Astronomers have discovered a faint, teardrop-shaped tail on the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS, which stretches approximately 56,400 kilometers behind the comet and points directly away from the Sun. This finding was reported by physicist Avi Loeb in a Medium article, following observations made using the Gemini South 8.2-meter telescope located atop Cerro Pachón in the Chilean Andes.

This discovery is significant as it adds to the growing list of peculiar features surrounding 3I/ATLAS, which appears to diverge from typical cometary behavior. Researchers are now examining the implications of this unique tail and the object's composition, which could redefine classifications of interstellar bodies.

Key facts

  • Discovery of a teardrop-shaped tail measuring approximately 56,400 kilometers.
  • The tail points in the anti-solar direction, aligned southeast.
  • A coma surrounding the object spans about 18,800 kilometers.
  • 3I/ATLAS releases carbon dioxide at a rate of 130 kilograms per second.
  • The object generates 6.6 kilograms per second of water and 14 kilograms per second of carbon monoxide.
  • Diameter of 3I/ATLAS measured at 46 kilometers, suggesting a mass approximately one million times greater than interstellar comet 2I/Borisov.
  • Perihelion is expected on October 29, 2025, increasing exposure to solar heat and radiation.

Timeline

NASA's TESS mission first detected a persistent glow from 3I/ATLAS in May 2025, when the object was six astronomical units from the Sun.

Observations in early August 2025, using the SPHEREx mission, suggested a diameter of 46 kilometers for 3I/ATLAS.

As the perihelion approaches on October 29, 2025, further studies are anticipated to reveal more about this interstellar visitor.

Official statements

“A high stress environment elicits confessions,” said Avi Loeb, indicating that forthcoming studies may uncover the secrets of 3I/ATLAS.

Context

3I/ATLAS's characteristics challenge current astrophysical models, which typically predict that smaller interstellar bodies should be more common and detectable. The object’s trajectory is also notably aligned with the ecliptic plane, a rarity among interstellar objects.

Previous observations of 3I/ATLAS have revealed a unique composition, with a significant carbon dioxide plume and unusual absence of iron, suggesting a potential industrial process rather than natural astrophysical events.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS as it approaches the Sun, with upcoming studies expected to provide insights into the object's origins and behaviors.
