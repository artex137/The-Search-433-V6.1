ExoMars and Mars Express Capture Comet 3I/ATLAS Observations

Recent observations from the ExoMars and Mars Express orbiters have captured significant data regarding comet 3I/ATLAS, detected on July 1, 2025. This comet is the third confirmed interstellar comet observed to date, following 1I/ʻOumuamua and 2I/Borisov. On October 3, 2025, the comet approached its closest point to Mars, approximately 30 million kilometers away from the two orbiters.

The observations matter because they provide insights into the composition and behavior of interstellar comets. The European Space Agency (ESA) plans to analyze the data collected by both orbiters in the coming weeks and months.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS detected on July 1, 2025.
  • Closest approach to Mars occurred on October 3, 2025.
  • Distance from orbiters during closest approach: approximately 30 million kilometers.
  • The nucleus of the comet is approximately one kilometer wide.
  • The coma extends several thousand kilometers.
  • Data from upcoming observations will be available in February 2026.
  • The Comet Interceptor mission is set to launch in 2029.

Timeline

On July 1, 2025, astronomers detected comet 3I/ATLAS, marking it as the third confirmed interstellar comet. On October 3, 2025, the comet made its closest approach to Mars, allowing the ExoMars Trace Gas Orbiter (TGO) and Mars Express to capture images. The TGO successfully used its Colour and Stereo Surface Imaging System (CaSSIS) to obtain images, while Mars Express faced limitations due to shorter exposure times.

In the following weeks and months, scientists will analyze the collected data to better understand the comet’s characteristics and behavior as it approaches the Sun.

Official statements

“This observation posed significant challenges. 3I/ATLAS is notably fainter than typical targets, making imaging complex,” said Nick Thomas, Principal Investigator of CaSSIS.
“The Mars orbiters continue to provide valuable insights into Martian science and at the same time contribute to the understanding of celestial phenomena,” stated Colin Wilson from ESA.

Context

Comet 3I/ATLAS presents a unique opportunity for scientists as it is an interstellar object that may be over three billion years older than our Solar System. The study of such comets can provide clues about the formation of worlds outside our solar system.

What’s next

Next month, the Jupiter Icy Moons Explorer (Juice) will observe 3I/ATLAS shortly after its closest approach to the Sun. Data from these observations will not be available until February 2026. Additionally, ESA is preparing for the Comet Interceptor mission set to launch in 2029, aimed at targeting pristine comets from the Oort Cloud and other interstellar objects.
