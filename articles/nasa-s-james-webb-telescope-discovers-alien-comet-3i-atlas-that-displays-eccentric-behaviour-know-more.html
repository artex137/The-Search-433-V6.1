NASA's James Webb Telescope Discovers Alien Comet 3I/ATLAS

NASA's James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) has discovered the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, which is currently moving through our solar system. The comet was first observed on August 6, 2025, using the telescope's Near-Infrared Spectrograph (NIRSpec) instrument.

This discovery is significant as it provides astronomers with a unique opportunity to study a celestial object that originated from outside our solar system. Researchers are eager to gather data before the comet moves out of reach for further observations.

Key facts

  • Object: Comet 3I/ATLAS
  • Date of Discovery: August 6, 2025
  • Instrument Used: Near-Infrared Spectrograph (NIRSpec)
  • Composition Detected: Carbon dioxide, water, water ice, carbon monoxide, carbonyl sulfide
  • Carbon Dioxide-to-Water Ratio: Approximately 8
  • Type: Interstellar comet, only the third of its kind discovered

Timeline

The discovery of 3I/ATLAS marks a significant milestone in the study of interstellar objects. On August 6, 2025, JWST captured the comet as it approached the Sun, allowing scientists to analyze its unique composition.

As the comet neared the Sun, its icy interior began a process called "outgassing," creating a halo of gas and dust around it. This phenomenon allowed NIRSpec to detect various gases and solids emitted by the comet.

Official statements

“Our observations are compatible with an intrinsically CO₂-rich nucleus, which may indicate that 3I/ATLAS contains ices exposed to higher levels of radiation than Solar System comets,” according to a study published by researchers at Cornell University.

Context

3I/ATLAS is notable because it is an interstellar comet, having originated from another star system. Unlike other comets that formed in our solar system, this comet has traveled across space for millions of years. Previous interstellar comets have provided insights into the formation and evolution of celestial bodies in different environments.

What’s next

Astronomers will continue to collect data on 3I/ATLAS as it moves through our solar system. Further studies may reveal more about its composition and the conditions under which it formed. No additional observations have been scheduled at this time.
