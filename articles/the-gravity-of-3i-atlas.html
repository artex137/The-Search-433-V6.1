The Gravity of 3I/ATLAS

The interstellar object 3I/ATLAS has passed through the orbits of Mars and Earth during October 2025, prompting scientists to assess its gravitational properties. No injuries or fatalities have been reported in relation to its passage.

This assessment is crucial as it helps researchers understand the nature of celestial bodies and their gravitational effects. Authorities in the scientific community are actively studying the characteristics of 3I/ATLAS to glean insights about interstellar objects.

Key facts

  • Object name: 3I/ATLAS
  • Passage speed: 67 kilometers per second
  • Minimum separation from Mars: 29 million kilometers
  • Estimated diameter: between 5 and 46 kilometers
  • Mass: more than 33 billion tons
  • Escape speed: between 1.3 and 12 meters per second
  • Rotation period: 16.16 hours

Timeline

On October 3, 2025, 3I/ATLAS passed Mars at a speed of 67 kilometers per second. The object was noted for its weak gravitational influence, which provided an insignificant velocity kick to Mars, measured at 2x10-12 meters per second.

Throughout October 2025, scientists have been evaluating the gravitational characteristics of 3I/ATLAS, comparing it with known celestial bodies like the Moon and black holes.

Official statements

“Despite its mass of more than 33 billion tons, the gravity of 3I/ATLAS is rather weak,” said Avi Loeb, head of the Galileo Project.
“This is a signature of its attention-grabbing gravitas and not its gravitational mass,” Loeb added.

Context

Previous studies of interstellar objects have revealed varying gravitational properties and physical characteristics. The study of 3I/ATLAS adds to the growing body of knowledge regarding the behavior of such objects as they traverse the solar system.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to analyze data from 3I/ATLAS to deepen understanding of its structure and gravitational effects. Further studies may provide insights into the formation and evolution of celestial bodies.
