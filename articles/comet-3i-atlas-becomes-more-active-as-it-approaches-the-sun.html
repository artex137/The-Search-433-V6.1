Comet 3I-Atlas Becomes More Active as It Approaches the Sun

Astronomers have reported that Comet 3I-Atlas is becoming increasingly active as it moves towards the sun. The National Science Foundation’s NoirLab has been tracking the comet using the Gemini South telescope in Chile. Recent images show the comet's tail of dust and gas expanding and intensifying since August.

This heightened activity is significant as 3I-Atlas is one of the rare interstellar objects recognized by astronomers. It is expected to make its closest approach to the sun in October before continuing towards Earth, where it will reach a minimum distance of about 167 million miles. Experts and enthusiasts are closely monitoring the comet to gather data on its composition and behavior.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I-Atlas is currently about 238 million miles from Earth.
  • It is expected to reach its closest approach to the sun in October 2023.
  • The comet will continue on a trajectory towards Earth, reaching approximately 167 million miles away.
  • 3I-Atlas is the third interstellar object identified entering our solar system.
  • Tracking is conducted using the Gemini South telescope located on Cerro Pachon, Chile.

Timeline

Comet 3I-Atlas was first identified as it entered our solar system. Since then, astronomers have observed its trajectory and activity levels. In August 2023, the comet's tail was noted to be less pronounced, but recent observations have shown a marked increase in its activity as it approaches the sun.

Official statements

“The dynamic activity of 3I-Atlas is a fascinating development as it approaches solar influences,” said a spokesperson from the National Science Foundation’s NoirLab.

Context

Comet 3I-Atlas is part of a growing interest in interstellar objects, following the discovery of others in recent years. Its unique trajectory and characteristics provide valuable data for astronomers studying the composition of such celestial bodies.

What’s next

As Comet 3I-Atlas approaches the sun, astronomers will continue to monitor its activity and trajectory, gathering further data on its composition and behavior during this visit to our solar system.
