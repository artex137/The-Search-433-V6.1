Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS Unleashes Multi-Million-Mile Anti-Tail Jets in New Photos, Astonishing Astronomers

Interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS has surprised astronomers with new images taken from New Mexico, showing three separate ionic jets and a large sunward anti-tail extending millions of miles. The photos were captured by Japanese astrophotographer Satoru Murata while the thin crescent Moon passed beside the comet.

This discovery is significant as it challenges current understanding of comet physics. The images have circulated widely in scientific circles and have reignited discussions about the comet's unusual behaviour as it continues its hyperbolic escape from the Solar System.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS is currently on a hyperbolic trajectory out of the Solar System.
  • New images captured by Satoru Murata from New Mexico.
  • Photographs reveal three ionic jets and a sunward anti-tail.
  • Anti-tail extends millions of miles from the comet.
  • Event occurred during the crescent Moon's passage beside the comet.

Timeline

Recently, Japanese astrophotographer Satoru Murata captured images of comet 3I/ATLAS from New Mexico. The photos showed the comet's jets and anti-tail, prompting renewed interest in its behaviour.

The discovery has led to discussions among astronomers regarding the implications for comet physics and the understanding of interstellar objects.

Official statements

“The images have reignited debate over the object’s unusual behaviour,” said an unnamed astronomer familiar with the findings.

Context

This is not the first time 3I/ATLAS has generated interest. Previous observations have indicated unusual characteristics, prompting ongoing research into its composition and trajectory. The comet's behaviour remains a topic of study as it moves away from the Solar System.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to examine the new images and gather data on comet 3I/ATLAS as it ventures further into interstellar space.
