Telescope Captures Striking Image Of Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS's Growing Tail

A team of astronomers and students has captured a new image of interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS, showcasing its growing tail and glowing coma. The observations were made on August 27 at the Gemini South telescope in Cerro Pachón, Chile, part of the International Gemini Observatory operated by NSF NOIRLab. No injuries or fatalities have been reported.

This observation is significant as it reveals the comet's increasing activity as it approaches the Sun, offering a unique opportunity to study the composition and characteristics of interstellar material. Authorities from the International Gemini Observatory have stated that the findings will contribute to the understanding of planetary system formation.

Key facts

  • Date of observation: August 27
  • Location: Cerro Pachón, Chile
  • Observatory: International Gemini Observatory, operated by NSF NOIRLab
  • Comet detected: July 1 by ATLAS
  • Third confirmed interstellar comet

Timeline

On July 1, 3I/ATLAS was first detected by the ATLAS (Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System). Subsequent observations on August 27 revealed a bright coma and an elongated tail, indicating increased activity as the comet nears the Sun. The team utilized the Gemini Multi-Object Spectrograph (GMOS) to capture these images.

Official statements

"We were excited to see the growth of the tail, suggesting a change in the particles from the previous Gemini images," stated Karen Meech, an astronomer at the University of Hawai‘i Institute for Astronomy, who led the program.
"The primary objectives of the observations were to look at the colors of the comet, which provide clues to the composition and sizes of the dust particles in the coma, and to take spectra for a direct measure of the chemistry," Meech explained.
"As 3I/ATLAS speeds back into the depths of interstellar space, this image is both a scientific milestone and a source of wonder," Meech said. "It reminds us that our Solar System is just one part of a vast and dynamic galaxy, and that even the most fleeting visitors can leave a lasting impact."

Context

3I/ATLAS is only the third confirmed interstellar comet, following 1I/ʻOumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019. Its hyperbolic orbit means it will soon exit the Solar System, making current observations particularly valuable for understanding the characteristics of interstellar objects.

What’s next

The team plans to continue analyzing the collected data to further investigate the composition and chemistry of Comet 3I/ATLAS before it leaves the Solar System.
