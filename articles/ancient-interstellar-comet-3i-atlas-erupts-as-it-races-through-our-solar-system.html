Ancient Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS Erupts as It Races Through Our Solar System

Interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS is currently making its way through our solar system, putting on a dramatic display with vivid jets and a gas cloud. The comet, which originates from another star, has prompted astronomers to direct every available telescope towards it as it approaches Earth. No injuries or fatalities have been reported.

This event is significant as it provides scientists with a rare opportunity to study matter from a distant part of the galaxy. The unusual chemical signature of 3I/ATLAS challenges existing theories about comet formation and the early solar system. Major space agencies are now focusing their efforts on gathering data from this ancient celestial body.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS originates from another star.
  • Size of the comet's nucleus is between 440 metres and 5.6 kilometres.
  • Comet's closest approach to Earth is on 19 December 2025, at approximately 270 million kilometres.
  • Carbon dioxide (CO2) is the predominant gas in the comet's coma, with an 8:1 CO2-to-water ratio.
  • NASA and ESA spacecraft involved include Lucy, MAVEN, Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter, and others.
  • Closest approach to Mars occurred in October 2025, at roughly 30 million kilometres.

Timeline

On 21 July 2025, images from the Hubble Space Telescope revealed a teardrop-shaped cocoon of dust around the comet's nucleus. This confirmed its identity as a comet rather than an asteroid.

In August 2025, the James Webb Space Telescope provided unexpected findings, indicating a high concentration of carbon dioxide in the gas cloud surrounding the comet.

3I/ATLAS made its closest approach to Mars in October 2025, allowing for significant imaging opportunities. NASA announced a livestream event for data release on 19 November 2025, following a delay due to a government shutdown.

Official statements

“It looks and behaves like a comet ... but this one came from outside our solar system, which makes it fascinating, exciting, and scientifically very important,” said NASA scientist Amit Kshatriya.

Context

Comets from within our solar system typically carry the chemical signatures of the early Sun's environment. In contrast, 3I/ATLAS offers a direct glimpse into the composition of another star system, potentially reflecting formative conditions that differ from our own.

Current observations have confirmed that 3I/ATLAS is indeed a comet and not an alien spacecraft. Its unique features contribute to its scientific value.

What’s next

As 3I/ATLAS approaches Earth, ground- and space-based telescopes will continue to gather data until its closest approach on 19 December 2025. Further analysis may provide insights into the comet's origins and chemical makeup.
