Gemini South Photographs Anti-Solar Tail of 3I/ATLAS

On September 2, 2025, astronomers reported that the Gemini South Observatory in Chile has photographed the anti-solar tail of comet 3I/ATLAS, an interstellar object first spotted on July 1, 2025. The discovery of 3I/ATLAS marks it as the third confirmed interstellar visitor to our solar system.

This discovery has prompted a global observational campaign involving multiple telescopes to study the comet's behavior as it approaches the Sun. Scientists aim to understand the characteristics of this ice-rich body from another star system, particularly as it exhibits unique features such as an anti-solar tail, which indicates rising activity.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS was first detected on July 1, 2025.
  • The object is approximately 420 million miles away at the time of discovery.
  • The anti-solar tail measured about 30 arcseconds long, equivalent to roughly 56,000 kilometers.
  • The coma of the comet is about 10 arcseconds across, or nearly 19,000 kilometers.
  • NASA and the International Gemini Observatory have been involved in the observations.
  • Hubble Space Telescope captured early images on July 21, 2025.
  • The James Webb Space Telescope provided spectral analysis in early August 2025.

Timeline

On July 1, 2025, the ATLAS survey detected 3I/ATLAS, identifying its hyperbolic trajectory within a day. By July 21, Hubble captured detailed images, revealing the nucleus to be about 5.6 kilometers across. Early in August, the James Webb Space Telescope analyzed the comet's gas composition, indicating a predominance of carbon dioxide.

By late August, Gemini South recorded the appearance of an anti-solar tail, confirming the comet's increased activity. This feature, distinct from typical comet tails, indicates that the object is actively shedding material as it approaches the Sun.

Official statements

“Seeing such a tail tells you the comet is actively shedding material. It also tells you which direction sunlight and the solar wind are pushing its gases and grains,” said an astronomer from the International Gemini Observatory.

Context

3I/ATLAS is only the third interstellar object observed, following previous discoveries. The rarity of interstellar comets makes each observation significant for understanding the formation and evolution of celestial bodies beyond our solar system. The comet's unusual chemistry, particularly its high carbon dioxide ratio, sets it apart from solar system comets at similar distances.

What’s next

Researchers plan to continue monitoring 3I/ATLAS as it nears perihelion. Upcoming observations will focus on tracking dust production and analyzing the comet's surface changes. The data gathered will help refine models of the object’s behavior and chemistry as it travels back into deep space.
