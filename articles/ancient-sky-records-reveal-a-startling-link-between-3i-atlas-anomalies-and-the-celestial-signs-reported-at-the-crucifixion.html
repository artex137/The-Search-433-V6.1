Ancient Sky Records Reveal a Startling Link Between 3I/ATLAS Anomalies and the Celestial Signs Reported at the Crucifixion

A 2,000-year-old Chinese astronomical record describes a celestial event in 31 AD that aligns with the biblical account of the crucifixion of Jesus of Nazareth, according to findings by historians and astronomers. This discovery emerged from the Mawangdui Silk manuscripts and Eastern Han astronomical annals, which were uncovered by Chinese archaeologists.

This finding is significant as it suggests a remarkable convergence between ancient observations and modern astrophysics. The records indicate that ancient astronomers documented an event that may have had global significance, prompting further investigation into how such anomalies in the sky were interpreted by different civilizations.

Key facts

  • Event date: 31 AD
  • Location: China
  • Civilization: Eastern Han Dynasty
  • Key phrase: “The Man from Heaven dies”
  • Phenomenon: Atmospheric darkening during daylight
  • Related celestial object: Comet 3I/ATLAS

Timeline

In 31 AD, Chinese astronomers recorded a significant celestial event that coincided with the crucifixion of Jesus, which is generally placed between 30 and 33 AD. The Han records describe a sudden atmospheric darkening and a phrase indicating a pivotal death linked to this event. Recent observations of comet 3I/ATLAS have revealed anomalies that echo these ancient descriptions.

The Mawangdui comet atlas, which categorized various cometary behaviors, demonstrates that ancient astronomers were capable of identifying when a celestial object deviated from expected patterns.

Official statements

“The Man from Heaven dies,” the ancient records state, linking this death to an eclipse-like dimming and highlighting its moral implications for humanity.

Context

This discovery adds to a growing body of evidence that suggests ancient civilizations recorded extraordinary sky events with precision. The convergence of these historical records with modern observations of comet 3I/ATLAS illustrates the enduring significance of celestial phenomena in shaping human history.

What’s next

As comet 3I/ATLAS approaches Earth on December 19, researchers will continue to monitor the object closely, seeking to understand its anomalies and their potential connections to historical celestial events.
