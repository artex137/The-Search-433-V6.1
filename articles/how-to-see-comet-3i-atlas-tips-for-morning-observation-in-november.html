How to see comet 3I/Atlas: tips for morning observation in November

Published 2025-11-03 23:17 UTC

Comet 3I/Atlas set to reappear in November

Comet 3I/Atlas, the third interstellar object detected, will reappear in the sky at the end of November 2025, according to astronomers in Santa Catarina. The comet, discovered in July 2025 by the ATLAS telescope in Chile, travels at a speed of 210,000 kilometers per hour.

This event is significant as it provides a unique opportunity for detailed observations of the comet, which approaches Earth at a distance of 270 million kilometers without any risk of collision. Experts from the Federal University of Santa Catarina (UFSC) have planned observation sessions to study the comet's unusual carbon dioxide-rich composition.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/Atlas travels at 210,000 km/h.
  • Approaches Earth at 270 million kilometers.
  • Brightness reaches magnitude 11.
  • Best visibility period is from November 27, 2025.
  • Initial discovery on July 1, 2025, near the constellation Sagittarius.
  • Perihelion reached on October 29, 203 million kilometers from the Sun.
  • Medium-sized telescopes will be used for observations from November 3 to 17.

Timeline

On July 1, 2025, astronomers discovered Comet 3I/Atlas near Sagittarius, initially classifying it as an asteroid. Subsequent analysis confirmed its cometary nature.

The comet reached perihelion on October 29, coming within the orbit of Mars at a distance of 203 million kilometers from the Sun. It passed 30 million kilometers from Mars on October 3, 2025.

On November 3, 2025, the comet will approach Venus at a distance of 97 million kilometers. The best visibility period begins on November 27, during the IAWN global campaign.

Official statements

“The comet’s unique composition offers insights into interstellar chemistry,” said Professor Daniel Ruschel Dutra from UFSC, who coordinates observation efforts in Santa Catarina.

Context

Comet 3I/Atlas is notable for its hyperbolic trajectory, indicating origins outside the Solar System. Its composition, rich in carbon dioxide, differs significantly from local comets, suggesting formation in a distant stellar environment. This aligns with the findings of previous interstellar objects like Oumuamua and Borisov.

What’s next

Following the observations, astronomers will analyze data collected during the comet's approach. The International Asteroid Warning Network (IAWN) will conduct exercises from November 27, 2025, to January 27, 2026, to improve orbital measurements using global telescope data.
