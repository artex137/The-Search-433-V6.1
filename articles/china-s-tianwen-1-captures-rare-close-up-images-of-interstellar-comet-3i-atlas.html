China’s Tianwen-1 Captures Rare Close-Up Images of Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS

China’s Tianwen-1 orbiter has captured rare images of interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS as it passed Mars on October 3, 2023. The images reveal the comet's hazy coma and tail from a distance of approximately 30 million km (18.6 million mi).

This observation is significant because it marks one of the closest views of 3I/ATLAS since its detection on May 7, 2025. The China National Space Agency (CNSA) noted that this achievement demonstrates the capabilities of the Tianwen-1 mission, which has been orbiting Mars for four years and eight months. The images, including a stitched animation of the comet's trajectory, highlight the comet's composition and its journey toward the Sun.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS observed on October 3, 2023.
  • Distance from Tianwen-1 to 3I/ATLAS: approximately 30 million km (18.6 million mi).
  • Diameter of 3I/ATLAS: 5.6 km (3.5 mi).
  • Speed of 3I/ATLAS: approximately 58 km/s (36 mi/s).
  • Relative speed of Tianwen-1: approximately 86 km/s (53.5 mi/s).
  • Mission duration of Tianwen-1: four years and eight months.

Timeline

In early September 2023, the Tianwen-1 team began preparations for the observation of 3I/ATLAS, which included simulations, theoretical modeling, and instrument checks. On October 3, the orbiter successfully captured images of the comet, despite the challenges posed by its distance, small size, and high speed.

On the same day, other Mars orbiters, including the European Space Agency’s Mars Express and ExoMars Trace Gas Orbiter, also imaged 3I/ATLAS using their respective cameras.

Official statements

“This is the latest in a long line of indications that 3I/ATLAS is an interstellar comet, composed of water and volatiles that have been outgassing since it began to approach the Sun,” said a representative from the China National Space Agency.

Context

3I/ATLAS is of particular interest to scientists due to its potential for astronomical and astrobiological research. Studying such interstellar objects can provide insights into the conditions present in other star systems. Previous missions have sought to explore similar topics, and the study of asteroids and comets remains a key area of focus in space exploration.

What’s next

In the coming years, scientists aim to prepare missions capable of intercepting future interstellar objects for close study. The European Space Agency’s Comet Interceptor mission is expected to be completed by 2029, potentially paving the way for further exploration of interstellar comets.
