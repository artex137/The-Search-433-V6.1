New analysis presents unusual features documented in the 3I/ATLAS trajectory and activity

The interstellar object 3I/ATLAS has shown a series of unusual features in its trajectory and physical behavior as it passed through the inner solar system, according to recent analyses. The findings, including a summary by Harvard Professor Avi Loeb, detail anomalies ranging from highly unlikely to minor deviations from expected cometary behavior.

This analysis is significant as it raises questions about the nature of 3I/ATLAS, which exhibits features not consistent with previously observed comets. Researchers have begun detailed documentation of these anomalies to better understand the object’s characteristics and origins.

Key facts

  • 3I/ATLAS is predicted to approach Jupiter at a distance of 53.445 million kilometers on March 16, 2026.
  • The object’s nucleus is approximately one million times more massive than 1I/`Oumuamua.
  • 3I/ATLAS has displayed sunward jets confirmed by HiRISE images taken near Mars.
  • The gas plume around 3I/ATLAS contains higher nickel concentrations compared to known comets.
  • Polarimetric measurements show extreme negative polarization not observed in prior interstellar comets.

Timeline

Recent observations have documented a series of anomalies in the trajectory and activity of 3I/ATLAS:

  • In July, August, and November 2025, 3I/ATLAS exhibited a sunward jet or anti-tail.
  • 3I/ATLAS's arrival direction aligns within nine degrees of the source of the radio "Wow! Signal."
  • Polarimetric measurements have been conducted and reported in peer-reviewed analyses.

Official statements

“The dataset on 3I/ATLAS presents a comprehensive inventory of anomalies categorized by probability and significance,” Professor Avi Loeb stated.

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third known interstellar object to pass through the inner solar system, following 1I/`Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov. Its unusual features, including its mass and gas composition, suggest it may provide insights into the origins of interstellar objects.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to observe 3I/ATLAS to refine measurements and further classify its unusual features. Further analyses are expected to yield more data on its trajectory and physical properties.
