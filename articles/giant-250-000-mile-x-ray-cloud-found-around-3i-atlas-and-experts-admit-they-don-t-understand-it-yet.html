Giant 250,000-mile X-ray Cloud Found Around 3I/ATLAS

Scientists have discovered a giant 250,000-mile-wide X-ray cloud surrounding the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS, which is currently passing through our solar system. The finding, made by the XRISM space mission, has left experts puzzled as they do not fully understand the phenomenon.

This discovery is significant because it challenges existing knowledge about comets. The X-ray glow is the first of its kind observed around an interstellar object, prompting scientists to conduct further investigations before 3I/ATLAS exits the solar system next year.

Key facts

  • X-ray cloud measures approximately 250,000 miles wide
  • Discovered by XRISM, a mission run by Japan's JAXA, NASA, and ESA
  • 3I/ATLAS is the third known interstellar object
  • First detection of X-rays from an interstellar visitor
  • Scientists believe the glow may be caused by charge exchange reactions
  • 3I/ATLAS will leave the solar system in 2024

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first identified as an interstellar object and has been under observation since its discovery. The XRISM mission detected the X-ray glow around 3I/ATLAS recently, marking a significant moment in the study of interstellar objects.

As 3I/ATLAS approaches the Sun, scientists are gathering data to understand the characteristics of the gas cloud and the source of the X-ray emission.

Official statements

“The X-ray picture covers a space millions of kilometers wide, showing a faint but real glow all around the object,” said Harvard professor Avi Loeb.

Context

3I/ATLAS is only the third known interstellar object, following 'Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov. Previous studies of comets have primarily focused on visible light, ultraviolet, radio waves, and infrared emissions. The detection of X-rays adds a new dimension to the understanding of these celestial phenomena.

What’s next

Scientists are preparing additional observations to measure the size of the gas cloud, assess the strength of the X-ray glow, and determine its characteristics compared to typical comets. Time is limited as 3I/ATLAS approaches its departure from the solar system.
