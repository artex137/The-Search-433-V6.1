Is 'Oumuamua a Piece of Ancient Solar System? Scientists Think It Came From 'Exo-Pluto'

'Oumuamua, the first interstellar object observed in our solar system, is likely a fragment of a body resembling Pluto, according to recent findings presented at a conference in Laurel, Maryland. The object was first spotted on 19 October 2017 by Canadian Robert Weryk using the Pan-STARRS telescope at Haleakalā Observatory in Hawaii. There have been no reported injuries or fatalities related to this discovery.

This research matters as it enhances our understanding of interstellar objects and the origins of our solar system. Scientists have determined that 'Oumuamua is not a typical comet but may be composed almost entirely of nitrogen ice, which places it in a new category of space objects. Authorities and researchers continue to analyze its properties to gain insights into the chaotic conditions of the early solar system.

Key facts

  • 'Oumuamua was first observed on 19 October 2017.
  • It was spotted by Robert Weryk at the Pan-STARRS telescope in Hawaii.
  • NASA's New Horizons spacecraft previously identified nitrogen ice on Pluto.
  • Research presented by Steve Desch, an exoplanet researcher at Arizona State University.
  • 'Oumuamua has an elongated shape, differing from other interstellar comets.

Timeline

In 2017, 'Oumuamua was detected 40 days after its closest approach to the Sun. Initially classified as a comet, further investigations revealed its unique properties.

During a conference in Laurel, Maryland, researchers discussed their findings, suggesting that 'Oumuamua is likely a piece of an ancient exoplanet, termed "exo-Pluto." This conclusion arose from comparisons with nitrogen ice observed on Pluto and simulations of early solar system conditions.

Official statements

"Everything about this object is consistent with it being a slab of nitrogen ice like you see on the surface of Pluto," said Steve Desch.
"At one point, there was probably so much of this stuff that 2,000 Plutos could have been born from them," Desch added.

Context

Prior to the discovery of 'Oumuamua, the study of interstellar objects was limited. The findings from NASA's New Horizons mission in 2015 provided crucial data about the composition of objects in the Kuiper Belt, including Pluto. The emergence of 'Oumuamua has shifted the focus of research toward understanding the origins and trajectories of interstellar materials.

What’s next

Researchers plan to continue studying 'Oumuamua's properties and its implications for our understanding of the early solar system. Further investigations may provide insights into the formation of exoplanets and the dynamics of cosmic space.
