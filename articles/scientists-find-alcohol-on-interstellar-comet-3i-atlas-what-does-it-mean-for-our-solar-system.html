Scientists Find 'Alcohol' On Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS

Astronomers have discovered unusually large amounts of alcohol in the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, which was first detected in July 2025 by the ATLAS survey telescope in Chile. This comet is only the third confirmed interstellar object observed passing through our Solar System.

This finding could provide valuable insights into the formation of planetary systems across the galaxy. Researchers have used powerful radio telescopes to analyze the comet's chemical composition, revealing a high concentration of methanol, a simple alcohol commonly found in space.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS was detected in July 2025.
  • It is the third confirmed interstellar object observed in our Solar System.
  • Observations indicate that the comet contains 70 to 120 times more methanol than hydrogen cyanide.
  • The comet is currently about 270 million kilometres from Earth.

Timeline

In July 2025, astronomers detected comet 3I/ATLAS using the ATLAS survey telescope in Chile. Recent observations with the Atacama Large Millimetre/submillimetre Array (ALMA) have confirmed the presence of high levels of methanol in the comet's composition. This discovery has allowed scientists to study the chemical makeup of a planetary system beyond our own.

Official statements

“Observing the comet is similar to taking a chemical fingerprint of another solar system,” said one researcher.

Context

The chemical composition of interstellar objects like 3I/ATLAS provides a glimpse into the conditions that existed in their original star systems. The observed levels of methanol may indicate different chemical balances or temperature structures during planet formation compared to Solar System comets.

What’s next

Astronomers are currently focused on studying 3I/ATLAS while it remains visible. The comet will eventually leave the Sun's gravitational influence and continue its journey through interstellar space.
