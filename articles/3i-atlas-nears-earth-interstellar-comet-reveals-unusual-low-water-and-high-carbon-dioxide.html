3I/ATLAS Nears Earth: Interstellar Comet Reveals Unusual Low Water And High Carbon Dioxide

The interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS is currently on a fast outbound trajectory away from the sun, with its closest approach to Earth scheduled for December 19, 2025. The comet, which was detected on July 1, 2025, has revealed a unique chemical composition, including a high level of carbon dioxide and a low concentration of water ice. No injuries or fatalities have been reported.

This discovery is significant for astronomers as it provides a rare opportunity to study the composition of an object from beyond our solar system. The European Space Agency (ESA) has coordinated a global response to gather data from various observatories, including the James Webb Space Telescope, to analyze the comet's characteristics before it accelerates further into interstellar space.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS detected on July 1, 2025.
  • Closest approach to Earth on December 19, 2025, at a distance of 269 million km (167.1 million mi).
  • High carbon dioxide and low water ice concentrations identified in comet's composition.
  • Observations conducted by ESA, NASA, JAXA, and various ground-based telescopes.
  • Comet expected to cross Jupiter's orbit in spring 2026.
  • Projected to be out of range of Hubble and JWST by the end of 2028.

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first detected on July 1, 2025. Following its detection, a coordinated surveillance effort began, involving multiple observatories worldwide. The comet emerged from behind the sun in early November 2025, leading to increased observations and data collection.

The ESA utilized various telescopes, including the XXM-Newton, JAXA's XRISM, and the James Webb Space Telescope, to study the comet's unique chemical makeup.

As the comet approaches Earth, astronomers are racing against time to gather as much information as possible before it continues its journey into interstellar space.

Official statements

“The unique ratio of elements challenges our current assumptions about how comets are formed,” said an ESA spokesperson. “This comet is a time capsule, allowing us to sample the original building blocks of an entirely different planetary system.”

Context

The arrival of 3I/ATLAS marks only the third confirmed interstellar object observed by astronomers. Previous interstellar visitors have provided insights into the formation of other solar systems, making this current investigation particularly important for understanding the chemical diversity in the galaxy.

What’s next

As 3I/ATLAS approaches its closest point to Earth, astronomers will continue to gather data to unlock the mysteries of its composition. This information will be crucial for future studies of interstellar objects and their implications for our understanding of the universe.
