New Research on 3I/ATLAS Revives Cryovolcanism Debate—but the Science Tells a More Nuanced Story

A recent study has sparked renewed discussion about the potential for cryovolcanism on comet 3I/ATLAS, according to a paper released on November 24 by researchers Josep M. Trigo-Rodríguez, Maria Gritsevich, and Jürgen Blum. The study has drawn significant attention, but a careful reading indicates a more complex scenario than the headlines suggest.

The significance of this research lies in its implications for our understanding of 3I/ATLAS, an interstellar object approaching Earth. While media reports have suggested that the comet is erupting with "ice volcanoes," the authors clarify that their use of the term "cryovolcanic activation" is metaphorical. They emphasize that 3I/ATLAS is not capable of sustaining internal heat or subsurface oceans, which are essential for true cryovolcanism.

Key facts

  • Study published on November 24, 2023.
  • Authors: Josep M. Trigo-Rodríguez, Maria Gritsevich, Jürgen Blum.
  • 3I/ATLAS is a kilometer-scale nucleus, roughly the size of Manhattan.
  • Comet's close pass to Earth is scheduled for December 19, 2023.
  • The study discusses processes such as sublimation and chemical activation.

Timeline

On November 24, 2023, a paper detailing the characteristics of comet 3I/ATLAS was published. The study analyzed various aspects, including thermal constraints and mineralogical modeling. Following its release, media coverage mischaracterized the findings, leading to widespread speculation about cryovolcanism. The authors have since clarified these points, stating that the observed phenomena are due to surface-driven processes rather than internal geological activity.

Official statements

“Cryovolcanism appears in the document not as a proven geological phenomenon occurring within 3I/ATLAS, but as a scientific analogy,” the authors stated.
“The real significance of the Trigo-Rodríguez study is not whether 3I/ATLAS hosts cryovolcanoes—it does not—but that its surface may exhibit pressure-driven activation events far more dynamic than models predicted,” they added.

Context

Prior research on comets has shown that bodies with internal heat, such as Enceladus and Pluto, can sustain cryovolcanism. However, 3I/ATLAS lacks the necessary geological features to support similar processes. The recent study highlights the unique characteristics of this interstellar object, which may provide valuable insights into the behavior of comets in general.

What’s next

As comet 3I/ATLAS approaches Earth, scientists will continue to monitor its activity closely. New images may reveal additional jets and structures, and the scientific community must remain cautious in interpreting these observations within appropriate geological frameworks.
