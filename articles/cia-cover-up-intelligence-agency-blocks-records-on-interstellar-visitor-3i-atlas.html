CIA Cover-Up? Intelligence Agency Blocks Records on Interstellar Visitor 3I/ATLAS

In the early hours of July 1, 2025, a telescope in the high deserts of Chile detected a faint moving speck identified as the comet 3I/ATLAS. This 'exocomet' is the third object from another star system observed entering our solar system, and its unusual behavior has raised concerns beyond astronomical interest.

The discovery has prompted scrutiny from both the scientific community and national security agencies. With limited high-resolution images and a lack of transparency from the U.S. government, some experts question the motives behind the classified status surrounding the object. The CIA has refused to confirm or deny the existence of records related to 3I/ATLAS, raising suspicions about its potential significance.

Key facts

  • Object 3I/ATLAS discovered on July 1, 2025, by the Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System in Rio Hurtado, Chile.
  • Object is moving at 61 km/s (140,000 mph) on a hyperbolic path from interstellar space.
  • Reached perihelion on October 29, 2025, at a distance of 1.36 AU (about 126 million miles) from the sun.
  • Passed Earth on December 19, 2025, at a distance of 1.8 AU.
  • CIA has issued a 'Glomar' response to a Freedom of Information Act request regarding records on 3I/ATLAS.
  • Harvard astrophysicist Avi Loeb has identified 16 anomalies associated with the object.
  • Next close approach to Jupiter is scheduled for March 16, 2026.

Timeline

On July 1, 2025, the comet 3I/ATLAS was discovered in Chile. It was observed moving at high speed, raising interest among scientists and intelligence agencies alike. The object reached its closest point to the sun on October 29, 2025, and subsequently passed Earth on December 19, 2025.

Following the discovery, the CIA received a Freedom of Information Act request from UFO researcher John Greenwald Jr. The agency's response has been a point of contention, with critics arguing that it suggests more than mere academic interest in the object's trajectory.

Official statements

“The CIA can neither confirm nor deny the existence or non-existence of records responsive to the request,” the agency stated, citing national security concerns.
Avi Loeb commented, “The secrecy surrounding the object hampers the scientific community's ability to understand it. If 3I/ATLAS is indeed a natural phenomenon, the classification of data seems unnecessary.”

Context

The classification of information related to 3I/ATLAS has sparked debate among scientists and researchers. Prior incidents involving unidentified aerial phenomena have led to increased scrutiny of government transparency regarding extraterrestrial objects. Critics argue that the CIA's response is indicative of a broader trend of secrecy surrounding potential national security threats.

What’s next

As 3I/ATLAS continues its journey away from the sun, upcoming observations are critical. The scientific community is urging the CIA to provide clarity on the object's status before its close approach to Jupiter in March 2026.
