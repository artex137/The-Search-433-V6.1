Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS is Making Its Way Closer to Earth—And New Research Suggests It Probably Isn’t Alone

Published 2025-09-25 11:37 UTC

Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS Approaches Earth, New Research Suggests More Could Follow

Astronomers have confirmed that the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS is on its way closer to Earth, with its closest approach expected next month. The comet, which is only the third confirmed interstellar object observed in our solar system, will pass Mars before moving behind the Sun, obscuring it from view.

This discovery is significant as researchers believe that 3I/ATLAS may not be a solitary visitor. Advanced detection methods are likely to reveal more interstellar objects entering our solar system, which could enhance our understanding of these cosmic phenomena. Aster Taylor, a Ph.D. student at the University of Michigan, stated that the Vera C. Rubin Observatory's ongoing survey will increase the chances of discovering similar objects in the future.

Key facts

  • 3I/ATLAS is currently on a trajectory that will take it past Mars next month.
  • This comet is the third confirmed interstellar object detected in our solar system.
  • The Vera C. Rubin Observatory has already discovered over 2,100 new asteroids during its commissioning phase.
  • The observatory's Large Synoptic Survey Telescope (LSST) features a 3.2-gigapixel camera.
  • Some studies suggest that the observatory may detect one to two interstellar objects each year.
  • Other estimates predict the potential discovery of up to 70 interstellar objects annually.

Timeline

The following key events outline the journey of 3I/ATLAS and the advancements in detection methods:

  • 3I/ATLAS was confirmed as an interstellar object by NASA.
  • The Vera C. Rubin Observatory began its commissioning phase earlier this year, successfully discovering over 2,100 new asteroids.
  • During this phase, 3I/ATLAS was photographed days before its official discovery.
  • The comet is expected to pass Mars next month and will then move behind the Sun.

Official statements

“We expect to discover many more interstellar objects in the coming decades, as the Vera C. Rubin Observatory’s Legacy Survey of Space and Time is underway,” said Aster Taylor, Ph.D. student at the University of Michigan.
“Having ~15 would be a massive improvement in the sample size and vastly improve our understanding of interstellar objects’ composition, ages, and dynamics,” Taylor added.

Context

Prior to 3I/ATLAS, astronomers had only detected two other interstellar objects. The enhanced detection capabilities of modern observatories, such as the Rubin Observatory, are expected to significantly increase the frequency of such discoveries, moving interstellar objects from rare curiosities to more common observations.

What’s next

As the Vera C. Rubin Observatory continues its survey, astronomers anticipate more encounters with interstellar objects like 3I/ATLAS. The increased detection rate could lead to a deeper understanding of these celestial visitors and their origins.
