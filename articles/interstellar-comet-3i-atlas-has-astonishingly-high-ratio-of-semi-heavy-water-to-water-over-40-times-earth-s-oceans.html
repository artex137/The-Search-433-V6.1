Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS Has Astonishingly High Ratio Of Semi-Heavy Water To Water, Over 40 Times Earth's Oceans

Comet 3I/ATLAS has been identified as an exceptionally rare interstellar object, with new observations revealing a significant ratio of semi-heavy water compared to regular water. These findings were published following analysis by the Atacama Large Millimeter Array (ALMA) shortly after the comet's closest approach to the Sun on October 29, 2025.

This discovery matters because it suggests that the conditions in the region where 3I/ATLAS originated are vastly different from those of our Solar System. Researchers aim to understand the implications for planetary formation and the history of interstellar objects.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS is the third known interstellar object.
  • Observations were taken by ALMA shortly after perihelion on October 29, 2025.
  • The ratio of semi-heavy water to regular water in 3I/ATLAS is over 40 times that found in Earth's oceans.
  • The ratio is 30 times larger than in Solar System comets.
  • Lead author: Luis E. Salazar Manzano, graduate researcher at the University of Michigan.
  • Published in the journal Nature Astronomy.

Timeline

On October 29, 2025, Comet 3I/ATLAS made its closest approach to the Sun, known as perihelion. ALMA conducted observations of the comet six days later, revealing its unusual composition. The analysis indicated an extraordinary amount of semi-heavy water, leading researchers to conclude that the comet's formation environment was significantly colder than that of our Solar System.

Official statements

“The chemical processes that lead to the enhancement of deuterated water are really sensitive to temperature and usually require environments colder than about 30 Kelvin, or about minus 406 degrees Fahrenheit,” said Luis E. Salazar Manzano.
“Each interstellar comet brings a little bit of its history, its fossils, from elsewhere. We don’t know exactly where, but with instruments like ALMA, we can begin to understand the conditions of that place and compare them to our own,” explained co-author Assistant Professor Teresa Paneque-Carreño.
“Our new observations show that the conditions that led to the formation of our Solar System are much different from how planetary systems evolved in different parts of our Galaxy,” said Salazar Manzano.

Context

Prior to 3I/ATLAS, the only known interstellar objects were 1I/’Oumuamua, discovered in 2017, and Comet 2I/Borisov in 2019. Both objects have provided limited insights into the conditions of their respective formation environments. The new findings from 3I/ATLAS offer a clearer contrast between the formation conditions of our Solar System and those of other planetary systems.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to analyze the data from ALMA to gain further insights into the formation of 3I/ATLAS and other interstellar objects. Future studies may focus on comparing the chemical compositions of various interstellar comets to enhance our understanding of planetary system evolution.
