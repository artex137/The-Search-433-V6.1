NASA spacecraft captures comet 3I/ATLAS on its way to Jupiter, revealing elements invisible to the human eye

NASA has released an image of the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, captured by the Europa Clipper spacecraft on its journey to Jupiter. The image was taken on 6 November 2025 from a distance of approximately 164 million km (about 102 million miles) away.

This observation is significant as it provides scientists with a unique view of 3I/ATLAS, an object that has traveled through space for an estimated 7 billion years. The Europa Clipper mission, launched in October 2024, aims to study Europa, one of Jupiter's largest moons, which is believed to harbor a subsurface ocean. NASA scientists have utilized the spacecraft's Europa Ultraviolet Spectrograph (Europa-UVS) instrument to gather data on the comet's composition and the elements within its coma.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS discovered on 1 July 2025
  • The comet is the oldest ever observed, older than the Sun
  • Image captured over a period of seven hours
  • Europa Clipper spacecraft launched in October 2024
  • Europa Clipper will reach the Jupiter system in 2030

Timeline

On 1 July 2025, astronomers discovered interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS. Following its discovery, scientists directed various telescopes to observe it. The Europa Clipper spacecraft, launched in October 2024, captured an image of the comet on 6 November 2025, utilizing its Europa-UVS instrument to analyze the comet's composition.

Official statements

The data from the Europa-UVS instrument will help scientists learn more about the composition and distribution of elements in the comet’s coma, according to NASA.

Context

Comet 3I/ATLAS is notable for not originating from the Solar System, unlike most comets. As it approaches the Sun, it heats up, producing a coma and tail from its frozen body of ice and dust. The Europa Clipper mission is part of NASA's broader efforts to explore celestial bodies and search for signs of life beyond Earth.

What’s next

The Europa Clipper spacecraft will continue its journey to Europa, where it will study the moon's atmosphere and surface and search for plumes of water that may indicate the presence of life.
