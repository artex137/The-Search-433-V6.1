Potential hostile alien threat hurtling through solar system has started bizarre action

An interstellar object known as 3I/ATLAS is currently moving through our solar system and has been observed exhibiting unusual behaviour as it approaches the Sun. NASA states that this object is only the third interstellar object ever observed in this region, and it will come within approximately 130 million miles of Earth on 30 October 2023.

This observation matters because it provides scientists with insights into the behaviour of comets and interstellar objects as they interact with the solar system. Authorities, including NASA and astronomers, are closely monitoring 3I/ATLAS with a series of telescopes to study its characteristics before it becomes too difficult to observe.

Key facts

  • Object: 3I/ATLAS
  • Closest approach to Earth: 130 million miles on 30 October 2023
  • Current distance from Earth: Approximately 170 million miles
  • First observed change: 27 August 2023 by the Gemini South telescope
  • Next anticipated visibility: December 2023

Timeline

On 27 August 2023, astronomers using the Gemini South telescope detected that 3I/ATLAS is developing a large, growing tail, indicating increased activity as it approaches the Sun. This change suggests that the ice and dust surrounding the object are heating up.

Harvard astronomer Avi Loeb has noted that 3I/ATLAS is also exhibiting an unusual 'anti-tail' that points towards the Sun, contrasting with typical comet behaviour. This discovery has raised questions about the nature of the object and whether it could be an alien spacecraft, although Loeb maintains a measured stance on this possibility.

Official statements

"The anti-tail is an extension of the glow of scattered sunlight around 3I/ATLAS towards the Sun and not away from it - as typically the case for comets," Loeb stated. "This anomalous anti-tail, not a result of geometric perspective, had never been reported before for solar system comets."

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third interstellar object observed in our solar system, following previous observations of two other unidentified objects. The behaviour of 3I/ATLAS may help scientists understand how interstellar objects can resemble comets from our own solar system, providing context for further research.

What’s next

Scientists will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS as it approaches the Sun. Further observations are expected in December 2023 when the object returns to a more observable position.
