Interstellar Object 3I/ATLAS's Tail Appears To Have Changed Direction, Nordic Optical Telescope Finds

New observations from the Nordic Optical Telescope in the Canary Islands, Spain, indicate that the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS has experienced a change in the direction of its tail, which now predominantly points away from the Sun. The discovery was made on July 1, when astronomers from the Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System (ATLAS) first identified the object as it traveled through the Solar System.

This finding is significant as it may provide insights into the object's characteristics and origin, which is believed to be a 10-billion-year-old time capsule from an earlier age of the universe. Astronomers are currently monitoring 3I/ATLAS closely to better understand its behavior and the implications of its unusual tail structure.

Key facts

  • Discovered on July 1, 2023, by ATLAS astronomers.
  • 3I/ATLAS is the third known interstellar object, following 1I/'Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov.
  • Observations have noted a change from a Sun-facing dust fan to an anti-solar dust tail.
  • Comet has lost approximately 2 million tons of mass since July.
  • The object is expected to be in view again in December 2023.

Timeline

On July 1, 2023, astronomers at ATLAS observed 3I/ATLAS moving through the Solar System. Following its discovery, they noted several unusual features of the comet. By September, the observations confirmed that the anti-tail had developed into a tail. The change in tail direction was attributed to the effects of solar radiation and the comet's unique composition.

As the comet approached the Sun, it was observed that the tail morphology transitioned, leading to the current observations of the anti-solar tail. The object has been tracked closely to study its mass loss and activity as it nears perihelion.

Official statements

“The morphology of the comet changes from a Sun-facing dust fan in the early July observations to one dominated by an anti-solar dust tail at later dates,” researchers noted in their findings.
“Observations of interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS at 3.8 au show an elongated coma similar to a cometary tail but pointing in the direction of the Sun,” stated a preprint paper by Harvard astronomers Avi Loeb and Eric Keto.

Context

Anti-tails are rare phenomena observed in comets. While they can be optical illusions, the current observations suggest that 3I/ATLAS's anti-tail is a true feature of the comet. Historical observations of anti-tails date back to at least 1974 with Comet Kohoutek. This makes the current findings noteworthy in the study of interstellar objects.

What’s next

Astronomers will continue to observe 3I/ATLAS as it approaches the Sun, with the next observations expected in December 2023. These studies aim to further explore the object's properties and its journey through the Solar System.
