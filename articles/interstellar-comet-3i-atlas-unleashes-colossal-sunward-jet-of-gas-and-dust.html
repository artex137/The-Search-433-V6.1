Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS Unleashes Colossal Sunward Jet of Gas and Dust

Astronomers have captured vivid images of interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS ejecting a massive jet of gas and dust toward the Sun on August 2, 2025. This event confirms the comet's active nature as it approaches perihelion, scheduled for October 30, 2025. No injuries or fatalities have been reported.

This discovery is significant for understanding the behavior of comets and the interstellar medium. Authorities, including NASA and various observatories, are monitoring the comet's trajectory and characteristics as it approaches the Sun, which may enhance models of interstellar material dynamics.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS discovered in late June 2025 and verified by NASA in early July.
  • Comet measures between 3 and 7 miles (5 to 11 kilometers) in diameter.
  • Closest approach to the Sun is on October 30, 2025.
  • Jet extends approximately 6,200 miles (10,000 kilometers) from the comet's surface.
  • Observations conducted using the Two-meter Twin Telescope at Spain's Teide Observatory.
  • Ultraviolet spectroscopy has detected cyanide (CN) and atomic nickel (Ni I) in the coma.
  • NASA's Hubble Space Telescope and James Webb Space Telescope have contributed data to the observations.

Timeline

Comet 3I/ATLAS was discovered in late June 2025. NASA verified its status as an interstellar object in early July 2025. On August 2, 2025, astronomers captured images showing the comet ejecting a jet of gas and dust.

As it approaches perihelion, the comet's trajectory has been closely monitored. It will pass within tens of millions of miles of Mars, Venus, and Jupiter while remaining unobservable from Earth during solar conjunction on October 21, 2025.

Official statements

“These observations affirm that 3I/ATLAS operates precisely as a comet should, providing invaluable data on the diversity of volatile chemistries across star systems,” said lead observer M. Serra-Ricart of the Instituto de Astrofísica de Canarias.

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third confirmed interstellar object to enter our solar system, with an estimated age predating the Sun by billions of years. This discovery adds to the understanding of primordial building blocks exchanged between stellar nurseries and the role of the interstellar medium in galactic chemical evolution.

What’s next

NASA and other agencies will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS for potential outbursts or fragmentation events that could alter its trajectory or brightness. Follow-up observations will involve satellites like GOES-19 as the comet moves closer to the Sun.
