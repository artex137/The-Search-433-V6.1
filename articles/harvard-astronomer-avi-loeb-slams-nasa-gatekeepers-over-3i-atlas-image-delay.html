Harvard Astronomer Avi Loeb Slams NASA 'Gatekeepers' Over 3I/ATLAS Image Delay

Harvard astronomer Avi Loeb has criticized NASA for delaying the release of HiRISE images of the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS due to a recent 43-day government shutdown. The images are expected to be released within days, as of November 14, 2025, after a prolonged wait that has frustrated the astronomical community.

The anticipated release of the HiRISE images is significant for scientists seeking to understand the unique characteristics of 3I/ATLAS, particularly its unusual anti-tail feature. NASA's delay has drawn criticism from experts who argue that time-sensitive scientific data should take precedence over bureaucratic procedures.

Key facts

  • NASA is expected to release HiRISE images of 3I/ATLAS shortly.
  • The release follows a 43-day government shutdown.
  • The last high-resolution observation of 3I/ATLAS was on July 21, 2025, by the Hubble Space Telescope.
  • The HiRISE images are expected to have a spatial resolution of approximately 30 kilometres per pixel.
  • 3I/ATLAS passed within 29 million kilometres of Mars on October 2-3, 2025.

Timeline

On July 21, 2025, the Hubble Space Telescope captured an image of 3I/ATLAS, revealing its anti-tail feature. The object passed near Mars on October 2-3, 2025. Following a 43-day government shutdown, NASA's release of the HiRISE images is now imminent, with expectations set for the coming days as of November 14, 2025.

Official statements

“This bureaucratic paralysis is precisely what I refer to as 'gatekeeping'—prioritising protocol over time-sensitive, crucial scientific discovery,” said Avi Loeb.
“The truth about the nature of 3I/ATLAS will be revealed by publicly shared data and not by any storyline of gatekeepers,” Loeb added.

Context

The delay in releasing the HiRISE images has reignited discussions about the importance of timely access to scientific data. Loeb's comments reflect a growing concern within the scientific community regarding the impact of bureaucracy on research and discovery.

What’s next

The astronomical community awaits the release of the HiRISE images, which are expected to provide critical insights into the characteristics of 3I/ATLAS. Scientists are hopeful that this release will prompt a reevaluation of how time-sensitive data is managed within NASA and other scientific agencies.
