Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS Now Visible From Earth – Here’s How to Spot It

Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS is now visible from Earth with the right equipment, following its closest approach to the Sun on October 30, 2025. Discovered on July 1, 2025, by the ATLAS telescope in Chile, the comet is a rare sight for both sky-watchers and scientists.

The significance of 3I/ATLAS lies in its status as the third interstellar object discovered in our solar system. Astronomers aim to study its composition, which is likely rich in carbon dioxide, to gain insights into how comets form in distant star systems. The comet is moving at over 210,000 kilometres per hour and will not return after this passage.

Key facts

  • Discovered: July 1, 2025, by the ATLAS telescope in Chile
  • Closest approach to the Sun: October 30, 2025
  • Current distance from Earth: 1.8 astronomical units (about 270 million kilometres)
  • Speed: Over 210,000 kilometres per hour
  • Visibility: Requires a medium-sized telescope; visible in early November and December 2025

Timeline

Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS was discovered on July 1, 2025. After passing its closest approach to the Sun on October 30, 2025, the comet is now making its way back into the night sky.

It is expected to be visible in the early morning sky starting in early November 2025, particularly low on the eastern horizon before dawn.

Official statements

“By studying the comet, astronomers hope to understand more about how comets form in other locations in the galaxy,” according to space research reports.

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third interstellar object detected in our solar system, providing a valuable opportunity for both professional astronomers and amateur sky-watchers to observe a celestial body that originated from another star system.

What’s next

Sky-watchers are encouraged to use medium-sized telescopes to observe 3I/ATLAS in early November through December 2025, as viewing conditions may vary based on weather and light pollution.
