Alcohol in comet? Rare interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS found packed with Methanol, stunning scientists

The interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS has been found to contain large amounts of methanol, according to astronomers using powerful telescopes in Chile. This discovery marks a significant moment in the study of celestial objects and their compositions.

The finding is important because it provides a unique opportunity for scientists to examine the original material from another star system. The comet, which is just the third interstellar object identified entering our solar system, was first spotted in July 2025 by the Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS was discovered in July 2025.
  • High levels of methanol were detected in the gas surrounding the comet.
  • The ratio of methanol to hydrogen cyanide ranged from 70 to 120.
  • The Atacama Large Millimeter/submillimeter Array (ALMA) in Chile was used for observations.
  • This comet is the third interstellar object identified in our solar system.

Timeline

In July 2025, astronomers identified the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS. As the comet approached the Sun, its icy surface heated up, releasing gases and dust into a glowing cloud known as the coma. Observations conducted using the ALMA telescope revealed the presence of methanol and hydrogen cyanide, with scientists finding an unusually high abundance of methanol.

Official statements

“The chemistry of the comet might assist astronomers in understanding the formation of planets and solar systems around other stars,” a spokesperson from the ALMA observatory stated.

Context

The discovery of methanol in 3I/ATLAS is significant as methanol is rarely found in space. The comet's chemical patterns suggest it formed under conditions different from most comets observed in our solar system. Previous studies of interstellar objects have also contributed to our understanding of the origins of solar systems.

What’s next

Ongoing observations of 3I/ATLAS will continue as scientists aim to gather more data about its unusual composition and its implications for understanding distant star systems.
