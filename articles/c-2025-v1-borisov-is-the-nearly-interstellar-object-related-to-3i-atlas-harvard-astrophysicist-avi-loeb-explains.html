C/2025 V1 Borisov: Is The 'Nearly Interstellar' Object Related To 3I/ATLAS? Harvard Astrophysicist Avi Loeb Explains

C/2025 V1 Borisov: New Comet Discovered Amid Speculation

A new celestial object, C/2025 V1 Borisov, has been detected between Earth and the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, drawing significant attention since its discovery on November 2, 2025. The comet does not appear to have a tail, a common feature of cometary bodies, according to astronomers.

This discovery is important for the scientific community as it challenges existing beliefs about the origins and characteristics of comets. Authorities, including astronomers from Harvard University, are examining the object's trajectory and potential links to 3I/ATLAS.

Key facts

  • Discovery date: November 2, 2025
  • Closest approach to Earth: November 11, 2025 at a distance of 103,119,940 kilometers (0.68 AU)
  • Perihelion date: November 16, 2025
  • Closest separation to 3I/ATLAS: 75 million kilometers (0.5 AU)
  • Distance without propulsion: 225 million kilometers (1.5 AU)
  • Origin: Oort Cloud
  • Discovery by: G. Borisov

Timeline

On November 2, 2025, G. Borisov discovered C/2025 V1 Borisov. The comet is set to reach its closest point to Earth on November 11, 2025, at a distance of 103,119,940 kilometers. Its perihelion, the point at which it is closest to the Sun, will occur on November 16, 2025.

Speculation about the comet's origin and its potential relationship to 3I/ATLAS has increased since its discovery. Harvard astrophysicist Avi Loeb has provided insights into the object's trajectory and characteristics.

Official statements

Avi Loeb stated, "C/2025 V1 Borisov is not related to 3I/ATLAS if it did not employ non-gravitational propulsion."
Loeb further explained, "The measured non-gravitational acceleration of 3I/ATLAS is insufficient to bridge the gap with C/2025 V1."

Context

Prior to the discovery of C/2025 V1 Borisov, 3I/ATLAS was already a subject of interest due to its interstellar origin. The Oort Cloud, where C/2025 V1 is believed to originate, is thought to contain billions of planetesimals created approximately 4.6 billion years ago. This context highlights the ongoing interest in celestial bodies originating from beyond our solar system.

What’s next

Scientists will continue to monitor C/2025 V1 Borisov as it approaches Earth. Further observations may clarify its characteristics and trajectory, contributing to our understanding of cometary bodies and their origins.
