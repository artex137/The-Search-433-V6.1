Comet 3I/ATLAS Reveals CO₂-Rich Secrets from Another Star System

Comet 3I/ATLAS, an interstellar visitor, has unveiled a CO₂-rich chemical composition during its closest approach to Earth, which occurred recently. Discovered by NASA's ATLAS survey telescope in Chile, this fast-moving comet is only the third confirmed interstellar object to enter our solar system.

This discovery is significant as it provides insights into the chemical diversity of materials formed around other stars. Scientists are utilizing data collected from various space missions to analyze the comet's unique composition and enhance models of planetary formation.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS has a hyperbolic trajectory, exceeding 57 km/s.
  • It was discovered by NASA's ATLAS survey telescope in Chile.
  • The comet has shown a pronounced CO₂ signature, differing from typical solar-system comets.
  • NASA's Europa Clipper and Parker Solar Probe have provided crucial observations of the comet.
  • ESA's XMM-Newton and Japan's XRISM missions have detected X-ray emissions from the comet.
  • Amateur astronomers can observe the comet, currently at an estimated magnitude of 12.8, near the constellation Leo.

Timeline

Comet 3I/ATLAS has followed a trajectory that brought it close to the Sun. Key events include:

  • 18 October to 5 November 2025: NASA's Parker Solar Probe captured daily images of the comet.
  • 3 December 2025: ESA's XMM-Newton observed the comet for X-ray emissions.
  • 26-28 November 2025: Japan's XRISM mission conducted observations of the comet.

Official statements

“We can see gases come off the comet, and water molecules break apart into hydrogen and oxygen atoms,” said researchers from NASA's Europa Clipper.

Context

The study of Comet 3I/ATLAS contributes to a growing body of knowledge regarding interstellar objects. Previous interstellar visitors have also provided insights into the formation of other star systems, highlighting the importance of studying such rare occurrences.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to analyze data from various missions to further understand the comet's composition and its implications for planetary formation models.
