Interstellar comet reveals how alien worlds may differ from ours

Scientists have discovered an interstellar comet, known as 3I/ATLAS, which is currently passing through our solar system. Officially identified in early July, the comet has shown unexpected activity while traveling through space, prompting researchers to study its characteristics before it becomes too faint to observe.

This discovery is significant as it provides insights into the composition of comets from other solar systems, which may differ from those within our own. Researchers, led by scientists at Michigan State University, have used data from NASA’s Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite (TESS) to analyze the comet’s unique behavior and chemical makeup.

Key facts

  • 3I/ATLAS is the third known interstellar comet.
  • Discovered in early July 2023, with observations dating back to May 2023.
  • Researchers used TESS images to identify the comet.
  • The comet has shown activity earlier than typical comets, suggesting a different composition.
  • Data is being collected using NASA’s James Webb Space Telescope.
  • Research published in The Astrophysical Journal Letters.

Timeline

In early July 2023, scientists officially discovered 3I/ATLAS. However, the comet had been in the sky since May 2023, initially going unnoticed due to its faintness. Researchers utilized TESS images to analyze the comet's trajectory and behavior, revealing signs of activity before it approached the Sun.

The discovery sparked a global effort among astronomers to gather data on 3I/ATLAS, as it will soon be too distant to observe effectively. The team continues to study the comet using various astronomical tools to better understand its properties.

Official statements

“There are so few interstellar objects that we’ve detected in our solar system, and they each seem to be unique,” said Adina Feinstein, an assistant professor of physics and astronomy at Michigan State University. “3I/ATLAS gives us an opportunity to study other solar systems up close and personal, without actually needing to visit them.”
“Capturing as many observations from this prediscovery period is essential to our understanding of how these objects ‘turn on’ as they approach our Sun,” said John Noonan, a postdoctoral fellow at Auburn University.

Context

The discovery of 3I/ATLAS adds to the growing list of interstellar objects detected in our solar system. Each object provides unique insights into the formation and composition of other solar systems. Previous studies have suggested that comets and asteroids from different systems may have distinct chemical makeups.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to observe 3I/ATLAS as long as it remains visible. They aim to publish new findings regarding its composition and behavior, contributing to our understanding of interstellar objects and the broader context of our place in the universe.
