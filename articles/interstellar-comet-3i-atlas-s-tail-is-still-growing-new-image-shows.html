Interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS's tail is still growing, new image shows

Interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS (C/2025 N1 ATLAS) has shown a significant increase in its ion tail as it travels through the inner solar system, according to a new image captured by the Virtual Telescope Project in Manciano, Italy. The image was taken at 11:31 p.m. EDT on November 10 (0431 GMT on November 11) and reveals a longer and more structured ion tail, indicating heightened activity from the comet.

This development is important for astronomers as it provides a unique opportunity to study the behavior of an interstellar comet under the influence of solar radiation. The Virtual Telescope Project plans to continue monitoring 3I/ATLAS as it approaches the inner solar system, offering insights into its composition and the conditions of its origin.

Key facts

  • Comet: 3I/ATLAS (C/2025 N1 ATLAS)
  • Date of image capture: November 10, 2023
  • Time of image capture: 11:31 p.m. EDT (0431 GMT November 11)
  • Location of observation: Manciano, Italy
  • Ion tail length: approximately 0.7 degrees across the sky
  • Previous detection: July 2023
  • Third confirmed interstellar object detected, following 1I/'Oumuamua (2017) and 2I/Borisov (2019)

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first detected in July 2023. Since then, astronomers have been observing its trajectory and activity, noting changes in its tail and overall brightness.

The latest image, captured on November 10, shows a sharp ion tail and a compact coma, indicating that the comet is venting gas and dust more vigorously as it approaches the sun.

Official statements

“Exploiting the unusual good weather of this season, we imaged the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS again, recording a much more developed ion tail,” said Gianluca Masi, founder of the Virtual Telescope Project.
“Looking at the picture, we see how the ion tail of 3I/ATLAS is clearly showing better and better,” Masi added.

Context

3I/ATLAS is notable as only the third confirmed interstellar object detected to date. Unlike its predecessors, this comet is bright enough for detailed study using ground-based telescopes. This allows scientists to gather information about the comet's composition and the conditions in the distant planetary system from which it originated.

The growing length and brightness of the ion tail suggest that the comet is releasing volatile materials, likely including carbon dioxide and dust, as it is heated by the sun.

What’s next

The Virtual Telescope Project will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS as it journeys through the inner solar system, providing further images and data on its activity and composition.
