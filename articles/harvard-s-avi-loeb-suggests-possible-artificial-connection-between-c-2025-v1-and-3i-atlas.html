Harvard's Avi Loeb Suggests Possible 'Artificial Connection' Between C/2025 V1 and 3I/ATLAS

Harvard astrophysicist Avi Loeb has proposed a controversial theory suggesting a potential artificial connection between the newly discovered comet C/2025 V1 and the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS. His claims were published in an essay on Medium, where he speculated this connection could involve non-gravitational propulsion, indicating an intelligent origin.

This theory has sparked interest and skepticism within the scientific community. Authorities and researchers are now closely monitoring both objects to assess their trajectories and behavior in the solar system.

Key facts

  • Discovery Dates: 3I/ATLAS detected on July 1, 2025.
  • Closest Approach: 3I/ATLAS will approach Earth on December 19, 2025, at approximately 1.8 AU (about 270 million kilometres).
  • Orbital Separation: The closest separation between the two objects is 75 million kilometres (0.5 AU).
  • Orbital Characteristics: C/2025 V1 is described as 'nearly interstellar' due to its extreme orbital eccentricity and inclination.

Timeline

On July 1, 2025, the ATLAS survey telescope in Hawaii first detected 3I/ATLAS, marking it as the third confirmed interstellar object to enter the solar system. Following this, Avi Loeb published his essay on Medium, proposing a potential connection between 3I/ATLAS and C/2025 V1. In this essay, he noted that C/2025 V1 is not related to 3I/ATLAS without non-gravitational propulsion. Currently, astronomers continue to track both objects as 3I/ATLAS approaches Earth.

Official statements

Avi Loeb stated, "One of the fundamental unknowns regarding 3I/ATLAS is whether it represents a technological mothership that released mini-probes into the inner solar system."
NASA's Tom Statler dismissed Loeb's claims, saying, "It looks like a comet. It does comet things."

Context

Loeb's theory about 3I/ATLAS adds to ongoing discussions about the origins of interstellar objects. Many scientists remain skeptical of claims suggesting technological origins for such celestial bodies. Previous observations of 3I/ATLAS have shown it behaving like a natural comet, with ultraviolet observations detecting hydroxyl emissions typical of water-driven outgassing.

What’s next

Astronomers will continue to monitor the trajectories and behaviors of C/2025 V1 and 3I/ATLAS. Observations will focus on any propulsion-like effects as 3I/ATLAS approaches Earth, while researchers will explore the uncertain origins of C/2025 V1.
