Rare Earth-Sun alignment enables new observations of interstellar object 3I/ATLAS

A near-opposition Earth-Sun alignment on January 22, 2026, allows astronomers to observe interstellar object 3I/ATLAS, placing Earth almost directly between the Sun and the object. The alignment is expected to enhance measurements of the object's dust properties, according to a January 12, 2026, post by Harvard professor Avi Loeb and co-author Mauro Barbieri.

This observation is significant as it enables scientists to quantify the "opposition surge" in brightness associated with 3I/ATLAS. The geometry of the alignment reduces the phase angle to 0.69 degrees, creating ideal conditions for assessing changes in brightness due to dust scattering. Researchers aim to gather data before and after the alignment to determine if this configuration allows for new measurements.

Key facts

  • Event date: January 22, 2026, at 13:00 UTC
  • Phase angle: 0.69 degrees
  • Heliocentric distance: 3.33 astronomical units
  • Geocentric distance: 2.35 astronomical units
  • Apparent V-band magnitude: 16.7
  • Velocity relative to the Sun: 57.7 kilometers per second
  • Previous interstellar objects: 1I/‘Oumuamua at 26.4 km/s, 2I/Borisov at 32.3 km/s
  • Observation window: January 19-26, 2026

Timeline

On July 1, 2025, the Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System identified 3I/ATLAS. Subsequent observations confirmed its hyperbolic orbit with an eccentricity near 6.139 and a perihelion distance of 1.356 astronomical units. These findings established the object’s interstellar origin.

As the Earth approaches the alignment on January 22, 2026, the phase angle remains below two degrees from January 19 through January 26, allowing for optimal observation conditions.

Official statements

Avi Loeb, co-author of the research, stated, “The alignment allows us to measure the opposition surge, providing insights into the dust characteristics of 3I/ATLAS.”

Context

Prior to 3I/ATLAS, only comet 67P/Churyumov–Gerasimenko has reported measurements of an opposition surge at similar phase angles. Other interstellar comets, such as 2I/Borisov, have been studied only at much larger angles, limiting the available data on their dust properties.

What’s next

Researchers plan to conduct observations spanning at least four days before and after January 22, 2026, while the phase angle remains below two degrees. Data collected will help assess the dust composition of 3I/ATLAS and provide insights into its grain structure.
