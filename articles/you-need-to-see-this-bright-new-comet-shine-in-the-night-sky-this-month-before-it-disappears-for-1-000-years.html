You Need to See This Bright New Comet Shine in the Night Sky This Month Before It Disappears for 1,000 Years

Amateur astronomers and skywatchers have the opportunity to view the newly discovered comet Lemmon (C/2025 A6) this October, with its peak brightness expected on October 21. The comet will be visible in the morning sky, offering a rare sighting accessible without the use of telescopes or binoculars.

This sighting is significant as comets are infrequent visitors to our skies. Lemmon was first identified in January 2023 by the Mount Lemmon Survey in Arizona. Observers have noted its unusually bright and active tail, which has allowed for predictions about its trajectory. The comet will pass closest to Earth at about 56 million miles (90 million kilometers) away.

Key facts

  • Comet Lemmon (C/2025 A6) expected to peak brightness on October 21, 2023.
  • Discovered in January 2023 by the Mount Lemmon Survey in Arizona.
  • Closest approach to Earth will be approximately 56 million miles (90 million kilometers).
  • Current orbital period is about 1,350 years, reducing to 1,150 years post-Sun pass.
  • Comet visibility will transition from morning to evening over the coming weeks.

Timeline

In January 2023, comet Lemmon was discovered by the Mount Lemmon Survey. Since then, it has traveled through various constellations, including a passage by Gemini last month and entry into Ursa Major earlier this month. Observers have noted the comet's brightness and active tail, which have aided in tracking its path.

On October 21, the comet is expected to reach its brightest point during the new moon, providing optimal viewing conditions.

Official statements

“This comet is developing very nicely and it is already an impressive object, well-placed for observation in the morning sky,” said Nick James of the British Astronomical Association to Spaceweather.com. “It is definitely worth getting up for!”
“So far, the comet has performed very well and there is no reason not to believe that it will continue to delight Northern Hemisphere observers for a few more weeks,” according to Space.com.

Context

Comets are often referred to as “cosmic snowballs,” composed of icy materials that orbit the Sun. Their appearances can be unpredictable. In 2023, several comets have been observed in the skies, but few are bright enough to be seen with the naked eye.

Past comets have provided valuable opportunities for both professional and casual astronomers to study celestial phenomena, making Lemmon’s appearance noteworthy.

What’s next

After its closest approach to the Sun in November, Lemmon's orbit will be adjusted, and it will not return until at least the year 3175. Observers are encouraged to take advantage of the upcoming viewing opportunities in the weeks ahead.
