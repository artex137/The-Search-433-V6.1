Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS on 18 November 2025: New Multi‑Tailed Image, NASA’s Big Reveal and a Planetary‑Defense Rehearsal

On 18 November 2025, astronomers have announced significant developments regarding interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, including the release of a new multi-tailed image and plans for a live global broadcast by NASA. NASA will unveil the sharpest images of the comet from multiple spacecraft and telescopes during an event scheduled for 19 November at 3 p.m. EST from Goddard Space Flight Center. There have been no reported injuries or fatalities linked to this event.

These announcements highlight the importance of 3I/ATLAS as a test case for planetary defense against potential cosmic threats. The European Space Agency (ESA) has reported a significant improvement in the comet's predicted path, enhancing the accuracy of tracking methods that could be crucial for future planetary defense efforts.

Key facts

  • Date of announcement: 18 November 2025
  • NASA event: 19 November 2025, 3 p.m. EST
  • Discovery date: 1 July 2025 by ATLAS in Río Hurtado, Chile
  • Closest approach to Earth: 19 December 2025, approximately 170 million miles (≈270 million km)
  • Comet size: Estimated nucleus size between 0.3–5.6 km
  • Current visibility: Magnitude 9–10 in the constellation Virgo

Timeline

3I/ATLAS, formally known as C/2025 N1 (ATLAS), was discovered on 1 July 2025 by the Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System (ATLAS) in Chile. It follows a hyperbolic trajectory, indicating it is not bound to the Sun and will not return.

On 3 October 2025, the comet made a close approach to Mars, allowing ESA's ExoMars Trace Gas Orbiter to capture astrometric measurements that improved the predicted path of the comet. This marked a significant milestone in planetary defense efforts.

On 18 November 2025, a New Mexico astrophotographer released a striking image of 3I/ATLAS, showcasing its green coma and multi-tailed structure. NASA confirmed its upcoming live event to share further details about the comet.

Official statements

“We are excited to share the latest images and data from our spacecraft,” said NASA in a media advisory for the upcoming broadcast.
“This exercise demonstrates how we can utilize multiple spacecraft to enhance tracking of fast-moving objects,” stated ESA regarding the comet's trajectory improvements.

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third confirmed interstellar object detected in our Solar System, following 1I/ʻOumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019. The comet's discovery has sparked interest in understanding its origins and composition, as well as its implications for planetary defense.

Recent discussions surrounding the comet have included speculation about its nature, with some, like Harvard astronomer Avi Loeb, suggesting it might be something other than a comet. However, most planetary scientists continue to reject these claims, attributing observed phenomena to natural cometary processes.

What’s next

NASA's live event on 19 November will provide new insights and visuals of 3I/ATLAS. As it approaches Earth, astronomers will continue to monitor its trajectory and behavior, contributing to ongoing planetary defense research and preparation for future cosmic threats.
