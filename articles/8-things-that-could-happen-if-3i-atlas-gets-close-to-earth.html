8 things that could happen if 3I/ATLAS gets close to Earth

As comet 3I/ATLAS approaches the Sun, it is expected to create a bright coma and tail, making it potentially visible to the naked eye under dark skies. Astronomers have confirmed that 3I/ATLAS poses no collision threat to Earth, as its hyperbolic orbit ensures it will pass safely at millions of kilometres away.

This event is significant for both amateur and professional astronomers. NASA and other space agencies are actively tracking the comet's trajectory, which offers a unique opportunity to study interstellar matter and engage the public in space science.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS is expected to create a bright coma and tail due to solar heating.
  • It poses no collision threat to Earth, confirmed by astronomers.
  • The comet will pass millions of kilometres away from Earth.
  • Observers may see a faint greenish glow streaking across the sky.
  • Potential minor meteor showers could occur if debris intersects Earth’s orbit.
  • NASA and other agencies are tracking its trajectory closely.
  • Mobile apps and websites are available for tracking and observing the comet.
  • Historical interstellar objects have sparked public interest and speculation.

Timeline

As comet 3I/ATLAS approaches the Sun, solar heating has begun to cause its icy nucleus to sublimate. This process has resulted in the release of gas and dust, forming a bright coma and tail. Astronomers have monitored its trajectory and confirmed its safe passage through the Solar System.

Currently, NASA and other space agencies are tracking the comet closely. Current models indicate that 3I/ATLAS will remain a safe distance from Earth, making it a visual spectacle rather than a threat.

Context

Historically, interstellar objects like ʻOumuamua and 2I/Borisov have led to public speculation about alien contact and doomsday scenarios. Experts caution that social media may exaggerate the proximity of 3I/ATLAS, which could lead to unnecessary panic.

The data gathered from 3I/ATLAS can aid scientists in planning future missions to interstellar objects. Understanding its composition and trajectory will help refine spacecraft design for potential intercept missions.

What’s next

NASA and other agencies will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS as it approaches the Sun. Educational events and viewing opportunities are expected to be organized by schools and amateur clubs to engage the public in this rare astronomical event.
