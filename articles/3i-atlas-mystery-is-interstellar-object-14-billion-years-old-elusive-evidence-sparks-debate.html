3I/ATLAS Mystery: Is Interstellar Object 14 Billion Years Old? Elusive Evidence Sparks Debate

A newly discovered interstellar object, 3I/ATLAS, has sparked significant debate within the scientific community about its potential age and origins. The object was identified on July 1, 2025, by the Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System in Chile. Initial assessments suggested it was a common near-Earth asteroid, but further analysis revealed its hyperbolic trajectory, indicating it is an interstellar visitor.

This discovery raises questions about the nature of 3I/ATLAS, with some researchers suggesting it could be as old as 14 billion years, predating the Sun and possibly the universe itself. Michele Bannister and her team at the University of Canterbury in New Zealand are investigating these claims, which, if confirmed, could reshape our understanding of cosmic history. However, the scientific community remains cautious due to a lack of physical evidence supporting these extraordinary age estimates.

Key facts

  • Object discovered on July 1, 2025, by the Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System in Chile.
  • Estimated speed of 3I/ATLAS is approximately 137,000 miles per hour relative to the Sun.
  • Closest approach to Earth occurred on December 19, 2025, at a distance of 1.8 astronomical units (about 170 million miles).
  • Current location as of January 2026 is within the constellation of Leo.
  • 3I/ATLAS displays a high CO2-to-water ratio and unusual emissions of nickel and iron.

Timeline

On July 1, 2025, the Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System in Chile discovered 3I/ATLAS. Initially thought to be a near-Earth asteroid, follow-up analysis revealed its interstellar trajectory. As scientists began studying the object, Michele Bannister's team proposed the hypothesis that it could be as old as 14 billion years.

On December 19, 2025, 3I/ATLAS made its closest approach to Earth, passing at a safe distance of 1.8 astronomical units. Observations have continued into January 2026, with the object fading to magnitude 13.

Official statements

“While 3I/ATLAS is clearly an outsider, it shares several characteristics with Oort Cloud comets, suggesting it could be a far more 'youthful' 4.5 billion years old,” said a NASA spokesperson.

Context

The scientific community has a history of encountering unusual celestial objects, but 3I/ATLAS presents unique challenges due to its potential age and composition. Previous interstellar visitors have prompted reevaluation of existing astrophysical models, and 3I/ATLAS could further complicate our understanding of cosmic history and the formation of celestial bodies.

What’s next

Investigations into 3I/ATLAS will continue as astronomers seek to gather more data and refine their models. The lack of physical samples remains a significant barrier to confirming any age estimates, and further observations will be critical in understanding this enigmatic object as it leaves our solar system.
