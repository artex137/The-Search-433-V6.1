Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS captured speeding through the solar system by Jupiter-bound spacecraft

On February 28, 2026, the European Space Agency’s (ESA) Jupiter Icy Moons Explorer (JUICE) spacecraft has captured images of the interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS as it speeds through the solar system. The comet was observed in November 2025, traveling at over 150,000 kilometres per hour during its closest approach to the sun. No injuries or fatalities have been reported.

This observation is significant as it provides valuable data about interstellar objects and their behavior. Scientists aim to understand the comet's origins and trajectory, which remains a mystery. ESA and NASA have deployed their spacecraft to monitor the comet as it continues its journey through our cosmic neighborhood.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS was first spotted in July 2025.
  • It reached speeds of 137,000 miles per hour (approximately 220,000 km/h) during its initial observations.
  • The comet's shape has been described as almost egg-shaped with a gas veil obscuring its nucleus.
  • Only three interstellar objects have been discovered passing through our solar system to date.
  • The JUICE spacecraft captured images of the comet in November 2025.

Timeline

Comet 3I/ATLAS was first detected in July 2025. Its extraordinary speed and trajectory indicated that it had been traveling through interstellar space for possibly billions of years. In November 2025, the JUICE spacecraft captured images of the comet as it passed between the orbits of Earth and Mars.

ESA has noted that the comet's behavior aligns with that of a typical comet, despite its interstellar origins. Scientists have expressed interest in gathering more data about this rare visitor as it continues its path through the solar system.

Official statements

“While 3I/ATLAS is a visitor from interstellar space, travelling from outside the Solar System, its behaviour is completely in line with that expected from a ‘normal’ comet,” the European Space Agency stated.
“You can’t project that back with any accuracy to figure out where it started on its path,” said David Jewitt, director of the Institute for Planets and Exoplanets at the University of California, Los Angeles.

Context

Comet 3I/ATLAS is one of only three known interstellar objects to have been detected in our solar system. Its rapid movement and unusual trajectory have drawn significant attention from scientists, highlighting the ongoing interest in understanding the origins of such celestial bodies.

The study of interstellar comets contributes to the broader understanding of the cosmos and the formation of planetary systems beyond our own.

What’s next

Space agencies will continue to monitor Comet 3I/ATLAS as it travels through the solar system. Researchers are expected to analyze data collected by JUICE and other spacecraft to gain insights into the comet's structure and origins.
