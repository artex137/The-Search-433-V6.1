New Cosmic Visitor C/2025 V1 Discovered Days After 3I/ATLAS Perihelion

Astronomer Gennady Borisov has discovered a new object, designated C/2025 V1, on November 2, 2025, just days after the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS made its closest approach to the Sun. Both objects were seen in the same telescope frame, an unprecedented event in astronomical observation.

This discovery has raised questions about a potential connection between C/2025 V1 and 3I/ATLAS. Scientists are now preparing to analyze 3I/ATLAS using the Webb Space Telescope when it reaches its closest point to Earth on December 19, 2025. They aim to determine whether 3I/ATLAS is a natural comet or something technological.

Key facts

  • Discovery of C/2025 V1 on November 2, 2025, by Gennady Borisov.
  • 3I/ATLAS closest approach to the Sun occurred on October 31, 2025.
  • C/2025 V1 has an orbit tilted at 113 degrees and an eccentricity of 1.0095.
  • 3I/ATLAS will reach perigee, its closest approach to Earth, on December 19, 2025.
  • Distance between 3I/ATLAS and C/2025 V1 at closest point is at least 75 million kilometers.

Timeline

On October 31, 2025, interstellar object 3I/ATLAS made its closest approach to the Sun. Less than 48 hours later, on November 2, 2025, Gennady Borisov discovered C/2025 V1. Both objects were observed in the same field of view, creating a unique observational opportunity for astronomers.

Currently, astronomers are preparing for the analysis of 3I/ATLAS as it approaches Earth. Data collection will focus on jet speeds and composition to better understand the nature of the object.

Official statements

“The timing creates an interesting backdrop for the holiday season. Whatever 3I/ATLAS turns out to be, we will know much more by the time it passes closest to our planet in mid-December,” said Harvard astronomer Avi Loeb.

Context

This discovery follows Borisov’s earlier identification of the interstellar comet 2I/Borisov in 2019. The recent findings suggest a complex interaction between these cosmic visitors and the dynamics of our solar system.

Understanding the nature of these objects could have significant implications for the study of comets and the formation of solar systems.

What’s next

Astronomers will use the Webb Space Telescope to gather data on 3I/ATLAS as it approaches Earth. Key measurements will include the speed of the outflowing gas, the mass being ejected, and the chemical composition of the jets. Results are expected to clarify whether 3I/ATLAS is a natural object or something technological.
