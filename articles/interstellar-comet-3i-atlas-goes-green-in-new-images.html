Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS Goes Green in New Images

New images of interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS show it has developed a diffuse greenish coma as it travels through the solar system. This change has occurred since its earlier pass and has been captured by astronomers using advanced telescopes.

The green hue provides insight into the comet's chemistry, as various ices vaporize and fluoresce in sunlight. Authorities, including NASA and the European Space Agency (ESA), are conducting a wide-spectrum program to study the comet's composition while it remains visible.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS is traveling at approximately 220,000 kilometers per hour.
  • It is the third confirmed interstellar visitor, following 1I/‘Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov.
  • The green color is attributed to fluorescing diatomic carbon (C2) at a wavelength near 516 nm.
  • Astronomers have used the Gemini North telescope in Hawai‘i and the European Space Agency’s XMM-Newton space telescope for observations.
  • NASA and ESA are utilizing multiple instruments, including the Hubble and James Webb Space Telescopes.

Timeline

Comet 3I/ATLAS has been observed as it approaches the Sun. Early observations showed a redder appearance, indicating a coma dominated by dust and red-emitting species. As it nears perihelion, the comet's color has shifted to green due to increased emissions of C2.

In a coordinated effort, astronomers have captured high-resolution images and conducted nearly 20 hours of X-ray observations to understand gas production rates and the chemical composition of the coma.

Official statements

“This is a graceful demonstration of both the comet’s path on the sky and how its coma’s spectrum changed,” said a representative from NOIRLab.

Context

Comet 3I/ATLAS is unique, as it is a frozen time capsule from another star system. Unlike local comets, its composition shows a carbon dioxide-to-water ratio that deviates from typical measurements. Preliminary analyses suggest the comet has distinct characteristics influenced by its native system.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS as it moves away from the Sun. They aim to gather more data to understand its composition better and how it differs from comets originating in our solar system.
