Scientists Startled by What Happens When They Point Hubble at Comet

Astronomers have observed comet C/2025 K1 (ATLAS) breaking apart while using the Hubble Space Telescope from November 8 to November 20, 2025. The comet, which is approximately 250 million miles from Earth, began to disintegrate shortly after the team initiated their observations.

This unexpected event has provided scientists with a unique opportunity to study the evolution of comets. John Noonan, a research physicist at Auburn University and coauthor of a study published in the journal Icarus, emphasized the rarity of such an occurrence, noting that it allows researchers to examine almost "pristine" samples of the comet's composition right after fragmentation.

Key facts

  • Comet observed: C/2025 K1 (ATLAS)
  • Observation dates: November 8 to November 20, 2025
  • Closest approach to the Sun (perihelion): October 8, 2025
  • Distance from Earth: 250 million miles
  • Number of fragments observed: Four
  • Research published in: Icarus

Timeline

On October 8, 2025, comet C/2025 K1 (ATLAS) completed its closest approach to the Sun. Shortly after, from November 8 to November 20, astronomers at Auburn University began observing the comet using the Hubble Space Telescope. During these observations, the comet began to break apart, resulting in the discovery of four distinct fragments.

John Noonan reported that this observation was a rare occurrence, as it allowed scientists to analyze the comet’s composition immediately after it fragmented, a significant advantage over previous studies.

Official statements

“Sometimes the best science happens by accident,” said John Noonan, coauthor of the study. “And right when we observed it, it happened to break apart, which is the slimmest of slim chances.”
“Never before has Hubble caught a fragmenting comet this close to when it actually fell apart,” Noonan added. “This is telling us something very important about the physics of what’s happening at the comet’s surface.”
“They’ve been heated; they’ve been irradiated by the sun and by cosmic rays,” explained Dennis Bodewits, principal investigator of the study. “So, when looking at a comet’s composition, the question we always have is, ‘Is this a primitive property or is this due to evolution?’”

Context

Comet studies provide insights into the early solar system and the formation of planetary systems. Previous observations of fragmenting comets have typically occurred weeks to months after the event, limiting the ability to draw conclusions about their original properties. This recent observation represents a significant advancement in comet research.

What’s next

Astronomers will continue to analyze the gas readings and fragments of C/2025 K1 (ATLAS) to better understand the composition and evolution of comets. Further studies may provide insights into the conditions of the early solar system and the characteristics of other planetary systems.
