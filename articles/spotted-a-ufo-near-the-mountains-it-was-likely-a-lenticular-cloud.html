Spotted a UFO near the mountains? It was likely a lenticular cloud

People who thought they spotted a UFO near mountain ranges have likely seen lenticular clouds, according to meteorologist Hannah Sheehy from WGME. These unique, lens-shaped clouds form under specific atmospheric conditions and have no reported injuries or fatalities associated with them.

This information is significant as it clarifies the nature of these cloud formations, which are often mistaken for unidentified flying objects. Authorities, including meteorologists and aviation experts, emphasize the importance of understanding lenticular clouds due to their implications for aviation safety.

Key facts

  • Officially named Altocumulus standing lenticular clouds
  • Form under specific atmospheric conditions
  • Stationary, unlike other clouds that drift with the wind
  • Commonly found over mountain ranges
  • Most often seen during colder months

Timeline

People have reported sightings of what they believed to be UFOs near mountains during various times, particularly in colder months. These clouds typically form when stable, moist air flows over mountains, creating standing waves in the atmosphere.

As the air rises, it cools and condenses into the characteristic lens shape. When the air descends, it warms and evaporates, leading to the flying-saucer appearance of lenticular clouds.

Official statements

“Lenticular clouds are often a sign of strong winds aloft and a stable atmosphere,” said meteorologist Hannah Sheehy. “Pilots pay close attention to them because mountain waves that create lenticular clouds can produce severe turbulence.”

Context

Lenticular clouds are rare but have been reported more frequently during colder months due to stronger winds at higher altitudes. Their unique formation process and appearance have led to frequent misidentifications as UFOs, highlighting the need for public education on cloud types and atmospheric phenomena.

What’s next

Authorities continue to monitor reports of unusual cloud formations to educate the public and enhance awareness about atmospheric conditions that create lenticular clouds.
