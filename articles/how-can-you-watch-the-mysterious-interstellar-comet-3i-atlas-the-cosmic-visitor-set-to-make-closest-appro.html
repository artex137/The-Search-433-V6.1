Comet 3I/ATLAS to Make Closest Approach to Earth on December 19

The interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS is set to pass closest to Earth on December 19, 2025, approaching within 167 million miles (270 million kilometers) of our planet. NASA has confirmed that the comet poses no risk to Earth as it will be located on the opposite side of the sun during its closest approach.

This rare comet has generated significant interest among scientists and astronomers, being only the third known object to enter our solar system from another star’s orbit. NASA and the European Space Agency have been monitoring the comet’s trajectory, and it is expected to remain visible to telescopes and space missions for a few more months before leaving the solar system.

Key facts

  • Closest approach date: December 19, 2025
  • Distance from Earth: 167 million miles (270 million kilometers)
  • Observation tools: Telescopes with at least an eight-inch aperture
  • Visibility: Not visible to the naked eye
  • Monitoring agencies: NASA, European Space Agency

Timeline

Comet 3I/ATLAS has been observed by several spacecraft from NASA and the European Space Agency, including those near Mars and the Juice spacecraft. The comet's unusual behavior, including its changing color and disappearance of its tail, has led to speculation about its nature, but scientists assert that its characteristics align with natural phenomena.

As of now, there is no information regarding the comet's visibility in India. Observers are encouraged to use telescopes and tracking tools to view the comet during its passage.

Official statements

“NASA has stated that 3I/ATLAS poses no threat to Earth and will get no closer than about 170 million miles (275 million kilometers) to our planet,” a spokesperson from NASA said.

Context

The comet has sparked discussions within the scientific community, especially regarding its emissions. Recent observations have shown that the comet emits X-rays, a phenomenon not uncommon among comets. Japanese scientists reported that the comet's emissions create a region of approximately 400,000 km around its nucleus due to interactions with solar wind.

What’s next

Observers are advised to prepare for viewing opportunities in the coming months as 3I/ATLAS continues its journey through the solar system. Further monitoring by NASA and other space agencies will continue as the comet approaches its closest point to Earth.
