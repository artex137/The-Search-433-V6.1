MSU astronomers watching comet from another solar system

LANSING – Michigan State University researchers have confirmed that a comet named 3I/Atlas, from another solar system, is displaying classic cometary features. The comet was first spotted moving from beyond the region where water is active, raising interest in its unique composition. No injuries or fatalities have been reported in relation to the comet.

This discovery matters as it marks the third interstellar comet ever detected. Researchers are studying the comet to learn more about its origins and the potential different elements in its ice composition. Authorities from MSU are actively monitoring the comet's activity and visibility.

Key facts

  • Comet name: 3I/Atlas
  • Type: Interstellar comet
  • First spotted: Not disclosed
  • Visibility: November and December 2025
  • Lead researcher: Professor Darryl Seligman
  • Location: Michigan State University

Timeline

The comet 3I/Atlas was first identified moving from a region beyond where water is active. MSU researchers have since studied the comet, concluding it exhibits typical cometary behavior.

Professor Darryl Seligman has led the research, emphasizing that the object is a natural comet and not a UFO, as some online rumors suggest.

Official statements

“Almost everybody in the field is in agreement that this object is a natural comet because it’s displaying classical evidence of cometary activity,” said Professor Darryl Seligman.
“There’s no controversy about it not being a comet because nobody thinks that except a very few people,” Seligman added.
“Everything about this points to it being a comet from somewhere else in the galaxy,” said Shannon Schmoll, director of the Abrams Planetarium.

Context

3I/Atlas is the third interstellar comet ever detected, following previous discoveries that have provided insights into the origins of such celestial objects. The unique characteristics of this comet, particularly its ice composition, could yield valuable information about the conditions in its home system.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to monitor 3I/Atlas as it becomes visible in the coming months. Further studies may reveal more about its composition and origins as it approaches the Sun.
