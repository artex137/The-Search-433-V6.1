Defence chiefs hoped to re-build Britain's military using alien UFO technology

Defence chiefs in the UK have sought to rebuild Britain’s military capabilities using technology believed to be of extraterrestrial origin, according to declassified documents. The initiative began in the 1990s after credible intelligence suggested that UFOs, or unidentified aerial phenomena (UAPs), could outmaneuver any known human craft. The Defence Intelligence Staff, a unit within the Ministry of Defence, was tasked with examining these reports.

This development is significant as it highlights the lengths to which military officials have gone to secure potential technological advantages. The documents reveal that British intelligence took UAP sightings seriously and aimed to reverse-engineer any recovered technology to enhance national defense capabilities.

Key facts

  • Initiative began in the 1990s.
  • Defence Intelligence Staff was responsible for investigating UAPs.
  • Documents indicate potential recovery of 10 crashed UFOs by U.S. defence chiefs.
  • Internal memo dated March 4, 1997, emphasized the need to take UAP sightings seriously.
  • Reports of sightings included thousands of witnesses in Belgium between November 1989 and April 1990.
  • Rendlesham Forest incident in December 1980 cited as a confirmed landing.
  • Defence Intelligence Staff was renamed Defence Intelligence in 2009.
  • Minister for Defence Readiness Luke Pollard stated in 2024 that similar analyses are no longer conducted.

Timeline

In the 1990s, the UK’s Defence Intelligence Staff began investigating UAPs based on credible intelligence. An internal memo dated March 4, 1997, highlighted the need to assess the potential threat posed by these phenomena. Reports from Belgium documented thousands of sightings of large, silent, low-flying triangular objects between November 1989 and April 1990.

In December 1980, the Rendlesham Forest incident near RAF Woodbridge in Suffolk was reported as a confirmed landing, further prompting inquiries into UAP technology. In 2009, the Defence Intelligence Staff was renamed to Defence Intelligence. In 2024, Defence Readiness Minister Luke Pollard announced that similar investigations were no longer taking place.

Official statements

“Logic would indicate that if significant numbers are reporting seeing strange objects in the sky then there may be a basis in fact,” the internal memo stated.
“The reported technology, if true, would represent technology we do not have,” another document noted.

Context

Prior to these investigations, interest in UAPs had been largely dismissed within military circles, often leading to ridicule. However, the emergence of credible reports and the potential implications for national security prompted a shift in perspective. Intelligence officers acknowledged that some sightings could not be explained by conventional means.

The reports emphasized the importance of maintaining an open mind regarding UAPs while recognizing the challenges in assessing their potential threat level. The Defence Intelligence Staff’s research faced skepticism, with officials aware that discussions on extraterrestrial technology could lead to public mockery.

What’s next

With the cessation of similar analyses in 2024, it remains unclear how the UK will approach future reports of UAPs. Investigations into these phenomena appear to have been significantly scaled back, with no current plans for further inquiry or technology acquisition.
