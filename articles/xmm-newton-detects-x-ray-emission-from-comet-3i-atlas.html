XMM-Newton detects X-ray emission from comet 3I/ATLAS

The European Space Agency’s XMM-Newton X-ray space observatory has detected X-ray emissions from interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS during a 20-hour observation on December 3, when the comet was approximately 282-285 million kilometers from the spacecraft.

This discovery is significant as it provides insights into the composition of interstellar objects. The X-ray emissions result from gas molecules in the comet colliding with the solar wind, generating X-rays. This data will enhance scientists' understanding of the comet and its gaseous components, which have been previously identified by other space telescopes.

Key facts

  • Observation date: December 3
  • Distance from spacecraft: 282-285 million kilometers
  • Observation duration: 20 hours
  • Instrument used: European Photon Imaging Camera (EPIC)-pn
  • Gases detected: Water vapor, carbon dioxide, carbon monoxide
  • Previous interstellar object: 1I/’Oumuamua, discovered in 2017

Timeline

On December 3, XMM-Newton conducted a detailed observation of comet 3I/ATLAS for 20 hours. During this time, the spacecraft captured low-energy X-ray emissions from the comet, which are a result of interactions between the comet's gases and the solar wind.

The X-ray emissions were visualized in an image where blue areas indicated low X-ray presence, while red highlighted the comet's emissions. This data is expected to provide valuable information regarding the comet's composition.

Official statements

“X-ray observations are particularly sensitive to gases like hydrogen (H₂) and nitrogen (N₂), which are nearly invisible to optical and ultraviolet instruments,” according to ESA.

Context

The detection of X-ray emissions from comet 3I/ATLAS builds on previous findings from interstellar objects. The discovery of 1I/’Oumuamua in 2017 opened discussions about the potential presence of exotic ices in such objects. While 1I/’Oumuamua has become too distant for further study, comet 3I/ATLAS presents a new opportunity for analysis.

What’s next

Scientists plan to use the X-ray observations from XMM-Newton to complement data from other instruments, enhancing the understanding of comet 3I/ATLAS's composition and the nature of interstellar objects.
