3I/ATLAS: ‘Fall Of Icarus’ Fame Astrophotographer Gets Striking Image Of Interstellar Comet Ahead Of Earth Flyby

Andrew McCarthy, an astrophotographer known for his “Fall of Icarus” portrait of the sun, has captured a striking image of the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS ahead of its closest approach to Earth on December 19. The comet will come within a distance of 1.79 astronomical units (AU), or approximately 270 million kilometers, from Earth.

This event is significant as 3I/ATLAS is the third interstellar object to pass through the solar system. The comet's unusual behavior and features, particularly its anti-tail, have spurred discussion among scientists and the public. Authorities have noted that this close flyby presents a rare opportunity for both skygazers and scientists to observe the comet more closely.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS will make its closest approach to Earth on December 19.
  • The distance at closest approach will be 1.79 AU (270 million kilometers).
  • McCarthy has shared his observations and images of the comet on social media.
  • Harvard astrophysicist Avi Loeb has flagged 14 anomalies associated with the comet.
  • The event coincides with a new moon, providing optimal viewing conditions.

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first identified as an interstellar object and has since exhibited unusual behavior, including the presence of an anti-tail. On December 17, 2025, Andrew McCarthy shared his image of the comet on social media, highlighting its similarities to other comets he has photographed.

As the comet approaches Earth, McCarthy has addressed claims regarding its origin, stating it behaves like typical comets, sublimating ice from its surface and altering its velocity accordingly.

Official statements

“It is behaving very similarly to every other comet I've photographed in terms of changes of speed. Like any other, this comet is sublimating ice from its surface, which adds a small amount of thrust to impact its velocity over time.”
“What's weird about it that I can observe? The angle. Comets in the solar system come in at odd angles relative to the ecliptic plane, making them move in odd directions relative to their tail.”

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third interstellar object documented to pass through the solar system, following 1I/2017 U1 (Oumuamua) and 2I/Borisov. The comet's behavior has raised questions about its origins and characteristics, drawing interest from both scientists and amateur astronomers alike.

The unusual features of 3I/ATLAS, such as its anti-tail, have prompted discussions regarding potential technological origins, though many experts maintain that its behavior is consistent with known cometary phenomena.

What’s next

As 3I/ATLAS approaches its closest flyby, astronomers and astrophotographers will continue to observe and document its trajectory and behavior to gain a better understanding of this interstellar object.
