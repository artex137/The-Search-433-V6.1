Chinese probe Tianwen-1 photographs comet 3I/ATLAS and confirms data on the object’s interstellar origin

The China National Space Administration (CNSA) has successfully captured high-resolution images of comet 3I/ATLAS using the Tianwen-1 mission orbiter while it orbited Mars. The operation took place recently, demonstrating advanced technical capabilities in deep space exploration.

This achievement is significant as it enhances understanding of celestial bodies that traverse the solar system. The data collected will aid scientists in studying the physical structure and activity of 3I/ATLAS, which is believed to originate from outside the solar system.

Key facts

  • Mission: Tianwen-1
  • Object: Comet 3I/ATLAS
  • Distance from object during capture: 30 million kilometers
  • Object speed: 58 kilometers per second
  • Nucleus size: Approximately 5.6 kilometers
  • Data shared with: European Space Agency (ESA) and NASA

Timeline

The Tianwen-1 mission, which previously landed the Zhurong rover on Mars, has been operational since its launch. The orbiter recently performed a complex maneuver to capture images of 3I/ATLAS while orbiting Mars. The mission control team calculated visual interception vectors to ensure precise imaging of the comet, which is moving at high speeds.

The successful capture of images of 3I/ATLAS validates the probe's autonomous navigation and control systems, marking a milestone in China's deep space exploration efforts.

Official statements

“The success of this capture validates the autonomous navigation and attitude control systems developed for the Chinese mission,” said a CNSA spokesperson.
“This achievement is an important step towards understanding the objects that transit through the cosmic neighborhood of Terra and Marte,” the spokesperson added.

Context

Comet 3I/ATLAS has drawn interest from the international astronomical community due to its unique orbital characteristics, suggesting an origin outside the solar system. This classification aligns with other notable interstellar objects such as ‘Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov.

The comet's hyperbolic trajectory indicates it is not gravitationally bound to the Sun, providing a rare opportunity to study materials from other stellar systems.

What’s next

The successful observations from Tianwen-1 pave the way for future missions, including the upcoming Tianwen-2 mission, which is scheduled for launch in the coming years. This mission aims to further explore and gather data on celestial objects of interest.
