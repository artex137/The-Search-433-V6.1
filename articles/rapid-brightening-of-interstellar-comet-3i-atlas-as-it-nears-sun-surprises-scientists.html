Rapid brightening of interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS as it nears sun surprises scientists

An interstellar comet, designated 3I/ATLAS, has dramatically brightened as it approaches the sun, surprising scientists. The comet was first observed on July 1, 2025, and is currently making its closest pass to the sun, reaching its perihelion.

This brightening is significant as it challenges existing knowledge about comet behavior. Astronomers have utilized space-based solar observatories, including SOHO, STEREO-A, and GOES-19, to monitor the comet, as its proximity to the sun has rendered it invisible to Earth-based telescopes.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS was first spotted on July 1, 2025.
  • It is only the third interstellar comet recorded.
  • The comet is expected to reach a geocentric V magnitude of approximately 9 at perihelion.
  • Its brightening rate is the most extreme ever observed in a comet.
  • The comet has shown a blue color, which is atypical for comets.
  • Researchers have noted a higher carbon dioxide content and unusual metals in its composition.

Timeline

On July 1, 2025, astronomers first detected comet 3I/ATLAS. As the comet approached the sun, it became increasingly difficult to observe from Earth due to solar glare. In response, scientists turned to space-based observatories, which confirmed the comet's unexpected brightening. Researchers published their findings on the arXiv preprint server, detailing the comet's rapid brightness increase and its unusual blue color.

Official statements

“Our cursory analysis of this data indicates the comet will likely emerge from conjunction considerably brighter than when it entered,” researchers stated in their paper.

Context

Comet 3I/ATLAS is notable for being only the third interstellar comet documented. Its unique characteristics, including a distinctive chemical composition, differentiate it from comets found within our solar system. Past observations have indicated that comets typically brighten slowly as they approach the sun, making 3I/ATLAS's rapid brightening particularly intriguing.

What’s next

As 3I/ATLAS moves away from the sun, astronomers will have opportunities to conduct more detailed studies using ground-based telescopes.
