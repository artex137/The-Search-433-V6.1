Comet 3I/ATLAS Approaches Earth This Month – NASA Clarifies Risks

Comet 3I/ATLAS is set to approach Earth on December 19, 2023, at a safe distance of approximately 270 million kilometers, according to NASA and the European Space Agency (ESA). There have been no reported injuries or fatalities associated with this event.

This comet, classified as an interstellar visitor, is significant for scientific research as it presents a unique opportunity to study materials from outside our Solar System. Authorities, including NASA, are actively monitoring its trajectory and characteristics using both space and ground-based telescopes.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS will reach its closest point to Earth on December 19, 2023.
  • The comet is approximately 270 million kilometers away during its closest approach.
  • It is the third identified interstellar object in the Solar System, following 1I/'Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov.
  • 3I/ATLAS was initially detected in July 2023 and later classified as a comet.
  • The object is estimated to be around seven billion years old.
  • NASA's SPHEREx mission has identified carbon dioxide in the comet's coma, which extends for about 350 kilometers.
  • 3I/ATLAS has emitted a radio signal during its journey through the Solar System.

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first detected in July 2023 under the temporary name A11pl3Z. It was quickly classified as an interstellar comet and received the designations C/2025 N1 (ATLAS) and 3I/ATLAS.

The comet reached its perihelion, or closest point to the Sun, on October 29, 2023. After passing close to Mars, it became visible again following a brief period of being obscured by the sun's glare.

Recent observations have indicated the presence of active "ice volcanoes" on its surface, and the scientific community has dismissed speculation regarding its potential alien origin.

Official statements

“The 3I/ATLAS spacecraft is not on a collision course with Earth,” said a NASA spokesperson. “The orbital calculations accumulated by observatories around the world confirm that the object does not pose a threat.”

Context

3I/ATLAS is notable as it is only the third interstellar object identified in our Solar System. Previous interstellar visitors include 1I/'Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov, which have also sparked significant scientific interest. The study of such objects can enhance our understanding of planetary formation processes and the composition of distant star systems.

What’s next

As December 19 approaches, astronomers will continue to monitor Comet 3I/ATLAS. There are no anticipated changes in its trajectory, and it is expected to exit the Solar System after its closest approach.
