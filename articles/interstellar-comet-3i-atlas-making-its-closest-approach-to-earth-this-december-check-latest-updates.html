Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS Approaches Closest Point to Earth in December

The interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS is set to make its closest approach to Earth on December 19, 2025, passing within approximately 270 million kilometers of the planet. The comet, which has garnered attention for its unique origin and speed, presents an opportunity for astronomers to observe a celestial body from another star system.

This event is significant for both scientists and the public as it allows for a rare glimpse of an interstellar object. Authorities, including NASA and the European Space Agency (ESA), are monitoring its trajectory and providing information on how to view the comet safely. They have confirmed that 3I/ATLAS poses no threat to Earth during its flyby.

Key facts

  • Closest Approach Date: December 19, 2025
  • Distance from Earth: Approximately 270 million kilometers (1.8 AU)
  • Speed: 137,000 mph (220,000 km/h) upon detection
  • Size Estimate: Between 1,400 feet and 3.5 miles (430 to 5,600 meters) across
  • Last Close Pass: October 30, 2025, within 130 million miles of the Sun
  • Visibility: Not visible to the naked eye; requires a small telescope for viewing

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first detected in July 2025 as the third known interstellar object entering our solar system. It traveled at an extraordinary speed of 137,000 mph and is believed to have originated from the direction of the Sagittarius constellation. The comet passed within 130 million miles of the Sun on October 30, 2025, and is now on course for its closest approach to Earth on December 19, 2025.

NASA has released high-resolution images of 3I/ATLAS on November 19, showcasing its tail and structure. The agency has also emphasized that there is no danger associated with the comet's trajectory.

Official statements

“The closest the comet will come to Earth is about 270 million km – about 1.8 times the distance between Earth and the Sun,” said ESA representatives. “During its closest approach, it will actually be on the other side of the Sun. It poses no danger to our planet or any other planets in the Solar System.”
NASA Acting Administrator Sean Duffy commented, “No aliens. No threat to life here on Earth,” addressing conspiracy theories about the comet.

Context

3I/ATLAS has gained attention due to its interstellar origin, which has inspired various conspiracy theories regarding its nature. Despite these claims, NASA has clarified that it is a natural comet and not an alien spacecraft. The comet's passage through our solar system provides valuable data for scientific study and enhances public interest in astronomy.

What’s next

As 3I/ATLAS approaches Earth, scientists will continue to monitor its trajectory and provide updates. The closest approach on December 19 will offer observers a unique opportunity to view the comet through telescopes until it exits the solar system, marking an important moment in space exploration.
