Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS Makes Closest Flyby Tonight — NASA Reveals Exact Time

The interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS will make its closest approach to Earth at 1 am EST (0600 GMT) on Friday, 19 December, passing at a distance of about 1.8 astronomical units, or approximately 168 million miles (270 million kilometres) away, according to NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL).

This event is significant for astronomers as interstellar objects are rare. NASA scientists have emphasized the importance of studying 3I/ATLAS to better understand how such bodies interact with solar radiation and move through space. The comet is not a threat to Earth, and it will not be visible to the naked eye.

Key facts

  • Event Time: 1 am EST (0600 GMT) on 19 December
  • Distance: 1.8 astronomical units (168 million miles or 270 million kilometres)
  • Visibility: Not visible to the naked eye
  • Livestream: Available at 11 pm EST on 18 December (0400 GMT on 19 December)
  • Scientific Importance: Provides insights into interstellar material and celestial mechanics

Timeline

On 18 December at 11 pm EST (0400 GMT on 19 December), the Virtual Telescope Project will begin a live online stream to allow viewers to observe 3I/ATLAS as it approaches its closest point. The comet will pass Earth at 1 am EST (0600 GMT) on 19 December.

This flyby is part of a brief observation window that allows scientists to gather data on the comet before it exits the solar system and becomes unreachable.

Official statements

“3I/ATLAS is not a threat to the Earth,” a NASA spokesperson stated. “The vast distance makes it impossible to see with the naked eye.”
Dr. Avi Loeb, a Harvard astrophysicist, noted, “Examining the compositions of such objects helps us understand how elements and compounds are built around other stars.”

Context

The flyby of 3I/ATLAS highlights the fleeting nature of interstellar visitors. Previous objects, such as Oumuamua, were only detected after they had passed Earth, limiting the time available for observations. The early detection of 3I/ATLAS has allowed astronomers to prepare and gather data, enhancing scientific understanding.

This event underscores the importance of international cooperation in astronomy, as telescopes worldwide align to take measurements during this limited observation period.

What’s next

Following its closest approach, 3I/ATLAS will continue on its trajectory out of the solar system and will not return. Scientists will analyze the data collected during the flyby to further knowledge of interstellar bodies and their characteristics.
