3I Atlas Latest Updates: Interstellar Comet 3i/Atlas News & Insights

Interstellar comet 3i/Atlas has entered our Solar System, first discovered on July 1, 2025, by the ATLAS survey telescope. The comet, which is not gravitationally bound to the Sun, will pass through the Solar System and continue outward, posing no risk to Earth.

This discovery is significant as it provides scientists with a unique opportunity to study an object from another star system. Observations of 3i/Atlas are expected to enhance understanding of comet composition and behavior, particularly due to its unusual high carbon dioxide (CO₂) content.

Key facts

  • Discovery date: July 1, 2025
  • Closest approach to the Sun: October 30, 2025, at approximately 1.4 AU
  • Closest approach to Earth: Approximately 1.8 AU
  • Nucleus size: Estimated between 320 meters and 5.6 kilometers in diameter
  • Coma composition: Dominated by CO₂, with smaller amounts of water and carbon monoxide
  • Current color change: From red to green

Timeline

3i/Atlas was first detected on July 1, 2025, by the ATLAS survey telescope. The comet has a hyperbolic trajectory, confirming it as an interstellar object. It is expected to make its closest approach to the Sun on October 30, 2025, at approximately 1.4 AU.

Recent observations indicate that the comet is developing a visible tail, which suggests that solar heating is causing gas and dust to escape from its nucleus. The changing color of the comet, shifting from red to green, may result from chemical reactions or increased gas emissions as it approaches the Sun.

Official statements

“3i/Atlas is a unique object that challenges our understanding of comet composition and behavior,” said a representative from the ATLAS survey team.

Context

3i/Atlas is the third confirmed interstellar object detected, following other notable discoveries. Its unique composition, particularly the high CO₂ content, poses challenges to existing models of comet formation and behavior.

The study of 3i/Atlas may provide insights into conditions in other star systems and enhance comparisons with Solar System comets.

What’s next

Scientists will continue to monitor 3i/Atlas as it approaches perihelion and then exits the Solar System. This monitoring will provide valuable data on interstellar objects and may improve understanding of comet dynamics and formation.
