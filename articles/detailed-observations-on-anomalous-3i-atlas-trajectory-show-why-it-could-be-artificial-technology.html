Detailed observations on anomalous 3I/Atlas trajectory show why it could be artificial technology

Harvard scientist Avi Loeb has flagged the trajectory of the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS as anomalous, raising questions about its origins. The object, weighing approximately 33 billion tonnes, is on a straight path that brings it close to several planets in the solar system. It passed within 29 million kilometres of Mars on October 3, 2023, and is set to encounter Jupiter on March 16, 2026, at a distance of 54 million kilometres.

This analysis matters because it suggests that the object may not be a natural space rock. Loeb has indicated that the chances of 3I/ATLAS following such a specific trajectory are extremely low, prompting discussions about the possibility of it being sent by an intelligent alien civilization.

Key facts

  • 3I/ATLAS passed 29 million kilometres from Mars on October 3, 2023.
  • The object will encounter Jupiter on March 16, 2026, at a distance of 54 million kilometres.
  • The chances of 3I/ATLAS aligning with the ecliptic plane is 0.002.
  • The probability of it arriving at the planets’ orbital radii is only 0.0002.
  • Loeb has compared the mass of 3I/ATLAS to the Starship, stating it is 6 million times heavier.

Timeline

On October 3, 2023, 3I/ATLAS passed within 29 million kilometres of Mars. This event prompted further examination of its trajectory by scientists, including Avi Loeb. The object is scheduled to encounter Jupiter on March 16, 2026, when it will be 54 million kilometres from the gas giant.

Loeb has published a blog post detailing the anomalies associated with the object’s trajectory, including its speed, light, and size. He has called for further observations from Mars orbiters and other missions.

Official statements

"For a natural object to take such a perfect path through our solar system seems unfathomable," said Avi Loeb.
"The telescopes used by the senders of 3I/ATLAS are capable of noticing transits by rocky objects that are smaller than Earth," Loeb added.

Context

Previous discussions around 3I/ATLAS have focused on its potential as a first contact scenario or an existential threat. The ongoing discourse is fueled by its unusual trajectory and the implications of extraterrestrial technology.

Loeb's findings challenge traditional understandings of interstellar objects and suggest a need for advanced observational capabilities to determine the true nature of such anomalies.

What’s next

Further observations from Mars orbiters, including Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter, Mars Express, TGO, MAVEN, Tianwen-1, and Hope, are expected to provide more data on 3I/ATLAS. Additional missions, including Juice and Juno, will also conduct observations in March 2026.
