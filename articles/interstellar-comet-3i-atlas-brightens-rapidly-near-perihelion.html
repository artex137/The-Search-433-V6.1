Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS Brightens Rapidly Near Perihelion

The interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS has brightened unexpectedly as it approaches its perihelion, which occurred on October 29. Observations indicate that the comet's brightness has increased much faster than scientists anticipated, according to a report by Science Alert. There have been no injuries or fatalities reported related to the comet's approach.

This rapid brightening is significant as it allows for potential observations with small amateur telescopes. Researchers from the Lowell Observatory and the U.S. Naval Research Laboratory are tracking the comet using space-based observatories, given that it has been positioned almost directly behind the Sun, making ground-based observations impossible.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS reached perihelion on October 29, 2023.
  • The comet has brightened roughly twice as fast as typical comets.
  • It has approached from about 2 astronomical units to 1.36 astronomical units from the Sun.
  • Spacecraft used for tracking include STEREO-A, SOHO, and GOES-19.
  • The comet displays a noticeable blue tint compared to sunlight.
  • Researchers suggest the blue tint may be influenced by cyanogen and ammonia emissions.

Timeline

In mid-September 2023, observations indicated that comet 3I/ATLAS was positioned nearly behind the Sun. During this time, it was tracked via space-based observatories due to the lack of visibility from the ground. By the end of October, the comet had reached perihelion, at which point its brightness increased sharply.

As 3I/ATLAS continues to move away from the Sun, it is expected to be observable from Earth in November and December 2023.

Official statements

“The rapid brightening of 3I/ATLAS is unusual and indicates significant changes on its surface,” said Kecheng Zhang of the Lowell Observatory.
“The behavior of 3I/ATLAS may depend on its composition and its high velocity as it approaches the Sun,” noted Karl Battams from the U.S. Naval Research Laboratory.

Context

Comet 3I/ATLAS is being studied as it is believed to be one of the oldest comets ever observed. The unusual brightness and color changes challenge existing models of comet behavior, prompting further investigation into the dynamics of interstellar objects.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS as it becomes visible from Earth in the coming months. Observations will help clarify the reasons behind its rapid brightening and unusual characteristics.
