‘Alien’ Comet 3I/ATLAS Gets Closest On Friday — Here’s How To See It

Comet 3I/ATLAS will make its closest approach to Earth on Friday, December 19, 2025, at a distance of approximately 270 million kilometers. Discovered on July 1, 2025, by the Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System (ATLAS) telescope in Río Hurtado, Chile, this comet has sparked interest due to its interstellar origins.

This event is significant as it marks the third known interstellar comet, following 1I/Oumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019. Authorities and astronomers are preparing viewing events to allow the public to observe the comet as it reaches its peak brightness. Dr. Gary Blackwood, a retired NASA Jet Propulsion Laboratory astronomer, will host a live viewing event.

Key facts

  • Closest approach to Earth: December 19, 2025.
  • Distance from Earth: 270 million kilometers (168 million miles).
  • Distance from the sun at closest approach: 203 million kilometers (126 million miles) on October 29, 2025.
  • Estimated weight of the comet: 44 million metric tons (48.5 tons).
  • Estimated radius: 260-370 meters (850-1,200 ft).
  • Live viewing event time: 04:00 UTC (11:00 p.m. EST on December 18, 2025).
  • Public skywatching event location: RESET Hotel, Twentynine Palms, California.

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was discovered on July 1, 2025, by the ATLAS telescope in Chile. The comet reached its closest point to the sun on October 29, 2025. It will approach Earth on December 19, 2025, providing a rare opportunity for observation.

Official statements

“After billions of years of galactic solitude and just moments in the warmth of our sun, interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS makes its closest approach to Earth on Friday,” said Dr. Gary Blackwood in an email.
“Comet 3I/ATLAS has become both a scientific and cultural phenomenon, and this week the eyes and telescopes of the world will be watching to learn everything we can,” Blackwood added.

Context

The discovery of interstellar comets has increased interest in celestial bodies originating outside our solar system. The previous two known interstellar comets, 1I/Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov, have also prompted discussions about extraterrestrial life and the nature of our universe.

What’s next

Following the closest approach, astronomers will continue to study 3I/ATLAS to gather data about its composition and behavior before it departs our solar system.
