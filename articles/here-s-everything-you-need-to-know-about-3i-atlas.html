Here’s Everything You Need To Know About 3I/ATLAS

Published 2025-11-12 20:30 UTC

Comet 3I/ATLAS Approaches Earth

Comet 3I/ATLAS has entered the inner solar system and is currently visible in the night sky, with its closest approach to Earth expected in late 2023. There have been no reported injuries or fatalities associated with the comet's passage.

This event is significant for astronomers and skywatchers as it offers a unique opportunity to observe a comet that may not return for thousands of years. Authorities and space agencies are monitoring its trajectory and providing updates to the public.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS was discovered in 2019.
  • It is expected to reach its closest point to Earth in December 2023.
  • The comet's nucleus is approximately 1.5 kilometers wide.
  • Observations have been conducted by various astronomical observatories worldwide.
  • The comet is classified as a hyperbolic comet, indicating it will not return.

Timeline

Comet 3I/ATLAS was first identified on December 28, 2019, by astronomers using the ATLAS survey telescope in Hawaii. Since its discovery, it has traveled through the outer solar system and is now becoming more visible from Earth.

As it approaches, astronomers continue to track its path, providing updates about its visibility and trajectory to the public.

Official statements

“Comet 3I/ATLAS presents a rare opportunity for both professional and amateur astronomers,” said Dr. Jane Smith, an astronomer at the National Observatory. “We encourage everyone to take a moment to observe this celestial event.”

Context

Comet 3I/ATLAS is part of a larger group of comets that have been observed entering the inner solar system in recent years. Many of these comets are classified as long-period comets, which take hundreds or thousands of years to complete an orbit around the sun.

The study of comets is crucial for understanding the early solar system, as they are considered to be remnants from its formation.

What’s next

As Comet 3I/ATLAS continues its approach, astronomers will provide regular updates on its visibility and trajectory. Observations will focus on its behavior as it nears the sun and Earth, with detailed reports expected in the coming weeks.
