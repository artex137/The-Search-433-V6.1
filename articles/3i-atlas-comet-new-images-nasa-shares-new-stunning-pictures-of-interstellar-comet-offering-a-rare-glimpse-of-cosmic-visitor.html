3I-ATLAS Comet New Images: NASA Shares New Stunning Pictures Of Interstellar Comet, Offering A Rare Glimpse Of Cosmic Visitor

Published 2025-11-20 05:45 UTC

NASA Releases Stunning Images of Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS

NASA has released new images of the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, providing scientists and space enthusiasts with a rare glimpse of a cosmic visitor from beyond our Solar System. The images, captured by various NASA assets, showcase the comet's journey through the inner Solar System since its discovery on July 1, 2023.

The release of these images marks an important milestone in space exploration, as 3I/ATLAS is the third confirmed interstellar object to enter our Solar System, following ‘Oumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019. NASA's observations are expected to yield valuable insights into the building blocks of the universe, as the comet traveled billions of kilometers before reaching Earth.

Key facts

  • Discovery Date: July 1, 2023
  • Speed: 30,000 mph
  • Closest Approach to Earth: December 19, 170 million miles
  • Notable Brightening: Near perihelion on October 29
  • Composition: Carbon dioxide, water ice, and nickel
  • Size: Estimated hundreds of meters to a few kilometers
  • Images Captured By: 12 NASA assets

Timeline

3I/ATLAS has been observed by various spacecraft since its discovery:

  • October 2: HiRISE on Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter captures images from approximately 19 million miles from Mars.
  • October 4: Perseverance Rover uses its Mastcam-Z to capture views of the comet.
  • October 9: MAVEN Orbiter conducts ultraviolet imaging of the comet.
  • September 11 - October 2: STEREO solar probes track the comet's motion.
  • October 15 - 26: SOHO captures additional observations.
  • December: The James Webb Space Telescope is scheduled to study the comet.

Official statements

“3I/ATLAS provides a rare chance to study material from another star system, offering insights into matter that has traveled billions of kilometers,” a NASA spokesperson said.

Context

The comet's unusual trajectory has sparked interest and speculation worldwide, drawing attention to NASA for its official observations. 3I/ATLAS is among a small group of interstellar objects that have entered our Solar System, presenting unique research opportunities for scientists.

What’s next

As 3I/ATLAS continues its journey, scientists look forward to upcoming studies by the James Webb Space Telescope, which promises to deliver detailed composition data about the comet.
