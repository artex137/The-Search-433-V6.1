NASA tracks rare interstellar comet carrying organic molecules

NASA has confirmed that the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS has brightened significantly after its passage near the Sun, releasing a surge of water vapor and carbon-rich molecules. This event marks a unique opportunity for scientists to study materials that have been sealed for billions of years in deep space.

The comet's delayed outburst offers insights into the composition of celestial bodies and the processes that govern their activity. NASA's SPHEREx telescope, designed to map the sky in infrared light, recorded the comet’s glow during a December 2025 observing run. Analyzing this data, researchers from the Johns Hopkins Applied Physics Laboratory have identified key organic compounds, including methanol, cyanide, and methane.

Key facts

  • Comet designation: 3I/ATLAS
  • First observed: July 1, 2025
  • SPHEREx mission launched: March 11, 2025
  • Comet's closest approach to the Sun: December 2025
  • Key compounds detected: methanol, cyanide, methane
  • Next observation planned: April 2026

Timeline

On July 1, 2025, astronomers discovered comet 3I/ATLAS using a telescope system designed to detect incoming asteroids and comets. The comet’s designation indicates its interstellar origin, with the “I” marking its unique status.

In December 2025, the comet made its closest approach to the Sun, during which it exhibited unexpected brightness, indicating a second phase of activity. NASA's SPHEREx telescope captured detailed observations of the comet's emissions, revealing a wealth of organic molecules.

As the comet continues its trajectory out of the solar system, SPHEREx plans to conduct another scan in April 2026 to gather additional data before it fades from view.

Official statements

“And since comets consist of about one-third bulk water ice, it was releasing an abundance of new, carbon-rich material that had remained locked in ice deep below the surface,” said Dr. Carey Lisse, a planetary scientist at Johns Hopkins Applied Physics Laboratory in Laurel, Maryland.

Context

3I/ATLAS is only the third known interstellar comet, making its study particularly valuable. Previous observations of such comets have provided limited insights, underscoring the significance of this opportunity for researchers.

The SPHEREx mission aims to map galaxies and search for life-friendly chemistry, with the recent observations of 3I/ATLAS showcasing the potential for unexpected discoveries during its broader survey operations.

What’s next

The SPHEREx team will continue to analyze the data collected from 3I/ATLAS, with plans for further observations in April 2026. Researchers hope to refine their understanding of the comet's composition and the processes that govern its activity as it exits the solar system.
