Interstellar visitor 3I/ATLAS sparks interest as a ‘Black Swan’ in space

Scientists have verified the presence of 3I/ATLAS, an interstellar object detected approximately 350 million km from Earth, first identified by the ATLAS survey in 2025. This comet will come closest to the Sun on 29th October and poses no threat to the planet.

The discovery of 3I/ATLAS is significant as it marks the third interstellar object detected, with researchers calling it a possible permanent "black swan event." This term refers to an occurrence that is rare, highly impactful, and difficult to predict. Authorities are now focused on studying the object as it approaches the Sun.

Key facts

  • Object Name: 3I/ATLAS
  • Distance: Approximately 350 million km from Earth
  • Closest Approach to the Sun: 29th October
  • Discovery Year: 2025
  • Observation Tools: Hubble Space Telescope and ground-based telescopes

Timeline

In 2025, the ATLAS survey identified 3I/ATLAS, marking it as an interstellar object. Recent observations have revealed its diffuse coma and early tail formation due to sublimating ices as it nears the Sun. The object will approach the Sun on 29th October before continuing its trajectory out of the Solar System.

Official statements

“This occurrence provides scientists with an opportunity to examine material that has been formed in other star systems,” a researcher stated regarding the significance of studying 3I/ATLAS.

Context

The term "Black Swan" is used in scientific circles to describe highly unlikely events that can have profound implications. The unusual trajectory and structure of 3I/ATLAS distinguish it from typical interstellar objects, prompting further investigation into its origins and properties.

What’s next

As 3I/ATLAS approaches the Sun, scientists will continue to study this interstellar visitor to gain insights into its material and formation, which may enhance understanding of extrasolar objects.
