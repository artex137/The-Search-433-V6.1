Has 3I/ATLAS Exploded Near The Sun?

The interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS may have exploded or broken apart as it approached the Sun, according to new observations by astronomers. Recent images taken by British astronomers Michael Buechner and Frank Niebling show the comet developing a significant “anti-tail” and a long trail of jets, which suggest it is losing material rapidly. The comet is expected to make its closest approach to Earth on December 19, 2023.

This development is significant because it raises questions about the nature of 3I/ATLAS and its origins. Authorities, including astronomers and astrophysicists, are currently analyzing the data to understand the comet's behavior and potential fragmentation. Harvard astrophysicist Avi Loeb has expressed concerns that this event may indicate a catastrophic breakup of the comet.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS is the third known interstellar object in the solar system.
  • Photographs indicate the comet is losing material at a rate that has increased dramatically.
  • The comet's mass loss rate jumped from 150 kilograms per second to nearly two million kilograms per second.
  • Avi Loeb estimates the comet’s effective absorbing area is about 1,600 square kilometers, nearly four times larger than earlier measurements.
  • The closest approach to Earth is scheduled for December 19, 2023.
  • The closest approach to Jupiter is expected in March 2026.

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first observed as it entered the solar system. Recent observations leading up to its perihelion have shown increasing activity, culminating in the current analysis of its potential breakup. Loeb's findings suggest that the comet experienced a catastrophic event as it reached its closest distance to the Sun.

Data analysis indicates that the comet's mass loss rate has surged in the weeks leading to perihelion, suggesting significant changes in its structure. As astronomers gather more evidence, they are also exploring alternative theories regarding the comet's nature.

Official statements

“The dramatic mass loss and brightening at perihelion could mean that 3I/ATLAS disintegrated,” said Avi Loeb. “A breakup into fragments would have increased its surface area and explained the sudden outburst of brightness.”
“The only natural explanation for such extreme activity is that the comet fragmented into at least 16 parts,” Loeb added.

Context

3I/ATLAS is notable for being one of the few objects identified as interstellar in origin. Previous studies of interstellar comets have provided insights into the composition and behavior of celestial bodies entering the solar system. The current analysis of 3I/ATLAS could offer further understanding of these phenomena.

What’s next

Astronomers will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS as it approaches Earth and gather more evidence regarding its status. Future studies may reveal more about the comet's fragmentation and potential implications for our understanding of interstellar objects.
