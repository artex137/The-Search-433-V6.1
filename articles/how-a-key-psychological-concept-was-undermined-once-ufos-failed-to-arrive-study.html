How a key psychological concept was undermined once 'UFOs failed to arrive': study

A book published in 1956, titled When Prophecy Fails, has come under scrutiny as a possible fabrication, according to an article by Anna Merlan in Mother Jones. The book, which has been influential among UFO believers, has been described as "an enduring classic in the fields of new religious studies, cult research, and social psychology."

This development raises questions about the psychological framework used to understand belief in UFOs and other phenomena. Authorities in the field of psychology are now reassessing the implications of this work on cult behavior and belief systems.

Key facts

  • Book Title: When Prophecy Fails
  • Publication Year: 1956
  • Authors: Leon Festinger, Henry Riecken, and Stanley Schachter
  • Source of Claims: Anna Merlan, Mother Jones
  • Status: Book's reliability questioned

Timeline

In 1956, When Prophecy Fails was published, exploring the behavior of a group awaiting a predicted UFO arrival. The authors studied the group's response when the prophecy did not materialize.

Recent discussions have emerged, citing concerns about the book's credibility and its influence on subsequent research in psychology and cult studies.

Official statements

“This book has shaped our understanding of how belief can persist in the face of contradictory evidence,” said Merlan in her article.

Context

The book has been a reference point for researchers examining how groups respond to failed prophecies. It has influenced various studies on cognitive dissonance and belief persistence in religious and cult-like groups.

What’s next

Scholars are expected to engage in further discussions and investigations regarding the implications of the book's credibility on existing psychological theories and practices.
