3I/ATLAS: Harvard Astrophysicist Avi Loeb Decodes The X-ray Emitted By Exocomet As Spotted By XRISM Xtend

Harvard astrophysicist Avi Loeb has analyzed the X-ray emissions from comet 3I/ATLAS, which was discovered on July 1, 2025, by the Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System (ATLAS). The analysis follows observations made by the XRISM spacecraft from November 26 to November 28, 2025, indicating significant findings related to the comet's composition.

This research is crucial as it provides insight into the nature of 3I/ATLAS, the third interstellar visitor recorded in the solar system, following 1I/Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov. Authorities, including the Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency (JAXA) and NASA, are collaborating on further studies to understand the comet's characteristics and its potential impact on our solar system.

Key facts

  • Discovery date: July 1, 2025
  • Observation dates: November 26, 2025, to November 28, 2025
  • Observing agency: XRISM (X-Ray Imaging and Spectroscopy Mission)
  • Distance of X-ray glow: approximately 400,000 km
  • Emission components detected: carbon, nitrogen, and oxygen

Timeline

On July 1, 2025, astronomers discovered comet 3I/ATLAS. Following its discovery, various Earth- and space-based observatories have tracked and studied its properties. Between November 26 and November 28, 2025, the XRISM spacecraft conducted a Target of Opportunity observation, revealing a faint X-ray glow around the comet.

On November 29, 2025, XRISM announced its preliminary findings in The Astronomer’s Telegram (ATel), highlighting the significant X-ray emissions detected.

Official statements

“The Xtend spectrum shows excess emission components associated with carbon, nitrogen, and oxygen beyond the background emission from the Milky-Way galaxy or the Earth’s atmospheric emission,” said Avi Loeb in his latest blog post.
“This is an important clue that the observed emission may indeed arise from charge-exchange interactions between the gas surrounding 3I/ATLAS and the solar wind,” Loeb added.
“It would be particularly interesting to check if there are any other X-ray features different from the expected signatures of charge-exchange reactions with the solar wind,” he concluded.

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third interstellar object recorded in the solar system. Its predecessors, 1I/Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov, have provided valuable insights into the characteristics of interstellar objects. The collaboration between JAXA and NASA on XRISM marks a significant advancement in our ability to study such celestial phenomena.

What’s next

Further studies are expected to continue as researchers analyze the X-ray emissions from 3I/ATLAS. Scientists aim to investigate additional features that may emerge from the comet's interactions with the solar wind.
