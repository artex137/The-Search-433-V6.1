James Webb Space Telescope Observes Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS for the First Time

The James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) has observed interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS for the first time on August 6, 2025, providing significant insights into this celestial body. The comet, discovered on July 1, 2025, by the ATLAS survey telescope, is only the third known object believed to originate from outside the solar system.

This discovery is important because it enhances the understanding of conditions in star systems beyond our own. Authorities and astronomers are now analyzing data collected by the JWST to learn more about the comet's properties and its origins.

Key facts

  • Observation date: August 6, 2025
  • Discovery date: July 1, 2025
  • Previous interstellar comets: 1I/'Oumuamua (2017), 2I/Borisov (2019)
  • Instruments used: JWST, Near-Infrared Spectrograph (NIRspec)
  • Key components identified: Carbon dioxide, water vapor, water ice, carbon monoxide, carbonyl sulfide
  • Comet age: Approximately 7 billion years

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first discovered on July 1, 2025, by the ATLAS survey telescope. Subsequent observations by the JWST occurred on August 6, 2025, utilizing advanced infrared technology to study the comet's characteristics.

Prior observations were made by the Hubble Space Telescope and the SPHEREx Observatory, laying groundwork for JWST's findings.

Official statements

“Studying interstellar comets like 3I/ATLAS offers vital information about the conditions that prevailed in the star systems from which they originated,” astronomers stated in a preprint paper detailing their findings.

Context

Prior to 3I/ATLAS, only two other interstellar comets had been recorded. These observations contribute to a growing understanding of celestial bodies that venture into our solar system from distant regions.

What’s next

As research continues, the JWST will further investigate 3I/ATLAS, aiming to uncover more secrets about this ancient comet and its origins.
