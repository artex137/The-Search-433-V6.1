ESO’s Very Large Telescope Tracks the Journey of Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS

Interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS has been tracked by the European Southern Observatory’s (ESO) Very Large Telescope (VLT) in Chile, following its discovery on 1 July 2025. This comet is only the third confirmed interstellar object to enter our Solar System, providing astronomers a rare opportunity to study material formed around another star.

The tracking of 3I/ATLAS is significant as it contributes to our understanding of interstellar objects and their composition. Major astronomical facilities have joined the observation efforts, including Hubble and the James Webb Space Telescope (JWST), enhancing the data available for study.

Key facts

  • Discovery Date: 1 July 2025
  • Imaging Date: 3 July 2025
  • Location: Atacama Desert, Chile
  • Closest Approach to Sun: 30 October 2025
  • Expected Reappearance: Early December 2025
  • Composition: Rich in carbon dioxide, water ice, and other volatiles

Timeline

Interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS was first discovered by the ATLAS survey on 1 July 2025. Two days later, astronomers at ESO used the VLT’s FORS2 instrument to capture a sequence of images over 13 minutes. This imaging provided the clearest view of the comet at that time.

Following the initial observations, Hubble imaged the comet in late July, and JWST followed with observations in early August. NASA’s SPHEREx mission also captured spectra of the comet later in August, contributing to the understanding of its composition.

Official statements

“The discovery of 3I/ATLAS adds to the list of interstellar visitors, enhancing our knowledge of objects that come from beyond our Solar System,” said a spokesperson from ESO.

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third interstellar object confirmed to have visited our Solar System, following 1I/ʻOumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019. These objects provide unique insights into the formation and composition of materials in other star systems.

What’s next

3I/ATLAS will reach its closest approach to the Sun on 30 October 2025. It will be lost in solar glare at that time and will not be observable from Earth. The comet is expected to reappear in early December 2025 as it moves away from the Sun, with a close approach to Earth anticipated in mid-to-late December. The comet poses no threat to our planet.
