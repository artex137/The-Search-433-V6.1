A Complex Jet Structure Emanates from 3I/ATLAS After Perihelion

On November 8, 2025, at 4:10 Universal Time (UT), the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS displayed a complex jet structure, as observed by M. Jäger, G. Rhemann, and E. Prosperi. The comet was located 29 degrees from the Sun in the sky, and observations revealed at least seven distinct jets extending from the comet's coma, which reached out to half a million kilometers.

This observation is significant as it provides insights into the behavior and characteristics of interstellar comets. The findings contribute to the growing body of knowledge regarding such objects and their interactions with the solar system. Observers reported that bright moonlight interfered with the visibility of the comet during the observations.

Key facts

  • Event Date: November 8, 2025
  • Observation Time: 4:10 UT
  • Location: 29 degrees from the Sun
  • Comet's Coma Size: Half a million kilometers (5 arcminutes)
  • Number of Observers: M. Jäger, G. Rhemann, E. Prosperi
  • Observation Conditions: Bright moonlight, twilight interference
  • Number of Exposures: 24 exposures in green filter, 2 in red filter, 2 in blue filter

Timeline

On November 8, 2025, at 4:10 UT, M. Jäger, G. Rhemann, and E. Prosperi began observing 3I/ATLAS. The comet was positioned 29 degrees from the Sun and was seen at an altitude of 7–10 degrees above the horizon. Observations continued until twilight conditions hindered visibility.

The team captured a series of images consisting of 24 exposures in a green filter, each lasting 35 seconds, along with additional exposures in red and blue filters. The resulting images revealed a complex jet structure with at least seven distinct jets.

Official statements

“The images show at least 7 distinct jets, some of which are anti-tails in the sunward direction,” said M. Jäger, emphasizing the complexity of the comet's behavior.

Context

This observation of 3I/ATLAS adds to the scientific understanding of interstellar objects. Previous studies of comets have indicated that their jet structures can provide clues about their composition and the processes occurring as they approach the Sun. The analysis of such phenomena is crucial for astrophysical research.

What’s next

Further analysis of the images and data collected from the observations is expected to enhance the understanding of interstellar comets. Researchers will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS as it travels through the solar system.
