ESA’s JUICE captures a detailed image of Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS

The European Space Agency’s (ESA) JUICE spacecraft has captured a detailed image of interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, which was first observed on July 1, 2025, by the ATLAS telescope in Chile. The comet, traveling on a hyperbolic trajectory, is notable for originating from interstellar space, a phenomenon that has been observed only twice before.

This observation is significant as it provides scientists with a rare opportunity to study material formed around another star. As 3I/ATLAS approached the Sun, it became active, shedding gas and dust, which allowed JUICE to capture images just days after the comet’s closest approach to the Sun.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS discovered on July 1, 2025.
  • JUICE took images between November 2 and 25, 2025.
  • 3I/ATLAS is traveling on a hyperbolic path, indicating it is from interstellar space.
  • JUICE’s observation occurred near the comet’s peak activity.
  • Data transmission from JUICE will be completed in February 2026.

Timeline

On July 1, 2025, astronomers first identified comet 3I/ATLAS using the ATLAS telescope in Chile. Following calculations of its orbit, scientists determined it was not from the Solar System due to its hyperbolic trajectory.

As the comet approached the Sun, it exhibited typical comet behavior, forming a coma and tails. By November 2025, JUICE, originally designed for a mission to Jupiter, redirected its instruments to capture images of 3I/ATLAS.

Between November 2 and 25, the spacecraft successfully captured images of the comet, revealing a bright coma and two distinct tails. The data from these observations will be transmitted back to Earth, with complete details expected in February 2026.

Official statements

“The fact that a spacecraft like JUICE, millions of kilometers from Earth, managed to capture a clean image is remarkable,” a spokesperson for ESA stated.

Context

Prior to 2025, only two interstellar comets, 1I/ʻOumuamua and 2I/Borisov, had been confirmed. The arrival of 3I/ATLAS presents a unique opportunity for scientists to study the composition of interstellar material, which may differ from Solar System comets.

Ground-based observations have suggested that 3I/ATLAS may contain higher levels of carbon dioxide compared to many Solar System comets, indicating it might have formed under different conditions.

What’s next

Scientists will analyze the data received from JUICE in February 2026. This analysis could provide insights into the composition of 3I/ATLAS and enhance our understanding of comet formation across different star systems.
