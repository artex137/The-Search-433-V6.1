Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS: NASA’s New Images, UN Planetary-Defense Drill and the Truth Behind the ‘Alien Visitor’ Hype

NASA has released new images of interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS as it speeds through the solar system, and the United Nations has confirmed that the comet is the focus of a global planetary-defense drill. The latest developments were reported on November 25, 2025, amidst ongoing discussions about the comet's nature and origins.

This drill is significant as it marks the eighth observing exercise by the International Asteroid Warning Network (IAWN) since 2017. Authorities are using 3I/ATLAS to practice precise position measurements of an active comet, which is more challenging than tracking asteroids. Scientists aim to gather more data on the comet while ensuring public safety, as it poses no threat to Earth.

Key facts

  • Discovery Date: July 1, 2025, by the ATLAS survey telescope in Rio Hurtado, Chile.
  • Closest Approach to Sun: October 29, 2025, at approximately 1.36 astronomical units (AU).
  • Distance from Earth: Will pass no closer than about 170 million miles (≈1.8 AU) in mid-December 2025.
  • Age: Estimated around 7–8 billion years, older than the solar system.
  • NASA Missions Involved: Twelve missions, including Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter and Perseverance rover.

Timeline

3I/ATLAS has been observed continuously since its discovery. Key events include:

  • July 1, 2025: Discovery of 3I/ATLAS by ATLAS telescope.
  • October 2, 2025: Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter captures close views of the comet.
  • November 19, 2025: NASA announces a comprehensive observing campaign involving multiple missions.
  • November 25, 2025: UN confirms the start of the planetary-defense drill.

Official statements

“3I/ATLAS poses no threat and was specifically chosen because it’s safe but scientifically interesting,” the UN/IAWN document states.

Context

This event follows the discovery of two previous interstellar objects, 1I/ʻOumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019. These observations have prompted increased interest in tracking such objects and understanding their origins.

What’s next

The IAWN observing campaign will run from November 27, 2025, to January 27, 2026, with a close-out teleconference scheduled for February 3, 2026. Scientists will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS and gather data on its composition and trajectory.
