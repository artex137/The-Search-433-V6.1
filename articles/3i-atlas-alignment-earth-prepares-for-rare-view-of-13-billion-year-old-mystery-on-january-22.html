3I/ATLAS Alignment: Earth Prepares For Rare View of 13-Billion-Year-Old Mystery on January 22

On January 22, 2026, Earth will align with the sun and the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS at 13:00 UTC, providing a unique opportunity to study this cosmic visitor. Discovered by the NASA-funded ATLAS survey telescope in Chile on July 1, 2025, 3I/ATLAS is only the third confirmed object from another star system, following 'Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov.

This alignment is significant as it may shed light on the unusual characteristics of 3I/ATLAS, which has perplexed astronomers since its discovery. The alignment will allow scientists to observe the object’s unique features, including its strong 'anti-tail' and a system of three 'mini-jets.' NASA and other astronomical agencies are preparing to gather data during this rare event.

Key facts

  • Date of alignment: January 22, 2026
  • Time of closest alignment: 13:00 UTC
  • Angle between sun-3I/ATLAS axis and sun-Earth axis: 0.69 degrees
  • Length of anti-tail: at least 400,000 kilometers
  • Number of mini-jets: three, arranged at 120-degree angles
  • Potential age of 3I/ATLAS: approximately 13 billion years
  • Initial spectroscopic findings: water ice, carbon dioxide, traces of nickel

Timeline

On July 1, 2025, the ATLAS survey telescope in Chile discovers 3I/ATLAS. Since then, astronomers have conducted numerous observations, noting its unusual features. The upcoming alignment on January 22, 2026, will allow for a close study of these characteristics.

The alignment will create a phenomenon known as the 'opposition surge,' making the object appear significantly brighter as Earth passes through a two-degree angle with 3I/ATLAS for approximately one week.

Official statements

“This rare alignment is more than a celestial photo opportunity; it is a high-stakes scientific moment that could finally explain why this 'interstellar vagabond' behaves like nothing ever seen in our own solar system,” said an astronomer involved in the study.

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third interstellar object confirmed to have entered our solar system. Its predecessors, 'Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov, exhibited different characteristics and behaviors. The unusual features of 3I/ATLAS have sparked debates about whether it is a natural comet or a product of active technology.

What’s next

Following the alignment, scientists will analyze data collected during this event to better understand the composition and origin of 3I/ATLAS. This may be the only opportunity to gather evidence of its long journey through space.
