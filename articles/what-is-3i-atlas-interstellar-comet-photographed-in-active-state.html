What is 3I/ATLAS? Interstellar comet photographed in 'active state'

The interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS has been confirmed as the third known object to visit our solar system from interstellar space, with its closest approach to Earth occurring on December 19, 2025. No injuries or fatalities have been reported related to the comet.

This discovery is significant for the scientific community as it provides a rare opportunity to study material from outside our solar system. Authorities, including NASA and the European Space Agency (ESA), have been actively observing and photographing 3I/ATLAS, capturing its unique features and behavior.

Key facts

  • Confirmed as interstellar in July 2025
  • Closest approach to Earth on December 19, 2025
  • Travels at approximately 137,000 mph
  • Originated from the direction of the constellation Sagittarius
  • Observed by ESA's Jupiter Icy Moons Explorer (Juice) from November 2 to November 25, 2025
  • Distance of closest approach was about 170 million miles from Earth
  • Visible until spring 2026 in the pre-dawn sky

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first spotted by a telescope in Chile, part of the NASA-funded Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System (ATLAS), in July 2025. The object was confirmed to be the third interstellar object entering our solar system.

On October 30, 2025, 3I/ATLAS made its closest approach to the sun, entering a very active state. The ESA's Juice orbiter observed the comet between November 2 and November 25, capturing images that revealed its gaseous and dusty halo. The images were received on Earth in February 2026.

The comet passed within approximately 170 million miles of Earth on December 19, 2025, and will continue to move out of the solar system, never to return.

Official statements

“3I/ATLAS shows all the signs of being a comet from outside our solar system,” said NASA officials in response to ongoing conspiracy theories about the object's nature.
“The new image captures 3I/ATLAS spewing gas and dust from 41 million miles away,” the ESA stated regarding the observations made by the Juice orbiter.

Context

The discovery of 3I/ATLAS builds on previous findings of interstellar objects, enhancing our understanding of materials ejected from other star systems. It represents a significant moment for astronomers as they study the dynamics and characteristics of such rare visitors.

Prior interstellar objects, such as 'Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov, have sparked interest and debate in the scientific community, prompting further investigations into the origins and behaviors of these celestial bodies.

What’s next

3I/ATLAS will continue to be monitored until spring 2026 as it exits our solar system. NASA's online simulation tools will provide updates on its path, allowing astronomers and the public to track its movements.
