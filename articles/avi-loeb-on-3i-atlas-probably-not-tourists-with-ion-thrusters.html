Avi Loeb On 3I/ATLAS: 'Probably Not Tourists' With 'Ion-Thrusters'

Harvard astrophysicist Avi Loeb has raised concerns that the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS may not be a typical comet, suggesting its features indicate engineered ion thrusters, rather than a natural phenomenon. Loeb's comments were published on the platform Medium.

This matter is significant as it challenges conventional theories about the object. Loeb argues that the behavior of 3I/ATLAS, particularly its jets, is inconsistent with natural cometary activity. He calls for further observations to better understand the object's nature and trajectory.

Key facts

  • Avi Loeb is the head of the Galileo Project.
  • 3I/ATLAS exhibits jets that Loeb suggests may act as technological thrusters.
  • The nucleus rotates approximately every 16.16 hours.
  • Loeb claims that 3I/ATLAS has not shown signs of fragmentation after a close pass around the Sun.
  • He estimates that the object would need a surface area of over 1,600 square kilometres to produce the observed jets.
  • Radio observations from the MeerKAT telescope detected hydroxyl radicals, indicative of water breakdown.

Timeline

Avi Loeb has analyzed the characteristics of 3I/ATLAS following its perihelion, or closest approach to the Sun. He noted that despite substantial solar heating, the object remained intact and active, contrary to expectations for typical cometary bodies.

In his analysis, Loeb observed that the jets of 3I/ATLAS maintain a consistent direction over large distances, a behavior he argues is inconsistent with natural outgassing.

Loeb published his findings on Medium, suggesting that the jets may serve as technological thrusters, pointing exhaust toward the Sun for propulsion.

Official statements

“The surface area required to account for the mass flow in the large-scale jets ... is untenable,” Loeb stated regarding the physical characteristics of 3I/ATLAS.
“This could be a deliberate post-perihelion manoeuvre ... employed by a spacecraft that aims to gain speed rather than slow down through gravitational assist,” he added.

Context

Previous studies of interstellar objects have often concluded that they are natural phenomena. However, Loeb's hypothesis introduces a new perspective that questions established comet models. Other scientists, including Darryl Seligman from Michigan State University, have expressed skepticism, noting that structural integrity is plausible for larger nuclei.

Additionally, non-gravitational acceleration is often observed in comets, further complicating the analysis of 3I/ATLAS.

What’s next

Astronomers will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS as it progresses on its trajectory. Loeb insists that upcoming observations could either support his thruster hypothesis or align with traditional comet theories.
