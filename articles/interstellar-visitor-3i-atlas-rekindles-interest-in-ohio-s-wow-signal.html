Interstellar Visitor 3I/ATLAS Rekindles Interest in Ohio’s “WOW!” Signal

As interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS speeds through the inner solar system, astronomers are drawing comparisons to the 1977 “WOW!” signal, a famous radio burst detected at Ohio State University’s Big Ear radio telescope. The comet, which is only the third confirmed interstellar object ever observed, is currently on a hyperbolic escape path and will not return after its passage.

The significance of 3I/ATLAS lies in its rapid, hyperbolic motion and high velocity, prompting renewed discussions about the “WOW!” signal. Astronomers are using advanced tracking techniques to study the comet's composition and trajectory while emphasizing the fleeting nature of both cosmic phenomena.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS is traveling at approximately 90,000 miles per hour.
  • It is the third confirmed interstellar object, following ‘Oumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019.
  • 3I/ATLAS began its journey through the solar system in early October 2023.
  • Initial observations suggest it contains traces of carbon monoxide and water ice.
  • The comet is expected to be visible for only a few months before it fades from view.

Timeline

3I/ATLAS entered the solar system from a direction near the Lynx constellation. It passed close to the orbit of Mars in early October 2023, continuing outward toward interstellar space. Preliminary tracking has established its hyperbolic escape path, indicating it will not return.

Official statements

“The discovery’s timing has renewed discussion of the ‘WOW!’ signal, which remains one of the most intriguing unexplained detections in radio astronomy history,” said an astronomer involved in the study.

Context

The “WOW!” signal was detected on August 15, 1977, and lasted for 72 seconds. It originated from the direction of the constellation Sagittarius and remains unexplained. The similarities between the “WOW!” signal and 3I/ATLAS highlight the challenges scientists face in studying transient cosmic events.

What’s next

Astronomers will continue to use spectroscopic analysis to gather more data on 3I/ATLAS’s composition as it approaches its peak brightness. The comet's rapid movement means that observations will be limited, emphasizing the urgency of ongoing studies.
