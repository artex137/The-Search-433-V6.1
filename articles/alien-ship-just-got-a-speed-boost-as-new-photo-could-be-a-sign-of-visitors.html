Comet 3I/ATLAS Raises Eyebrows with Unusual Behavior

The interstellar object 3I/ATLAS has displayed unexpected behavior, leading some scientists to speculate about its origins. According to NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL), the comet experienced a significant "non-gravitational acceleration" on October 30, 2025, shortly after its closest approach to the Sun.

This phenomenon is noteworthy as it deviates from typical comet behavior, prompting researchers to investigate further. The latest observations suggest that 3I/ATLAS may not be a natural comet but could potentially represent something engineered or artificial.

Key facts

  • Object Name: 3I/ATLAS
  • Non-gravitational acceleration: October 30, 2025
  • Closest approach to Earth: December 19, 2025
  • Observatories involved: Multiple independent telescopes
  • Notable scientists: Michio Kaku, Avi Loeb

Timeline

On October 30, 2025, 3I/ATLAS exhibited a clear acceleration that could not be explained by gravitational forces alone. This acceleration occurred shortly after the comet's closest approach to the Sun. Following this event, scientists began to analyze new images from a Utah-based observatory, which revealed unusual features in the comet's appearance.

The unusual characteristics included a bright, stable core and an "anti-tail" pointing towards the Sun, which contradicts typical comet behavior. The findings have been validated by three independent observers, increasing confidence in the observations.

Official statements

“The convergence of data makes 3I/ATLAS the most compelling candidate yet for an interstellar visitor of extraordinary origin,” said Avi Loeb from Harvard.
“Even if it behaves strangely, we must be careful not to rush to call it ‘alien,’” noted a researcher who preferred to remain anonymous.

Context

3I/ATLAS is not the first interstellar object to attract attention. Previous objects have generated interest, but none have exhibited the combination of features observed in 3I/ATLAS. The peculiarities of this comet are prompting discussions about the nature of interstellar objects and their potential origins.

Scientific models typically explain comet acceleration through jets of gas and dust expelled by solar heat. However, the required outgassing for 3I/ATLAS to achieve its measured boost seems implausible, as it would necessitate a substantial loss of mass, which has not been observed.

What’s next

Researchers are preparing for 3I/ATLAS's closest approach to Earth on December 19, 2025. This event will provide a critical opportunity for detailed observations and further investigation into the object's unusual characteristics.
