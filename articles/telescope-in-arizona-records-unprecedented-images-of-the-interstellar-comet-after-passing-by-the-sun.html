Telescope in Arizona records unprecedented images of the interstellar comet after passing by the Sun

A telescope in Arizona has captured unprecedented images of the interstellar comet 3I ATLAS after it passed by the Sun on February 21, 2026. The images reveal details about the comet’s structure and tail that have not been observed before.

This observation is significant as it enhances understanding of interstellar objects and their behavior in our solar system. Astronomers at the observatory are analyzing the data to gain insights into the composition and trajectory of this comet.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I ATLAS passed closest to the Sun on February 21, 2026.
  • Images were captured by a telescope located in Arizona.
  • The observations provide new insights into interstellar comet behavior.
  • Astronomers are currently analyzing the collected data.
  • No injuries or fatalities have been reported.

Timeline

On February 21, 2026, comet 3I ATLAS made its closest approach to the Sun. The Arizona telescope successfully recorded images of the comet during this event. The data collected is now under analysis by astronomers at the observatory.

Official statements

“These images are a breakthrough in our understanding of interstellar comets,” said a spokesperson for the observatory. “We are excited to analyze the data further.”

Context

This observation follows a growing interest in interstellar objects since the discovery of the first known interstellar comet, 1I/‘Oumuamua, in 2017. The study of such comets can provide valuable information about the formation of celestial bodies outside our solar system.

What’s next

Astronomers will continue to analyze the images and data gathered from the telescope to further understand the characteristics of comet 3I ATLAS and its implications for future studies of interstellar objects.
