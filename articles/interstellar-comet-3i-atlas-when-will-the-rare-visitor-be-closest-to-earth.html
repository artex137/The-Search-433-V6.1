Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS: When Will the Rare Visitor Be Closest to Earth?

Comet 3I/ATLAS, an interstellar visitor to our solar system, was first detected on July 1, 2025, by NASA’s ATLAS survey in Chile. The comet is moving at a speed of 220,000 km/h and is expected to reach its closest point to Earth on December 19, 2025, at a distance of 1.8 astronomical units (AU).

This discovery is significant as it marks the third confirmed interstellar object following ‘Oumuamua in 2017 and Borisov in 2019. Scientists are studying 3I/ATLAS to better understand the composition of interstellar materials and its implications for exoplanet studies.

Key facts

  • Discovery Date: July 1, 2025
  • Detection Location: ATLAS telescope, Rio Hurtado, Chile
  • Speed: 220,000 km/h
  • Closest Approach to Earth: December 19, 2025, at 1.8 AU
  • Perihelion Date: October 29, 2025, at 1.36 AU
  • Nucleus Size: Smaller than 5.6 km
  • Scientific Instruments Used: Hubble, JWST, Parker Solar Probe, PUNCH, STEREO, SOHO, XRISM

Timeline

Comet 3I/ATLAS was detected on July 1, 2025, during the ATLAS survey in Chile. Pre-discovery images from the Palomar Observatory dating back to June 14 revealed the comet as a faint object with a coma and tail. It reached perihelion on October 29, 2025, at a distance of 1.36 AU, and will be at its closest point to Earth on December 19, 2025.

NASA’s observatories have tracked the comet’s path, providing significant data. Hubble captured an image of the comet’s coma on July 21, while JWST studied its composition in August, finding active volatile ices at a distance of 6.4 AU. XRISM revealed emissions from the comet even at a distance of 248,000 miles.

Official statements

“3I/ATLAS provides pure interstellar material that has been created elsewhere and sent out into space,” said a NASA spokesperson. “This advances our understanding of the chemistry in distant star systems.”

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third interstellar object confirmed, following ‘Oumuamua and Borisov. Its unique trajectory and composition provide valuable insights into the materials that exist beyond our solar system. The study of such comets helps scientists learn more about the early solar system and the formation of celestial bodies.

What’s next

Ongoing observations by JWST in December 2025 are expected to yield deeper insights into 3I/ATLAS and its interstellar origins. Scientists will continue to analyze data collected from various instruments to enhance our understanding of rogue comets.
