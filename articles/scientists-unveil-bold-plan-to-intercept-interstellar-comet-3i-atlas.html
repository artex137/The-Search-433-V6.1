Scientists Unveil Bold Plan to Intercept Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS

Researchers from the Initiative for Interstellar Studies (i4is) have unveiled a plan to intercept the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, which is set to launch in 2035. This comet, only the third interstellar object detected, presents a significant opportunity for astronomers to study material from other star systems. The plan includes a Solar Oberth maneuver to overcome challenges posed by the comet's extreme velocity and late detection.

The mission aims to provide groundbreaking insights into the composition and origins of interstellar objects. Authorities are now focused on developing the required technology and mission architecture to ensure the success of the proposed intercept mission.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS is the third interstellar object ever detected.
  • The mission is scheduled for launch in 2035.
  • The comet travels at a velocity exceeding 60 km/s.
  • The Solar Oberth maneuver will be used to increase spacecraft velocity.
  • The mission proposal has been accepted for publication in the Journal of the British Interplanetary Society.
  • Key contributors include Adam Hibberd, T. Marshall Eubanks, and Andreas Hein.

Timeline

The initiative to intercept 3I/ATLAS began after the comet was detected, sparking multiple proposals for a rendezvous mission. The comet's late detection, occurring after it had already traveled inside Jupiter's orbit, prompted researchers to explore unconventional mission strategies. The i4is team has since developed a detailed plan leveraging a Solar Oberth maneuver.

Official statements

“The late detection of comet 3I/ATLAS meant that the optimal launch window for a direct mission had already passed,” said Adam Hibberd, lead author of the study.

Context

Previous missions to interstellar objects have primarily focused on chemical rockets or modifying existing missions. The challenges of intercepting fast-moving interstellar comets have led to innovative solutions, such as the proposed Solar Oberth maneuver, which utilizes the Sun's gravitational pull.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to refine the mission architecture and technology needed for the 2035 launch. The focus will be on overcoming technological challenges and preparing for the complexities of studying an interstellar object up close.
