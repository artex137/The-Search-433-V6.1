Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS on November 28, 2025: UN Planetary-Defence Drill, NASA’s Verdict and How to See It

On November 28, 2025, interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS has become the focus of a United Nations-endorsed planetary-defence exercise and a significant NASA observation campaign, according to multiple sources. This event marks the third time an object from another star system has entered our Solar System.

The importance of this event lies in its implications for planetary defence and scientific research. The UN and the International Asteroid Warning Network (IAWN) have confirmed that 3I/ATLAS is the official target for their latest global observing exercise, which aims to enhance international coordination in the event of a real threat.

Key facts

  • Comet Name: 3I/ATLAS (C/2025 N1)
  • Discovery Date: July 1, 2025
  • Closest Approach to Sun: October 29-30, 2025, at 1.36 AU
  • Closest Approach to Earth: December 19, 2025, at approximately 1.8 AU (about 270 million km)
  • Threat Level: Zero, according to NASA and international teams
  • Observation Campaign Duration: November 27, 2025, to January 27, 2026
  • Mid-Campaign Review: December 9, 2025

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was discovered on July 1, 2025, by the NASA-funded ATLAS survey telescope in Chile. It passed closest to the Sun on October 29-30, 2025, and will reach its closest point to Earth on December 19, 2025. The UN and IAWN have designated it as the target for their observing exercise starting November 27, 2025.

NASA's Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter and other spacecraft have provided crucial data to refine the comet's orbit, resulting in a ten-fold improvement in orbital precision. This data confirms that the comet will not come close to Earth in a hazardous manner.

Official statements

“3I/ATLAS looks and behaves like a comet: it has an icy nucleus, a bright coma and a real tail driven by outgassing as it warms,” said a NASA official during a briefing.
“No technosignatures – there’s nothing in the data that looks like artificial emissions or engineered propulsion,” the official added, reiterating that the comet poses no threat to Earth or other planets.

Context

This event is significant as it represents a collaborative effort among international agencies to prepare for potential threats from celestial objects. The IAWN has previously conducted drills, but 3I/ATLAS serves as a safe and scientifically valuable target for this exercise.

The ongoing discussion surrounding the comet has been fueled by claims on social media regarding its unusual appearance. However, astronomers have clarified that these claims do not indicate danger.

What’s next

The observation campaign will continue until January 27, 2026, with a mid-campaign review scheduled for December 9, 2025. NASA and international teams will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS to gather more data and refine their understanding of its trajectory.
