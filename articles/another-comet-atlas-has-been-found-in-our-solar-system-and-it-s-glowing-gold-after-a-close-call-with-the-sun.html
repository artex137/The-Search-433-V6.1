Another Comet ATLAS Has Been Found in Our Solar System — And It’s Glowing Gold After a Close Call with the Sun!

Published 2025-11-06 15:49 UTC

Comet C/2025 K1 (ATLAS) Glows Gold After Surviving Close Encounter with the Sun

A newly discovered comet, C/2025 K1 (ATLAS), has emerged with a striking golden hue after a perilous journey near the Sun, reaching its closest point on October 8, 2025. Astronomers at the Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System (ATLAS) first identified the comet in May 2025. There have been no reports of injuries or fatalities related to this cosmic event.

This discovery is significant as it challenges existing knowledge about comets and their behavior during close encounters with the Sun. Astronomers are keen to understand how C/2025 K1 not only survived but transformed into a rare golden color, which is uncommon among comets.

Key facts

  • Comet C/2025 K1 (ATLAS) discovered in May 2025.
  • Closest approach to the Sun (perihelion) on October 8, 2025, within 31 million miles.
  • Comet's apparent magnitude is currently 9, making it visible with telescopes or binoculars.
  • Expected closest point to Earth on November 25, 2025.
  • Visible in the morning sky, particularly before sunrise, between the constellations Virgo and Leo.

Timeline

May 2025: Comet C/2025 K1 (ATLAS) is discovered by astronomers at ATLAS.

October 8, 2025: The comet reaches perihelion, coming within 31 million miles of the Sun.

November 25, 2025: C/2025 K1 is expected to reach its closest point to Earth.

Official statements

“This comet was not supposed to survive its Oct. 8th perihelion. But it did survive, and now it is displaying a red/brown/golden color rarely seen in comets,” said astrophotographer Dan Bartlett, speaking to Spaceweather.com.
“This comet’s survival is a testament to the sheer unpredictability of these icy travelers,” noted Dr. Amelia Harris, an astronomer studying cometary behavior.

Context

Most comets typically display white or blue hues due to the sunlight reflecting off their icy surfaces. C/2025 K1’s golden hue is particularly puzzling, as it lacks many carbon-bearing molecules, which are usually present in comets and contribute to their color. This anomaly raises questions about the chemical composition and behavior of comets during close solar encounters.

What’s next

Astronomers will continue to observe C/2025 K1 as it approaches Earth. This comet presents a unique opportunity to study the relationship between a comet’s chemical composition and its color, potentially expanding our understanding of other icy bodies in the solar system.
