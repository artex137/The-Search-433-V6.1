3I Atlas update reveals new surprising secrets of the comet

Over 120 science-grade photos from the Juice spacecraft’s onboard camera have reached research teams, offering an unprecedented look at comet 3I/ATLAS, as of March 5, 2026. No injuries or fatalities have been reported.

The arrival of the JANUS images is significant for researchers studying this interstellar comet. The images provide crucial data that can enhance understanding of the comet's composition and activity, which is essential for ongoing research in planetary science.

Key facts

  • Date of image capture: November 6, 2025
  • Distance from comet during image capture: 66 million kilometers
  • Number of images received: 120+
  • Instruments involved: JANUS, MAJIS, UVS, SWI, PEP
  • Next meeting for researchers: Late March 2026

Timeline

The discovery of comet 3I/ATLAS occurred on July 1, 2025. This marked the first identification of an interstellar comet in an active outgassing phase.

In November 2025, the Juice spacecraft conducted an observing campaign that collected over 120 images, alongside data from four other instruments. The images were delayed due to the spacecraft's positioning, which limited data transmission.

Researchers plan a formal integration meeting in late March 2026 to analyze the data and discuss the implications of the findings.

Official statements

“The arrival of the full JANUS dataset changes the scope of what researchers can do with the information,” said a spokesperson for the Juice mission.

Context

Prior to this update, navigation camera shots had provided limited insights into comet 3I/ATLAS. These earlier images lacked the spectral range and resolution necessary for in-depth analysis.

The Juice spacecraft’s five active instruments working simultaneously during the comet’s solar approach is a rare opportunity, as most interstellar objects pass through with minimal observational capabilities.

What’s next

Researchers will focus on whether structural features visible in the JANUS images correspond to distinct chemical or dust signatures in the spectrometry and particle datasets during the upcoming March meeting. Further analysis may yield detailed conclusions about the comet's activity.
