The Alien Question Hiding Behind 3I/ATLAS's Faint Green Glow

3I/ATLAS, a faint object detected by the ATLAS telescope in Hawaii earlier this year, has generated interest among astronomers due to its unusual characteristics. Initially classified as a comet, it has since become one of the most puzzling interstellar objects recorded, sparking speculation about its possible artificial origins.

This matter is significant as 3I/ATLAS is only the third confirmed interstellar object to pass through our solar system, following 1I/ʻOumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019. Observations from NASA's James Webb Space Telescope have revealed unique chemical compositions and unexpected behaviors that challenge existing models of cometary activity.

Key facts

  • Object Name: 3I/ATLAS
  • Detection Date: Early 2023
  • Observatories Involved: ATLAS telescope in Hawaii, NASA's James Webb Space Telescope
  • Notable Composition: Carbon dioxide to water vapor ratio of 8:1
  • Orbital Path: Hyperbolic, indicating it will not return to the solar system
  • Current Status: Approaching the Sun, expected to leave the solar system soon

Timeline

In early 2023, astronomers detected 3I/ATLAS using the ATLAS telescope in Hawaii. Initially classified as a comet, further studies revealed its unusual trajectory and chemical makeup. By August 2025, analysis from NASA's James Webb Space Telescope indicated a significant carbon dioxide concentration, raising questions about its origin. In September, NASA data showed the comet releasing water vapor far from the Sun, challenging existing cometary models.

Official statements

“The detection of water activity so far from the Sun has challenged existing models of cometary behavior,” said a NASA spokesperson.
“The evidence is consistent with a natural body, reflecting the diversity of environments in the Milky Way,” said a planetary scientist regarding the debate over 3I/ATLAS's origins.

Context

The discovery of 3I/ATLAS follows the earlier findings of 1I/ʻOumuamua and 2I/Borisov, which also attracted speculation about extraterrestrial technology. The discussions surrounding these objects highlight a growing curiosity in the scientific community to explore unconventional explanations for cosmic phenomena.

What’s next

As 3I/ATLAS moves behind the Sun in the coming weeks, observatories worldwide are working to gather final data before it fades from view. Researchers will continue to analyze its composition and trajectory, seeking answers to the questions surrounding its origins.
