Interstellar object 3I/ATLAS approaches Earth on December 19 for decisive tests

The interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS will reach perihelion on December 19, 2025, at a distance of two astronomical units from Earth. There have been no reported injuries or fatalities associated with this celestial event.

This approach provides a unique opportunity for space agencies to conduct coordinated observations of 3I/ATLAS, the third confirmed interstellar object in the Solar System. Authorities, including NASA, ESA, and ESO, are mobilizing ground and space telescopes to analyze the comet’s composition and trajectory, focusing on additional forces beyond gravity that influence its path.

Key facts

  • 3I/ATLAS detected by the ATLAS system in 2025.
  • Perihelion date: December 19, 2025.
  • Distance from Earth at perihelion: two astronomical units.
  • NASA and ESA coordinating international observations.
  • Observations include spectroscopy for chemical elements.
  • 3I/ATLAS exhibits non-gravitational acceleration.

Timeline

The ATLAS system first identified 3I/ATLAS in 2025. Since then, scientists have recorded variable brightness and unusual structure, including an anti-tail pointing toward the Sun. NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory has detected radial and transverse forces affecting the comet, which deviate from standard gravitational models.

In November and December, NASA coordinates with ESA and ESO for a campaign to capture daily images of the comet. Telescopes in Hawaii and Atacama are actively collecting data, including chemical spectra to determine the object’s nature.

Official statements

“The approach of 3I/ATLAS allows for detailed studies without the risk of collision,” said a spokesperson from NASA.

Context

3I/ATLAS follows in the footsteps of previous interstellar objects, such as 'Oumuamua in 2017 and Borisov in 2019. Research on 'Oumuamua indicated acceleration without a visible coma, while Borisov exhibited an extensive gas tail. The unique characteristics of 3I/ATLAS combine traits from both previous objects, prompting further investigation into the physical explanations behind these phenomena.

What’s next

As the December 19 perihelion date approaches, scientists will continue to collect data on 3I/ATLAS to determine its chemical composition and trajectory. Observations will help define whether the object is natural or requires alternative explanations based on its observed accelerations and structure.
