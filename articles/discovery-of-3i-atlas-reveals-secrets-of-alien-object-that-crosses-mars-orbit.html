Discovery of 3I/Atlas reveals secrets of alien object that crosses Mars’ orbit

A telescope in Chile detected the interstellar comet 3I/Atlas on July 1, 2025. This marks the third confirmed object from outside the Solar System. The comet exhibits cometic activity with a solid core that releases gas as it approaches the Sun, according to astronomers from various agencies.

The discovery is significant because it offers insights into the composition and behavior of interstellar objects. Agencies like NASA and the European Space Agency (ESA) are actively monitoring 3I/Atlas to analyze its chemical makeup and trajectory, ensuring no risk of collision with Earth.

Key facts

  • Date of discovery: July 1, 2025
  • Location of detection: Rio Hurtado, Chile
  • Speed: Approximately 57 km/s
  • Core diameter: Between 440 meters and 5.6 kilometers
  • Perihelion date: October 30, 2025
  • Closest approach to Earth: December 19, 2025, at 1.8 astronomical units (270 million kilometers)
  • Observing agencies: NASA, ESA, International Asteroid Warning Network

Timeline

The ATLAS system, funded by NASA, initially detected the object as potentially interstellar due to its excessive speed. Preliminary observations from June 14, 2025, confirmed its presence retroactively before official detection on July 1.

On July 2, telescopes in Arizona and Hawaii recorded activity, validating its nature. The comet reached perihelion on October 30, 2025, and passed within 29 million kilometers of Mars on October 3.

After perihelion, observations indicated the comet developed a 0.7-degree long ionic tail by November 11, 2025. Ground-based telescopes have recorded brightness ranging from magnitude 12 to 14.

Official statements

“The unique chemical composition of 3I/Atlas offers a glimpse into the conditions of ancient stars in the Milky Way,” said a representative from NASA.

Context

This discovery follows the detection of other interstellar objects, such as 2I/Borisov, and underscores the growing interest in tracking and studying such phenomena. The chemical composition of 3I/Atlas suggests it formed in a cold protoplanetary disk, indicating a different origin compared to typical Oort Cloud comets.

What’s next

The International Asteroid Warning Network will coordinate measurements until January 27, 2026, to refine models of the comet’s sublimation behavior. Observations will continue as 3I/Atlas approaches its closest point to Earth in December.
