3I/Atlas Comet (not an Alien Spaceship)

The 3I/Atlas comet is currently traveling at a speed of 137,000 miles per hour and is 219 million miles from Earth. It is not expected to be visible to the naked eye or with most telescopes, according to astronomers.

This information matters as it highlights the vastness of the universe and the challenges in observing distant celestial objects. Authorities and astronomers have noted that the comet will come as close as 170 million miles to Earth, but will continue to move away day by day.

Key facts

  • Current speed: 137,000 mph
  • Distance from Earth: 219 million miles
  • Closest approach to Earth: December 19, 2023
  • Earth's orbital speed: 67,000 mph
  • Time to reach Proxima Centauri: 20,831 years at current speed

Timeline

As of today, the 3I/Atlas comet is moving away from Earth’s orbit. Earth's orbit will bring it to its closest point on December 19, 2023. Despite this proximity, it will remain too small and distant to be easily observed.

The comet's journey illustrates the enormous distances in space, with Proxima Centauri, the nearest star to our Sun, located 25 trillion miles away. It would take over 20,000 years to reach it at the comet's current speed.

Official statements

“Even at its closest point, 3I will never be visible to the naked eye,” astronomers have stated regarding the limitations of observing the comet.

Context

Previous observations of comets have shown that many are too small and distant to be visible, reinforcing the challenges faced by astronomers in studying such celestial bodies. The 3I/Atlas comet's path emphasizes the scale of the universe and the limitations of current observational technologies.

What’s next

As the comet approaches its closest point to Earth in December, astronomers will continue to monitor its trajectory, though visibility remains unlikely.
