Has a telescope in Chile captured evidence that 3I/ATLAS is a comet?

A telescope in Chile has likely captured evidence that 3I/ATLAS is a comet. The discovery was made by the Gemini South telescope at Cerro Pachón, with new images showing the interstellar object developing a visible tail and a dense coma. This finding may resolve ongoing debates among scientists regarding the nature of 3I/ATLAS since its discovery.

The significance of this discovery lies in the potential to clarify the classification of 3I/ATLAS, which has sparked considerable debate in the scientific community. The Gemini Multi-Object Spectrograph (GMOS) has provided clear evidence that supports the view of most scientists who believe 3I/ATLAS is indeed a comet, despite previous claims from some, including Harvard scientist Avi Loeb, who have questioned its classification.

Key facts

  • Discovery made by Gemini South at Cerro Pachón, Chile.
  • New images captured on August 27, 2025.
  • 3I/ATLAS is moving at a speed of 60,000 km/second.
  • Will fly by Mars on October 3, 2025.
  • Closest approach to the Sun (perihelion) on October 29, 2025.
  • Nearest approach to Earth on December 19, 2025, at a distance of 269 million km.

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was discovered on July 1, 2025. Since its discovery, scientists have debated its classification due to the absence of a clear tail. On August 27, 2025, the Gemini South telescope captured images revealing a cometary tail and a broad coma, indicating that 3I/ATLAS is likely a comet.

During a public outreach initiative organized by NSF NOIRLab, students from Hawai‘i and La Serena, Chile, observed 3I/ATLAS in real time. The session aimed to photograph the interstellar object and gather data on its spectrum, which can provide insights into its composition.

Official statements

Avi Loeb claimed that since there is no clear tail, 3I/ATLAS cannot be classified as a comet. However, the scientific community does not agree with Loeb. Almost all of them believe that 3I/ATLAS is a comet.

Context

Prior to this discovery, 3I/ATLAS had generated significant interest due to its unusual trajectory and behaviour. Some scientists speculated it might represent a new type of cosmic object. The recent images from GMOS may provide critical data that could enhance understanding of such interstellar visitors.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to study 3I/ATLAS as it approaches Earth, gathering more data to confirm its classification and explore its composition. The upcoming flyby of Mars and perihelion will be key opportunities for further observations.
