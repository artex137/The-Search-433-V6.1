Interstellar visitor 3I/Atlas travels at 57 km/s on a trajectory that defies the Sun’s gravity

The interstellar comet 3I/Atlas has been observed travelling at a speed of 57 kilometres per second on a trajectory that defies the gravitational pull of the Sun. The comet is currently moving through the solar system, with its path monitored by astronomers worldwide.

This discovery is significant as it provides insights into the characteristics of interstellar objects and their interactions with solar gravity. Scientists and space agencies have intensified their observations to gather data on 3I/Atlas's composition and trajectory, which can enhance our understanding of similar celestial bodies.

Key facts

  • Object: Comet 3I/Atlas
  • Speed: 57 km/s
  • Current location: Solar system
  • Trajectory: Defies Sun’s gravity
  • Monitoring agencies: Various international space agencies

Timeline

3I/Atlas was first detected as it entered the solar system. Since then, astronomers have tracked its speed and trajectory, noting its unusual path that does not conform to typical comet behaviour influenced by the Sun's gravity.

Data collection efforts have increased since the comet's discovery, with various space agencies collaborating to study its characteristics and potential origins.

Official statements

“The trajectory of 3I/Atlas challenges our understanding of gravitational influences on interstellar objects,” said an astronomer involved in the monitoring efforts.

Context

Previous interstellar visitors, such as 'Oumuamua, have prompted significant interest in studying celestial objects outside our solar system. These observations can shed light on the formation and evolution of planetary systems.

What’s next

Scientists will continue to monitor 3I/Atlas as it travels through the solar system, focusing on gathering data that could inform future studies of interstellar objects.
