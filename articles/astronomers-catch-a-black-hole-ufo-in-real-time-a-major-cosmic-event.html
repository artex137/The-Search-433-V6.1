Astronomers catch a black hole “UFO” in real time – a major cosmic event

Astronomers have observed an ultrafast outflow (UFO) forming immediately after a black hole for the first time in history. The event, which occurred in July 2024, took place 130 million light-years away and revealed the outflow reached 19% of the speed of light. No injuries or fatalities were reported.

This observation marks a significant advancement in understanding black hole dynamics. Scientists used the European Space Agency’s (ESA) XMM-Newton and Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency’s (JAXA) XRISM telescopes to capture the event. The ultrafast wind helps regulate star formation and connects black hole activity with galaxy evolution, according to researchers.

Key facts

  • Event occurred in July 2024.
  • Distance from Earth: 130 million light-years.
  • Outflow velocity: 19% of the speed of light.
  • Instruments used: ESA’s XMM-Newton and JAXA’s XRISM telescopes.
  • Ultrafast wind appeared 12 hours after a solar-flare-like magnetic reconnection.

Timeline

The significant event unfolded as follows:

  • July 2024: A solar-flare-like magnetic reconnection near the black hole triggers the formation of an ultrafast outflow.
  • 12 hours later: The ultrafast wind is observed, marking a rapid response on a cosmic timescale.

Official statements

“We’ve never watched a black hole create winds this quickly before,” says Liyi Gu from the SRON – Space Research Organisation Netherlands.
“It’s astonishing how familiar these processes look across radically different cosmic scales,” explains ESA astronomer Erik Kuulkers.
“Catching an event like this in real time is like seeing lightning and thunder together for the first time,” adds Gu.

Context

This event highlights the ongoing research into black holes and their role in galaxy evolution. The NGC 3783 galaxy, which provided a direct, face-on view of the black hole, has a well-mapped structure that aids in anomaly detection. This galaxy serves as a training ground for understanding how black holes feed and erupt.

What’s next

Researchers plan to analyze the data from this event over the coming months. This observation will serve as a benchmark dataset for Active Galactic Nucleus (AGN) simulations in the 2030s.
