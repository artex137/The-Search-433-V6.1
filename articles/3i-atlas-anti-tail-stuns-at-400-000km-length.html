3I/ATLAS Anti-Tail Stuns At 400,000km Length

Scientists have discovered that the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS has an anti-tail measuring over 400,000 kilometres that points toward the Sun. This finding was made in December 2025 by astronomers Toni Scarmato and Teerasak Thaluang, who captured images revealing the structure of the anti-tail using gradient filters. No injuries or fatalities have been reported.

This discovery is significant as it challenges existing theories about cometary behavior. The characteristics of 3I/ATLAS’s anti-tail raise questions about the nature of interstellar objects, prompting scientists to investigate the particles behind the glow. Authorities are now focused on understanding the unusual dynamics of this object and its implications for our knowledge of cometary physics.

Key facts

  • Object: 3I/ATLAS
  • Anti-tail length: Over 400,000 kilometres
  • Observation date: December 2025
  • Astronomers: Toni Scarmato, Teerasak Thaluang
  • Mass loss rate: 150 kilograms per second (increased to 500 kilograms per second before perihelion)

Timeline

In December 2025, astronomers Toni Scarmato and Teerasak Thaluang captured images of 3I/ATLAS's anti-tail using gradient filters. These images revealed a unique structure of the tail, which differs from typical cometary jets.

Following these observations, scientists calculated that the particles contributing to the anti-tail must be larger than those typically found in solar-system comets, raising questions about the nature of 3I/ATLAS.

Official statements

Avi Loeb noted, "Only direct spectroscopy of jet velocities will provide indisputable answers."

Context

Historically, cometary jets are dominated by sub-micron dust particles that scatter sunlight, creating a visible halo. However, 3I/ATLAS's anti-tail exhibits characteristics that challenge this understanding, as larger particles dominate its glow. This anomaly suggests that interstellar comets may not adhere to existing models based on solar system examples.

What’s next

Scientists plan to use the James Webb Space Telescope to measure the Doppler shifts of the jets in 3I/ATLAS. This information could refine estimates of particle size and velocity, helping to determine whether 3I/ATLAS is an unusual comet or something entirely different.
