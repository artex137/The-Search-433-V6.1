Is the Nucleus of 3I/ATLAS 15 Kilometers in Diameter?

New data from the Zwicky Transient Facility has identified the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS, which was first observed on May 15, 2025, at a distance of 6.1 astronomical units (AU) from the Sun. The findings indicate significant mass loss from the comet, leading to a glow observed in Hubble Space Telescope images taken on July 21, 2025.

This discovery matters because it raises questions about the size and mass of the nucleus of 3I/ATLAS, which could be approximately 15 kilometers in diameter, significantly larger than previous estimates for similar interstellar objects. Scientists, including those involved in the study, are now analyzing the implications of these findings for our understanding of interstellar material.

Key facts

  • Object: 3I/ATLAS
  • First identified: May 15, 2025
  • Distance from Sun at observation: 6.1 AU
  • Hubble observation date: July 21, 2025
  • Estimated nucleus diameter: 15 kilometers (proposed)
  • Comparison object: 2I/Borisov
  • Next close approach: October 3, 2025, at 29 million kilometers from Mars

Timeline

On May 15, 2025, the Zwicky Transient Facility first observed 3I/ATLAS at a distance of 6.1 AU from the Sun. By early June 2025, data indicated a plateau in the object's effective size, suggesting stability before significant mass loss began. On July 21, 2025, images captured by the Hubble Space Telescope revealed a glow around 3I/ATLAS, attributed to ice fragments evaporating from the nucleus.

Upcoming observations are scheduled for October 3, 2025, when 3I/ATLAS will pass close to Mars, allowing additional imaging from the HiRISE camera on the Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter.

Official statements

“The simplest interpretation of this early plateau is that it represents the bare nucleus, before substantial mass loss was initiated.” - Avi Loeb
“The high-resolution image from the HiRISE camera will serve as a detective’s magnifying glass in constraining the nucleus diameter of 3I/ATLAS.” - Avi Loeb

Context

3I/ATLAS is the second interstellar object detected after 2I/Borisov, which had a significantly smaller estimated nucleus diameter. The potential size difference between these two objects raises questions about the distribution of interstellar material and the frequency of such large objects entering the solar system.

What’s next

Scientists will analyze the upcoming data from the HiRISE camera to better determine the size and mass of 3I/ATLAS. This analysis may clarify the nature of interstellar objects and the processes affecting their trajectories and compositions.
