Time-Domain Photometry and Activity Evolution of Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS with BHTOM

Researchers have conducted a detailed study of the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, focusing on its pre-perihelion photometric behavior and dust activity from July 4 to September 11, 2025. The study utilized the Black Hole Target and Observation Manager (BHTOM) platform and involved 16 telescopes capturing 1,554 images during this period.

This research is significant as it provides insights into the characteristics and evolution of rare interstellar objects, which have limited observing opportunities. The findings will enhance understanding of cometary behavior and contribute to future observations of similar celestial phenomena.

Key facts

  • Study period: July 4 – September 11, 2025
  • Number of telescopes used: 16
  • Total images captured: 1,554
  • Rotation period of 3I/ATLAS: 15.98 ± 0.08 hours
  • Dust production rate increased from ≤ 217 kg/s to ≤ 328 kg/s
  • Activity index measured at n = -1.24 ± 0.02
  • Pre-perihelion light curve range: Rh = 3.18 – 2.19 astronomical units (au)

Timeline

From July 4 to September 11, 2025, researchers conducted time-domain photometric monitoring of 3I/ATLAS. They processed and calibrated the data using the BHTOM pipeline. The study measured the rotation period and color evolution of the comet, as well as quantifying its dust activity through Afp measurements.

Throughout the observation period, the comet exhibited a steady increase in brightness, approximately 3 magnitudes, with no signs of anomalous behavior. The researchers noted a consistent dust coma, with the activity index indicating substantial dust production.

Official statements

“The study has provided a comprehensive view of the comet's activity, which is essential for understanding its evolution,” said A. Fraser Gillan, one of the researchers involved in the project.

Context

Interstellar comets like 3I/ATLAS are rare, making studies like this crucial for advancing knowledge in astrophysics. Previous observations of similar comets have offered limited data, highlighting the importance of coordinated efforts using advanced technology such as BHTOM.

What’s next

The research team plans to submit their findings to the journal Astronomy & Astrophysics. Future studies may focus on further monitoring of 3I/ATLAS as it approaches perihelion and any subsequent interstellar objects that may be discovered.
