The European Space Agency (ESA) has achieved a new scientific feat by observing comet 3I/ATLAS

The European Space Agency (ESA) has detected X-ray emissions from the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS during a nearly 20-hour observation on 3 December 2025. The comet was approximately 282–285 million kilometres from the XMM-Newton X-ray space observatory at the time of detection.

This observation is significant as it marks the first time X-ray emissions have been detected from an interstellar comet, providing valuable insights into materials formed around other stars. The ESA plans to continue monitoring the comet to gather more data on its composition and behaviour.

Key facts

  • Date of observation: 3 December 2025
  • Distance from XMM-Newton: 282–285 million kilometres
  • Discovery date of 3I/ATLAS: 1 July 2025
  • Other observatories involved: Hubble Space Telescope
  • Type of emissions detected: Low-energy X-ray brightness
  • Comet's trajectory: Hyperbolic, confirming its interstellar origin

Timeline

On 1 July 2025, the ATLAS telescope in Chile discovered comet 3I/ATLAS. Following its discovery, the comet's hyperbolic trajectory confirmed its external origin and high speed relative to the Sun. On 3 December 2025, the XMM-Newton observatory conducted a detailed observation of the comet, detecting X-ray emissions resulting from interactions between the comet's gases and the solar wind.

Official statements

“Detecting X-ray emissions from an interstellar object like 3I/ATLAS provides rare insights into the materials formed around other stars,” said an ESA spokesperson.

Context

3I/ATLAS is only the third confirmed interstellar object to enter the solar system, following ʻOumuamua and 2I/Borisov. The combination of X-ray, visible, and ultraviolet data allows scientists to create a more complete understanding of this unique cosmic traveler.

What’s next

ESA plans to continue monitoring 3I/ATLAS to gather additional data on its composition and activity as it travels through the solar system.
