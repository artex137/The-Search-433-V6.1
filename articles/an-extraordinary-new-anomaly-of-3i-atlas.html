An Extraordinary New Anomaly of 3I/ATLAS

On November 22, 2025, astronomers have reported a significant anomaly regarding the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS, which is expected to make its closest approach to Jupiter on March 16, 2026. The details of this anomaly indicate a precise alignment with Jupiter's gravitational influence, raising questions about the object's origins and trajectory.

This discovery is important as it may suggest that 3I/ATLAS has been influenced by forces beyond simple gravitational interactions. Researchers are now analyzing the data to determine if the object could be of technological origin, potentially designed to interact with Jupiter. NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL) has been actively involved in tracking the object's path and measuring its non-gravitational acceleration.

Key facts

  • Closest approach to Jupiter: March 16, 2026
  • Distance from Jupiter at closest approach: 53.445 million kilometers (± 0.06 million kilometers)
  • Jupiter-Sun separation on March 16, 2026: 783.8 million kilometers
  • Non-gravitational acceleration measured: 5 x 10-7 astronomical units per day squared
  • Data collected from: Approximately 230 observatories

Timeline

3I/ATLAS has been observed since its discovery, but significant analysis has occurred during its perihelion passage. The following key events have transpired:

  • November 22, 2025: Anomaly reported regarding trajectory and gravitational influence.
  • March 16, 2026: Expected closest approach to Jupiter.

Official statements

“The near coincidence of the minimum distance and Jupiter’s Hill radius suggests a finely tuned trajectory, potentially indicating technological origins,” said Avi Loeb, an astrophysicist involved in the research.

Context

3I/ATLAS is the second recognized interstellar object, following 'Oumuamua, which sparked discussions about extraterrestrial technology. The recent findings may continue to shift our understanding of such objects and their potential purposes.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS as it approaches Jupiter, focusing on its trajectory and any changes in its acceleration. Further analysis is expected to clarify the object's nature and origins.
