Comet 3I/ATLAS Interstellar Discovery: Could It Be 14 Billion Years Old?

Scientists have discovered Comet 3I/ATLAS, an interstellar object spotted on July 1, 2025, using the Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System (ATLAS) telescope in Chile. This comet, which came from beyond our solar system, has sparked interest due to its potential age of up to 14 billion years, possibly making it older than the Sun.

The significance of this discovery lies in its potential to provide insights into the early universe and the formation of matter. Authorities are currently studying the comet's composition and trajectory to better understand its origins and implications for astronomy.

Key facts

  • Discovery Date: July 1, 2025
  • Location: Chile
  • Speed: Approximately 58 km/s relative to the Sun
  • Estimated Age: Between 7 to 14 billion years (theoretical)
  • Size: Nucleus may be 0.32–5.6 km wide
  • Composition: High levels of carbon dioxide, carbon monoxide, water vapor, and metals
  • Previous Interstellar Visitors: 1I/ʻOumuamua (2017) and 2I/Borisov (2019)

Timeline

On July 1, 2025, Comet 3I/ATLAS was first observed by the ATLAS telescope in Chile. The comet's hyperbolic orbit indicates it originated from interstellar space. Researchers have since been analyzing its composition and potential age, proposing that it could be as old as 14 billion years. The comet's unique characteristics, including its unusual gas emissions, have added to the excitement surrounding this discovery.

Official statements

“Comet 3I/ATLAS may be a rare cosmic fossil, offering clues about matter formation long before Earth existed,” said a leading astronomer involved in the study.

Context

Comet 3I/ATLAS is the third known interstellar object, following 1I/ʻOumuamua and 2I/Borisov. The discovery of such objects is significant for understanding the formation of star systems and the early universe. The potential age of 3I/ATLAS challenges existing knowledge about the timeline of cosmic events.

What’s next

Astronomers will continue to track Comet 3I/ATLAS with powerful telescopes as it moves away from Earth. Ongoing studies aim to uncover more about its composition and origins, contributing to our understanding of the universe.
