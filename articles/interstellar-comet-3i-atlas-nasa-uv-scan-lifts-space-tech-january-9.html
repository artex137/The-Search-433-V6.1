Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS: NASA UV Scan Lifts Space Tech — January 9

Published 2026-01-09 04:47 UTC

NASA Scans Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS with Europa Clipper

NASA has conducted a successful ultraviolet scan of comet 3I/ATLAS using its Europa Clipper spacecraft on January 9, 2023. The scan has provided new insights into the comet's gases and dust, but no injuries or fatalities have been reported.

The scan is significant as it helps validate the instruments that will be used in the upcoming Jupiter mission. Authorities and space agencies are optimistic about the findings, which may enhance the understanding of interstellar objects and improve the reliability of technology used in deep-space missions.

Key facts

  • Date: January 9, 2023
  • Mission: Europa Clipper
  • Object scanned: Comet 3I/ATLAS
  • Data type: Ultraviolet signatures
  • Purpose: Validate instruments for the Jupiter mission
  • Reported by: Specialist outlets, including Sky at Night Magazine

Timeline

NASA's Europa Clipper has successfully captured ultraviolet data from comet 3I/ATLAS. This early science serves as a critical validation step for the spacecraft's instruments ahead of the planned Jupiter mission.

As the data is processed, it reveals how the comet's gases and dust react to solar radiation. This information will help scientists refine existing models of the comet's composition and activity.

Official statements

“The scan shows how gases and dust respond to solar radiation, which helps scientists refine models of composition and activity,” a NASA representative stated.

Context

Comet 3I/ATLAS is an interstellar object passing through the Solar System. The successful scan underscores the ongoing interest in ultraviolet spectrograph capabilities and the importance of cross-mission testing. Prior to this, similar missions have highlighted the need for precision in optics and detectors for deep-space observations.

What’s next

NASA plans to continue processing the data collected from the scan. Future observations and analyses will help clarify some puzzling features detected during the initial scan and guide subsequent tests related to the Jupiter mission.
