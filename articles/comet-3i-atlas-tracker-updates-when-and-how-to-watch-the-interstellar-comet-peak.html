Comet 3I/ATLAS Tracker Updates: When and How to Watch the Interstellar Comet Peak

Comet 3I/ATLAS, a rare interstellar comet, is set to reach its closest point to the Sun at approximately 11:47 Universal Time (UT) on October 29, 2025. This event marks only the third time an interstellar comet has entered our Solar System, following 1I/ʻOumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019. There have been no reported injuries or fatalities related to this astronomical event.

This comet's perihelion will occur just inside the orbit of Mars, at a distance of about 1.4 astronomical units (210 million kilometers) from the Sun. Astronomers anticipate a peak visibility period for Earth-based observers between November and early December 2025, when the comet will be in a more favorable position for viewing.

Key facts

  • Comet Name: 3I/ATLAS
  • Perihelion: October 29, 2025, at 11:47 UT
  • Distance from Sun: 1.4 astronomical units (210 million kilometers)
  • Expected Visibility: Late November to early December 2025
  • Closest Approach to Earth: About 167 million miles (270 million kilometers)

Timeline

3I/ATLAS has been on a trajectory toward the Sun, with its perihelion occurring on October 29, 2025. The comet will not be visible from Earth until it reappears from behind the Sun in late November 2025. Astronomers expect it to be best observed shortly after sunset in early December, when it will appear as a faint dot in the night sky.

Official statements

“The best opportunity to see 3I/ATLAS will be shortly after sunset in early December, when it will appear as a faint, fuzzy white dot in the night sky,” said an astronomer from NASA.

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third interstellar comet recorded in our Solar System. The previous comets, 1I/ʻOumuamua and 2I/Borisov, have contributed to our understanding of celestial bodies from outside our solar system. The arrival of such comets provides valuable data for astronomers and enhances our knowledge of cosmic phenomena.

What’s next

As the comet approaches its perihelion, NASA and the European Space Agency (ESA) will continue to provide updates through digital trackers. The best viewing opportunities will occur in early December 2025, when astronomers will attempt to observe the comet with telescopes.
