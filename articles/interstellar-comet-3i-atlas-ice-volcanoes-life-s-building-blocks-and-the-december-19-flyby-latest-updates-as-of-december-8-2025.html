Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS: Ice Volcanoes, Life’s Building Blocks and the December 19 Flyby — Latest Updates as of December 8, 2025

As of December 8, 2025, interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS has become a significant focus for scientists and the public alike as it approaches Earth for a flyby on December 19. The comet has exhibited cryovolcano-like eruptions and an atmosphere rich in prebiotic molecules, according to NASA and ESA reports. There are no reported injuries or fatalities related to the comet.

This discovery matters because 3I/ATLAS provides a unique opportunity to study materials that may contribute to the formation of planets and life. Observations from various space agencies have revealed critical details about the comet's composition and behavior, which could enhance our understanding of similar celestial bodies.

Key facts

  • Origin: 3I/ATLAS comes from interstellar space and has a hyperbolic orbit.
  • Discovery: First spotted on July 1, 2025, by the ATLAS survey telescope in Río Hurtado, Chile.
  • Size: The comet's nucleus is estimated to be between 0.3 and 5.6 km across, likely under 1 km.
  • Speed: It travels at approximately 130,000 mph (210,000 km/h).
  • Closest approach: Will not come closer than about 1.8 AU (270 million km) to Earth.
  • Composition: Contains high levels of methanol and hydrogen cyanide, which are important for prebiotic chemistry.
  • Upcoming observations: Detailed data from the JUICE spacecraft will arrive in early 2026.

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first detected on July 1, 2025, by astronomers in Chile. Since then, it has garnered attention for its unusual activity and composition. The comet reached perihelion, its closest point to the Sun, on October 29, 2025. Observations have revealed significant outgassing of carbon-based chemicals, including methanol and hydrogen cyanide, which are crucial for understanding the origins of life.

As of December 3, NASA's Psyche mission provided additional insights by capturing images of the comet, contributing to a comprehensive tracking network across the Solar System.

Official statements

“3I/ATLAS is a chemically exotic object, yet it contains many of the same kinds of organic molecules we see in Solar-System comets,” said a NASA scientist, reinforcing the comet's significance for research in prebiotic chemistry.

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third confirmed interstellar object observed in our Solar System, following 1I/ʻOumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019. Its discovery has sparked interest in the study of interstellar objects and their potential contributions to our understanding of planetary formation and the ingredients necessary for life.

What’s next

Scientists will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS as it approaches Earth, with detailed observations expected from the JUICE spacecraft in early 2026. Ongoing studies will focus on its chemical composition and potential cryovolcanic activity.
