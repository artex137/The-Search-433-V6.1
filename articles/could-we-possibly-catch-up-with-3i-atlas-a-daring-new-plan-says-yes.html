Could we possibly catch up with 3I/ATLAS? A daring new plan says 'yes'

The interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS is currently speeding away from Earth, prompting researchers to propose an ambitious plan to catch up with it. The plan, outlined in a study published on arXiv, aims to launch a spacecraft toward the comet in 2035, with the spacecraft expected to fly by the comet in 2085.

This mission is significant as it represents humanity's ongoing quest to explore interstellar objects. Researchers believe that achieving a close encounter with 3I/ATLAS could provide valuable insights into the composition and behavior of such celestial bodies. The study highlights the innovative use of the Solar Oberth Maneuver (SOM), which relies on gravitational forces to accelerate spacecraft.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS is expected to be 68 billion miles from the Sun in 2085.
  • The spacecraft will weigh approximately 500 kilograms.
  • The launch is planned for 2035, with a flyby scheduled for 2085.
  • The mission aims to achieve a delta-V of at least 5.1 miles per second.
  • The proposed spacecraft will require advanced heat shielding to withstand high temperatures near the Sun.

Timeline

In 2035, researchers plan to launch the spacecraft toward 3I/ATLAS. The spacecraft will utilize the Solar Oberth Maneuver to gain speed as it approaches the Sun. By 2085, the spacecraft is expected to conduct a flyby of the comet, which will be located at a significant distance from the Sun.

Official statements

“Pretty much every launch uses the Oberth effect,” said T. Marshall Eubanks, a former NASA scientist and chief scientist at Space Initiatives Inc. “However, I cannot find a record of a straight-out Oberth maneuver of the type we propose, which is a major rocket burn at closest approach in a flyby.”

Context

The study highlights the ongoing interest in interstellar objects, especially as more are discovered. As technology advances, the feasibility of missions to these distant bodies improves, making what was once thought impossible increasingly achievable.

What’s next

The researchers will continue to refine their plans and technology as they prepare for the proposed launch in 2035. Further studies will focus on heat shielding and mission viability as the date approaches.
