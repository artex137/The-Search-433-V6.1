A Rare Interstellar Object Zooming Through Our Solar System Could Be Fundamental To Planets Of The Future

Astronomers have been tracking the interstellar object known as 3I/ATLAS, which passed by Mars on October 3, 2025. This carbon-dioxide heavy comet is only the third such object to enter our solar system and is significantly larger than other identified interstellar objects.

This discovery matters because researchers in Germany have suggested that 3I/ATLAS could play a vital role in the formation of future planets. Professor Susanne Pfalzner of Forschungszentrum Jülich explained that interstellar objects like 3I/ATLAS could provide essential building blocks for new planets, particularly around higher-mass stars.

Key facts

  • Object: 3I/ATLAS
  • Type: Interstellar comet
  • Passage by Mars: October 3, 2025
  • Expected departure from solar system: March 2026
  • Researcher: Professor Susanne Pfalzner, Forschungszentrum Jülich

Timeline

3I/ATLAS has been monitored by astronomers for several months. It passed by Mars on October 3, 2025, and will continue its trajectory past Venus and Jupiter before leaving the solar system in March 2026.

Official statements

“Interstellar objects may be able to jump start planet formation, in particular around higher-mass stars,” said Professor Susanne Pfalzner. “Higher-mass stars are more efficient in capturing interstellar objects in their discs. Therefore, interstellar object-seeded planet formation should be more efficient around these stars, providing a fast way to form giant planets.”

Context

The study of interstellar objects is an emerging field in astronomy. 3I/ATLAS is the third known interstellar object to pass through our solar system. The accretion theory, which describes how planets form, has been a longstanding concept in the study of planetary formation.

What’s next

As 3I/ATLAS continues its journey, astronomers will observe its path and study its composition, further understanding its potential role in future planetary formation.
