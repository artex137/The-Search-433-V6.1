Interstellar object 3I/ATLAS continues to display a Sun-facing anti-tail

The interstellar object 3I/ATLAS is currently exhibiting a Sun-facing anti-tail as it travels through the inner Solar System. Observations have confirmed this feature is physical, not a temporary perspective effect. The latest images were captured by Teerasak Thaluang on December 13, 2025, using a 0.26-meter telescope in Rayong, Thailand.

This phenomenon is significant for astronomers studying the characteristics of interstellar objects. Both Earth-based and space-based instruments are actively monitoring 3I/ATLAS to track any changes in its position, brightness, or tail pattern as it approaches its closest point to Earth.

Key facts

  • 3I/ATLAS is approximately 270.5 million kilometers from Earth as of December 14, 2025.
  • It will reach its closest distance, or perigee, on December 19, 2025, at 269.9097 million kilometers.
  • Observations from the Hubble Space Telescope on July 21 and November 30, 2025, confirmed the anti-tail at different distances of 2.98 and 1.91 astronomical units, respectively.
  • Gemini Multi-Object Spectrograph imaged 3I/ATLAS on November 26, 2025, revealing a glowing halo likely caused by diatomic carbon molecules.
  • ESA’s X-ray space observatory XMM-Newton observed the object for 20 hours on December 3, 2025.

Timeline

On July 21, 2025, the anti-tail of 3I/ATLAS was first identified in Hubble Space Telescope images taken when the object was 2.98 astronomical units from Earth.

On November 30, 2025, Hubble captured further images of the anti-tail at a distance of 1.91 astronomical units.

On November 26, 2025, images from the Gemini Multi-Object Spectrograph revealed the anti-tail and a glowing halo around the object.

Official statements

“The anti-tail is a consistent feature of 3I/ATLAS, confirmed across multiple observation platforms,” said Harvard Professor Avi Loeb, who has studied the object.
“These observations are significant for understanding the behavior of interstellar objects as they interact with solar radiation,” Loeb added.

Context

The anti-tail phenomenon has been studied in the context of sunlight scattering by ice fragments shed from 3I/ATLAS. Previous studies have proposed that these particles evaporate before being pushed away by solar radiation, preventing a conventional cometary tail.

This is the first confirmed occurrence of an anti-tail observed in an interstellar object, marking a significant finding for astronomers.

What’s next

Continuous monitoring of 3I/ATLAS is ongoing to gather more data about its structure, trajectory, and material composition. Updates will be provided as new information becomes available.
