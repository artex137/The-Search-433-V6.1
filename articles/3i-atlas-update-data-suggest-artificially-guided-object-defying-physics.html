3I/ATLAS Update: Data Suggest 'Artificially Guided' Object Defying Physics

A massive object designated 3I/ATLAS is travelling through the solar system and exhibiting unusual behaviour. Discovered by the Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System telescope, this interstellar comet has shown signs of non-natural acceleration and a striking blue colour as it approaches the Sun. It is expected to make its closest pass to Earth on December 19, 2023.

This discovery has raised significant interest among astronomers and scientists. Authorities are currently analysing data collected from various space probes to understand the object's true nature and the implications of its unexpected characteristics. The findings could challenge existing theories about interstellar objects and their behaviours.

Key facts

  • Object: 3I/ATLAS, third confirmed interstellar object.
  • Discovered by: Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System (ATLAS).
  • Closest approach to Earth: December 19, 2023, at roughly 267 million kilometres.
  • Notable features: Non-gravitational acceleration, 'startlingly blue' colour.
  • NASA's position: Claims it is a comet, dismissing alien theories.
  • Key figure: Avi Loeb, Harvard astrophysicist, proposes alternative explanations.
  • Data source: Measurements from US Naval Research Lab and sun-orbiting crafts.

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was discovered by ATLAS, which tracks potential Earth-impacting asteroids, and has since become a focal point of scientific debate. As it neared the Sun, NASA reported unusual acceleration and a change in colour.

On October 1, 2023, the object passed close to Mars, but NASA has not released images from the Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter due to a federal budget impasse. This has led to speculation about the nature of 3I/ATLAS.

As scientists await its December 19 pass, they hope to gather more data to understand its characteristics better.

Official statements

“NASA's observations show that this is the third interstellar comet to pass through our solar system. No aliens. No threat to life here on Earth.” — Sean Duffy, NASA Acting Administrator.
“If we do not observe a massive cloud of gas around 3I/ATLAS in December, it could be an indicator of a propulsion system.” — Avi Loeb, Harvard astrophysicist.

Context

3I/ATLAS follows the earlier discoveries of 1I/'Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov, which have also sparked discussions about the nature of interstellar objects. The scientific community has been divided on the implications of these findings, particularly regarding the possibility of artificial technology.

The discrepancies in 3I/ATLAS's observed colour and behaviour compared to typical comets have further fueled speculation about its origin and nature.

What’s next

Researchers will closely monitor 3I/ATLAS during its December 19 pass to gather crucial data. The findings may either confirm its classification as a comet or suggest a more extraordinary explanation involving technology.
