Interstellar comet 3I/Atlas is rich in alcohol

Astronomers have detected an unusually high amount of methanol in the interstellar comet 3I/Atlas, significantly more than hydrogen cyanide, as it approached the Sun in late 2025. No injuries or fatalities have been reported in connection with this discovery.

This finding is important as it provides insights into the chemistry of other planetary systems. The team observed the comet on multiple occasions and analyzed the gas and dust released in a halo, called a coma, around the nucleus, revealing the conditions under which 3I/Atlas was formed.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/Atlas has a methanol to hydrogen cyanide ratio of about 70 and 120.
  • The comet is among the most methanol-rich comets ever studied.
  • Observations were made using the James Webb Space Telescope.
  • 3I/Atlas is the third known interstellar object to visit the solar system.
  • The research has been published in the Astrophysical Journal Letters.

Timeline

In late 2025, astronomers observed 3I/Atlas as it approached the Sun. The proximity caused the comet to release gas and dust, forming a coma around its nucleus. The team collected data on the composition of the coma, focusing on the methanol and hydrogen cyanide present.

The researchers identified that hydrogen cyanide primarily originated from the nucleus, while methanol was released from both the nucleus and icy grains in the coma. This detailed tracing of outgassing processes marks a significant advance in the study of interstellar objects.

Official statements

“The composition of the planet is unlike any comet previously studied,” said an unnamed member of the research team.
“Studying these objects allows scientists to better understand distant planetary systems,” the researcher added.

Context

3I/Atlas is the third interstellar object to be documented in the solar system, following 1I/'Oumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019. Previous studies have shown similar outgassing behaviours in some Solar System comets, but this is the first instance where such processes have been traced in detail for an interstellar object.

What’s next

Further analysis is expected to continue as researchers delve deeper into the implications of the findings on 3I/Atlas and its formation conditions, contributing to the understanding of other planetary systems.
