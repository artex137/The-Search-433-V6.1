Harvard Astronomer Claims “Major Anomaly” As Interstellar Object 3I/ATLAS Measured Over 33 Billion Tons

Harvard scientists have reported that interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, detected on July 1, 2025, is significantly more massive than previously observed interstellar objects, weighing over 33 billion tons. This finding comes from a comprehensive analysis of data collected from 227 observatories worldwide.

The discovery of 3I/ATLAS has prompted researchers to reassess their understanding of interstellar objects. It challenges existing models of how these celestial bodies form and behave. Scientists are examining the implications of this anomaly, particularly regarding the distribution and characteristics of interstellar objects in our galaxy.

Key facts

  • Detected on July 1, 2025.
  • Estimated mass: over 33 billion tons.
  • Nucleus diameter: approximately 5 kilometers.
  • Mass loss rate: about 150 kilograms per second.
  • Analysis conducted by researchers Richard Cloete, Avi Loeb, and Peter Vereš from Harvard.
  • Observations used data from 227 global observatories.
  • Closest approach to the Sun obscured observations until December 2025.

Timeline

On July 1, 2025, astronomers detected interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, noting its high velocity. From May 15 to September 23, 2025, researchers analyzed observational data to determine the comet’s characteristics. The findings revealed significant mass and unusual behavior compared to other interstellar objects.

The James Webb Space Telescope documented the comet’s outgassing, raising questions about its mass and acceleration. Initial observations indicated minimal non-gravitational acceleration, despite the considerable outgassing. This discrepancy has led to ongoing investigations into the comet’s nature.

Official statements

Avi Loeb stated, “The true anomaly lies not in the comet’s size itself but in the statistical improbability of detecting such a massive object before observing numerous smaller ones.”
Tom Statler, lead scientist for Solar System small bodies at NASA, emphasized, “The object displays characteristic cometary behavior in virtually every observable aspect.”

Context

Prior to 3I/ATLAS, astronomers had detected only two interstellar visitors: 1I/’Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov. The discovery of 3I/ATLAS has raised questions about the population of interstellar objects and the processes that lead to their formation. The mass difference between 3I/ATLAS and its predecessors is significant, suggesting gaps in the current understanding of these celestial bodies.

What’s next

Astronomers expect to gather higher resolution imagery of 3I/ATLAS as it approaches optimal viewing positions. The HiRISE camera aboard the Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter is scheduled to observe the comet on October 3, 2025. Continued monitoring will help clarify the nature of this interstellar object and its implications for astronomical studies.
