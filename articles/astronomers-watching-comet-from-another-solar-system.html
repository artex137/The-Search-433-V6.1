Astronomers watching comet from another solar system

Michigan State University researchers have confirmed that comet 3I/Atlas, the third interstellar comet ever detected, is a natural comet and not a UFO, as some online rumors suggest. The comet is currently visible through telescopes during parts of November and December.

Researchers emphasize the importance of understanding 3I/Atlas, as it may provide insights into the composition of its home solar system. They have observed unusual characteristics of the comet, particularly regarding the ice in its nucleus, which likely differs from typical water ice found in most comets.

Key facts

  • Comet Name: 3I/Atlas
  • Location: Observed from Earth, visible in November and December
  • Lead Researcher: Professor Darryl Seligman, Michigan State University
  • Unique Feature: Ice in nucleus likely not water
  • Number of Interstellar Comets Detected: Three

Timeline

3I/Atlas was first spotted moving from beyond the region where water ice is active. Researchers began studying the comet shortly after its discovery, leading to the current analysis of its unique characteristics.

Professor Darryl Seligman and his team have conducted extensive observations, confirming that the comet displays classical evidence of cometary activity, including a tail.

Official statements

“Almost everybody in the field is in agreement that this object is a natural comet because it’s displaying classical evidence of cometary activity,” said Professor Darryl Seligman.
“There’s no controversy about it not being a comet because nobody thinks that except a very few people,” Seligman added.
“Everything about this points to it being a comet from somewhere else in the galaxy,” said Shannon Schmoll, director of the Abrams Planetarium.

Context

3I/Atlas is the third interstellar comet detected, following previous discoveries that have sparked interest in the study of celestial bodies originating from outside the solar system. Researchers continue to investigate the characteristics of these comets to understand their formation and the conditions of their home systems.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to observe 3I/Atlas as it approaches the sun, gathering data that could shed light on its composition and the environment from which it originated.
