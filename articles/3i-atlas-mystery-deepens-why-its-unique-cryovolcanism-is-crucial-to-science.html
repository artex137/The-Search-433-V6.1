3I/ATLAS Mystery Deepens: Why Its Unique Cryovolcanism Is Crucial To Science

Astronomers studying the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS have reported that its behaviour is becoming increasingly unusual, with indications of cryovolcanism observed as it travels through space. This discovery, announced by NASA Science, could significantly alter the scientific understanding of how small celestial bodies form in distant star systems.

The findings matter because they offer insights into the composition and activity of 3I/ATLAS, which is only the third confirmed interstellar object observed. Scientists are working urgently to decode its origin and the unusual volcanic activity it displays as the object races toward deep space on a one-way trajectory.

Key facts

  • 3I/ATLAS was first detected on 1 July 2025 by the ATLAS telescope in Chile.
  • It is travelling at approximately 246,000 kilometres per hour.
  • The object's size has been modelled between 440 metres and 5.6 kilometres.
  • Infrared data from the James Webb Space Telescope indicates a gas cloud dominated by carbon dioxide.
  • The CO₂-to-water ratio is roughly 8 to 1, one of the highest recorded in a comet-like body.
  • Researchers have only months left to gather data before 3I/ATLAS disappears back into interstellar space.

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was detected on 1 July 2025. Early modelling based on Hubble Space Telescope data estimated its size and confirmed its interstellar trajectory. In late October 2025, it made its closest approach to the Sun, travelling at high speed. Observations have since shown jets of gas and dust erupting from the object, leading to the hypothesis of cryovolcanism.

As scientists continue to analyze data, they are focusing on the unusual high metal content and the potential implications for traditional comet models.

Official statements

“3I/ATLAS may preserve chemical signatures from conditions very different from those that shaped the Solar System,” said a representative from NASA Science.
“Whether or not cryovolcanism is confirmed, the insights from 3I/ATLAS will help rewrite models of how small bodies evolve and how planets form,” stated an astronomer involved in the research.

Context

This discovery adds to the growing body of knowledge regarding interstellar objects. Previous studies have indicated that such objects could contain foundational materials from long-vanished planetary systems. The potential confirmation of cryovolcanism would further suggest that geological activity is not unique to our Solar System, as seen on icy moons like Europa and Enceladus.

What’s next

Scientists will continue to gather and analyze data from 3I/ATLAS in the coming months before it exits the Solar System. The urgency of this research highlights the object's potential to reshape current understandings of planetary formation and the evolution of small bodies in space.
