Never mind 3I/ATLAS, here’s a comet you can see for yourself. This is why it’s getting astronomers excited

Published 2025-09-26 06:56 UTC

Comet C/2025 A6 (LEMMON) Visible in Morning Sky

Comet C/2025 A6 (LEMMON) has been visible to northern observers in the morning sky since mid-August 2025, drawing attention from amateur astronomers and comet enthusiasts. Discovered on 3 January 2025 by astronomer David C. Fuls, the comet is currently drifting through the constellation Lynx, positioned between Jupiter and the stars of the Plough/Big Dipper.

This comet matters to astronomers as it may become visible to the naked eye by the end of October 2025. Authorities and observatories are closely monitoring its brightness and activity, as predictions suggest it could reach a magnitude of +3, making it accessible to a wider audience under optimal viewing conditions.

Key facts

  • Discovery Date: 3 January 2025
  • Discovered by: David C. Fuls
  • Current Brightness: Approximately 8th to 9th magnitude
  • Location: Constellation Lynx
  • Expected Peak Brightness: +2 magnitude or higher
  • Observation Period: Morning sky, northern hemisphere

Timeline

Comet C/2025 A6 (LEMMON) was first identified on 3 January 2025 during the Mount Lemmon Survey, a program operated by the University of Arizona's Steward Observatory. Initially, it was observed as a faint dot of light.

After follow-up observations indicated cometary activity, it received its official designation. The comet passed solar conjunction on 2 July 2025, temporarily disappearing from view. Upon re-emergence in mid-August, it was notably brighter than anticipated, prompting renewed interest from observers.

As of late September 2025, observers report that the comet has developed a tail and is visible through binoculars and small telescopes.

Official statements

“Comets love surprising the people who observe them, and proving them wrong if possible,” said an unidentified astronomer involved in the monitoring of C/2025 A6 (LEMMON).

Context

Comet C/2025 A6 (LEMMON) is part of a recent trend of comet discoveries that have excited both amateur and professional astronomers. Previous comets have shown unpredictable behavior, often exceeding expectations in brightness and visibility.

Historically, comets have fascinated observers and have been the subject of numerous studies regarding their composition and behavior in the solar system.

What’s next

Observers will continue to monitor Comet C/2025 A6 (LEMMON) as it approaches its potential peak visibility in late October 2025. Updates will be provided regularly to track its brightness and visibility status.
