3I/ATLAS Comet Viewing in US: When and Where to See the Rare Interstellar Visitor

A rare celestial event will occur this month as interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS travels through the solar system, becoming visible to skywatchers across the United States. The comet, discovered in July 2025, is approximately 7 billion years old and will pass closest to Earth on December 19, 2025, coming within 1.8 astronomical units (AU) of the planet.

This event is significant as it marks only the third confirmed interstellar object to visit our solar system, after ʻOumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019. Astronomers expect that this rare occurrence will provide valuable insights into the composition of celestial bodies from beyond our solar system.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS will be visible from the U.S. from early November to mid-December 2025.
  • Best viewing times are about an hour and a half before sunrise.
  • Brightness will range from magnitude 12 to 18, requiring a large telescope (10-inch aperture or more) for visibility.
  • Comet's closest approach to Earth is on December 19, 2025, at 1.8 AU (269 million km).
  • Visibility will vary by region due to light pollution and weather conditions.

Timeline

Comet 3I/ATLAS was discovered in July 2025. It reached its closest point to the Sun on October 30, 2025, at a distance of 1.4 AU. The comet will approach Earth on December 19, 2025, marking its passage through the solar system.

Official statements

“3I/ATLAS offers a glimpse into the building blocks of other planetary systems that formed billions of years ago,” said an astronomer involved in monitoring the comet's trajectory.

Context

Comet 3I/ATLAS is unique because it is an interstellar traveler, entering the solar system from deep space. Unlike typical comets that orbit the Sun, it will leave the solar system after its trajectory carries it beyond the Sun’s influence. This opportunity allows scientists to compare it with comets that originated within our solar system.

What’s next

As 3I/ATLAS approaches, NASA and global observatories will continue to monitor its composition and behavior, providing updates on viewing conditions and scientific findings.
