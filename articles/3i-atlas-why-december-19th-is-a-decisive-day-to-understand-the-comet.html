3I/ATLAS: December 19th is a Decisive Day to Understand the Comet

Astronomers from various global institutions are focusing on the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS today, December 19, 2025, as it reaches its closest point to Earth, approximately 270 million kilometers away. This event presents a rare opportunity to study the comet, which has shown unique characteristics since its discovery in July 2025.

The approach of 3I/ATLAS is significant for scientific research. Authorities have mobilized high-precision telescopes to monitor its trajectory and analyze its chemical and physical properties. The comet's unusual features, including a hyperbolic trajectory and high levels of carbon dioxide, suggest it originates from outside our solar system, providing insights into the formation of celestial bodies.

Key facts

  • Date: December 19, 2025
  • Distance from Earth: Approximately 270 million kilometers
  • Discovery: July 2025 using the Atlas telescope system in Rio Hurtado, Chile
  • Estimated age: Seven billion years
  • Trajectory: Hyperbolic, indicating interstellar origin
  • Monitoring agencies: NASA, ESA, and various observatories in Chile and Hawaii

Timeline

In July 2025, astronomers discovered 3I/ATLAS using the Atlas telescope system in Chile. Initial observations indicated that the comet's orbit did not follow the typical elliptical patterns of solar-system comets. By December 19, 2025, the comet has approached Earth, allowing for detailed studies of its coma and nucleus.

During this time, researchers have detected unprecedented radio signals emanating from the comet, marking the first observation of such emissions from an interstellar object. These findings have prompted further investigation into the comet's interaction with solar wind.

Official statements

“The detection of radio frequencies from 3I/ATLAS opens new avenues for understanding interstellar objects,” said David Jewitt from UCLA.
“This is a milestone for contemporary science, as it may provide insights into the conditions of distant exoplanetary systems,” added a representative from NASA.

Context

3I/ATLAS is only the third confirmed interstellar object to visit the solar system, following ‘Oumuamua and comet Borisov. Its unique characteristics, including high levels of carbon dioxide and a hyperbolic trajectory, set it apart from typical comets, which usually originate within our solar system.

This event highlights the advancements in astronomical observation and the global collaboration among scientists to study celestial phenomena.

What’s next

As 3I/ATLAS moves away from the Sun after today, researchers will continue to analyze the data collected. The results are expected to contribute to scientific publications in the coming year, offering new insights into the origins of interstellar objects.
