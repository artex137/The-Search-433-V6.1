3I/ATLAS: Exocomet Anti-Tail Larger Than Earth-Moon Distance

As the countdown to 3I/ATLAS’s closest approach to Earth has begun, Harvard astrophysicist Avi Loeb has reported that the exocomet features an anti-tail that extends unusually far from its nucleus. The event will occur on December 19, 2025, when 3I/ATLAS will be approximately 1.79 AU closer to Earth, before heading towards Jupiter.

This discovery matters because it challenges existing knowledge about comet behavior. The anti-tail, observed in images from December 14 and 15, 2025, extends to a distance of 500,000 kilometers, exceeding the average distance to the Moon, which is 384,400 kilometers. Researchers are now investigating the implications of this phenomenon.

Key facts

  • Exocomet: 3I/ATLAS
  • Closest approach to Earth: December 19, 2025
  • Distance from Earth at closest approach: 1.79 AU
  • Anti-tail length: 500,000 kilometers
  • Average distance to the Moon: 384,400 kilometers
  • Sunward speed of material in anti-tail: at least 130 meters per second

Timeline

On December 14 and 15, 2025, researchers captured images of 3I/ATLAS, revealing its prominent anti-tail. This observation has prompted further analysis into the nature of the anti-tail, particularly regarding its unprecedented length.

On December 19, 2025, 3I/ATLAS will make its closest approach to Earth, before proceeding on its path out of the solar system towards Jupiter.

Official statements

“To reach a scale of 500,000 kilometers over the past 45 days after perihelion, the sunward speed of the material in the anti-tail must be at least 130 meters per second relative to the nucleus of 3I/ATLAS,” Loeb stated in his latest blog on Medium.
“The alternative is a jet from a technological thruster,” Loeb added, suggesting a potential explanation for the unusual characteristics of the anti-tail.

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third known interstellar object to enter the solar system, following 1I/Oumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019. The discovery of its anti-tail adds to the growing body of knowledge surrounding interstellar objects and their behaviors as they interact with solar forces.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to study the anti-tail of 3I/ATLAS to understand its origins and behavior. Further observations will occur as the comet approaches Earth and subsequently heads towards Jupiter.
