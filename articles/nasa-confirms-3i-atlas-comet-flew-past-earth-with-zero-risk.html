NASA Confirms 3I/ATLAS Comet Flew Past Earth With Zero Risk

The interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS has safely passed Earth's orbit without any change in its velocity or direction, NASA confirmed on 30 October 2025. There have been no injuries or fatalities reported related to this event.

This confirmation addresses recent online speculation regarding the comet's trajectory. NASA and the European Space Agency (ESA) have stated that 3I/ATLAS poses no threat to Earth, Mars, or Venus as it continues its journey through the inner Solar System.

Key facts

  • Velocity: 68.3 km/s, unchanged since detection in July 2025
  • Closest approach: 268 million km from Earth on 19 December 2025
  • Trajectory: Hyperbolic, meaning it will leave the Solar System permanently
  • Minimum separation from Mars: early October 2025
  • Alignment with Venus: 3 November 2025 at a safe 97 million kilometres

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was detected in July 2025. Observations confirmed its stable trajectory and velocity at 68.3 km/s. Online speculation about its potential danger emerged following its perihelion, the closest point to the Sun. NASA and ESA released findings on 30 October 2025, confirming the comet's safe passage and steady path.

“No manoeuvring toward Earth; it continues on its expected trajectory,” NASA stated, reaffirming there is no cause for concern.

Context

Previous interstellar objects, such as ʻOumuamua and 2I/Borisov, have similarly followed hyperbolic trajectories, indicating they are not gravitationally bound to the Sun. Observing these objects provides valuable insights into the chemical composition of materials from ancient planetary systems.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS using the Hubble Space Telescope, ESA's JUICE mission, and ground-based observatories across Asia and Europe. The comet will fade from view early next year as it exits the Solar System, contributing valuable data for future scientific studies.
