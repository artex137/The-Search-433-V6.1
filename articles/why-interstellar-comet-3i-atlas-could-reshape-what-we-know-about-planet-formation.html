Why interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS could reshape what we know about planet formation

Published 2025-12-15 16:00 UTC

Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS Set for Close Approach to Earth in December 2025

Interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS is scheduled to make its closest approach to Earth on December 19, 2025, providing a unique opportunity for astronomers to study the comet in detail. This event will mark the third known interstellar object discovered passing through the solar system, according to Space.com.

The close proximity of 3I/ATLAS will allow both professional and amateur astronomers to observe the comet with small telescopes and binoculars. Observations during this period are critical, as they will help scientists learn about the comet's formation and physical structure. The comet is believed to have originated from a star system other than our own and has been traveling through the Milky Way for approximately one billion years.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS will be closest to Earth on December 19, 2025.
  • The comet is the third known interstellar object, following 1I/Oumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019.
  • Observations will help scientists compare star formation in other systems to that of the Solar System.
  • Space-based instruments, including the James Webb Space Telescope, have detected carbon dioxide in 3I/ATLAS.
  • Early monitoring has been ongoing since the comet was discovered.

Timeline

Comet 3I/ATLAS was discovered recently and has been observed by astronomers since its identification. As it approaches the Sun, it will be tracked closely to gather data on its surface ices and gases.

On December 19, 2025, the comet will reach its nearest point to Earth, allowing for enhanced observations. This will be the first time the comet gets close to a star in its lifetime, marking a significant moment for astronomers.

Official statements

“When 3I/ATLAS is closest to the Earth, all the features that we are looking for will be easier to detect with our telescopes,” said astronomer Darryl Seligman.

Context

Prior interstellar visitors, such as 1I/Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov, have provided valuable data, albeit limited due to their short visitation periods. These objects have opened a new avenue for understanding the formation of planetary systems beyond our own.

The composition of comets gives insights into their formation regions. Most comets in our solar system are rich in water ice, while 2I/Borisov showed high levels of carbon monoxide, suggesting differing formation locations.

What’s next

As 3I/ATLAS approaches, astronomers will conduct follow-up observations to gather more information about its origin, motion, and physical properties. The data collected will enhance understanding of comet formation across the galaxy.
