3I/ATLAS: Avi Loeb Decodes Exocomet’s Hubble Images Captured During Its Rare Full Moon Phase

Harvard astrophysicist Avi Loeb has analyzed six images of the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, captured by NASA’s Hubble Space Telescope during its rare “full moon phase” on January 22 or 23, 2026. The images reveal a brightness surge as the comet aligned with the Sun-Earth axis. No injuries or fatalities have been reported.

This analysis is significant as it provides new insights into the structure and behavior of 3I/ATLAS, the third interstellar object identified in the solar system. NASA’s Hubble captured the images during a time of heightened brightness, revealing complex features of the comet’s halo and jet system. The findings may have implications for understanding the nature of interstellar comets.

Key facts

  • Event Date: January 22 or 23, 2026
  • Object: 3I/ATLAS
  • Images Captured: Six by NASA’s Hubble Space Telescope
  • Brightness Surge: Observed during full moon phase
  • Jet System: Includes four jets, with one prominent anti-tail
  • Mini-jets: Three, separated by 120 degrees
  • Wobble Period: Every 7.2 hours by +/- 20 degrees

Timeline

3I/ATLAS entered a full moon phase on January 22 or 23, 2026, aligning with the Sun-Earth axis. During this time, NASA’s Hubble Space Telescope captured six images of the comet, revealing significant details about its structure.

Loeb and his collaborator, Toni Scarmato, processed the images using the Larson-Sekanina Rotational Gradient filter. The analysis showed a glowing halo around the comet, elongated in the direction of the Sun, and a system of jets, including an anti-tail and three mini-jets.

Official statements

“We still do not know the nature of the anti-tail that allows it to penetrate hundreds of thousands of kilometers through the Solar wind and radiation without being deflected away from the Sun,” said Loeb.

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third interstellar object discovered in our solar system, following 1I/Oumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019. Each of these objects has provided unique insights into the composition and dynamics of interstellar bodies.

The presence of jets and anti-tails in comets is not uncommon, but the features observed in 3I/ATLAS raise questions about their formation and behavior in the context of interstellar travel.

What’s next

Further research is planned to investigate the nature of the anti-tail and the mini-jets of 3I/ATLAS. Loeb has proposed several hypotheses regarding their composition, which may guide future studies.
