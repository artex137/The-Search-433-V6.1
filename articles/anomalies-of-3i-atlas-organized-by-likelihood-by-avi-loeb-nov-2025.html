Anomalies of Comet 3I/ATLAS Summarized by Avi Loeb

On November 22, 2025, astronomer Avi Loeb published an analysis of the anomalies associated with the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS. The report categorizes these anomalies by their likelihood and suggests potential implications for future research. No injuries or fatalities have been reported in relation to the comet.

This analysis matters as it raises questions about the nature of 3I/ATLAS and its trajectory. Authorities in the scientific community are examining these anomalies to understand whether they indicate something extraordinary, such as artificial technology or unusual physical characteristics.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS is set to encounter Jupiter on March 16, 2026.
  • The predicted perijove distance is 53.445 million kilometers.
  • Loeb categorizes anomalies into major and medium likelihoods, with probabilities ranging from P=0.00004 to P=0.006.
  • 3I/ATLAS has shown a sunward jet and unusual gas compositions.
  • The comet is roughly a million times more massive than 1I/'Oumuamua.

Timeline

In July and August 2025, 3I/ATLAS displayed a sunward jet, confirmed by images taken during its closest approach to Mars. On March 16, 2026, it is expected to encounter Jupiter, allowing further observation of its anomalies.

Official statements

“The forecasted perijove distance of 3I/ATLAS during its encounter with Jupiter might indicate intentional behavior,” Loeb stated in his analysis. “This raises the possibility that the comet could release technological devices.”

Context

This report follows the discovery of other interstellar objects, such as 1I/'Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov, which have also sparked interest in the nature of objects entering the solar system. Previous studies have focused on the physical characteristics and origins of these celestial bodies.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS as it approaches Jupiter in March 2026, with an emphasis on analyzing its trajectory and composition to determine the validity of the anomalies reported by Loeb.
