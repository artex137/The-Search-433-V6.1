Chaos in the Caribbean: The Story of UFO's No Place To Run

UFO, the British rock band, has faced turmoil in their history, particularly during the late 1970s when guitarist Michael Schenker left the group on the cusp of greater success. This pivotal moment occurred while they were working on their live album, Strangers In The Night, and propelled the band to record No Place To Run in a studio on the Caribbean island of Montserrat without him.

This event is significant as it marked a turning point for UFO, showcasing their resilience and ability to adapt after losing a key member. The band continued to evolve, bringing in Paul Chapman as Schenker's replacement, which contributed to their ongoing legacy in rock music.

Key facts

  • UFO recorded No Place To Run in Montserrat.
  • Michael Schenker left the band in 1978.
  • Paul Chapman rejoined UFO after Schenker's departure.
  • UFO's farewell tour was cancelled in 2022.
  • Phil Mogg suffered a heart attack, affecting the band's status.
  • Paul Chapman and Pete Way passed away in 2020; Paul Raymond died in 2019.

Timeline

In 1978, Michael Schenker, the band's lead guitarist, departed during the mixing of their live album. This departure was marked by tensions within the band, particularly between Schenker and lead vocalist Phil Mogg. Following Schenker’s exit, UFO regrouped and decided to record No Place To Run in Montserrat.

In 2022, UFO's farewell tour was abruptly cancelled due to Phil Mogg suffering a heart attack, marking a significant end to their touring history. The band's legacy continued, however, as they had successfully navigated the challenges posed by lineup changes and personal struggles over the decades.

Official statements

“It was a shame, because by then UFO were right on the cusp of moving from arenas into stadiums,” Mogg said, reflecting on Schenker's departure.
“George was like one of the boys, really,” Pete Way said in a 2016 interview about the producer George Martin’s influence during the recording of No Place To Run.

Context

UFO, formed in London, gained prominence in the 1970s with a series of successful albums featuring Schenker. His departure led to a reshaping of the band, but they proved that they could continue to thrive creatively. Their experience highlights the volatility often seen in the music industry, especially within rock bands dealing with personal and professional conflicts.

The band faced multiple challenges over the years, including lineup changes and health issues, yet they managed to maintain a loyal fan base and a significant place in rock history.

What’s next

As of now, UFO's future remains uncertain following the cancellation of their farewell tour. Investigations into the health and wellbeing of remaining members, particularly Phil Mogg, will likely dictate any potential future activities for the band.
