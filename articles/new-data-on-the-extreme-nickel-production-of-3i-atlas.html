New Data on the Extreme Nickel Production of 3I/ATLAS

A new report from the Very Large Telescope in Chile has revealed significant findings regarding the unusual nickel and iron production rates of the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS. The report, published on October 1, 2025, indicates that the comet displays unexpected levels of nickel and iron in its gas plume, even at low surface temperatures.

This discovery is important as it challenges existing theories about the composition of cometary materials and the conditions required for the sublimation of metals. Researchers are now analyzing the implications of these findings and planning further observations of 3I/ATLAS as it approaches Mars.

Key facts

  • Date of report: October 1, 2025
  • Location of observations: Very Large Telescope, Chile
  • Heliocentric distances observed: 3.14 to 2.14 AU
  • Nickel detected at all observed distances; iron detected below 2.64 AU
  • Diameter of 3I/ATLAS: larger than 5 kilometers
  • Upcoming observations by NASA’s Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter and ESA’s Mars orbiters: October 1–7, 2025
  • Closest approach to Mars: 29 million kilometers

Timeline

On July 21, 2025, the Hubble Space Telescope captured the highest resolution image of 3I/ATLAS from a distance of 570 million kilometers. This image provided initial insights into the comet's structure.

The report from the Very Large Telescope was published on October 1, 2025, detailing the anomalous nickel and iron production rates. Observations are set to continue as 3I/ATLAS approaches Mars.

Official statements

“At the distances at which comets are observed, the temperature is far too low to vaporize silicate, sulfide, and metallic grains that contain nickel and iron atoms. Therefore, the presence of nickel and iron atoms in cometary coma is extremely puzzling.” - Avi Loeb

Context

Previous interstellar comets, such as 2I/Borisov, have shown nickel and iron in their spectra, but the abundance ratios have been consistent with solar system comets. 3I/ATLAS's extreme properties, including its high nickel to iron ratio, set it apart from these earlier observations.

What’s next

NASA and ESA will conduct further observations of 3I/ATLAS in early October 2025, as it passes close to Mars, to gather more data on the comet's composition and behavior.
