3I/ATLAS: Harvard Astrophysicist Avi Loeb Flags Interstellar Object’s 17th Anomaly Discovered In SPHEREx Data

Harvard astrophysicist Avi Loeb has identified the 17th anomaly associated with the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS, based on data gathered from NASA's SPHEREx observatory. The findings, published on December 19, 2025, follow the object's perihelion on October 27, 2025, and its close approach to Earth at a distance of 1.8 astronomical units (AU), or approximately 270 million kilometers.

This discovery is significant as it enhances our understanding of the composition and behavior of interstellar objects. Loeb and his team have documented various anomalies of 3I/ATLAS, raising questions about its origins and characteristics. The research indicates that 3I/ATLAS may not conform to the typical properties of known comets.

Key facts

  • 3I/ATLAS was discovered on July 1, 2025, by the ATLAS observatory.
  • The object entered perihelion on October 27, 2025.
  • It passed Earth on December 19, 2025, at a distance of 1.8 AU.
  • SPHEREx conducted observations from December 8 to 15, 2025.
  • New findings reveal a 20-fold increase in water-gas emissions post-perihelion.
  • The next observations of 3I/ATLAS are planned for April 2026.

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first observed on July 1, 2025. It reached perihelion on October 27, 2025, after which it approached Earth on December 19, 2025, at a distance of 1.8 AU. Following its perihelion, NASA's SPHEREx mission conducted observations between December 8 and 15, 2025, leading to the identification of the 17th anomaly by Avi Loeb.

Official statements

“The new images of 3I/ATLAS were taken in the wavelength range of 0.75–5.0 microns,” said Loeb. “On these large scales, the brightness maps of dust and organics were found to be pear-shaped, with an anti-tail elongation in the direction of the Sun.”
Loeb noted, “Compared to the pre-perihelion data reported from SPHEREx in August 2025, the continuum spectral signature of water-ice absorption has mostly disappeared, replaced by scattered light plus thermal emission from organo-silicaceous dust grains.”

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third recorded interstellar object, following 1I/Oumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019. The peculiar nature of 3I/ATLAS has prompted ongoing research into its composition and potential origins. Previous studies have suggested that interstellar objects may differ significantly from known comets.

What’s next

Observations of 3I/ATLAS will continue, with the next scheduled study by SPHEREx set for April 2026, following its rendezvous with Jupiter’s irregular moon Eupheme in March.
