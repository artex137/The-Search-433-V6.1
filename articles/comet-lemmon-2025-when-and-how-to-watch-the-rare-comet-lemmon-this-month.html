Comet Lemmon 2025: When And How To Watch The Rare Comet Lemmon This Month

Comet 3I/ATLAS, also known as Comet Lemmon (C/2025 A6), is set to become visible in the night sky starting October 21, 2025, when it is expected to shine at its brightest. The comet was first detected in January 2025 by the Mount Lemmon Survey in Arizona, and it will be visible without the need for a telescope or binoculars.

This event is significant for skywatchers, as only a few comets become bright enough to be seen with the naked eye each year. Observers have noted that Lemmon’s strong luminosity and active tail make it a noteworthy sight. Nick James of the British Astronomical Association stated, “This comet is developing very nicely and it is already an impressive object, well-placed for observation in the morning sky. It is definitely worth getting up for!”

Key facts

  • Comet Name: Comet Lemmon (C/2025 A6)
  • Discovery: January 2025, by Mount Lemmon Survey, Arizona
  • Brightest Visibility: October 21, 2025
  • Closest Approach: Approximately 56 million miles (90 million kilometres) from Earth
  • Orbital Period: Currently around 1,350 years, expected to shorten to 1,150 years after passing the Sun
  • Current Visibility: Early morning skies, shifting to evenings as the month progresses
  • Constellations: Recently passed through Gemini, now in Ursa Major

Timeline

In January 2025, Comet Lemmon was first detected by astronomers at the Mount Lemmon Survey in Arizona. Observations have shown that the comet is developing well and has become an impressive object in the sky. As October approaches, it is expected to reach its peak brightness on October 21, coinciding with a new moon, thus providing optimal viewing conditions.

As the month progresses, the comet will transition from being visible in the early morning to appearing in the evening skies, making it accessible to more observers. Astronomers have noted that the comet's brightness can be unpredictable, but its performance thus far has exceeded expectations.

Official statements

Context

Comets are often referred to as “cosmic snowballs,” consisting of frozen gases, rock, and dust that orbit the Sun. While many comets are observed each year, only a select few become bright enough to be seen without optical aids. Comet Lemmon's exceptionally bright tail has allowed astronomers to map its path with clarity, making it a significant event for both amateur and professional astronomers.

What’s next

Skywatchers should prepare for optimal viewing of Comet Lemmon on October 21, 2025, as it is expected to provide a spectacular display. Observers are encouraged to monitor its visibility as it transitions to evening skies later in the month.
