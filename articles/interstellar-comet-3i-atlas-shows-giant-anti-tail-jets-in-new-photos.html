Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS Shows Giant Anti-Tail Jets in New Photos

The interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS has captured the attention of astronomers after Japanese astrophotographer Satoru Murata took new images from New Mexico on the morning of October 17, 2023. The photographs reveal three distinct ionic jets and a dramatic sunward-pointing anti-tail as the comet approached the galaxy NGC4691 under a thin crescent Moon.

This discovery is significant as it challenges existing assumptions about cometary physics. Murata's images confirm that 3I/ATLAS is following a hyperbolic trajectory, indicating it will permanently leave the Solar System. The unusual features of the comet may reshape scientific understanding of interstellar objects.

Key facts

  • Photographs taken by Satoru Murata on October 17, 2023.
  • Comet 3I/ATLAS is 326 million kilometres from Earth.
  • Main tail extends 2.85 million kilometres from the Sun.
  • Sunward-facing anti-tail jets stretch 0.95 million kilometres.
  • Outflow speeds of around 0.4 km/s indicate material release.
  • Estimated monthly mass loss of roughly 50 billion tonnes.
  • Closest approach will occur on December 19, 2025, at 269 million kilometres.

Timeline

On October 17, 2023, Satoru Murata captured images of comet 3I/ATLAS showing its unique jets and anti-tail for the first time. This observation coincided with the comet's proximity to the galaxy NGC4691. The images quickly gained attention among scientists studying the comet's behaviour.

NASA's Juno mission is scheduled to pass 53 million kilometres from the comet in March 2026, while ESA's Juice probe will be 64 million kilometres away, but neither will be able to intercept particles from the comet.

Official statements

“I captured the interstellar comet early this morning as a thin crescent moon passed immediately next to it. Still was able to capture three separate jets of ionic tail, and an anti tail? Or the dust tail?” - Satoru Murata

Context

3I/ATLAS is not the first interstellar object to challenge existing scientific theories. The arrival of 'Oumuamua in 2017 also prompted new discussions about the nature of such objects. The current observations of 3I/ATLAS build on this trend, offering a rare chance to study an interstellar visitor.

What’s next

Scientists will continue to gather data on 3I/ATLAS as it moves beyond the orbit of the outer planets. Space telescopes like Hubble and James Webb will be essential for ongoing observations, as no spacecraft will be able to sample the comet directly.
