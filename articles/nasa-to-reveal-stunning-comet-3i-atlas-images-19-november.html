NASA to reveal stunning comet 3I/ATLAS images 19 November

NASA is set to release the most detailed images of interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS during a live global broadcast on November 19 at 3 PM EST. The event will showcase high-resolution imagery and data that scientists believe could reshape our understanding of planetary systems.

The upcoming reveal is significant as it may provide insights into the formation of exoplanets. The images, collected by the Hubble Space Telescope and other missions, will include animations and dust-trail mapping, offering a comprehensive look at this unique celestial body.

Key facts

  • Event date: November 19, 2023
  • Time: 3 PM EST (2000 GMT)
  • Location: NASA's broadcasting channels
  • Notable telescopes: Hubble Space Telescope, ground-based telescopes
  • Image sources: Close-approach imagery from Mars-orbiting instruments
  • Agency: NASA

Timeline

3I/ATLAS, the third confirmed interstellar object to enter our solar system, has been attracting attention due to its unusual composition and behavior. In July 2025, Hubble captured striking images of the comet from 277 million miles away, revealing unique fragmentation patterns.

The images were delayed earlier this year due to a federal shutdown, which NASA attributed to manpower shortages, not secrecy. Acting NASA Administrator Sean Duffy confirmed that the images would be released only after a thorough scientific review.

Official statements

“The imagery gathered could fundamentally reshape understanding of how planetary systems form,” NASA stated in its announcement.
“The postponement was due to manpower shortages, not secrecy,” said Acting NASA Administrator Sean Duffy.

Context

3I/ATLAS differs from comets originating in the Kuiper Belt or Oort Cloud, having formed around a distant star before being ejected into interstellar space. This unique origin may offer direct clues about the building blocks of exoplanets and contribute to refining models of comet physics.

What’s next

The live event on November 19 will be streamed across various platforms, including NASA+, the NASA app, and YouTube. The release is expected to engage the public and highlight the importance of monitoring interstellar visitors.
