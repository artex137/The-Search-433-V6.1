Mysterious Object Headed Into Our Solar System Is Changing Shape, New Image Shows

Astronomers have observed a significant change in the shape of 3I/ATLAS, a comet-like object entering our solar system from interstellar space. New images taken by the Gemini South telescope in Cerro Pachón, Chile, have shown that the object’s tail is growing longer, and its chemical composition resembles that of other comets in our solar system. There are no reports of injuries or fatalities related to this phenomenon.

This discovery is important as it challenges prior assumptions about the composition of interstellar objects. Observations from four NASA telescopes indicate that 3I/ATLAS has a higher proportion of carbon dioxide gas than expected. This finding suggests that interstellar comets may share similar evolutionary processes with local comets, potentially reshaping our understanding of these celestial bodies.

Key facts

  • Object: 3I/ATLAS
  • Location of observation: Cerro Pachón, Chile
  • Observation conducted by: Gemini South telescope
  • Expected perihelion: Late October 2023
  • Significance: Third confirmed interstellar object

Timeline

Astronomers have been monitoring 3I/ATLAS as it approaches our solar system. Observations have revealed that the object is emitting gases and developing a longer tail as it nears its perihelion, which is expected to occur in late October 2023.

Recent images taken by the Gemini South telescope have provided new insights into the comet’s chemical makeup, indicating that it shares similarities with other comets formed in our solar system.

Official statements

“The primary objectives of the observations were to look at the colors of the comet, which provide clues to the composition and sizes of the dust particles in the coma, and to take spectra for a direct measure of the chemistry,” said Karen Meech, astronomer at the University of Hawai‘i Institute for Astronomy.
“We were excited to see the growth of the tail, suggesting a change in the particles from the previous Gemini images, and we got our first glimpse of the chemistry from the spectrum,” Meech added.
“As 3I/ATLAS speeds back into the depths of interstellar space, this image is both a scientific milestone and a source of wonder,” Meech stated. “It reminds us that our Solar System is just one part of a vast and dynamic galaxy — and that even the most fleeting visitors can leave a lasting impact.”

Context

3I/ATLAS is notable for being only the third interstellar object confirmed to have entered our solar system. Its observations have sparked interest among astronomers, as they provide a rare opportunity to study the characteristics of interstellar material and its similarities with local comets.

What’s next

Astronomers will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS as it approaches its perihelion, gathering more data to better understand its composition and behavior as it travels through the solar system.
