Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS Approaches Earth, Delighting Astronomers Before Christmas

Comet 3I/ATLAS, an interstellar comet, is set to make its closest approach to Earth on December 19, 2023, just six days before Christmas. Discovered in July 2023, it is the third known comet identified as originating from outside our solar system. There have been no reported injuries or fatalities related to this event.

Darryl Z. Seligman, an Assistant Professor of Physics and Astronomy at Michigan State University, has highlighted the significance of this close encounter for astronomers and enthusiasts. The upcoming approach presents a unique opportunity for observation and study, allowing scientists to gather valuable data on the composition and behavior of interstellar objects.

Key facts

  • Comet Name: 3I/ATLAS
  • Closest Approach: December 19, 2023
  • Discovery Date: July 2023
  • Previous Interstellar Comets: 1I/’Oumuamua (2017), 2I/Borisov (2019)
  • Composition: Rich in carbon dioxide

Timeline

Comet 3I/ATLAS was discovered in July 2023 after being ejected from its home star system, likely due to gravitational interactions with a giant exoplanet. It has traveled through the Milky Way’s interstellar medium for billions of years. The comet will reach its nearest point to Earth on December 19, 2023, providing a rare chance for observation.

Official statements

“The close passage of comet 3I/ATLAS provides astronomers with a rare chance to investigate the formation and evolution of comets throughout the galaxy,” said Darryl Z. Seligman, Assistant Professor of Physics and Astronomy at Michigan State University.

Context

The discovery of 3I/ATLAS contributes to the ongoing study of interstellar objects, which have unique hyperbolic orbits. Unlike native solar system bodies, these objects pass through the solar system without returning. This suggests that significant processes are occurring in other planetary systems, similar to those that have affected our own solar system.

What’s next

As December 19 approaches, astronomers and skywatchers prepare for the close encounter, utilizing small telescopes and powerful binoculars to observe the comet. This event may lead to new insights into planetary formation and the nature of our solar system.
