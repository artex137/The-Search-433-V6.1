Interstellar Comet 3I/Atlas Confirms Hyperbolic Trajectory and Reaches Speed of 57 km/s in 2025

The interstellar comet 3I/Atlas has been confirmed to be moving at a speed of 57 kilometers per second as it passes through the Solar System in 2025, according to astronomers. This comet, cataloged as 3I/Atlas, follows a hyperbolic trajectory, which means it will not be captured by the Sun's gravity and will only make a single pass through our Solar System before returning to deep space.

This discovery is significant as it positions 3I/Atlas as the third confirmed interstellar object observed, following ‘Oumuamua in 2017 and Borisov in 2019. Scientists have noted that the high speed of 3I/Atlas surpasses that of its predecessors, offering a rare opportunity to study material originating from another star system, which could provide insights into the formation of planetary systems.

Key facts

  • Speed: 57 km/s
  • Trajectory: Hyperbolic
  • Observations: Confirmed by astronomers in 2025
  • Previous interstellar objects: ‘Oumuamua (2017) and Borisov (2019)
  • Current studies: Analyzing chemical composition and trajectory

Timeline

In 2017, the first interstellar visitor, ‘Oumuamua, was detected, followed by Borisov in 2019. The detection of 3I/Atlas in 2025 marks a significant milestone in the study of interstellar objects.

Throughout 2025, astronomers have conducted continuous observations to accurately map the comet's outward path into deep space. The main interaction with the Sun will occur over a few weeks, with predictions for its perihelion, the point of maximum approach.

Official statements

“The high velocity of 3I/Atlas reinforces its extrasolar origin and allows scientists to gather valuable data on the conditions that formed it,” said an astronomer involved in the study.

Context

The passage of interstellar objects like 3I/Atlas provides critical information about the diversity of planetary systems in the galaxy. Unlike comets originating from our Solar System, interstellar comets maintain a high velocity due to their galactic environment. Previous interstellar visitors have displayed unique characteristics that have intrigued scientists and expanded our understanding of cosmic phenomena.

What’s next

The global astronomical community will continue to monitor 3I/Atlas throughout its passage in 2025, utilizing telescopes and spectrographs to gather as much information as possible about this rare celestial object.
