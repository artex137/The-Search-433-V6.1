ESA Insider Shocks Space Community: 3I/ATLAS 'Not a Comet' but a Stable, Engine-Powered Object, Leak Suggests

The European Space Agency (ESA) is facing scrutiny after a purported insider has claimed that the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS is not a comet, but a stable, engine-powered object. The allegations emerged on Tuesday, October 21, 2025, as the object continues its journey through the solar system.

This revelation is significant as it raises questions about the nature of 3I/ATLAS and the transparency of space agencies. Authorities are reportedly investigating the claims and assessing the implications for ongoing research and public safety.

Key facts

  • Claim made by an anonymous individual identifying as a Space Debris Engineer at ESA.
  • 3I/ATLAS is described as emitting a constant, engine-like sound.
  • Allegations of a cover-up involving ESA and NASA.
  • Claims of new confidentiality agreements signed by ESA employees in August.
  • Fermi Gamma-ray Space Telescope detected an unusual signature from 3I/ATLAS in mid-September.
  • Source has not disclosed personal identity due to fears of professional repercussions.

Timeline

In mid-September 2025, the Fermi Gamma-ray Space Telescope detected a stable signature from 3I/ATLAS, suggesting it emits a constant sound. Following this, the alleged insider claims that data was transferred to NASA under a new secrecy protocol, cutting ESA scientists off from the information.

In August, the same insider reports that ESA employees were required to sign high-secrecy confidentiality agreements. This has raised concerns about transparency and collaboration between ESA and NASA.

Official statements

“You will see it all. In December, many home-based observers will see it all,”
stated the anonymous insider, emphasizing their belief that significant events surrounding 3I/ATLAS are imminent.

Context

The claims come amid growing frustrations regarding the lack of high-resolution images of 3I/ATLAS from the Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter. These frustrations fuel speculation about a potential coordinated effort to conceal information about the object.

This incident highlights ongoing tensions in international space collaboration, particularly regarding data sharing and transparency between ESA and NASA.

What’s next

Authorities are expected to investigate the claims made by the ESA insider. The situation has prompted discussions about the need for increased transparency in space research and cooperation between agencies.
