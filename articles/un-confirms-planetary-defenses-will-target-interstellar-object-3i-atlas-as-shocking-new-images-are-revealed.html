UN confirms planetary defenses will target interstellar object 3I/ATLAS as shocking new images are revealed

The United Nations has confirmed that Earth's planetary defenses will focus on the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS starting on November 27, 2025. A team of global scientists will track the object as it approaches Earth, reaching its closest point on December 19, 2025. No injuries or fatalities have been reported.

This initiative is significant as it serves as a massive drill for the International Asteroid Warning Network (IAWN) to prepare for potential future threats. While 3I/ATLAS is not expected to pose any danger, the campaign will help refine tracking methods for celestial objects.

Key facts

  • Date of closest approach: December 19, 2025
  • Tracking campaign start: November 27, 2025
  • Duration of campaign: Two months
  • Image capture dates: November 22-24, 2025
  • Key contributors: Harvard professor Avi Loeb, amateur astronomers
  • IAWN operated by: UN Office for Outer Space Affairs

Timeline

On November 19, 2025, NASA held a press conference to discuss 3I/ATLAS but faced criticism for presenting unclear images of the object. On November 22, amateur astronomers began capturing clearer images, revealing detailed features of the comet.

On November 26, 2025, the UN announced the planetary defense campaign, which will involve tracking 3I/ATLAS until January 2026. The campaign is designed to test and improve observational techniques for future celestial threats.

Official statements

“Comet 3I/ATLAS presents a great opportunity for the IAWN community to perform an observing exercise due to its prolonged observability from Earth and high interest to the scientific community,” IAWN stated.

Context

3I/ATLAS was discovered in July 2025. Since then, it has raised interest due to its unusual characteristics, including a cometary tail that points toward the sun and changes in color. These anomalies have led some, including Professor Loeb, to speculate about the object's nature.

This campaign is part of a broader effort by the UN and NASA to enhance global preparedness for potential asteroid threats.

What’s next

The IAWN will monitor 3I/ATLAS until January 2026, refining tracking methods. While the UN and NASA affirm the object is a comet, skepticism remains in some circles regarding the nature of the object and the purpose of the campaign.
