Unprecedented brightening of interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS reported in new research

Scientists have reported an unprecedented and rapid increase in brightness of Comet 3I/ATLAS as it approaches the Sun, with the phenomenon detected by multiple space-based observatories. This event is significant as it marks the comet's behaviour leading up to its closest point to the Sun, known as perihelion, which will occur on October 29, 2025.

The unusual brightening of 3I/ATLAS has raised questions among researchers about the comet's composition and behaviour compared to typical Oort Cloud comets. As the third known interstellar object to enter the solar system, the comet offers unique insights into the chemical makeup of distant planetary systems. Researchers are currently investigating the causes of the rapid brightening and its implications for understanding interstellar comets.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS is the third interstellar object observed in the solar system.
  • Perihelion is expected on October 29, 2025.
  • The comet's brightening has occurred more rapidly than typical Oort Cloud comets.
  • Detection has involved the Solar and Heliospheric Observatory (SOHO), GOES-19, and NASA's STEREO-A and STEREO-B.
  • Ground-based telescopes will not be able to observe the comet until mid- to late-November 2025.

Timeline

Comet 3I/ATLAS has exhibited rapid brightening as it nears perihelion, leading to increased interest from the scientific community. The brightening was detected by several observatories, including SOHO and NASA's twin spacecraft, STEREO-A and STEREO-B. Researchers have published their findings in a new paper on arXiv, detailing the unexpected nature of the brightening.

As of now, the comet is lost in the Sun's glare, preventing further observations until it enters its post-perihelion phase.

Official statements

“The reason for 3I’s rapid brightening, which far exceeds the brightening rate of most Oort Cloud comets at similar radial distances, remains unclear,” said Karl Battams of the Naval Research Laboratory.
“If the internal composition of 3I/ATLAS is different from that of the nuclei of Oort Cloud comets, it could suggest that the planetary system from which it originates has a unique chemical makeup,” the authors noted.

Context

Comet 3I/ATLAS follows the discovery of ‘Oumuamua in October 2017 and 2I/Borisov in August 2019, both of which have provided valuable insights into interstellar objects. The brightening behaviour of 3I/ATLAS contrasts with typical expectations for comets originating from the Oort Cloud.

Researchers continue to explore the implications of these findings on our understanding of the composition and evolution of planetary systems beyond our own.

What’s next

Scientists will continue to monitor Comet 3I/ATLAS as it moves into its post-perihelion phase. Future observations may shed light on the comet's behaviour and contribute to a better understanding of interstellar comets.
