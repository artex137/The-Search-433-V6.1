Did Mysterious Interstellar Visitor 3I/ATLAS Explode?

A recent observation of the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS has raised questions about its stability and classification. The comet, which swung around the Sun in late October 2023, has reportedly exhibited unusual behavior, including the potential for disintegration. There have been no reported injuries or fatalities related to this event.

This matter is significant as it has implications for our understanding of interstellar objects and potential extraterrestrial phenomena. Scientists are actively monitoring 3I/ATLAS to gather more data, with a close pass to Earth scheduled for December 19, 2023.

Key facts

  • Object: 3I/ATLAS
  • Observation date: Late October 2023
  • Material loss: Up to 4.4 million pounds per second
  • Estimated size increase: Surface area 16 times larger than previously observed
  • Next close pass to Earth: December 19, 2023
  • NASA's Juno spacecraft will observe near Jupiter in March 2024

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first identified as a comet, but its behavior has prompted further investigation. In late October 2023, the object exhibited significant outgassing, with a massive “anti-tail” observed nearly two million miles long. This event raised questions about the object's classification and whether it might be disintegrating.

Harvard astrophysicist Avi Loeb suggested that the object could have broken apart into at least 16 pieces due to its intense outburst. Meanwhile, other scientists, including Qicheng Zhang from Lowell Observatory, have disagreed, stating that the object appears to be a normal comet without signs of disintegration.

Official statements

“All the images I’ve seen show a fairly ordinary/healthy-looking comet,” said Qicheng Zhang. “There’s no sign at all that the nucleus broke apart.”
Avi Loeb has raised the possibility that “technological thrusters require a much smaller mass loss in order to produce the observed jets around 3I/ATLAS,” suggesting alternative explanations for the object’s behavior.

Context

Previous observations of interstellar objects have often led to debates about their origins and classifications. 3I/ATLAS is notable for its unusual activity, which has spurred discussions about potential alien technology versus natural phenomena. The detection of radio absorption lines from hydroxyl radicals signifies that the object is releasing water, which is of interest to astronomers studying the potential for life in other planetary systems.

What’s next

Scientists will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS closely, especially as it approaches Earth on December 19, 2023. Further observations will be conducted by NASA’s Juno spacecraft when the object nears Jupiter in March 2024.
