3I/ATLAS: Live Coverage And Complete Information On The Interstellar Comet That’ll Fly Closest To Earth On December 19

3I/ATLAS, an interstellar comet discovered on July 1, 2025, by the Atlas Terrestrial-Impact Last Alert System (ATLAS), will approach Earth at a distance of 1.79 astronomical units (AU) on December 19, 2025. The comet, located in the constellation of Leo, flew closest to the Sun on October 29, 2025, an event known as perihelion.

This event is significant as 3I/ATLAS has presented unusual characteristics that have intrigued scientists and stargazers. Major space observatories have attempted to capture images of the comet but have returned only grainy portraits. Observations have led to several studies, with experts estimating the comet's age between 7.6 and 14 billion years. The comet is believed to originate near the constellation Sagittarius, close to the Milky Way's galactic center.

Key facts

  • Discovered: July 1, 2025
  • Perihelion: October 29, 2025
  • Closest approach to Earth: December 19, 2025
  • Distance at closest approach: 1.79 AU
  • Speed: 210,000 km/h, increasing to 246,000 km/h at perihelion
  • Estimated size: 10 kilometers
  • Origin: Near the constellation Sagittarius

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first detected on July 1, 2025, by ATLAS. The comet's perihelion occurred on October 29, 2025, when it passed closest to the Sun. This event sparked interest due to the comet's speed and unusual properties. Observations in November confirmed the presence of hydroxyl molecules through a faint radio signal detected by the MeerKAT radio telescope.

In addition, the James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) revealed the comet's chemical composition, showing an abundance of carbon dioxide. Further analyses by the ALMA observatory indicated that the comet contains methanol and hydrogen cyanide. Notably, researchers Darryl Z. Seligman and Rohan Rahatgaonkar reported the detection of glowing nickel vapor surrounding the comet.

Official statements

“Our team has detected glowing nickel vapor in the gas surrounding the incoming interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS at an extraordinary distance from the sun, where temperatures remain far too cold for metals to normally vaporize,” said researchers Darryl Z. Seligman and Rohan Rahatgaonkar.

Context

3I/ATLAS is the largest interstellar object observed to date, surpassing 1I/Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov. The estimated age of the comet raises questions about the origins of celestial bodies beyond our solar system, particularly when compared to the solar system's age of 4.6 billion years.

What’s next

As 3I/ATLAS approaches Earth, astronomers will continue to monitor its trajectory and chemical properties. Observatories like NASA, SETI, and The Virtual Telescope Project will provide livestreams and tracking updates for enthusiasts and researchers alike.
