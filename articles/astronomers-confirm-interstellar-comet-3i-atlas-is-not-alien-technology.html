Astronomers Confirm Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS Is Not Alien Technology

Astronomers have confirmed that the interstellar comet known as 3I/ATLAS is not alien technology, concluding it is a naturally formed comet from another star system. This confirmation follows extensive observations using advanced optical and radio telescopes, taking place in December 2025 during the comet's closest approach to Earth.

This finding is significant as it addresses ongoing speculation regarding the origins of interstellar objects. Authorities, including NASA and the European Space Agency, have stated that 3I/ATLAS aligns with established cometary physics and does not pose any threat to Earth.

Key facts

  • 3I/ATLAS reached its closest point to Earth on December 19, 2025, at a distance of approximately 170 million miles.
  • The comet travels at a speed of about 220,000 kilometers per hour (137,000 miles per hour).
  • It is the third confirmed interstellar object detected passing through the Solar System.
  • The comet’s nucleus measures between 427 meters and 1,100 meters in diameter.
  • No evidence of artificial signals or technosignatures has been found.
  • The study was conducted by the Breakthrough Listen program using the Green Bank Telescope.
  • NASA and the European Space Agency independently reviewed observational data, reaching similar conclusions.

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first identified in July 2025. Following its discovery, astronomers observed the comet's trajectory and speed, confirming its interstellar classification.

On December 19, 2025, the comet made its closest approach to Earth, allowing for targeted radio observations to search for any signs of artificial technology.

Following these observations, scientists reported no detected signals, reinforcing the conclusion that the object is a natural comet.

Official statements

“The absence of radio technosignatures strongly supports the conclusion that 3I/ATLAS is a natural object rather than extraterrestrial technology,” said the research team involved in the observations.
“All observed characteristics align with established cometary physics,” stated officials at NASA.

Context

The speculation surrounding 3I/ATLAS was partly influenced by earlier debates over the first known interstellar object, ʻOumuamua, which exhibited unusual acceleration. This history has led to increased public interest in interstellar objects and their potential origins.

Avi Loeb, an astrophysicist, has argued for careful examination of interstellar objects for potential technological signatures, contributing to the ongoing discussion regarding the nature of such celestial bodies.

What’s next

Astronomers will continue to track 3I/ATLAS as it moves away from the Sun. As solar heating decreases, the comet's activity will fade, making further observations increasingly challenging. Researchers expect to gather as much data as possible before it permanently exits the Solar System.
