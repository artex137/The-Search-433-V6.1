Interstellar Comet 3I/Atlas Sparks Online Debate Over Possible Rotation

On 24 November 2025, new images and videos of the interstellar comet 3I/Atlas have circulated widely on social media, igniting debate over whether the comet is rotating or exhibiting unusual motion. Scientists have not confirmed any rotation or unusual activity associated with the comet.

This matter is significant as it highlights public interest in astronomical phenomena and the potential for misinterpretation of visual data. NASA and the European Space Agency (ESA) are currently studying the comet to clarify its characteristics and behavior.

Key facts

  • Discovered on 1 July 2025 in Chile
  • Travels at over 210,000 km/h
  • Closest approach to the Sun occurred on 30 October 2025
  • Best viewing time: before dawn, low in the east
  • Telescope required: at least 20 cm (8 inches) aperture
  • Cannot be seen with the naked eye
  • Prior observed interstellar objects: Oumuamua and Borisov

Timeline

3I/Atlas was discovered on 1 July 2025. It originates from outside the Solar System and is on a hyperbolic path, meaning it will not return. The comet made its closest approach to the Sun on 30 October 2025. Recent social media activity has focused on various videos claiming to show the comet’s rotation, although these claims remain unverified.

As videos circulated, comparisons were made with older footage from ESA’s ExoMars, leading to speculation about the comet’s motion. Some clips have shown bright spots or shapes near the nucleus, prompting further discussion among hobby astronomers and astrophotographers.

Official statements

“Most of these visual effects are likely due to how cameras capture light, not actual rotation,” said a spokesperson from NASA. “Currently, there is no evidence that the comet is spinning.”

Context

Before the discovery of 3I/Atlas, only two interstellar objects—Oumuamua and Borisov—had been observed. These rare celestial bodies offer insights into the composition of other star systems. The interest in 3I/Atlas reflects the growing public fascination with space exploration and the potential for new discoveries.

What’s next

NASA and ESA will continue to monitor 3I/Atlas as it moves outward from the Sun. Further studies are expected to clarify its characteristics and dispel any ongoing speculation regarding its rotation.
