UFOs Nearly Started World War 3? Soviet Nuclear Missiles 'Self-Activated' During 1982 Swarm

UFOs were reportedly involved in a near-catastrophic missile incident in Usovo, Ukraine, in 1982, when Soviet nuclear missiles aimed at New York City began a launch sequence without authorization, according to newly disclosed documents. The claims are based on accounts from Red Army personnel and Soviet defence investigators, although they remain unverified.

The significance of this incident lies in its potential implications for Cold War tensions. Authorities are now reviewing the documents, which were released by American television reporter George Knapp in January. The files suggest that the Soviet military was studying unexplained aerial phenomena, despite public denials of UFO existence.

Key facts

  • Incident occurred in 1982 near Usovo, Ukraine.
  • Nuclear missiles were aimed at New York City.
  • Documents were taken out of Russia in 1993.
  • Reportedly, missiles entered launch procedure without authorization.
  • Unidentified craft exhibited unusual movements, according to soldiers.
  • Knapp released the documents publicly in January 2023.

Timeline

In 1982, multiple unidentified flying objects appeared near a Soviet missile site outside Usovo. Witnesses reported the craft exhibiting sudden shifts in shape and colour, hovering capabilities, and abrupt disappearances. Following this, a Red Army propaganda chief reported that intercontinental ballistic missiles began their launch procedure without authorization while the unidentified objects were present.

The situation reportedly escalated, raising the prospect of a nuclear strike on New York. Minutes before launch, radar reports indicated that the missiles were deactivated as the unidentified objects departed, averting a potential catastrophe.

Official statements

“The reports present the allegation, not proof,” said an anonymous source familiar with the incident.

Context

The incident aligns with a broader history of Soviet interest in UFOs, despite official dismissals. In 1953, the Soviet government declared UFOs to be an invention of 'American imperialists.' However, the state later commissioned studies on unexplained aerial phenomena, including the 'Network-AN' programme starting in 1979. Subsequent projects included 'Galaxy-MD' from 1981 to 1985 and 'Pluton 7' in 1989 and 1990.

This contradiction illustrates a common bureaucratic tendency to investigate what is publicly dismissed. The Usovo incident has drawn attention due to its similarity to a 1967 event at Malmstrom Air Force Base in Montana, where a UFO was reported during a missile failure.

What’s next

Investigations into the documents and the claims they contain are ongoing. Authorities have yet to provide a timeline for further reviews or possible actions based on the findings.
