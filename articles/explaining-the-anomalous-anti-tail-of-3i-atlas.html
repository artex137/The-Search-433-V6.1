Explaining the Anomalous Anti-Tail of 3I/ATLAS

Researchers have observed an anomalous anti-tail on the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS, which was imaged by the Hubble Space Telescope on July 21, 2025, at a heliocentric distance of 3.8 astronomical units (AU) from the Sun. This unusual feature extends the glow of scattered sunlight towards the Sun, differing from the typical cometary tail that extends away from it. There have been no reported injuries or fatalities associated with this celestial observation.

This discovery is significant as it challenges previous assumptions about comet behavior and enhances understanding of interstellar objects. The researchers, including co-author Eric Keto, have published a new model explaining the formation of the anti-tail, based on the scattering of sunlight by ice fragments from the object’s surface. This finding invites further investigation into the characteristics and behavior of 3I/ATLAS as it approaches the Sun.

Key facts

  • Imaging by the Hubble Space Telescope occurred on July 21, 2025.
  • 3I/ATLAS was located at a distance of 3.8 AU from the Sun.
  • The anti-tail is an extension of the glow of scattered sunlight towards the Sun.
  • The object primarily emits carbon dioxide (CO2) gas, constituting 87% of its mass.
  • The size of the ice fragments is approximately half a micrometer.
  • 3I/ATLAS is aligned with the ecliptic plane, with a chance probability of 0.5%.

Timeline

On March 28, 2025, the ATLAS telescope team released data indicating that 3I/ATLAS was active earlier than previously thought. The Hubble Space Telescope captured an image of the object on July 21, 2025, revealing the anomalous anti-tail. Observations on August 25, 2025, showed a steep rise in cyanide production, correlating with the object’s decreasing distance from the Sun.

Official statements

“The anti-tail represents an extension of the snow line, or survival distance of a sublimating ice grain, in the direction of the Sun,” said Eric Keto, co-author of the study.

Context

This discovery of an anti-tail is unprecedented for solar system comets. Prior interstellar objects, such as 1I/`Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov, exhibited different behavior, with 1I/`Oumuamua showing no signs of gas or dust and 2I/Borisov behaving like a typical comet. Understanding the differences among these objects is crucial for advancing knowledge in planetary science.

What’s next

As 3I/ATLAS approaches the Sun, researchers expect to learn more about its composition and behavior. Further observations will continue to refine the understanding of the anti-tail and the physical conditions surrounding this interstellar object.
