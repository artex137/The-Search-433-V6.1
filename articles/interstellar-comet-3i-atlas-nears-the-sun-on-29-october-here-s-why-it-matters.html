Interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS nears the Sun on 29 October

On 29 October 2025, the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS will reach its closest point to the Sun, providing a unique opportunity for scientists to study material from beyond our Solar System. There are no reported injuries or fatalities associated with this event.

This close approach, known as perihelion, will allow astronomers to conduct intense observation efforts. The comet's unusual trajectory and composition make it a significant subject of interest, as researchers aim to gain insights into its origins and the materials it carries.

Key facts

  • Date: 29 October 2025
  • Closest point to the Sun: 1.36 astronomical units (AU)
  • Location: Near Mars's orbit
  • Expected phenomena: Enhanced outgassing
  • Observation challenges: Comet will be nearly behind the Sun from Earth's perspective

Timeline

Interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS has been on a trajectory that brings it into the inner Solar System. It is expected to reach perihelion on 29 October 2025, where it will be at a distance comparable to Mars's orbit. Scientists anticipate that solar heating will maximize outgassing, allowing for detailed analysis of the comet's coma and tail.

NASA has indicated that while the comet will not come close to Earth, space-based observatories positioned at advantageous angles may still be able to gather critical data during this event.

Official statements

“This close solar passage will give researchers the best chance to analyze the comet’s unusual features,” a NASA spokesperson said.

Context

The study of interstellar comets like 3I/ATLAS is vital for understanding the materials that exist beyond our Solar System. Previous observations of such objects have provided insights into the formation and evolution of solar systems.

What’s next

Following the perihelion on 29 October 2025, astronomers will analyze data collected from telescopes and space-based observatories to further understand the composition and origin of 3I/ATLAS.
