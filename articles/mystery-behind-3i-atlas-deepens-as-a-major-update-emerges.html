Mystery Behind 3I/ATLAS Deepens as a Major Update Emerges

The European Space Agency (ESA) has provided a significant update on the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS, revealing a stunning new image captured by the Juice science camera on November 6, 2025. The image shows the comet surrounded by a bright halo of gas known as the coma, although the tiny nucleus of the comet remains not visible.

This update is important as 3I/ATLAS represents a rare interstellar visitor to our solar system. Scientists are studying the comet to learn more about its composition and origins, enhancing our understanding of cosmic phenomena. The Juice cameras, including JANUS, PEP, UVS, SWI, and MAJIS, collaborated to uncover these details.

Key facts

  • Object: 3I/ATLAS
  • Date of image capture: November 6, 2025
  • Distance from the Sun: Approximately 66 million kilometers
  • Agencies involved: European Space Agency (ESA)
  • Cameras used: JANUS, PEP, UVS, SWI, MAJIS

Timeline

On November 6, 2025, the ESA's Juice science camera captured a new image of 3I/ATLAS. This occurred after the comet reached its closest point to the Sun.

The image shows the comet's coma, a bright halo of gas, but the nucleus itself is not visible. This marks a significant moment for scientists studying interstellar objects.

Official statements

“The tiny nucleus of the comet is surrounded by a bright halo of gas known as the coma,” the ESA stated in its report.

Context

3I/ATLAS is one of the few interstellar objects observed in our solar system, providing unique opportunities for scientific research. Previous studies of similar objects have enhanced understanding of cosmic phenomena and the formation of celestial bodies.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to analyze data from the Juice cameras to learn more about 3I/ATLAS and its composition. Further updates from the ESA are expected as new findings emerge.
