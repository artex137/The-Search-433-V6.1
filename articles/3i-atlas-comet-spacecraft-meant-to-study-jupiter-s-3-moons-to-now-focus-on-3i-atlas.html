3I/ATLAS comet: Spacecraft meant to study Jupiter’s 3 moons to now focus on 3I/ATLAS

The European Space Agency's Jupiter Icy Moons Explorer (JUICE) is set to observe interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS from November 2 to November 25. The spacecraft will approach the comet within 64 million kilometers during its observation campaign, with the closest approach occurring on November 4, according to Olivier Witasse, ESA Project Scientist.

This observation is significant as it provides an unexpected opportunity to study 3I/ATLAS, the third interstellar visitor recorded in the solar system. The data collected will contribute to understanding the comet, although JUICE is primarily designed for studying Jupiter's moons and is not scheduled to arrive at Jupiter until the early 2030s.

Key facts

  • Observation period: November 2 to November 25, 2023
  • Closest approach: November 4, 2023, at 64 million kilometers
  • Spacecraft: Jupiter Icy Moons Explorer (JUICE)
  • Instruments used: Camera, near-infrared imaging instrument, UV spectrometer, sub-millimeter instrument, neutral atom sensor
  • Data download estimate: February 2026
  • Current position: 0.5 Astronomical units from Earth

Timeline

JUICE has been traveling toward Jupiter's icy moons Ganymede, Callisto, and Europa since its launch. The decision to observe comet 3I/ATLAS was made recently, prompted by the comet's unique characteristics. The spacecraft will conduct two observation periods, with the first scheduled for early November.

On November 4, JUICE will achieve its closest distance to 3I/ATLAS, marking a key moment in the observation campaign. The second observation date details have not been disclosed but are expected to provide further insights into the comet.

Official statements

“All this campaign was unexpected for everybody!”

— Olivier Witasse, ESA Project Scientist

“We are far away (0.5 Astronomical units), therefore, only remote sensing.”

— Olivier Witasse, ESA Project Scientist

Context

Comet 3I/ATLAS is notable as the third interstellar object documented in the solar system. The previous interstellar visitors have provided valuable data about the composition and behavior of such objects. The current observation campaign by ESA signals a growing interest in studying interstellar comets.

What’s next

Following the observations, data collection and transmission from JUICE to Earth will face significant delays, with expected downloads occurring in February 2026. The second observation date will yield additional data on 3I/ATLAS, enhancing our understanding of this interstellar comet.
