Global monitoring details trajectory and chemistry of interstellar object 3I/ATLAS near Jupiter

Astronomers from several space agencies have maintained rigorous surveillance over comet 3I/ATLAS, the third confirmed interstellar object crossing our planetary system, since early 2026. Originally detected in mid-2025 by the ATLAS survey system, the comet follows a hyperbolic trajectory and is currently navigating towards Jupiter.

This observation is significant as it provides a critical window for data collection before the comet departs into deep space. The international scientific community has mobilized a complex network of instruments, including high-power ground-based telescopes and space probes, to study the comet’s characteristics and composition.

Key facts

  • Object: 3I/ATLAS
  • Detection: Mid-2025 by ATLAS survey
  • Current location: Near Jupiter
  • Approach to Jupiter: Scheduled for March 2026
  • Maximum brightness: December 2025
  • Exit speed: Greater than 30 km/s

Timeline

In mid-2025, astronomers detected comet 3I/ATLAS using the ATLAS survey system. Since then, it has followed a hyperbolic path, nearing its perihelion, the closest point to the Sun. The comet exhibits an active core rich in ice and dust, allowing for extensive data collection.

In November 2025, NASA’s Europa Clipper mission conducted scheduled observations of the comet, providing high-resolution images in ultraviolet and infrared wavelengths. These observations complemented ground-based data and helped track the distribution of gases in the comet’s coma.

The comet is set to approach Jupiter in March 2026, where its trajectory will be altered by the gas giant’s gravitational influence, propelling it into deep space.

Official statements

“The passage of this object represents a unique opportunity for modern astrophysics,” said a spokesperson for the international scientific community involved in the observation campaign.

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third interstellar object detected, following ‘Oumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019. While ‘Oumuamua was characterized as a rocky and inactive body, 2I/Borisov displayed a prominent tail and high carbon monoxide concentration. 3I/ATLAS, in contrast, shows vigorous activity with a vibrant coma and distinct volatile elements.

The differences among these objects suggest varied origins in different star systems, highlighting the complexity of planetary ejection processes across the galaxy.

What’s next

The observation window for 3I/ATLAS is closing as its distance increases and brightness decreases. Telescopes like Hubble will continue to track the object until it is no longer within range. The data collected will be analyzed for decades, contributing to our understanding of interstellar materials and the conditions for prebiotic chemistry.
