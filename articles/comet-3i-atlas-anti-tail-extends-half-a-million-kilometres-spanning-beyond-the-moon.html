Comet 3I/ATLAS Anti-Tail Extends Half a Million Kilometres — Spanning Beyond The Moon

On December 15, 2025, at 02:28:12 UTC, astrophotographer Toni Scarmato captured an image of comet 3I/ATLAS in Calabria, Italy, revealing an extraordinary anti-tail extending approximately half a million kilometres from its nucleus. This distance surpasses the average space between Earth and the moon, prompting scientific inquiry into the comet's unusual features.

The discovery of comet 3I/ATLAS's massive anti-tail is significant as it challenges current understandings of interstellar objects. Scientists are now investigating the physical mechanisms behind its formation and behaviour, as traditional models of cometary activity do not adequately explain the observed phenomena.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS was observed on December 15, 2025, in Calabria, Italy.
  • The anti-tail measures approximately 500,000 kilometres, exceeding the distance to the moon.
  • As of December 15, the comet was about 270 million kilometres from Earth.
  • Closest approach is scheduled for December 19, 2025, at approximately 268.9 million kilometres.
  • The anti-tail formed rapidly, with material expelled at a speed of around 130 metres per second.

Timeline

On December 15, 2025, Toni Scarmato captured an image of comet 3I/ATLAS, revealing a massive anti-tail. This feature was confirmed through rigorous analysis, ruling out visual artefacts. The comet's closest approach to Earth is set for December 19, 2025, during which scientists will conduct further observations.

Official statements

“The possibility of technological origin should not be dismissed out of hand,” said Avi Loeb, a Harvard astrophysicist, regarding the unique behaviour of 3I/ATLAS.

Context

Comets typically exhibit tails that point away from the sun. The discovery of 3I/ATLAS's sun-facing anti-tail challenges conventional cometary behaviour and raises questions about the classification of interstellar objects. Current detection methods, such as Pan-STARRS and ATLAS, primarily identify larger objects, leaving a significant gap in understanding smaller interstellar visitors.

What’s next

As comet 3I/ATLAS approaches its closest point to Earth, observatories worldwide will intensify their monitoring efforts. Researchers will continue to analyze the comet's behaviour and its implications for planetary defence strategies.
