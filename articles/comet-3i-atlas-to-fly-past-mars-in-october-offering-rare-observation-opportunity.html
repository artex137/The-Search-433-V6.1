Comet 3I/ATLAS to Fly Past Mars in October, Offering Rare Observation Opportunity

Comet 3I/ATLAS will pass within 30 million kilometres of Mars on 3 October 2025, providing a unique observation opportunity for space agencies. Europe’s Mars orbiters, including Mars Express and the ExoMars Trace Gas Orbiter, are preparing to capture images and data from this interstellar visitor.

This flyby is significant as it marks only the third confirmed interstellar object to enter our solar system. Researchers aim to study the comet's properties, including outgassing and tail structure, to enhance understanding of how interstellar comets differ from those formed within our solar system. Authorities are coordinating efforts to ensure that multiple spacecraft are ready to observe the comet during its closest approach.

Key facts

  • Date of flyby: 3 October 2025
  • Distance from Mars: 30 million kilometres
  • Spacecraft involved: Mars Express, ExoMars Trace Gas Orbiter, NASA’s MAVEN, Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter, China’s Tianwen-1, ESA’s JUICE probe
  • Previous interstellar visitors: ʻOumuamua (2017), Borisov (2019)

Timeline

Comet 3I/ATLAS has been identified as an interstellar object, and it is set to approach Mars on 3 October 2025. This event will occur one month before the comet reaches perihelion in November, when its activity may increase.

The comet’s trajectory has been tracked since its discovery, allowing scientists to prepare for this rare opportunity to observe its characteristics as it passes through our solar system.

Official statements

“Even if the comet remains faint, the attempt itself provides valuable insight for designing future missions to track interstellar visitors,” said a representative from the European Space Agency.

Context

Interstellar objects are rare, with only two previously confirmed visitors, ʻOumuamua and Borisov. Their passing has prompted interest in understanding the properties of materials that originate outside our solar system. Observations of 3I/ATLAS may contribute to ongoing research in planetary science and the dynamics of interstellar materials.

What’s next

As the flyby approaches, scientists will finalize their observation strategies and ensure all instruments are operational. The data collected during this event will be analyzed to enhance knowledge about interstellar comets and inform future space missions.
