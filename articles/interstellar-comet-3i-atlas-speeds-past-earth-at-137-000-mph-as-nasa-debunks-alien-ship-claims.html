Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS Speeds Past Earth at 137,000 mph as NASA Debunks ‘Alien Ship’ Claims

The interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS has entered its closest approach to Earth in mid-December, traveling at a speed of approximately 220,000 km/h. NASA confirmed the comet's trajectory and composition, noting that it is not gravitationally bound to the sun. There have been no reports of injuries or fatalities linked to the comet's passage.

This event has garnered significant scientific interest, as astronomers study the comet, which is a rare object formed in another star system. NASA's confirmation of its interstellar origin has prompted researchers to analyze its characteristics and implications for understanding celestial bodies.

Key facts

  • Comet Name: 3I/ATLAS
  • Discovered: July by the Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System in Chile
  • Speed: 137,000 mph (220,000 km/h)
  • Closest Approach: Mid-December
  • Interstellar Status: Third verified interstellar visitor after 'Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov

Timeline

The comet was discovered in July 2023 by the Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System in Chile. NASA has since tracked its trajectory, confirming it is not bound by the sun's gravity. As it approaches Earth in mid-December, astronomers are preparing to observe and study its characteristics closely.

Official statements

Nasa described the object as “whizzing at about 137,000 mph,” indicating its speed and trajectory confirm its interstellar nature.

Context

3I/ATLAS is notable as the third verified interstellar object, following 'Oumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019. The study of interstellar comets helps scientists understand the formation and evolution of celestial bodies beyond our solar system.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS as it approaches Earth, gathering data that could provide insights into its composition and origin.
