Is 3I/ATLAS Returning to Earth? Experts Unveil 'Solar Slingshot' Plan to Catch Interstellar Visitor

NASA has confirmed that comet 3I/ATLAS is an interstellar visitor on a one-way journey through our solar system, with its closest approach to Earth at about 1.8 astronomical units. The agency has stated that the comet poses no danger to Earth.

This information is significant as it reveals public interest in the comet's trajectory, with many people curious if it could somehow return. NASA has clarified that 3I/ATLAS is not on a looped path like other comets, and while a direct interception has been missed, a proposal for a 2035 launch aims to capture the comet using a 'Solar Oberth' maneuver.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS is traveling at approximately 61 km/s (221,000 km/h).
  • Closest approach to Earth is about 1.8 AU (approximately 270 million kilometers).
  • 3I/ATLAS was reported by ATLAS on July 1, 2025.
  • A proposed mission could launch in 2035, with an intercept expected after 35-50 years.
  • The Solar Oberth maneuver would occur about 3.2 solar radii from the sun's center.
  • The mission may require two or three solid propellant boosters for the Oberth burn.

Timeline

NASA discovered comet 3I/ATLAS and reported its trajectory. The comet travels too fast to be captured by the sun and will not return to Earth. The closest approach was determined to be 1.8 AU from Earth. On July 1, 2025, ATLAS reported the comet's position, marking a missed opportunity for direct interception.

Following the discovery, a team led by Adam Hibberd proposed a potential mission to chase 3I/ATLAS, utilizing gravity assists and a Solar Oberth maneuver. They identified 2035 as the most efficient launch year, with the intercept likely occurring decades later.

Official statements

“3I/ATLAS poses no threat to Earth,” NASA stated on its public tracking page, emphasizing the comet's safe distance from our planet.
Adam Hibberd from the Initiative for Interstellar Studies mentioned, “A mission exists in the future,” referring to the proposed launch in 2035.

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third confirmed interstellar object, a category that includes only a few known comets. Unlike typical comets that loop back towards the sun, 3I/ATLAS follows a hyperbolic trajectory, indicating its origin from outside the solar system. This unique characteristic has generated interest in capturing it, especially as this may be a once-in-a-lifetime opportunity.

What’s next

The proposed mission is still in the planning stages, with further studies needed to assess the feasibility of the Solar Oberth maneuver and the necessary technology to support such a long-term project.
