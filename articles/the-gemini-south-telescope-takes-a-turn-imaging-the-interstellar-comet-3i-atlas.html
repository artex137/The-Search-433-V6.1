The Gemini South Telescope Takes A Turn Imaging The Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS

The Gemini South Telescope in Chile has captured new images of the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS on August 27, 2025. This comet is notable as it is only the third confirmed interstellar object to visit our Solar System, following Oumuamua and Comet 2L/Borisov. No injuries or fatalities have been reported in connection with this astronomical event.

The observations of Comet 3I/ATLAS are significant because they allow astronomers to study the comet's chemical makeup as it approaches the Sun. The National Science Foundation's NOIRLab operates the Gemini South Telescope, which is part of a public outreach initiative called Shadow the Scientists. This program connects astronomers with the public, allowing participants to engage in real-time observations and discussions.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS was discovered on July 1, 2025, at approximately 4.5 astronomical units (au) from the Sun.
  • The images were captured on August 27, 2025, using the Gemini Multi-Object Spectrograph (GMOS).
  • Participants included students and members of the public from Hawaii, Chile, Europe, New Zealand, and South America.
  • The telescope has an aperture of 8.1 meters and is one of two Gemini telescopes operated by NOIRLab.

Timeline

Comet 3I/ATLAS was first detected on July 1, 2025, as it entered the inner Solar System. On August 27, 2025, astronomers at the Gemini South Telescope conducted observations of the comet, capturing images of its growing coma and tail. The event included a public outreach session where participants could interact with astronomers via Zoom.

Official statements

“The primary objectives of the observations were to look at the colors of the comet, which provide clues to the composition and sizes of the dust particles in the coma, and to take spectra for a direct measure of the chemistry,” said Karen Meech, an astronomer at the University of Hawai‘i Institute for Astronomy, and leader of the scientific program.
“As 3I/ATLAS speeds back into the depths of interstellar space, this image is both a scientific milestone and a source of wonder,” Meech added.
“These observations provide both a breathtaking view and critical scientific data,” said Bryce Brolin, a research scientist present during the session.

Context

Comet 3I/ATLAS joins a small group of interstellar objects that have been observed in our Solar System. Prior to this, Oumuamua and Comet 2L/Borisov were the only other confirmed ISOs. Studying these comets helps scientists understand the formation and evolution of planetary systems beyond our own.

What’s next

A follow-up session will be held by Brolin when Comet 3I/ATLAS reappears from behind the Sun, using the Gemini North Telescope on Mauna Kea, Hawaii. This session will continue the public outreach initiative and provide further insights into the comet's characteristics.
