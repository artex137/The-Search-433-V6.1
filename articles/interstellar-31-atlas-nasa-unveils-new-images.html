NASA Unveils New Images of Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS

NASA has released new images of the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS, a comet likely older than our solar system, during a briefing in Greenbelt, Maryland, on Wednesday. The comet poses no threat to Earth, passing at a distance of 170 million miles.

The significance of 3I/ATLAS lies in its age and composition. Discovered in July 2023 by an ATLAS telescope in Chile, it is believed to have formed over eight billion years ago, making it older than the solar system, which is approximately 4.5 billion years old. NASA officials, including Amit Kshatriya and Nicola Fox, have monitored the comet due to its unique trajectory through the solar system.

Key facts

  • Object: 3I/ATLAS (interstellar comet)
  • First discovered: July 2023 in Chile
  • Closest approach to Earth: 170 million miles
  • Closest approach to Mars: 19 million miles (last month)
  • Age: Over 8 billion years
  • Previous interstellar objects: 1I/’Oumuamua (2017), 2I/Borisov (2019)

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first identified in July 2023 by an ATLAS telescope in Chile. NASA has since monitored its trajectory, noting its closest approach to the sun in October 2023. The comet is currently leaving the solar system, with its closest pass to Earth anticipated next month.

Official statements

“All evidence supports the conclusion that 3I/ATLAS is a natural object, not an alien spacecraft,” said University of Hawaii astronomer Larry Denneau.

Context

3I/ATLAS is only the third interstellar object ever observed, following 1I/’Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov. Previous studies of interstellar comets have enhanced understanding of the materials and processes that exist beyond our solar system.

What’s next

NASA will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS as it exits the solar system and plans to analyze further data from the comet's observations in the coming months.
