Telescopes record changes in the trajectory of interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS with new detailed images

Astronomers have released new images of the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, captured by the Telescópio Espacial Hubble on January 7, 2026. The images highlight a notable anti-tail jet, a structure formed by dust released in the opposite direction to the Sun. The comet continues its hyperbolic trajectory through the Solar System and is expected to make a final encounter with Jupiter in March 2026.

This discovery is significant as Comet 3I/ATLAS, discovered on July 1, 2025, by the ATLAS system, represents the third confirmed interstellar object. Its remote origin, possibly formed about 7 billion years ago around another star, offers a unique opportunity to study primitive materials in the universe. Observations from both ground-based and space-based telescopes reveal changes in the comet’s color and brightness, which scientists track to better understand its composition.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS was discovered on July 1, 2025.
  • First detailed images were captured by Hubble in January 2026.
  • The comet has an estimated diameter of 600 to 800 meters.
  • It is expected to encounter Jupiter in March 2026.
  • The comet travels at a speed of approximately 58 km/s.
  • Observations indicate a deviation of four arc seconds from its predicted trajectory.
  • Images show changes from reddish tones to deep blue in the comet’s color.

Timeline

On July 1, 2025, the ATLAS monitoring system detected Comet 3I/ATLAS as it entered the inner Solar System. Within weeks, astronomers confirmed its interstellar trajectory. The comet made a close approach to Earth on December 18, 2025, at a safe distance of millions of kilometers. Observations continued, leading to detailed imaging by the Hubble Space Telescope in January 2026.

Notable images from the Gemini North telescope in Hawaii were recorded on November 26, 2025, showing the nucleus surrounded by a luminous coma. The Europa Clipper probe captured an image from 164 million kilometers away on November 6, 2025, highlighting the separation of blue-green gas and red dust in the coma.

Official statements

“The changes in brightness and color of Comet 3I/ATLAS provide valuable insights into its composition and activity,” said an astronomer involved in the study.

Context

Comet 3I/ATLAS is the third cataloged interstellar object, following ʻOumuamua and 2I/Borisov. Unlike ʻOumuamua, which did not exhibit a coma, 3I/ATLAS behaves like a classic comet indicating evident activity. Comparatively, it has a greater estimated age and higher speed than 2I/Borisov. Each interstellar visitor offers a unique chemical signature from its original formation, contributing to the understanding of objects ejected from other stellar systems.

What’s next

Global observatories will maintain continuous monitoring of Comet 3I/ATLAS until it becomes indistinguishable. The data collected will serve as a reference for future interstellar detections and enhance models of planetary formation in exosystems.
