NASA's interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS carrying record methanol levels races Earth on December 19

NASA's interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS is approaching Earth for a closest approach on December 19, 2025, carrying unprecedented levels of methanol. The comet will come within approximately 1.8 astronomical units (AU), or 170 million miles, from Earth.

This discovery is significant as it provides insights into the building blocks of life. The presence of high concentrations of methanol suggests that the comet may contain molecular materials that predate the formation of our solar system. NASA scientists are monitoring the comet's trajectory and composition closely as it nears Earth.

Key facts

  • Closest approach on December 19, 2025, at 1.8 AU (170 million miles)
  • Comet carries record methanol levels, second highest ever observed
  • Traveling at approximately 137,000 mph
  • Discovered on July 1, 2025, by the ATLAS project
  • This is the third interstellar visitor ever detected

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was discovered on July 1, 2025, by the ATLAS project. Since its discovery, astronomers have studied its chemical composition using the Atacama Large Millimeter Array (ALMA). The comet has been found to contain the highest concentrations of methanol and hydrogen cyanide (HCN) ever detected in a comet.

The comet’s closest approach to Earth is set for December 19, 2025, where it will pass safely at a distance of 1.8 AU.

Official statements

“The unusual composition and extreme chemical richness of 3I/ATLAS make it an important object for understanding the origins of life,” said a NASA spokesperson.

Context

3I/ATLAS is notable for being the second most methanol-rich comet on record, only surpassed by C/2016 R2. The study of such comets helps scientists learn about the conditions of the early solar system and the materials that may have contributed to the emergence of life on Earth.

What’s next

NASA scientists will continue to observe the comet as it approaches Earth. They will analyze the data collected to better understand the implications of its chemical composition for astrobiology and the history of our solar system.
