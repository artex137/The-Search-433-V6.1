The comet Borisov misidentified as 3I/ATLAS, skims past Earth on November 11

A new comet, named C/2025 V1 (Borisov), will pass about 103 million kilometres from Earth on Tuesday, November 11. The celestial object, discovered by Ukrainian astronomer Gennadiy Borisov, poses no threat to the planet and is expected to reach its closest point on that date.

This sighting is significant as it provides astronomers with an opportunity to study a comet that follows a near-interstellar orbit but is confirmed to be solar in origin. Authorities, including various observatories, are preparing to track the comet’s movement to gather data that could enhance understanding of icy bodies in outer space.

Key facts

  • Comet Name: C/2025 V1 (Borisov)
  • Discovered by: Gennadiy Borisov on November 2
  • Closest Approach: November 11, 2023
  • Distance from Earth: 103 million kilometres (about 270 times the distance to the Moon)
  • Apparent Magnitude: 13.8 (too faint for naked-eye observation)
  • Previous Discovery: Borisov also discovered the interstellar comet 2I/Borisov in 2019

Timeline

On November 2, 2023, Gennadiy Borisov discovered the comet using his telescope. Initial speculation linked C/2025 V1 to the interstellar-like comet 3I/ATLAS, but researchers later confirmed that the two objects are not connected. The comet will pass Earth on November 11, 2023, at a safe distance.

Official statements

“C/2025 V1 likely formed in the solar system’s distant outer regions,” researchers stated, explaining that its elongated path resembles those of interstellar travelers.

Context

This discovery follows Gennadiy Borisov's previous identification of the interstellar comet 2I/Borisov in 2019, which was notable for being the first observed interstellar comet. C/2025 V1 adds to the growing interest in comets and their origins within our solar system.

What’s next

Observatories will continue to monitor C/2025 V1's trajectory, with hopes that data collected will provide insights into the characteristics of icy bodies in the outer solar system. Future missions may aim to study such comets in greater detail.
