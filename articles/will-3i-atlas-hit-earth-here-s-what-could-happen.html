Will 3I/ATLAS Hit Earth? Here's What Could Happen

Astronomers and space agencies are monitoring 3I/ATLAS, a rare interstellar object discovered on 1 July 2025 by the Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System (ATLAS) telescope in Chile. NASA has confirmed that 3I/ATLAS poses no immediate threat to Earth, but its unusual trajectory has raised concerns among scientists about potential impact scenarios.

This situation is significant as it highlights the importance of cosmic risk assessment. Although current data indicate no collision risk, 3I/ATLAS has been added to the global planetary-defence monitoring list, allowing researchers to use it as a test case for future threats.

Key facts

  • Discovered: 1 July 2025
  • Closest approach to Earth: Approximately 1.8 astronomical units (∼270 million km)
  • Closest point to the Sun: Scheduled for 30 October 2025 at about 1.4 AU (∼210 million km)
  • Estimated diameter: Between 440 metres and 5.6 kilometres
  • Classification: Comet, not an asteroid

Timeline

On 1 July 2025, the ATLAS telescope in Chile discovered 3I/ATLAS. Since then, NASA and other space agencies have monitored the object to assess its trajectory and potential risks. As of now, 3I/ATLAS will approach no closer than 1.8 astronomical units from Earth, with its closest point to the Sun expected around 30 October 2025.

Researchers have noted that although 3I/ATLAS has a hyperbolic trajectory indicating it came from outside the Solar System, there is no data supporting a collision risk with Earth.

Official statements

“Though 3I/ATLAS is not on a collision path, it serves as a reminder of what cosmic objects can do and why preparedness matters,” NASA stated regarding the monitoring of the object.

Context

NASA classifies 3I/ATLAS as a comet due to its activity, which includes a coma—a cloud of gas and dust. Comets differ from asteroids, which are mainly rocky and do not exhibit such outgassing. The presence of ice and the development of a dust shell as it approaches the Sun further substantiate its classification as a comet.

Prior to 3I/ATLAS, other interstellar objects have been observed, but this particular object provides a unique opportunity for scientific discovery and planetary defence preparation.

What’s next

NASA and other agencies will continue to observe 3I/ATLAS to gather data that may improve forecasting models for potential future threats. The ongoing monitoring is crucial for understanding cosmic risks and enhancing early-warning systems.
