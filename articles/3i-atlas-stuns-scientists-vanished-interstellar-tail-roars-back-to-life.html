3I/ATLAS Stuns Scientists: Vanished Interstellar Tail Roars Back to Life

Scientists have observed a dramatic resurgence of the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, which has reappeared with a vibrant tail after seemingly vanishing following its pass near the Sun. The new images were captured on November 10 at 11:31 p.m. EDT (04:31 GMT on November 11) by the Virtual Telescope Project in Manciano, Italy.

This resurgence is significant as it offers insights into the behavior and composition of interstellar objects. Researchers are analyzing the new observations to better understand the comet's activity and its implications for the study of interstellar visitors.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS reappeared with a visible tail after previously losing it.
  • New images taken on November 10, 2023, at 11:31 p.m. EDT (04:31 GMT on November 11).
  • Photograph captured by the Virtual Telescope Project in Manciano, Italy.
  • Comet was observed at a low elevation of 14 degrees above the horizon.
  • Observation conditions included a 61 per cent illuminated Moon.
  • 3I/ATLAS is the third confirmed interstellar object detected in our solar system.
  • Next significant encounter with Jupiter projected for March 2026.

Timeline

3I/ATLAS, initially detected in 2019, passed near the Sun earlier in 2023. During this approach, astronomers noted the comet appeared to lose its tail, which led to confusion and curiosity within the scientific community. After a period of inactivity, new observations on November 10 showed that the comet's tail had not only reappeared but had also become longer and more structured.

The successful imaging of the comet's tail came after favorable weather conditions allowed for clearer observations. The Virtual Telescope Project utilized robotic telescopes to capture the new images, showcasing a significant development in the comet's behavior.

Official statements

“Exploiting the unusual good weather of this season, we imaged the interstellar comet again, recording a much more developed ion tail,” said Gianluca Masi, founder of the Virtual Telescope Project.
“If confirmed, it will be the third known interstellar object... providing more evidence that such interstellar wanderers are relatively common in our galaxy,” stated Mark Norris, Senior Lecturer in Astronomy at the University of Central Lancashire.

Context

3I/ATLAS is only the third confirmed interstellar object to be detected in our solar system, following 1I/'Oumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019. Each of these interstellar visitors offers a unique opportunity to study materials from beyond our solar system, contributing to our understanding of such objects.

The comet's activity and its subsequent disappearance raised questions about the processes involved in the sublimation of its materials, highlighting the complexities of interstellar bodies.

What’s next

Both NASA and the European Space Agency (ESA) will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS as it approaches Jupiter in March 2026, providing scientists with ongoing opportunities to study its behavior and gather valuable data about interstellar comets.
