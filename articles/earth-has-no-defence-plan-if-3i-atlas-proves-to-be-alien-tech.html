Earth Has No Defence Plan if 3I/ATLAS Proves To Be Alien Tech

The interstellar object 3I/ATLAS is set to make its closest approach to Earth on 19 December 2023, according to Harvard physicist Avi Loeb. Loeb has warned that if 3I/ATLAS turns out to be alien technology, humanity lacks a protocol for such an encounter.

This situation is significant because it raises concerns about humanity's preparedness for potential risks posed by advanced technological objects from beyond Earth. Loeb has highlighted that the current institutional gap could leave humanity vulnerable in the event of an unexpected contact with 3I/ATLAS.

Key facts

  • Closest approach of 3I/ATLAS is on 19 December 2023.
  • Avi Loeb is a physicist at Harvard University.
  • The object exhibits anomalous behaviours not typical of natural comets.
  • Loeb has noted the lack of a response protocol for extraterrestrial technology.
  • Loeb predicts that a potential crisis would prompt investment in defence systems.

Timeline

3I/ATLAS has been observed since its discovery, but its upcoming close approach on 19 December has intensified scrutiny. Loeb has outlined concerns over its unusual orbital mechanics, suggesting a non-natural origin. The scientific community has begun preparing observation tools to gather more information about the object’s composition and trajectory.

Official statements

“We do not have a response protocol for extraterrestrial technology, but after the first encounter – if we survive – there will be political will to invest trillions of dollars in an alert system of interceptors to take close-up photographs of anomalous interstellar objects.”
“Let's hope we don't receive unwanted gifts for the holidays.”

Context

Loeb's warnings highlight a broader issue regarding humanity's readiness to confront the possibility of encountering alien life. The discussion about the potential for intelligent life in the Milky Way has intensified, given the vast number of planets that may possess Earth-like conditions.

What’s next

Astronomers will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS's trajectory and prepare for its close approach on 19 December. They aim to gather data that could provide clarity on the object's true nature and origin.
