Interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS packed with alcohol stuns astronomers

Astronomers have discovered high levels of methanol in the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS as it passes through our Solar System, marking a significant finding in cosmic chemistry. The observations were made using the Atacama Large Millimetre/submillimeter Array (ALMA) in Chile in late 2025, revealing that 3I/ATLAS has a methanol-to-hydrogen cyanide (HCN) ratio much higher than typically found in Solar System comets.

This discovery is important because it provides insight into the chemical conditions of other solar systems, potentially altering our understanding of planet formation. Researchers have stated that this comet's unique composition might serve as a rare opportunity to study alien planetary chemistry.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS is only the third confirmed interstellar visitor.
  • ALMA detected methanol levels with a methanol-to-HCN ratio of approximately 70 and 120.
  • The observations took place over several days in late 2025.
  • Nathan Roth is the lead researcher and a professor at American University.
  • The comet has shown unusual methanol release patterns, differing from Solar System comets.

Timeline

In late 2025, astronomers using ALMA observed comet 3I/ATLAS as it approached the Sun. The comet released gas and dust, creating a glowing halo or coma around its nucleus. Through analysis of this coma, researchers detected unexpectedly high levels of methanol, revealing a distinct chemical profile compared to typical comets from our Solar System.

Previous observations with the James Webb Space Telescope indicated unusual carbon dioxide dominance at larger distances from the Sun, setting the stage for the more recent findings about methanol.

Official statements

“It’s like taking a fingerprint from another solar system,” said Nathan Roth, highlighting the significance of the comet's composition.

Context

Comet 3I/ATLAS follows in the footsteps of other interstellar visitors, such as 1I/‘Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov, which have also generated significant scientific interest. Each of these comets provides valuable insights into the formation of planets and comets beyond our Solar System. The presence of methanol in 3I/ATLAS suggests that different star systems may produce a diverse array of chemical compositions.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to analyze data from 3I/ATLAS to refine models of comet chemistry and planetary formation. Future studies may further explore the implications of this comet's unique chemical makeup on our understanding of the universe.
