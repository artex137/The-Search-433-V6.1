Gigantic Comet Could Pass Through Our Solar System This Fall, Sparking Wild Theories

Published 2025-08-25 20:42 UTC

Colossal Comet 3I/ATLAS to Pass Through Solar System in October

A colossal interstellar object, designated as 3I/ATLAS, is set to make a close approach to Mars on October 3, 2023. Originally known as “A11pl3Z,” this comet has raised concerns among scientists due to its potential size and trajectory. Observations indicate that 3I/ATLAS has a diameter estimated at approximately 5.6 kilometers, though earlier estimates suggested it could be as large as 20 kilometers.

The significance of 3I/ATLAS lies in its proximity to Mars and the potential implications of its trajectory. Scientists are monitoring its path closely, as a collision with any planetary body could result in catastrophic consequences. The object is expected to travel through the solar system at a speed of about 209,000 kilometers per hour.

Key facts

  • Object Name: 3I/ATLAS
  • Diameter: Estimated at 5.6 kilometers
  • Close Approach to Mars: October 3, 2023, at a distance of 0.19 astronomical units (AU)
  • Speed: Approximately 209,000 kilometers per hour
  • Closest Approach to Sun: October 30, 2023
  • Closest Approach to Earth: December 19, 2023, at approximately 1.8 AU

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first observed and designated as A11pl3Z before receiving its current name. Initial estimates suggested a much larger size, but subsequent observations have narrowed its diameter to around 5.6 kilometers. As it approaches Mars on October 3, the object will travel at an unprecedented speed, raising alarms among scientists about its potential impact.

Following its close encounter with Mars, 3I/ATLAS will reach its closest point to the Sun on October 30, and then approach Earth on December 19, 2023.

Official statements

“If this thing actually hit Mars it would be a cataclysm unlike anything that any of us have ever seen,” said Harvard astrophysicist Avi Loeb. “The trajectory of 3I/ATLAS will take it so close to Mars, Venus, and Jupiter, which raises questions about its nature.”

Context

The trajectory of 3I/ATLAS has drawn comparisons to past near-Earth objects, which have also raised concerns about potential collisions. The unique path of 3I/ATLAS, coming from the center of the Milky Way, is statistically improbable, prompting discussions about its origin and nature.

What’s next

Scientists will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS as it approaches Mars and Earth. Observations will aim to confirm its size and trajectory, as well as to assess any potential risks associated with its passage through the solar system.
