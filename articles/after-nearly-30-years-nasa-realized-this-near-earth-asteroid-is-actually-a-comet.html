After nearly 30 years, NASA realized this near-Earth asteroid is actually a comet

Published 2026-07-22 19:17 UTC

NASA Identifies Near-Earth Asteroid as Comet After Nearly 30 Years

A near-Earth object previously classified as an asteroid has been confirmed as a comet by NASA after nearly three decades of observation. The object, known as 1998 SH2, was reclassified following a close pass about 3 million kilometers from Earth in August 2025, revealing its active cometary nature.

This discovery is significant for planetary defense, as it highlights the importance of accurately identifying near-Earth objects. Understanding the differences between asteroids and comets can improve predictions of their orbits and potential risks to Earth, according to NASA officials.

Key facts

  • Object originally discovered in 1998 as 1998 SH2.
  • Close pass from Earth occurred in August 2025, approximately 3 million kilometers away.
  • Reclassification to P/1998 SH2 took place after analysis of its motion.
  • Study published on July 10 in the journal Nature Astronomy.
  • Research led by Davide Farnocchia from NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory.

Timeline

The object was discovered in 1998 and initially classified as an asteroid. For nearly 30 years, it followed a predictable orbit around the Sun, showing no signs of cometary activity.

In August 2025, during a close approach, researchers using NASA's Deep Space Network detected irregularities in the object's position, prompting further investigation.

After analyzing its motion, scientists identified nongravitational forces consistent with gas jets escaping from the surface, leading to the conclusion that it is an active comet. Observations with the European Southern Observatory's Very Large Telescope confirmed the presence of a comet tail.

Official statements

"After we measured the nongravitational perturbations affecting the motion of 1998 SH2 and recognized they weren't compatible with the object being an asteroid, we suspected the object could be an active comet," said Davide Farnocchia, lead author of the study.
"This work shows the importance of continuously tracking near-Earth objects," Farnocchia added. "Because of outgassing, the motion of comets is more significantly perturbed than that of asteroids."

Context

The identification of 1998 SH2 as a comet underscores the challenges in classifying near-Earth objects. Many comets may remain hidden due to their lack of visible tails or comas, leading to potential misidentification as asteroids.

As the population of near-Earth objects continues to grow, the findings from this study suggest that more comets may be uncovered through precise tracking of their movements.

What’s next

Researchers plan to continue monitoring near-Earth objects to detect subtle changes in their orbits, which may indicate hidden cometary activity. This ongoing analysis could enhance understanding of potential impact risks to Earth.
