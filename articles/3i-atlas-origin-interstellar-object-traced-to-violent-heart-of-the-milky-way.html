3I/ATLAS Origin: Interstellar Object Traced To Violent Heart Of The Milky Way

The interstellar object known as 3I/ATLAS has been traced back to the constellation of Sagittarius, revealing its origins in the chaotic environment of the Milky Way. Discovered on 1 July 2025 by the ATLAS survey in Río Hurtado, Chile, 3I/ATLAS has captivated astronomers with its unique characteristics and trajectory.

This discovery is significant in understanding the nature of interstellar objects and their origins. Researchers contend that studying 3I/ATLAS can provide insights into the evolution of planetary systems and the conditions under which these objects form. The findings have prompted agencies like NASA and the Jet Propulsion Laboratory to emphasize the importance of context in studying such cosmic visitors.

Key facts

  • Object Name: 3I/ATLAS
  • Date of Discovery: 1 July 2025
  • Location of Discovery: Río Hurtado, Chile
  • Origin: Constellation of Sagittarius, approximately 26,000 light-years away
  • Closest Approach to Earth: 19 December 2025, at a distance of 168 million miles
  • Velocity: 58 km/s (approximately 130,000 mph)
  • Estimated Diameter: Between 440 metres and 3.5 miles

Timeline

On 1 July 2025, 3I/ATLAS was discovered by the ATLAS survey in Chile. Following this, astronomers traced its origin to the constellation of Sagittarius, highlighting the intense and violent conditions from which it emerged. On 19 December 2025, the object made its closest approach to Earth, allowing for further observations.

Data from the Hubble Space Telescope has since refined estimates regarding the object's size and composition, revealing unique characteristics that set it apart from typical comets and asteroids found in our solar system.

Official statements

“The context of an object's origin is a critical piece of the puzzle,” said a spokesperson from NASA. “The anomalies we see are not random errors; they are the consistent, persistent scars of an ancient, violent journey.”
“We cannot expect visitors from these chaotic regions to resemble the well-behaved comets and asteroids of our own Kuiper Belt,” noted Avi Loeb, a prominent researcher in the field.

Context

Previous studies of interstellar objects have provided insights into the diverse environments from which they originate. Objects like 3I/ATLAS, which come from the Galactic Centre, face conditions that can profoundly alter their composition and behaviour. This highlights the need for a nuanced understanding of interstellar visitors.

The study of 3I/ATLAS also raises concerns regarding the potential unpredictability of future interstellar objects, which may enter our solar system at high velocities and with unusual characteristics.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to analyze data from 3I/ATLAS to uncover more about its origins and characteristics. This ongoing study aims to enhance our understanding of planetary system evolution and prepare for future interstellar objects that may arrive with even less predictability.
