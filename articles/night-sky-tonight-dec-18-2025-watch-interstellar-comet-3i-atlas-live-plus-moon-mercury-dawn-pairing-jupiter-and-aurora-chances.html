Published 2025-12-18 10:35 UTC

Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS Approaches Earth on December 18, 2025

On December 18, 2025, the interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS makes its closest approach to Earth, providing a unique opportunity for skywatchers to view the comet via a free livestream starting at 11 p.m. EST (0400 GMT). The event coincides with a thin crescent Moon positioned near Mercury, visible in the pre-dawn sky, along with Jupiter and Saturn, as well as a forecast for potential auroras.

This event is significant as Comet 3I/ATLAS is only the third confirmed interstellar object to pass through our solar system, following 'Oumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019. According to NASA, the comet is currently approximately 167–168 million miles (about 269–270 million kilometers) from Earth, posing no danger to our planet.

Key facts

  • Livestream start: 11 p.m. EST (0400 GMT) on December 18
  • Closest approach: 1 a.m. EST (0600 GMT) on December 19
  • Comet distance: Approximately 167–168 million miles (about 269–270 million kilometers)
  • Comet size: Estimated between 440 meters and 5.6 kilometers
  • Aurora forecast: G1 (minor) geomagnetic storm expected on December 18
  • Key viewing time for Moon–Mercury pairing: 30–60 minutes before sunrise

Timeline

Throughout the evening of December 18, Jupiter rises shortly after sunset, dominating the eastern sky. Saturn is visible in the west early in the night. As dawn approaches, a very thin waning crescent Moon appears low near Antares, with Mercury positioned nearby. The livestream of Comet 3I/ATLAS begins at 11 p.m. EST, allowing viewers to observe the comet until its closest approach at 1 a.m. EST on December 19.

Official statements

“This is a rare opportunity to view an interstellar comet making its closest approach to Earth. We encourage everyone to tune in to the livestream,” said a representative from the Virtual Telescope Project, which will host the event.

Context

Interstellar comets like 3I/ATLAS provide valuable insights into the formation and evolution of planetary systems beyond our own. The previous interstellar objects, 'Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov, have sparked significant scientific interest, leading to increased observation efforts for future interstellar visitors.

What’s next

Following the closest approach, Comet 3I/ATLAS will continue its trajectory out of the solar system, fading from view. Astronomers will analyze data collected during its pass to enhance understanding of interstellar objects.
