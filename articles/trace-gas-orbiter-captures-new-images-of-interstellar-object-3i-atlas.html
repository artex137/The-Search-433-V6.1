Trace Gas Orbiter Captures New Images of Interstellar Object 3I/ATLAS

On October 3, 2025, the European Space Agency's ExoMars Trace Gas Orbiter (TGO) captured new images of the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS during its closest approach to Mars, located 30 million km away. The images were taken using the Colour and Stereo Surface Imaging System (CaSSIS) on board TGO.

This observation is significant as it provides valuable data on 3I/ATLAS, a comet that has intrigued scientists since its discovery. Authorities at ESA plan to conduct further observations of the comet using the Jupiter Icy Moons Explorer (Juice) between November 2 and 25, 2025.

Key facts

  • Date of closest approach: October 3, 2025
  • Distance from TGO to 3I/ATLAS: 30 million km
  • Instrument used: Colour and Stereo Surface Imaging System (CaSSIS)
  • Resolution of CaSSIS camera: 11.36 micro-radians (2.34 arcseconds) per pixel
  • Estimated nucleus diameter: 5 km to 46 km
  • Next observations: November 2-25, 2025, using Juice
  • Data from Juice expected: February 2026

Timeline

On October 3, 2025, TGO successfully captured images of 3I/ATLAS during its closest approach to Mars.

Between November 2 and 25, 2025, ESA plans to use Juice to observe 3I/ATLAS, aiming to gather additional data as the comet is expected to be in a more active state following its closest approach to the Sun.

Official statements

“This was a very challenging observation for the instrument,” said Dr. Nick Thomas, principal investigator of CaSSIS at ESA.
“Though our Mars orbiters continue to make impressive contributions to Mars science, it’s always extra exciting to see them responding to unexpected situations like this one,” said Dr. Colin Wilson, Mars Express and ExoMars project scientist at ESA.

Context

The observations of 3I/ATLAS mark an important event in the ongoing study of interstellar objects. Previous missions, like those using Hubble, have provided insights into the characteristics of such comets, but the images from TGO offer a unique perspective from Mars orbit.

What’s next

ESA researchers will analyze the data from the images captured by TGO and anticipate further insights following the upcoming observations with Juice, expected to yield data in February 2026.
