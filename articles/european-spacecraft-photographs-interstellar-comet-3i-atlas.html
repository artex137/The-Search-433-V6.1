European spacecraft photographs interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS

The European Space Agency (ESA) has released an image of the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS taken by the JUICE spacecraft. The photograph was captured on November 6, 2025, when JUICE was approximately 66 million kilometres away from the comet.

This event is significant as it marks one of the first detailed observations of an interstellar comet by a spacecraft. ESA has stated that the data collected will enhance the understanding of comets and their behaviour, contributing to ongoing research in planetary science.

Key facts

  • Photograph taken on November 6, 2025.
  • JUICE spacecraft was about 66 million kilometres from comet 3I/ATLAS.
  • The spacecraft took a total of 120 images of the comet.
  • Images transmitted to Earth took several months due to the Sun's position.
  • JUICE was launched in 2023 to study Jupiter’s icy moons.

Timeline

JUICE was launched in 2023 on a trajectory involving multiple gravitational maneuvers near Venus and Earth. This trajectory kept JUICE in the inner Solar System, allowing it to photograph 3I/ATLAS.

On November 6, 2025, JUICE captured images of the comet just after it passed the perihelion of its orbit. The spacecraft transmitted these images back to Earth over several months, primarily using a weaker medium-gain antenna due to the Sun's interference.

Official statements

“The photograph clearly shows the bright cloud of dust and gas surrounding the comet’s nucleus, called the coma,” ESA stated. “In addition to taking photographs, JUICE also studied the comet using its instruments.”

Context

3I/ATLAS is notable as a visitor from interstellar space, arriving from beyond the Solar System. Its behaviour aligns with that of typical comets, which provides valuable insights into the nature of such celestial objects.

This mission continues the legacy of space exploration, building on previous studies of comets and enhancing the understanding of their roles in the Solar System.

What’s next

Scientists are currently analyzing the data collected by JUICE. Further research will focus on understanding the comet's composition and behaviour as more information becomes available.
