Interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS displays unusually straight tail in Japanese images

On November 22, 2025, Japanese astronomer Mitsunori Tsumura captured images of interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS using a 0.5-meter telescope. The photographs reveal an unusual straight tail of ions and dust, differing from typical comet behavior. No injuries or fatalities have been reported.

The findings are significant as 3I/ATLAS is the third confirmed interstellar comet. Its unusual tail structure raises questions about the dynamics of such objects in space. Authorities, including NASA, are closely monitoring the comet's trajectory and behavior as it approaches Jupiter in 2026.

Key facts

  • Date of observation: November 22, 2025
  • Location of observation: Japan
  • Telescope size: 0.5 meters
  • Maximum diameter estimate of comet: 5.6 km
  • Closest approach to Jupiter: 53.5 million km
  • Previous perihelion passage: October 2025 at 1.4 AU from the Sun
  • Monitoring telescopes located in Japan, Chile, and Hawaii

Timeline

In October 2025, 3I/ATLAS passed perihelion at a distance of 1.4 AU from the Sun. Observations conducted by various missions, including Lucy and Psyche, gathered data during the comet's approach to Mars. On November 22, Tsumura captured images showing the comet's straight and narrow tail.

NASA has indicated that the comet will experience non-gravitational acceleration, adjusting its orbit to approach Jupiter in 2026. The predicted distance of closest approach is 53.5 million km, with a margin of error of a few thousand kilometers.

Official statements

“The tail remains straight even in a hyperbolic trajectory, which is not typical for comets,” said Mitsunori Tsumura regarding his observations.
“The structural rigidity of 3I/ATLAS throughout the observed range is remarkable,” stated a NASA representative.

Context

Previous interstellar comets, such as 2I/Borisov, have exhibited curved and dispersed tails. The behavior of 3I/ATLAS challenges existing understanding of comet dynamics and their interactions with solar wind.

The symmetrical brightness of the coma and the compact nucleus without a diffuse halo of dust further distinguish 3I/ATLAS from traditional comets found in the Solar System.

What’s next

Ground-based observatories will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS as it moves away from the Sun. New images are expected in the coming weeks to further analyze its trajectory and structural characteristics.
