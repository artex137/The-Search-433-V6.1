Interstellar Comet 3I/Atlas Unleashes Massive X-Ray Glow Stretching 250,000 Miles Through Space

The interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS has emitted a powerful X-ray glow, measured at approximately 400,000 kilometers long, during observations from two advanced space telescopes between November 26 and December 3, 2025. No injuries or fatalities have been reported in connection with this cosmic event.

This discovery is significant as it marks the first time an interstellar comet has been detected emitting X-ray radiation. Scientists are utilizing this data to better understand the composition and behavior of deep space objects, particularly those formed around other stars. The findings could reshape our understanding of how interstellar comets differ from those in our solar system.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS was first identified in 2023.
  • The comet is the third confirmed interstellar object, following 1I/ʻOumuamua and 2I/Borisov.
  • Observations were conducted by Japan’s XRISM and ESA’s XMM-Newton, totaling over 37 hours.
  • The X-ray emissions extend approximately 250,000 miles, about the distance from Earth to the Moon.
  • 3I/ATLAS will make its closest approach to Earth on December 19, 2025.
  • No spacecraft is currently positioned to intercept the comet.

Timeline

On November 26, 2025, Japan’s XRISM began observing 3I/ATLAS for over 17 hours, capturing a halo of X-ray emissions. Following this, on December 3, ESA’s XMM-Newton conducted a 20-hour observation, revealing a vivid red glow at the comet’s nucleus.

The observations indicated that the X-ray glow originated not from the comet itself but from a charge exchange process involving solar wind ions and gases released from the comet's nucleus. This interaction allows for the detection of elements typically invisible to traditional telescopes.

Official statements

“This data reveals gases otherwise almost invisible to optical and ultraviolet instruments,” said an ESA official, highlighting the significance of the observed emissions.

Context

Previous interstellar visitors, such as 1I/ʻOumuamua and 2I/Borisov, posed observational challenges. 1I/ʻOumuamua lacked a visible coma, while 2I/Borisov passed quickly, limiting data collection opportunities. In contrast, 3I/ATLAS offers a sustained look at its composition and behavior, providing researchers with valuable insights.

What’s next

As 3I/ATLAS approaches its closest point to Earth, observatories worldwide are racing to gather as much data as possible across various wavelengths. This research will help scientists understand the comet's composition and dynamics, potentially serving as a template for future interstellar studies.
