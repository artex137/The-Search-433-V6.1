Scientists confirm mysterious interstellar visitor is not an alien craft

Astronomers have captured new images of the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, dispelling theories that it is an alien spacecraft. The comet, first detected in 2020, is currently racing through the solar system at approximately 221,000 km/h. The latest findings were announced following multi-colored images taken on August 27 at the Gemini South Observatory in Chile.

This discovery is significant as it provides a clear, natural explanation for the comet's unusual brightness, which had previously led to speculation about its origins. The findings prompt scientists to further examine the characteristics of 3I/ATLAS and its composition, offering insights into the formation of comets around other stars.

Key facts

  • Object: 3I/ATLAS, an interstellar comet
  • Speed: 221,000 km/h
  • First detected: 2020
  • Images taken: August 27, 2025
  • Location of observations: Gemini South Observatory, Chile

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first detected in 2020 as it entered the solar system. Since then, astronomers have monitored its trajectory and composition. On August 27, 2025, new images taken from the Gemini South Observatory revealed the comet's tail and coma, confirming its identity as a comet.

Before this confirmation, speculation regarding its nature included theories that it could be an artificial spacecraft. The new images have effectively put these theories to rest.

Official statements

“These new images very clearly demonstrate that 3I/ATLAS is a comet,” said Dr. Mark Norris of the University of Lancashire. “As 3I/ATLAS approaches the Sun, it is being bathed in more and more sunlight, which increases the amount of material outgassing from the comet.”
“Little green men certainly aren’t responsible,” said Dr. Matthew Genge, a planetary scientist at Imperial College London.
“Every interstellar comet is a messenger from another star system,” said Bryce Bolin, a research scientist with Eureka Scientific.

Context

The speculation surrounding 3I/ATLAS has drawn comparisons to other interstellar objects, such as ‘Oumuamua, which was detected in 2017. The unusual characteristics of such objects often lead to theories about extraterrestrial origins, but recent findings have provided more concrete scientific explanations.

Understanding the composition of comets like 3I/ATLAS helps researchers learn about the formation of celestial bodies in different star systems, contributing to the broader field of planetary science.

What’s next

Scientists will continue to study 3I/ATLAS to better understand its chemical composition and the processes involved in its formation. Further observations may yield additional insights into the characteristics of interstellar objects.
