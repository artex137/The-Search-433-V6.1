3I/ATLAS: Interstellar Comet to Fly by Mars in October 2025

The interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS will pass by Mars on October 3, 2025, providing scientists a unique opportunity to study material that may be older than the solar system. No injuries or fatalities have been reported related to this event.

The European Space Agency (ESA), NASA, and other agencies are preparing for the comet’s approach, which could yield insights into planetary systems that predate our own. Harvard astrophysicist Avi Loeb has also raised the possibility that 3I/ATLAS may represent alien technology.

Key facts

  • Event: Flyby of 3I/ATLAS
  • Date: October 3, 2025
  • Distance from Mars: Approximately 30 million kilometers
  • Closest approach to Earth: Around 270 million kilometers in December 2025
  • Instruments used: HRSC, CaSSIS, NOMAD, OMEGA, SPICAM
  • Discovery date: July 1, 2025

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was discovered on July 1, 2025, by the Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System (ATLAS). It is only the third known interstellar object to pass through our solar system, following 1I/ʻOumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019.

As it approaches the sun, 3I/ATLAS will shed gas and dust, forming a tail and coma. This material may contain organic compounds and water ice from outside the solar system. The flyby will allow for close observation, making it an important event for planetary science.

Official statements

“3I/ATLAS offers a once-in-a-lifetime chance to study interstellar materials,” said an ESA spokesperson, highlighting the significance of the comet’s passage.

Context

Interstellar visitors like 3I/ATLAS are rare. Many may pass unnoticed, making this event a crucial opportunity for researchers. The Vera Rubin Observatory, now operational, may help detect more such objects in the future.

Scientists have compared 3I/ATLAS to a cosmic refrigerator from billions of years ago, now opening to reveal its preserved contents. Most researchers believe it is an interstellar comet, and there is no cause for concern regarding its approach.

What’s next

Agencies will continue preparations for the flyby and data collection. Observations from Mars orbiters and other spacecraft will provide valuable information about 3I/ATLAS and its potential implications for understanding the early universe.
