Astronomers left disappointed as green comet meets fiery end before it can be seen

A newly discovered comet, Comet SWAN, has likely met a fiery end during its close encounter with the sun, disappointing astronomers hoping for a vibrant celestial display. The comet, which originated from the Oort Cloud beyond Pluto, is believed to have fragmented as it swung past the sun, diminishing its brightness and eliminating the possibility of naked-eye visibility.

This event matters to astronomers and space enthusiasts who eagerly anticipated the comet’s appearance. The loss of Comet SWAN means that observers will miss out on a rare opportunity to view a celestial object that may have been on its first journey through the inner solar system.

Key facts

  • Comet SWAN was discovered by amateur astronomers.
  • The comet originated from the Oort Cloud, far beyond Pluto.
  • Experts believe it fragmented during its close encounter with the sun.
  • Astrophysicist Karl Battams stated, “We'll soon be left with just a dusty rubble pile.”
  • The comet was designated C/2025 F2.
  • Comet SWAN was not expected to swing close to Earth.

Timeline

Comet SWAN was discovered by amateur astronomers using images from a NASA and European Space Agency spacecraft. As it approached the sun, experts noted its distinctive tail and potential for visibility. However, during the close encounter, the comet likely broke apart, leading to its rapid loss of brightness.

Following its flyby, the remnants of Comet SWAN are expected to disappear into the outer reaches of the solar system, beyond its point of origin.

Official statements

“It’s going to go so far out that we have no idea if it’s ever going to return,” said Karl Battams, astrophysicist with the U.S. Naval Research Laboratory.
“These are relics from when the solar system first formed,” said Jason Ybarra, director of the West Virginia University Planetarium and Observatory.

Context

Comet SWAN is part of a long history of comet sightings, including notable flybys such as Tsuchinshan-Atlas last year, Neowise in 2020, and Hale-Bopp and Hyakutake in the 1990s. Each of these comets provided unique viewing experiences for astronomers and the public alike.

What’s next

There are no plans for further investigations into Comet SWAN, as its remnants will likely be lost to the outer solar system. Astronomers will continue to monitor other comets and celestial events in the future.
