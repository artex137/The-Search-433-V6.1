Jupiter Mission Captures Rare Shot of Interstellar Comet 3I/Atlas on Its Way Out

The European Space Agency’s (ESA) Jupiter mission has captured detailed images of interstellar comet 3I/Atlas as it drifts away from the Sun. The spacecraft JUICE, which stands for JUpiter ICy moons Explorer, observed the comet on November 6, 2025, just a week after its closest approach to the Sun on October 29, 2025. There are no reported injuries or fatalities related to this event.

This observation is significant because it allows scientists to study the composition of a comet originating from outside our solar system. Interstellar comets are rare, and this mission provides a unique opportunity to gather data on celestial objects that may enhance our understanding of other star systems. ESA has directed five instruments aboard JUICE to analyze the comet, and the findings will be discussed in late March.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/Atlas was first spotted in June 2025.
  • Closest approach to the Sun occurred on October 29, 2025.
  • JUICE captured 120 images of the comet on November 6, 2025.
  • ESA confirmed the comet's interstellar origin through the Minor Planet Center.
  • JUICE launched on April 14, 2023, and is scheduled to reach Jupiter in 2031.

Timeline

In June 2025, astronomers detected comet 3I/Atlas using data from the Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System (ATLAS). The International Astronomical Union confirmed its interstellar origin shortly thereafter. The comet passed its closest approach to the Sun on October 29, 2025, and JUICE captured images of the comet on November 6, 2025. These images revealed the comet's nucleus surrounded by a bright halo of gas, known as the coma, along with its elongated tail.

Official statements

“The images we have obtained offer tantalizing clues about the behavior and composition of this interstellar object,” said a representative from ESA.

Context

Interstellar comets are a rare phenomenon, with 3I/Atlas being the third discovered. Previous discoveries have provided insights into the materials and conditions present in other solar systems, contributing to our understanding of the cosmos.

What’s next

ESA scientists are currently analyzing the data collected by JUICE, with plans to present their findings in late March 2026.
