Observations of the Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS with the PRL 1.2m telescope at Mount Abu

Scientists from the Physical Research Laboratory (PRL) have observed the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS at the PRL’s 1.2m telescope in Mount Abu, India, from November 12 to 15, 2025. There have been no reported injuries or fatalities linked to these observations.

These observations are significant as they provide insights into the comet's activity as it travels out of the inner Solar System after its perihelion passage. Authorities have indicated that further observations will continue as the comet moves into darker parts of the night sky.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS observed from November 12-15, 2025.
  • Observations conducted using the PRL 1.2m telescope at Mount Abu.
  • Comet's production rate measured at approximately 1025 molecules/sec.
  • Observations included imaging and spectroscopic analysis.
  • Located at an altitude of 1680 m near Gurushikhar.

Timeline

On November 12, 2025, scientists began observing comet 3I/ATLAS using the PRL 1.2m telescope. These observations continued through November 15, 2025, focusing on both imaging and spectroscopic techniques. The team documented the comet's coma and assessed its production rate.

During the observations, a false colour image of the comet was captured, showing a near-circular coma and two fainter background stars. Additionally, the analysis of the light spectrum revealed prominent emission features typical of Solar System comets.

Official statements

“The production rate ratios seem to place this comet in the class of ‘typical comets’ of the solar system,” said a PRL scientist involved in the research.

Context

Comet 3I/ATLAS is one of the few known interstellar comets, providing a unique opportunity to study material from beyond our Solar System. Previous observations of other comets have contributed to our understanding of cometary behavior and composition.

What’s next

Further observations of comet 3I/ATLAS will continue as it transitions into darker portions of the night, allowing scientists to gather more data on its activity and composition.
