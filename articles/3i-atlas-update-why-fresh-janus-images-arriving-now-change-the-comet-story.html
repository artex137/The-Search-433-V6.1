3I Atlas Update: Why fresh JANUS images arriving now change the comet story

New images from the JANUS camera on the Juice spacecraft have provided significant insights into comet 3I/ATLAS, which passed through the Solar System in July 2025. The images were captured on 6 November 2025, seven days after the comet's closest approach to the Sun.

This update is crucial as it shifts the focus from simple detection to detailed structural analysis. Researchers now have access to over 120 images that reveal a complex coma, a long tail, and multiple jets from the comet, allowing for a better understanding of its behaviour as it nears the Sun. Various instrument teams are now preparing to analyze this data, which is expected to enhance our understanding of interstellar comets.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS was discovered on 1 July 2025.
  • Key science images were taken on 6 November 2025.
  • The spacecraft was approximately 66 million km from the comet during imaging.
  • JANUS captured more than 120 images across a wide wavelength range.
  • Data from JANUS, MAJIS, SWI, PEP, and UVS were collected in November 2025.
  • A meeting to discuss findings is planned for late March 2026.

Timeline

1 July 2025 — Comet 3I/ATLAS is discovered as it travels through the Solar System.

6 November 2025 — The JANUS camera captures key science images of the comet, just one week after its closest solar approach.

Late March 2026 — Instrument teams will convene to compare and synthesize their findings from the data.

Official statements

“The images show a bright halo of gas around the comet's head, a long tail, and visible rays, jet streams, and filaments emanating from the nucleus region,” said a spokesperson for the mission. “This dataset is particularly rich for understanding the comet's outgassing behaviour.”

Context

This is not the first time researchers have observed comet 3I/ATLAS. Prior to these detailed images, navigation-camera shots were circulated, but they lacked the resolution and spectral breadth that JANUS provides. The new images enable a technical examination of coma morphology and dust-gas interactions.

The combination of high-resolution imaging and simultaneous spectrometry is uncommon for hyperbolic trajectory objects, making this campaign a significant milestone for studying interstellar visitors.

What’s next

Teams will continue to analyze the imagery and complementary data. Further technical results are expected after the planned late-March discussions, which will outline the next steps in their research.
