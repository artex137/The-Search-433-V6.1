How astronomers predict the future path of 3I/ATLAS

Scientists have tracked the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, which is moving on a hyperbolic path and will not return, as it approaches the Sun. The comet is currently about 1.8 astronomical units (270 million kilometres) away from Earth and poses no risk of collision.

This tracking is crucial for ensuring Earth’s safety while also providing insights into the behaviour of cosmic objects. Authorities, including the NASA-funded ATLAS network and various global telescopes, are actively monitoring the comet’s speed, position, and outgassing to refine predictions about its orbit.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS moves on a hyperbolic path, indicating it will not return.
  • Current distance from Earth: 1.8 astronomical units (270 million kilometres).
  • Predicted close approach to Earth: December 2025.
  • No risk of collision with Earth.
  • Multiple telescopes measure the comet’s brightness and position several times a night.
  • NASA's ATLAS network and Hubble Space Telescope are involved in data collection.

Timeline

Scientists have observed 3I/ATLAS since its discovery, continuously tracking its trajectory. As the comet nears the Sun, it experiences increased temperatures that cause ice to evaporate, leading to jets of gas and dust that alter its movement.

Researchers employ Newton’s laws and numerical models to simulate the comet's motion, incorporating gravitational influences and outgassing effects. The data collected through repeated observations allows for iterative adjustments to predicted paths.

Official statements

“Accurate prediction of comets like 3I/ATLAS allows us to prepare for any potential threats and understand the behaviour of interstellar objects,” a NASA representative said.

Context

The study of comets like 3I/ATLAS is essential for improving global space safety. Advanced technologies, including machine learning and enhanced telescope networks, are being developed to refine predictions and enhance planetary defence strategies.

What’s next

Scientists will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS as it approaches the Sun, improving data collection methods and prediction algorithms to prepare for future encounters with other cosmic objects.
