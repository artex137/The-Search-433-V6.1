Behind The Sun, Interstellar Object 3I/ATLAS Shows Signs Of Non-Gravitational Acceleration And Is Now Blue

Published 2025-12-06 17:56 UTC

3I/ATLAS Shows Non-Gravitational Acceleration and Color Change

The interstellar object 3I/ATLAS has exhibited signs of non-gravitational acceleration and a notable color change since its discovery on July 1, 2023, by the Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System. The object is currently located behind the Sun from Earth’s perspective, which has limited direct observations.

This discovery is significant because it marks 3I/ATLAS as the third confirmed interstellar visitor, following 1I/’Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov. Scientists are closely monitoring its behavior and composition, as understanding this object could provide valuable insights into the conditions of the early universe.

Key facts

  • 3I/ATLAS was detected on July 1, 2023.
  • It has an eccentricity value between 6.1 and 6.2.
  • The object is approximately 10 billion years old.
  • During its perihelion on October 29, 2023, it reached a brightness of approximately magnitude 9.
  • It experiences a radial acceleration of about 135 kilometers per day squared.
  • Transverse acceleration measures roughly 60 kilometers per day squared.

Timeline

On July 1, 2023, 3I/ATLAS was first detected by the Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System. The object was classified as a comet due to its outgassing behavior.

On October 29, 2023, during its perihelion, 3I/ATLAS was obscured from Earth’s view by the Sun. Despite this, observations from multiple space-based instruments indicated a significant increase in brightness and a color change to a distinct blue.

Post-perihelion observations are expected to provide further data on the object’s behavior and composition.

Official statements

“The acceleration patterns observed are not solely due to gravitational forces,” said Davide Farnocchia, a navigation engineer at NASA’s Jet Propulsion Laboratory. “This indicates active cometary processes.”

Context

3I/ATLAS is only the third interstellar object confirmed by astronomers, following notable discoveries such as 1I/’Oumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019. These objects have sparked interest in studying the origins and compositions of materials from beyond our solar system.

The unique acceleration patterns and color changes observed in 3I/ATLAS challenge existing models of comet behavior and highlight the need for ongoing research into interstellar bodies.

What’s next

Scientists will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS through ground-based observatories and space-based platforms. Observations in November and December 2023 are crucial for understanding the object’s mass loss and behavior as it returns to interstellar space.
