Is 3I/ATLAS Leaking Alien Tech? Avi Loeb Tracks Suspicious Streaming Gas in Anti-Tail Jet

Harvard astronomer Avi Loeb has identified a potential indication of extraterrestrial technology linked to the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS. Loeb's observations of the comet's unusual sun-facing 'anti-tail' have raised questions about its propulsion mechanisms, with implications for our understanding of celestial objects. The scrutiny has intensified following the comet's closest approach to the Sun.

This investigation is significant as it could challenge existing models of comet behaviour. Authorities are now focusing on whether the gas emissions from 3I/ATLAS suggest an artificial origin rather than a natural process. Ground-based observatories are poised to assist in this analysis.

Key facts

  • Object: 3I/ATLAS
  • Observation period: Past two months following closest approach to the Sun
  • Current heliocentric distance: Approximately twice the Earth-Sun separation
  • Maximum gas outflow speed: V=0.2 km/s for natural comets
  • Potential technological thruster speeds: V=5 km/s for chemical thrusters, V=90 km/s for ion thrusters
  • Total mass loss estimate: Mdot∼150 kg/s from James Webb Space Telescope, enhanced to Mdot∼500 kg/s for post-perihelion calculations

Timeline

3I/ATLAS has undergone significant scrutiny since its closest approach to the Sun, where its unusual anti-tail jet was first noted. Over the past two months, observations have focused on the behaviour of gas emissions, leading to a mathematical evaluation of cometary models.

Loeb has proposed specific observations to test the nature of the emissions, with scientists planning to use molecular tracers to analyze the gas along the anti-tail jet.

Official statements

Avi Loeb stated, "This interstellar visitor is currently being scrutinised for anomalies that defy standard cometary models."
Loeb emphasized, "Science is a learning experience. The best way to learn is by observing nature rather than forcing it into a popular narrative."

Context

The investigation into 3I/ATLAS is part of a broader scientific effort to understand interstellar objects and their characteristics. Previous studies of comets have typically not included the possibility of artificial propulsion, making this analysis a potential turning point in the field.

What’s next

Ground-based observatories like Keck, VLT, or ALMA are expected to contribute to the ongoing investigation. Researchers aim to gather data that could confirm or refute the potential artificial origin of the emissions from 3I/ATLAS.
