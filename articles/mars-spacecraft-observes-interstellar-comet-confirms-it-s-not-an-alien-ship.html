Mars Spacecraft Observes Interstellar Comet, Confirms It’s Not an Alien Ship

In early October, the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS made its closest approach to Mars, passing within 30 million kilometers on October 3. The European Space Agency’s (ESA) robotic spacecraft, Mars Express and ExoMars, captured images of the comet during this event. No injuries or fatalities were reported.

This observation is significant as 3I/ATLAS, which originated from another solar system, offers insights into cosmic formation processes. Authorities from ESA have indicated that further analysis post-perihelion will help determine the comet's composition and its implications for understanding the universe.

Key facts

  • Name: 3I/ATLAS
  • Date of Closest Approach to Mars: October 3
  • Distance from Mars: 30 million kilometers
  • Next Closest Approach to the Sun: October 30
  • Agencies Involved: European Space Agency (ESA)

Timeline

On October 3, 3I/ATLAS passed within 30 million kilometers of Mars. ESA's Mars Express and ExoMars spacecraft successfully captured images of the comet during its closest approach.

As the comet continues its journey, it will next approach the Sun on October 30. Researchers plan to conduct further analysis after this perihelion to study the comet's characteristics.

Official statements

“The comet likely contains ices and dust that it releases as it heats up from exposure to the sun,” researchers noted.
“There are no reflective surfaces, no signals, and the comet releases a cloud of ice and dust—characteristics inconsistent with artificial spacecraft,” ESA representatives stated.

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third known interstellar object to pass through our solar system, following 1I/Oumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019. Each observation has sparked speculation regarding extraterrestrial origins, particularly in popular culture.

The study of interstellar comets like 3I/ATLAS enhances our understanding of cosmic formation processes and whether similar processes occur elsewhere in the Milky Way galaxy.

What’s next

ESA plans to launch the Comet Interceptor mission in 2029, aimed at closely studying an unknown interstellar visitor to gain deeper insights into the universe.
