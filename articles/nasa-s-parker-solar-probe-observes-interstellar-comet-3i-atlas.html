NASA’s Parker Solar Probe Observes Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS

NASA’s Parker Solar Probe has observed interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS from October 18 to November 5, 2025, using its WISPR (Wide-Field Imager for Solar Probe) instrument. The spacecraft captured approximately ten images of the comet daily during this observation period.

This observation is significant because it provides insights into a comet not visible from Earth at the time due to its proximity to the Sun. The data collected will help scientists study this interstellar object more comprehensively.

Key facts

  • Observation period: October 18 to November 5, 2025
  • Distance from the Sun: Approximately 130 million miles
  • Number of images captured: About ten per day
  • Closest point to the Sun: Just outside the orbit of Mars
  • Discovered by: NASA-funded ATLAS survey telescope in Rio Hurtado, Chile, in July 2025
  • Other missions involved: PUNCH, STEREO, and SOHO

Timeline

On September 15, 2025, Parker Solar Probe completed its 25th solar flyby. Following this event, the spacecraft began its observation of comet 3I/ATLAS on October 18. Over the next few weeks, the probe captured images of the comet as it moved behind the Sun from Parker’s point of view, until the observation period concluded on November 5.

The initial images taken are still undergoing final calibration and processing. The WISPR team is working to remove stray sunlight and adjust for varying exposure times, which has affected the comet's apparent brightness in the images.

Official statements

“The final images will ultimately help scientists better study this interstellar visitor,” said Mara Johnson-Groh from NASA’s Goddard Space Flight Center.

Context

Comet 3I/ATLAS is the third known object from outside our solar system to be discovered within our solar neighborhood. The comet was first identified by the ATLAS survey, which is designed to detect near-Earth objects and provide alerts for potential impacts.

What’s next

The WISPR team will continue to process the data collected during the observation to ensure accurate representations of the comet. Final images are expected to be released following the calibration process, contributing to ongoing studies of interstellar comets.
