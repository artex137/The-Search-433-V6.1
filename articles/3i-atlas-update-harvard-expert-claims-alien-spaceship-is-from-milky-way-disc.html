3I/ATLAS Update: Harvard Expert Claims 'Alien Spaceship' Is From Milky Way Disc

Avi Loeb, a Harvard astrophysicist, has claimed that the comet 3I/ATLAS, discovered in July 2025, is likely not an "alien spaceship" but rather a visitor from the main disc of the Milky Way. His assertion, based on the comet's galactic trajectory and composition, adds complexity to the discussions surrounding the object, which is catalogued as C/2025 N1 and is only the third known interstellar object to enter the Solar System.

This discovery is significant as it sheds light on the nature and origin of interstellar objects. Astronomers confirmed that 3I/ATLAS has a trajectory similar to that of other materials in the Milky Way, suggesting a more conventional origin rather than an extraterrestrial one. Loeb's analysis aims to clarify the ongoing debate about the comet's nature and its implications for our understanding of the galaxy.

Key facts

  • 3I/ATLAS was spotted in July 2025.
  • It is catalogued as C/2025 N1.
  • It is the third known interstellar object to enter the Solar System.
  • Avi Loeb suggests the comet could be up to 14 billion years old.
  • 3I/ATLAS is rich in carbon dioxide, carbon monoxide, water vapour, and trace elements.
  • The nucleus is estimated to be between 300 metres and over 1,000 metres across.

Timeline

In July 2025, astronomers discovered the comet 3I/ATLAS while conducting observations from Chile. Since its detection, scientists have been analyzing its trajectory and chemical composition. The object has sparked discussions about its origins, with Loeb's recent claims adding a new dimension to the debate.

Loeb's assertion that 3I/ATLAS is likely from the Milky Way's stellar disc contrasts with earlier speculations that it could be artificial. His evaluation of the object's composition and age has prompted further investigation into its nature.

Official statements

Avi Loeb stated, "The Galactic orbit of 3I/ATLAS suggests a likely origin in the disc of the Milky-Way galaxy," highlighting its trajectory's similarity to that of stars in the galaxy.
Loeb also noted, "Most of its mass is associated with heavy elements," indicating a complex formation history for the comet.

Context

Prior to 3I/ATLAS, only two interstellar objects, ʻOumuamua and comet 2I/Borisov, had been confirmed to have entered the Solar System. These earlier cases quickly sparked debates about their exotic origins versus more conventional explanations. The discovery of 3I/ATLAS has introduced a different set of questions, focusing on its chemical makeup and potential age.

Loeb's previous claims regarding the nature of ʻOumuamua have made him a controversial figure in astrophysics. The ongoing study of interstellar objects is crucial for understanding the composition and dynamics of our galaxy.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS to refine measurements of its motion and chemical composition. The scientific community is hopeful that further observations will provide insights into the origins of this interstellar visitor and its implications for our understanding of the Milky Way.
