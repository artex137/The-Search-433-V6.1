Scientists Await Cosmic Bombshell as 3I/ATLAS Nears Earth

Scientists are preparing for a significant cosmic event as interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS approaches Earth, with its closest pass scheduled for 19 December 2025. The comet, discovered in 2025, has shown unusual chemical properties and behavior that could enhance our understanding of comet formation beyond our Solar System.

This close encounter represents a critical opportunity for researchers to gather data on how interstellar comets compare with those formed around the Sun. Authorities and scientists are gearing up to observe the comet as it passes at a safe distance of approximately 1.8 astronomical units, or about 270 million kilometres, from Earth.

Key facts

  • Comet Name: 3I/ATLAS
  • Closest Pass: 19 December 2025
  • Distance from Earth: Approximately 1.8 astronomical units (270 million kilometres)
  • Discovery Date: 1 July 2025
  • Diameter: Between 440 metres and 5.6 kilometres
  • Water Production Rate: Approximately 40 kilograms per second
  • Active Surface Area: Roughly 19 square kilometres

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first identified by the NASA-funded ATLAS survey telescope in Chile, with its discovery reported on 1 July 2025. Observations confirmed that its hyperbolic trajectory means it is not bound to the Sun, categorizing it as the third confirmed interstellar object ever observed, following 1I/ʻOumuamua and 2I/Borisov.

By late July 2025, the Neil Gehrels Swift Observatory detected signs of water-ice sublimation from the comet. The Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter captured images of 3I/ATLAS during a close fly-by in early October 2025, marking a significant achievement in observing interstellar objects.

Official statements

“This close encounter provides a unique opportunity to test and potentially revise our understanding of comet formation across the galaxy,” a spokesperson from NASA stated.

Context

3I/ATLAS is notable for its unusual chemical composition, primarily composed of carbon dioxide (CO2), with a ratio of CO2 to water (H2O) of approximately 8:1. This composition challenges current models of comet formation and suggests that interstellar comets may have different origins compared to those in our Solar System.

Prior to 3I/ATLAS, only two interstellar objects had been confirmed: 1I/ʻOumuamua and 2I/Borisov. Each of these encounters has provided valuable insights into the nature of materials from outside our Solar System.

What’s next

As 3I/ATLAS approaches its closest pass, scientists will continue to monitor the comet's activity and composition. Observations during the December flyby could lead to significant revelations about the differences between interstellar comets and those formed in our Solar System.
