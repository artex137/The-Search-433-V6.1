3I Atlas Update as ESA’s Jupiter-bound spacecraft returns a new wave of comet images

The European Space Agency (ESA) has shared a new image of interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, captured by its Jupiter Icy Moons Explorer (Juice) spacecraft on 6 November 2025. The image shows the comet actively releasing dust and gas when it was near the Sun, but the comet's nucleus is not visible.

This update is significant as ESA's mission teams begin detailed analysis of over 120 images taken by Juice across multiple scientific instruments. The observations will help scientists understand the comet's behavior and composition, contributing to broader studies of potential life on Jupiter's icy moons.

Key facts

  • Date of image capture: 6 November 2025
  • Distance from comet at the time of capture: approximately 66 million km
  • Instruments used: JANUS, MAJIS, SWI, PEP, and UVS
  • Number of images captured: more than 120
  • Next analysis meeting: Late March 2026

Timeline

On 6 November 2025, Juice captured a new image of 3I/ATLAS while it was about 66 million km from the comet. This was seven days after the comet's closest approach to the Sun. Throughout November 2025, Juice utilized five scientific instruments to gather data on the comet.

After the observations, Juice was positioned on the opposite side of the Sun from Earth, which limited data transmission capabilities. The mission team received the data last week and is now analyzing it.

Official statements

“The current phase is about extracting meaning from multiple viewing and processing methods rather than relying on a single unprocessed frame,” ESA stated regarding the data analysis process.

Context

ESA has emphasized the scientific importance of studying 3I/ATLAS, as its behavior corresponds with expectations for a "normal" comet. The observations serve not only to understand the comet but also to contribute to Juice's broader goal of studying Jupiter's icy moons, which are considered promising locations for potential life due to their subsurface oceans.

What’s next

The instrument teams will convene in late March 2026 to discuss their findings, integrating various analyses to develop a comprehensive understanding of the comet.
