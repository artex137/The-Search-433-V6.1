Detection of an Anti-Solar Tail for 3I/ATLAS

On August 27, 2025, astronomers at the Gemini South telescope in the Chilean Andes have detected a teardrop-shaped tail in the anti-Sun direction of the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS. The object was approximately 2.59 times the distance from Earth to the Sun at the time of the observation. No injuries or fatalities have been reported.

This discovery is significant as it provides new insights into the composition and behavior of 3I/ATLAS, which is believed to be an interstellar comet. The findings have prompted further analysis and observations from various space observatories, including the Webb Telescope and SPHEREx, to better understand the object's characteristics and activity.

Key facts

  • Date of detection: August 27, 2025
  • Location: Gemini South Observatory, Cerro Pachón, Chile
  • Distance from Earth: 2.59 times the Earth-Sun separation
  • Length of tail: approximately 56,000 kilometers
  • Width of coma: approximately 18,800 kilometers
  • Mass loss rates: 130 kg/s (CO2), 6.6 kg/s (H2O), 14 kg/s (CO)

Timeline

On August 6, 2025, data from the Webb Telescope confirmed the presence of a carbon dioxide gas plume around 3I/ATLAS. This was followed by deep imaging on August 27, revealing the tail structure. Earlier data from NASA's Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite indicated that 3I/ATLAS may have been active as far back as May 2025.

Initial observations in early July 2025 showed a compact appearance of 3I/ATLAS, which has since changed significantly. The new data indicates a more extended coma and tail shape, influenced by solar wind and radiation.

Official statements

“The detection of the anti-solar tail is a remarkable confirmation of the cometary activity of 3I/ATLAS and offers an opportunity to study its composition in detail,” said Avi Loeb, an astronomer involved in the observations.

Context

Prior to this event, 3I/ATLAS was noted for its unusual characteristics as an interstellar object. The findings from various telescopes and observatories have shifted the understanding of its behavior and how it differs from typical solar system comets.

What’s next

Further investigations are expected to focus on the composition of the tail and coma, as well as the dynamics of the gas plumes. Observatories will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS to gather more data on its activity and trajectory as it moves through the solar system.
