3I/ATLAS Mystery: CIA Refusal to Confirm Records Sparks Interstellar Security Row

A fast-moving interstellar object named 3I/ATLAS, officially known as C/2025 N1, has become the subject of controversy after the CIA refused to confirm or deny the existence of records relating to it. The object was discovered by the Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System (ATLAS) in Chile on July 1, 2025, moving at a speed of 58 kilometers per second. No injuries or fatalities have been reported.

The situation raises questions about state secrecy versus scientific openness. The CIA's refusal has sparked debate within the scientific community and among former intelligence officials about the implications of this response on transparency and public trust. The object is only the third confirmed interstellar visitor, following 'Oumuamua in 2017 and comet Borisov in 2019.

Key facts

  • Object discovered: July 1, 2025
  • Speed of 3I/ATLAS: 58 kilometers per second
  • Discovery location: Chile
  • CIA response date: January 5, 2026
  • FOIA request filed by: John Greenewald Jr., founder of The Black Vault
  • Potential final planetary assist from Jupiter: March 2026

Timeline

On July 1, 2025, 3I/ATLAS is discovered by ATLAS in Chile. The object is noted for its high speed and is classified as an interstellar traveler. Following this discovery, John Greenewald Jr. files a Freedom of Information Act request in November 2025, seeking information about the object. On January 5, 2026, the CIA issues a 'Glomar' response, stating it can neither confirm nor deny the existence of records related to 3I/ATLAS.

Official statements

“If 3I/ATLAS is just a natural comet, as NASA says, why does the intelligence community need to keep its data so secret?” - Professor Avi Loeb
“The response is likely a protective measure for 'sources and methods'.” - Former intelligence officials

Context

The CIA's use of a 'Glomar' response is typically reserved for serious national security issues, such as secret satellites or experimental technologies. The scientific community largely believes that 3I/ATLAS is a natural body, with estimates suggesting it could be up to 14 billion years old. However, the hypothetical scenario of it being artificial raises significant implications for global politics and scientific advancement.

What’s next

As 3I/ATLAS continues its trajectory back into interstellar space, it is set to receive a final planetary assist from Jupiter in March 2026. The ongoing debate regarding the CIA's response may prompt further inquiries into governmental transparency and the future of space situational awareness initiatives.
