Comet 3I/ATLAS Offers Insights into Other Solar Systems

Michigan State University (MSU) researchers have uncovered images of comet 3I/ATLAS, revealing its activity from as early as May 2023. The comet, which is only the third interstellar object ever detected, was initially spotted in early July and is expected to be visible for only a few more months before it moves beyond the reach of telescopes.

This research is significant as it suggests that 3I/ATLAS may originate from a solar system with different characteristics than our own. The findings provide a unique opportunity for scientists to study materials from beyond our solar system, highlighting the importance of early observations and ongoing research.

Key facts

  • Object Name: 3I/ATLAS
  • Detection Date: Early July 2023
  • Initial Images Date: May 2023
  • Research Institution: Michigan State University
  • Published Paper: Astrophysical Journal Letters
  • Visibility Duration: A few more months

Timeline

In early July 2023, 3I/ATLAS was officially detected as an interstellar object. Following this, MSU researchers published a paper detailing their findings just days later. The team analyzed images from NASA's Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite (TESS), revealing evidence of the comet as early as May 2023.

The images indicated that 3I/ATLAS had been active farther from the sun than previously expected, suggesting it may release different molecules compared to comets in our solar system. Researchers have since worked rapidly to document the comet's characteristics before it becomes invisible.

Official statements

“There are so few interstellar objects that we’ve detected in our solar system, and they each seem to be unique,” said Adina Feinstein, assistant professor of physics and astronomy at MSU and lead author of the study.
“Capturing as many observations from this prediscovery period... is essential to our understanding of how these objects ‘turn on’ as they approach our sun,” said John Noonan, co-author and postdoctoral fellow at Auburn University.

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third interstellar object detected, following 'Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov. The study of such objects has the potential to reveal differences in the composition and behavior of comets and asteroids from different solar systems. MSU's recent findings underscore the value of all-sky surveys, which can provide critical data for astrophysics research.

What’s next

Researchers at MSU will continue to observe 3I/ATLAS and analyze its composition using data from NASA’s James Webb Space Telescope. Ongoing studies aim to deepen understanding of interstellar objects and their origins before the comet moves out of range.
