3I/ATLAS Mystery: Harvard Scientist Avi Loeb Reveals 'Weird' Hubble Discovery Regarding Interstellar Visitor

On January 14, 2026, the Hubble Space Telescope captured a series of images of the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, presenting a puzzling phenomenon that has intrigued scientists, including Harvard's Professor Avi Loeb. The images reveal a massive halo of material surrounding the comet's nucleus, extending over 130,000 kilometres towards the sun, significantly altering our understanding of cometary behaviour.

This discovery is significant as it challenges existing knowledge of solar physics and cometary structures. Researchers are now analysing the implications of these findings, particularly regarding the unusual behaviour of 3I/ATLAS as it approaches the sun.

Key facts

  • Date of Hubble observations: January 14, 2026
  • Distance of halo: more than 130,000 kilometres
  • Next critical observation date: January 22, 2026
  • Angle of Earth alignment to 3I/ATLAS: 0.69 degrees
  • Wobble of jet system: approximately 20 degrees every 7.1 hours

Timeline

On December 2025, previous Hubble data indicated changes in 3I/ATLAS, leading to further investigation. On January 14, 2026, Hubble captured new images, revealing a peculiar arrangement of outflows, including an 'anti-tail' directed towards the sun. Following this, on January 22, 2026, a rare celestial alignment will occur, allowing scientists to observe the comet in maximum brightness.

Official statements

“The brightness map captured by Hubble represents the state of 3I/ATLAS after its perihelion, showing how the interstellar guest is reacting to the intense heat of our local star,” said Professor Avi Loeb.

Context

The behaviour of 3I/ATLAS marks a departure from typical cometary characteristics, where materials are generally pushed away from the sun by solar wind and radiation. Previous observations of interstellar objects have not shown such a pronounced anti-tail or the specific arrangements of jets seen in 3I/ATLAS.

What’s next

As 3I/ATLAS approaches opposition on January 22, 2026, scientists will focus on measuring its brightness and the polarisation of light during this alignment. This information is expected to provide insights into the composition of the comet and its unique features.
