From orbit around Mars, Chinese probe Tianwen-1 captures unprecedented images of the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS

Published 2026-01-02 00:57 UTC

Chinese Probe Captures Images of Comet 3I/ATLAS from Mars Orbit

In October 2025, the Chinese probe Tianwen-1 captured high-resolution images of the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS from its orbit around Mars, marking the first time an interstellar object has been photographed from another planet. The images were taken approximately 30 million kilometers from the comet, a significant achievement for planetary science and global astronomy.

The successful imaging of comet 3I/ATLAS is important for understanding the composition and trajectory of interstellar objects. The Chinese National Space Administration (CNSA) has mobilized to analyze the data, which offers insights into the formation of other stellar systems. Several international space agencies have collaborated to collect and cross-reference data about the comet.

Key facts

  • Event: Imaging of comet 3I/ATLAS
  • Date: October 2025
  • Distance from comet: Approximately 30 million kilometers
  • Diameter of comet: About 5.6 kilometers
  • Speed of comet: 58 kilometers per second
  • Agencies involved: CNSA, ESA, NASA

Timeline

In July 2020, China launched the Tianwen-1 mission, which entered Martian orbit in February 2021. The mission included an orbiter, a lander, and the Zhurong rover. In May 2021, the Zhurong rover landed on Mars and operated for about one Earth year, collecting geological data.

In October 2025, Tianwen-1 successfully captured images of comet 3I/ATLAS. The probe's high-resolution camera, adapted for this task, allowed for detailed observations of the comet's core and coma.

Official statements

“This achievement consolidates a significant advance for planetary science and global astronomy,” said a CNSA spokesperson.

Context

Comet 3I/ATLAS is the third confirmed interstellar object, following ‘Oumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019. Its hyperbolic orbit indicates it is not part of our solar system. Ground-based observatories in Chile first detected the comet.

The successful imaging of the comet highlights the growing capabilities of the CNSA and the importance of international collaboration in space exploration.

What’s next

Following the successful observations, CNSA plans to utilize the data to inform future missions, including the Tianwen-2 mission, which aims to collect samples from an asteroid and study a comet from the main belt.
