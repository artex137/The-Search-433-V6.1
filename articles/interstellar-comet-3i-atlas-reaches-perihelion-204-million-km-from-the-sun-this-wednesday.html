Interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS reaches perihelion 204 million km from the Sun this Wednesday

Comet 3I/ATLAS, the third confirmed interstellar object, has reached perihelion on October 29, 2025, at 8:47 Brasilia time, at a distance of 204 million kilometers from the Sun. No injuries or fatalities have been reported.

This event is significant as it allows astronomers to study the comet's composition and behavior as it approaches the Sun. The perihelion event activates the sublimation of ice, forming a coma and tail that can be observed from Earth. Experts are closely monitoring the comet's trajectory and brightness during this period.

Key facts

  • Perihelion date: October 29, 2025
  • Exact time: 8:47 Brasilia time
  • Distance from the Sun: 204 million kilometers (1.36 astronomical units)
  • Discovery: July 2025
  • Observation location: Constellation of Virgo until November 17, 2025
  • Close approach to Earth: December 19, 2025, at 269 million kilometers
  • Future close approach to Jupiter: March 2026, at 54 million kilometers

Timeline

Comet 3I/ATLAS was discovered in July 2025 through images from the CCOR-1 coronagraph on the GOES-19 satellite. An amateur astronomer processed daily data to identify the comet. The object reached perihelion on October 29, 2025, where solar heat activated the sublimation of ice, forming visible structures. After perihelion, the comet will begin moving away from the Sun, with visibility returning on November 3, 2025.

The comet will remain visible in the constellation of Virgo until November 17, 2025, and will make a close approach to Earth on December 19, 2025.

Official statements

“The maximum heat transforms ice into gas in the core, releasing dust that creates structures visible from Earth,” said a representative from an astronomical observatory.

Context

Comet 3I/ATLAS is notable for its origin outside the Solar System, traced back to an unknown star in the Milky Way. Its chemical composition provides insights into planetary formation in distant systems. Previous interstellar objects have garnered significant attention due to their potential to inform scientists about the early solar system.

What’s next

Following perihelion, astronomers will continue to monitor the comet's trajectory and brightness until it moves away from the Sun. Observations will be conducted using telescopes, and data will be updated by the Jet Propulsion Laboratory to track its orbital parameters.
