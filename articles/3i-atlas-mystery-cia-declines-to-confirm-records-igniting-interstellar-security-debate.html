3I/ATLAS Mystery: CIA Declines to Confirm Records, Igniting Interstellar Security Debate

The Central Intelligence Agency (CIA) has issued a rare "Glomar response" to a Freedom of Information Act (FOIA) request regarding the interstellar object known as 3I/ATLAS, raising questions about national security implications. This response, which neither confirms nor denies the existence of relevant records, has triggered widespread debate among scientific and security communities. The object, detected in July 2025, is considered a natural comet by NASA.

Authorities are now examining the implications of the CIA's response, which some experts interpret as an indication of potential threats or classified investigations related to 3I/ATLAS. The controversy surrounding the object has intensified discussions on transparency and security measures regarding interstellar phenomena.

Key facts

  • 3I/ATLAS was first detected in July 2025.
  • The object is projected to approach Jupiter in March 2026.
  • NASA describes 3I/ATLAS as an ordinary interstellar comet.
  • The CIA has invoked FOIA exemptions due to national security concerns.
  • The object exhibited anomalies, including a nickel-rich outer shell and apparent course adjustments.
  • The CIA's response is known as a Glomar response, typically reserved for sensitive operations.

Timeline

In July 2025, astronomers detected the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS using the Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System (ATLAS). It was later identified as the third confirmed interstellar visitor to our solar system.

In October 2025, 3I/ATLAS made a close approach to Mars. Following this, in November 2025, John Greenewald Jr. submitted a FOIA request to the CIA for any related assessments or reports. The CIA responded with a Glomar response, citing national security as the reason for its ambiguity.

On November 19, 2025, NASA Administrator Nicky Fox held a press conference, affirming that data available did not support claims of anything beyond a natural comet.

Official statements

"It’s very interesting that they did not dismiss the existence of documents within the CIA on this matter," said Avi Loeb, a Harvard astrophysicist who has raised concerns about the implications of 3I/ATLAS.

Context

This incident follows a history of government secrecy regarding unidentified aerial phenomena and interstellar objects. The CIA's Glomar response is a significant deviation from typical responses to inquiries about celestial bodies, which usually result in a straightforward denial of records.

NASA's position has remained consistent, asserting that 3I/ATLAS is composed of ice and dust, despite criticism regarding the quality of images released and the withholding of data during its closest approach to Mars.

What’s next

As 3I/ATLAS continues its trajectory toward Jupiter, experts call for international collaboration to deploy advanced telescopes and probes during the March 2026 encounter. The ongoing debate emphasizes the need for transparency in interstellar events and the implications for national security.
