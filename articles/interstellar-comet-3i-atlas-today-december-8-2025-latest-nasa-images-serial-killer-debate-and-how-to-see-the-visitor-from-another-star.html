Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS Today (December 8, 2025)

On December 8, 2025, interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS is travelling through the inner solar system, headed for a flyby of Earth on December 19. Recent observations from NASA's Hubble Space Telescope and other missions have highlighted the comet's unique characteristics and its significance for scientific research.

This comet, also known as C/2025 N1 (ATLAS), is the third confirmed interstellar object detected in our solar system. Authorities have emphasized its importance for understanding the formation of celestial bodies and potential life in other star systems.

Key facts

  • Discovered: July 1, 2025, by the ATLAS survey telescope in Río Hurtado, Chile.
  • Origin: Came from deep interstellar space, approaching from the direction of Sagittarius.
  • Orbit: Extremely hyperbolic with an eccentricity of approximately 6.1, indicating it will exit the solar system forever.
  • Speed: Approximately 58 km/s (about 130,000 mph) relative to the Sun, making it the fastest recorded comet.
  • Size: Estimated nucleus size of about 3.5 miles (approximately 6 km) across.
  • Age: Potentially several billion years old, possibly older than the solar system itself.
  • Chemical composition: Dominated by carbon dioxide, with water, cyanide compounds, and nickel present.
  • Upcoming flyby: Will approach no closer than about 170 million miles (1.8 AU) from Earth.

Timeline

Here is a brief overview of significant events regarding 3I/ATLAS:

  • July 1, 2025: Comet 3I/ATLAS is discovered by the ATLAS survey telescope.
  • October 3, 2025: NASA’s MAVEN spacecraft captures images of the comet when it is approximately 18.6 million miles from Mars.
  • November 30, 2025: Hubble Space Telescope captures detailed images of 3I/ATLAS.
  • December 4, 2025: ESA’s Juice spacecraft observes the comet, providing additional scientific data.
  • December 19, 2025: Expected flyby of Earth.

Official statements

“3I/ATLAS is the start of a whole new field of study of interstellar comets,” said researchers involved in the project.

Context

3I/ATLAS is notable as the third interstellar object observed in our solar system, following 1I/ʻOumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019. The study of such comets can provide insights into planetary formation and the distribution of organic materials in space.

What’s next

As 3I/ATLAS approaches its flyby, scientists will continue to collect data from various missions, including MAVEN and PUNCH, to study its characteristics and behavior as it travels through the solar system.
