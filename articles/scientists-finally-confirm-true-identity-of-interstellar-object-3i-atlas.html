Scientists finally confirm true identity of interstellar object 3I/ATLAS

Scientists have confirmed that the interstellar object known as 3I/ATLAS is a comet, not an alien spacecraft, following new observations made at the Gemini South Observatory in Chile. The update comes as the object races toward the Sun at a speed of 137,000 miles per hour.

This confirmation is significant as it dispels conspiracy theories surrounding the object's origin. The observations, which included the first multi-color images of 3I/ATLAS captured on August 27, have provided clarity on its composition and characteristics, according to astronomers.

Key facts

  • Object: 3I/ATLAS
  • Speed: 137,000 miles per hour
  • Observation date: August 27
  • Location of observation: Gemini South Observatory, Chile
  • Discovery of a CO2-rich atmosphere

Timeline

On August 27, astronomers at the Gemini South Observatory captured the first multi-color images of 3I/ATLAS. These images revealed the comet's icy nucleus and its surrounding coma, as well as a long tail extending away from the Sun. Following this observation, Dr. Mark Norris from the University of Lancashire confirmed that the object is a comet, countering earlier claims that it was an alien spacecraft.

Dr. Norris explained that the comet's brightness increases as it approaches the Sun, leading to its larger and brighter appearance. Scientists have also noted that 3I/ATLAS has a CO2-rich atmosphere with less water than most comets found in our solar system.

Official statements

“The new images clearly demonstrate that 3I/ATLAS is a comet,” said Dr. Mark Norris, challenging previous theories proposed by some astrophysicists.
“As 3I/ATLAS is moving towards the Sun, it gets more and more exposed to the heat,” Dr. Norris added, explaining the comet's unusual brightness.

Context

This confirmation of 3I/ATLAS as a comet follows a growing interest in interstellar objects, particularly after the discovery of 'Oumuamua in 2017. The debate surrounding the nature of such objects often includes speculation about extraterrestrial origins, which has been a topic of discussion among scientists and the public alike.

What’s next

Scientists will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS as it approaches the Sun, gathering more data about its composition and behavior in the solar system.
