3I/ATLAS growing a tail? Fresh photos captured by scientists reveal major discoveries as the comet nears Earth

Fresh images of comet 3I/ATLAS have been released, showing a significant tail growing on the interstellar object as it approaches Earth. The pictures were captured on August 27, 2025, by a team of scientists and students using the Gemini South telescope in Cerro Pachón, Chile. No injuries or fatalities have been reported.

This discovery is important as it confirms the comet's increasing activity as it travels through the solar system. The National Science Foundation’s NoirLab, which operates the telescope, has stated that the comet poses no threat to Earth. Comet 3I/ATLAS is only the third known interstellar object found in the solar system, believed to have originated from another star.

Key facts

  • 3I/ATLAS was 238 million miles (384 million kilometers) from Earth as of the latest images.
  • The comet will make its closest approach to the sun at the end of October 2025.
  • It will pass closest to Earth in December 2025, at a distance of 167 million miles (269 million kilometers).
  • The comet was discovered on July 1, 2025, by the ATLAS (Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System).
  • This is the third interstellar object identified in the solar system, following 'Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov.

Timeline

On July 1, 2025, comet 3I/ATLAS was discovered by the ATLAS program. The comet has since been monitored as it approaches the sun and Earth. On August 27, 2025, detailed images of the comet, showing its growing tail, were captured using the Gemini South telescope.

As the comet continues its journey, it is expected to reach its closest point to the sun by the end of October 2025, followed by its closest approach to Earth in December 2025.

Official statements

“This image is both a scientific milestone and a source of wonder,” said Karen Meech, team leader and astronomer at the University of Hawaii Institute for Astronomy. “It reminds us that our solar system is just one part of a vast and dynamic galaxy, and that even the most fleeting visitors can leave a lasting impact.”

Context

Comet 3I/ATLAS is part of a growing list of interstellar objects identified since the first detection of 'Oumuamua in 2017. The discovery of these objects provides insights into the formation and evolution of planetary systems beyond our own.

Previous interstellar objects, such as 2I/Borisov, have also shown similarities to comets originating within our solar system, indicating shared formation processes.

What’s next

As 3I/ATLAS continues its journey through the solar system, scientists will monitor its activity and gather more data. The next significant event will be the comet's closest approach to the sun at the end of October 2025.
