NASA’s Assurance on Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS: No Extraterrestrial Involvement

The National Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA) has addressed speculation regarding the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, confirming that the object poses no threat and contains no evidence of alien life. Acting administrator Sean Duffy made this statement in response to an inquiry from celebrity Kim Kardashian on October 30, 2025.

This clarification comes amid ongoing debates within the scientific community about the comet's origins. NASA's response aims to alleviate public concern and maintain transparency as discussions surrounding the object intensify.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS discovered in 2025 by NASA's Asteroid Terrestrial-Impact Last Alert System (ATLAS).
  • Closest approach to the Sun occurred on October 29, 2025.
  • Inquiries about the comet's data have been made by scientists, including Harvard astrophysicist Avi Loeb.
  • U.S. Representative Anna Paulina Luna submitted a request for data on October 31, 2025.
  • No injuries or fatalities reported.

Timeline

Comet 3I/ATLAS was discovered in 2025. On October 29, 2025, it made its closest approach to the Sun. The following day, on October 30, Kim Kardashian posted on social media, prompting a response from NASA's Sean Duffy. On October 31, U.S. Representative Anna Paulina Luna formally requested additional data from NASA.

Official statements

No aliens. No threat to life here on Earth. —Sean Duffy, NASA Acting Administrator
While Duffy swiftly addressed Kardashian, requests for data from scientists have gone unanswered. —Avi Loeb, Harvard Astrophysicist

Context

This incident highlights the ongoing tension between public engagement and scientific transparency within NASA. Critics have raised concerns that celebrity interactions may detract from the agency's accountability in addressing scientific inquiries. The recent U.S. government shutdown has also contributed to delays in data release, prompting further scrutiny.

What’s next

NASA has not disclosed a timeline for data release but maintains that 3I/ATLAS is classified as a natural interstellar comet. A United Nations-backed planetary defense group has announced plans for enhanced monitoring of the object to improve early detection of future interstellar visitors.
