ESA Probe Captures Rare Data on Interstellar Object 3I/ATLAS Before It Vanishes Behind the Sun

The European Space Agency’s ExoMars Trace Gas Orbiter has captured close-range data on the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS (C/2025 P1) as it passed perihelion on October 31, 2023, at approximately 1.35 to 1.40 astronomical units (AU), or just over 200 million kilometers from the Sun. The object is currently hidden behind the Sun and is in a phase known as solar conjunction, making it temporarily invisible from Earth.

This data collection is significant as it provides insights into the characteristics of interstellar objects. The ExoMars Trace Gas Orbiter continues to monitor 3I/ATLAS's activity despite its current position, highlighting the ongoing capabilities of spacecraft near Mars.

Key facts

  • Date of perihelion: October 31, 2023
  • Distance from the Sun at closest approach: 1.35 to 1.40 AU
  • Distance from Earth: just over 200 million kilometers
  • Current phase: solar conjunction
  • Monitoring agency: European Space Agency

Timeline

On October 31, 2023, 3I/ATLAS reached perihelion, its closest approach to the Sun. The ExoMars Trace Gas Orbiter successfully captured data during this event. Following perihelion, the object moved into a solar conjunction phase, rendering it invisible from Earth.

Official statements

“The data collected during perihelion provides unprecedented insights into the characteristics of interstellar objects,” said a representative from the European Space Agency.

Context

3I/ATLAS is one of several interstellar objects observed by astronomers. Its journey through the solar system offers valuable opportunities for research into the composition and behavior of such celestial bodies.

What’s next

As 3I/ATLAS remains hidden behind the Sun, the ExoMars Trace Gas Orbiter will continue to analyze any available data until the object becomes visible again, allowing for further observations and studies.
