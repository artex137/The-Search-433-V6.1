Gemini North observations show changes in Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS after perihelion

On November 26, 2025, astronomers at the Gemini North telescope on Maunakea, Hawai’i, observed the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, revealing changes in its appearance and activity following its closest approach to the Sun on October 30, 2025.

This observation is significant as it provides insights into the behavior of the comet after perihelion. The data could enhance understanding of interstellar objects and their interactions with solar radiation.

Key facts

  • Date of observation: November 26, 2025
  • Location: Gemini North telescope, Maunakea, Hawai’i
  • Closest approach to the Sun: October 30, 2025
  • Comet speed: approximately 210,000 kilometers per hour
  • Next closest approach to Earth: December 19, 2025, at around 273 million kilometers
  • Notable gases detected: diatomic carbon

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first detected in late June 2025 while traveling through the solar system. After its perihelion on October 30, 2025, the comet became visible again on November 26, 2025, near the triple-star system Zaniah in the constellation Virgo.

Observations at Gemini North indicated a change from a red hue seen earlier at Gemini South to a faint green coloration, attributed to the presence of diatomic carbon in the comet's coma.

Official statements

“The changes in the coma and tail of 3I/ATLAS after perihelion are indicative of the comet's evolving activity,” said Bryce Bolin from Eureka Scientific.

Context

3I/ATLAS is recognized as the largest and oldest interstellar object recorded, having displayed sun-facing jets and signs of exposure to cosmic radiation during its journey. Observations of its behavior contribute to understanding the formation of early star systems in the Milky Way.

What’s next

Astronomers will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS as it approaches Earth on December 19, 2025. They will look for changes in brightness and activity, which could indicate further outbursts or emissions from the comet.
