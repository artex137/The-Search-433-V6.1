3I/ATLAS Caught on Camera: China’s Tianwen-1 Captures the Interstellar Wanderer

China's Tianwen-1 Mars orbiter has captured detailed images of the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS on October 3, 2025, from a distance of approximately 29-30 million kilometers (about 18 million miles). The images show the comet as a faint, fuzzy dot amid a field of stars. No injuries or fatalities have been reported.

This milestone highlights the capabilities of international space missions to observe rare cosmic phenomena. The China National Space Administration (CNSA) released the images, which demonstrate the orbiter's ability to adapt its scientific payload for unique astronomical opportunities. The Tianwen-1 mission has primarily focused on Mars but has now expanded its research to include interstellar objects.

Key facts

  • Event Date: October 3, 2025
  • Distance from Mars: 29-30 million kilometers (18 million miles)
  • Comet Speed: Approximately 130,000 mph (210,000 km/h)
  • Comet Diameter: Estimated between 3 to 7 miles (5 to 11 kilometers)
  • Discovery Date of 3I/ATLAS: July 1, 2025
  • Perihelion Date: October 30, 2025

Timeline

Key events leading up to the observation of 3I/ATLAS include:

  • July 1, 2025: Discovery by the NASA-funded ATLAS telescope in Rio Hurtado, Chile.
  • Early September 2025: The Tianwen-1 team begins planning observations.
  • October 3, 2025: Tianwen-1 captures images of 3I/ATLAS during its closest approach to Mars.
  • October 4, 2025: NASA's Perseverance rover potentially captures a streak of the comet.
  • October 30, 2025: 3I/ATLAS reaches perihelion, closest to the sun.
  • Early December 2025: The comet is expected to become visible to Earth-based telescopes again.
  • March 2026: 3I/ATLAS will pass near Jupiter before exiting the solar system.

Official statements

“The scientific value of these images is immense. Interstellar objects like 3I/ATLAS serve as time capsules from other star systems, offering insights into the composition, evolution, and early history of exoplanets,” a CNSA representative said.

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third object detected entering our solar system, following 1I/'Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov. Discovered by ATLAS, it is the largest known interstellar object, surpassing previous discoveries in size. The comet's hyperbolic orbit suggests it originated from outside our solar system, potentially offering insights into the early universe.

What’s next

Ongoing analysis by CNSA, ESA, and NASA teams will continue to investigate the comet's characteristics. Ground-based telescopes are expected to observe 3I/ATLAS starting in early December 2025, as it brightens near the sun.
