Interstellar visitor 3I/Atlas crosses the solar system at a speed of 57 km/s on a hyperbolic route

A new interstellar comet, named 3I/Atlas, is currently traversing the solar system at a speed of 57 kilometers per second. This comet is on a hyperbolic trajectory, which means it cannot be captured by the Sun's gravity. Its path has been confirmed through detailed telescopic observations.

The discovery of 3I/Atlas underscores the increasing frequency of detection of interstellar objects. Authorities from various observatories worldwide are tracking its movement to refine calculations of its trajectory and to gather data on its composition.

Key facts

  • Name: 3I/Atlas
  • Speed: 57 km/s
  • Trajectory: Hyperbolic
  • Previous interstellar visitors: 1I/’Oumuamua (2017), 2I/Borisov (2019)
  • Observation method: Telescopic observations

Timeline

3I/Atlas has been confirmed as the third interstellar visitor observed by astronomers. The first two were 1I/’Oumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019. Each of these objects has provided unique insights into the nature of interstellar bodies.

As 3I/Atlas approaches the Sun, its high speed prevents it from being captured into a stable orbit, making it a unique object of study for astronomers worldwide. Observatories are currently monitoring its path to gather more data on its characteristics.

Official statements

“The high speed of 3I/Atlas confirms its interstellar nature, as it is not gravitationally bound to the Sun,” said an astronomer involved in the observations.

Context

3I/Atlas is part of a growing group of interstellar objects that challenge our understanding of the formation and behavior of celestial bodies. Unlike comets that originate from the solar system, these objects are believed to be remnants of planet formation from other star systems.

The study of 3I/Atlas and its predecessors provides researchers with valuable information about the chemical conditions present in other planetary systems, contributing to the broader understanding of the Milky Way.

What’s next

Observatories will continue to track 3I/Atlas as it moves through the solar system, collecting data to refine its trajectory and analyze its chemical composition. This ongoing research aims to enhance understanding of interstellar objects and the environments from which they originate.
