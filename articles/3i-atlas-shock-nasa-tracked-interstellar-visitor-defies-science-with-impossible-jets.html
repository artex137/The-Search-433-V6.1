3I/ATLAS Shock: NASA-Tracked Interstellar Visitor Defies Science With 'Impossible' Jets

An interstellar object known as 3I/ATLAS has baffled astronomers with its unusual behavior since its discovery in July 2025. The comet, which is currently moving through space at a speed of 58 km/s, has exhibited three perfectly symmetrical jets of material, a phenomenon rarely seen in comets. Observations from the Gemini North telescope in Hawaii and NASA's Hubble Space Telescope have confirmed this extraordinary jet configuration.

The significance of this discovery lies in its potential to reshape our understanding of cometary behavior and the origins of such objects. Researchers from NASA and the Breakthrough Listen project are closely monitoring 3I/ATLAS as it approaches Jupiter’s moon Eupheme in March 2026. They emphasize the need for caution, as the object is rapidly moving away from the inner Solar System.

Key facts

  • Object: 3I/ATLAS
  • Discovery: July 2025
  • Current speed: 58 km/s
  • Observation period: Late November to late December 2025
  • Upcoming close approach: March 2026, near Jupiter's moon Eupheme

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first identified in July 2025 as an interstellar visitor. Subsequent observations from late 2025 revealed its unique jet structure, with three symmetrical jets that do not exhibit the typical chaotic behavior of most comets. Researchers have since been analyzing its composition and behavior, seeking to understand its origins and the implications for cometary science.

“This geometry remains locked in place, resisting the usual fluctuations driven by rotation or uneven solar heating,” scientists noted in their findings.

Context

The behavior of 3I/ATLAS challenges established cometary models, which typically predict irregular jet patterns. Most comets display single dominant jets or chaotic sprays, making the symmetrical jets of 3I/ATLAS an anomaly. This discovery may indicate that the object has an unusual internal structure or composition, possibly enriched in carbon dioxide far beyond typical levels.

What’s next

As 3I/ATLAS continues its journey through the Solar System, scientists will monitor it closely for further data, particularly during its approach to Jupiter's moon Eupheme. Researchers aim to gather as much information as possible before the comet becomes too distant for observation.
