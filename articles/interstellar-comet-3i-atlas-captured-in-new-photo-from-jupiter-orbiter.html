Interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS captured in new photo from Jupiter orbiter

The interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS has been photographed by the European Space Agency's Jupiter Icy Moons Explorer (Juice) orbiter, which observed the comet between November 2 and November 25, 2025. The comet, confirmed in July 2025 as the third known object from interstellar space, made its closest approach to Earth on December 19, 2025.

This discovery is significant as it provides scientists with a rare opportunity to study material from another star system. The comet is set to exit our solar system and will not return. Authorities from NASA and the ESA are working to analyze the data and images collected during the comet's brief visit.

Key facts

  • Object Name: 3I/ATLAS
  • Closest Approach to Earth: December 19, 2025
  • Speed: 137,000 mph (220,000 km/h)
  • Distance from Earth at Closest Approach: 170 million miles (273 million km)
  • Observation Period: November 2 - November 25, 2025
  • Image Release Date: February 2026
  • Location of Origin: Constellation Sagittarius

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first detected by a telescope in Chile in July 2025. The object was identified as an interstellar comet, showing characteristics distinct from comets originating within our solar system. It made its closest approach to the sun on October 30, 2025, and subsequently passed Earth on December 19, 2025.

Between November 2 and November 25, 2025, the Juice orbiter captured images of 3I/ATLAS during a period of high activity. The comet's images were transmitted back to Earth and released to the public in February 2026.

Official statements

“3I/ATLAS shows all the signs of being a comet from outside our solar system,” said a NASA spokesperson.
“The images captured by Juice will provide valuable data about the composition of interstellar objects,” noted an ESA official.

Context

3I/ATLAS is only the third object confirmed to have originated from outside the solar system, following previous interstellar objects such as ‘Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov. The study of interstellar comets is crucial for understanding the formation and evolution of other star systems.

This comet has attracted attention not only for its scientific value but also due to conspiracy theories regarding its origin, which NASA officials have firmly dismissed as unfounded.

What’s next

As 3I/ATLAS continues its trajectory out of the solar system, astronomers will observe it until spring 2026. Data from the ESA and NASA will be analyzed to enhance our understanding of interstellar comets and their role in the cosmos.
