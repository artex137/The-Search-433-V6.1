Interestellar comet passes Earth tonight: How to watch once-in-a-lifetime event

Published 2025-12-19 09:01 UTC

Interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS approaches Earth tonight

Interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS will make its closest approach to Earth today, December 18, 2023, at 1 a.m. EST (6 a.m. GMT). The comet, which was first spotted in June 2025, poses no threat to Earth.

This event is significant as 3I/ATLAS is a rare interstellar object, providing a unique opportunity for scientists to study material from beyond our solar system. Authorities and astronomers are encouraging the public to observe this celestial phenomenon, although it will not be visible to the naked eye.

Key facts

  • Closest approach: 1 a.m. EST (6 a.m. GMT) on December 18, 2023
  • Distance from Earth: 270 million kilometers
  • First spotted: June 2025 by ATLAS survey telescope in Chile
  • Previous interstellar visitors: 1I/ʻOumuamua (2017) and 2I/Borisov (2019)
  • Visibility: Not visible to the naked eye or regular binoculars
  • Livestream: The Virtual Telescope Project, starting at 11 p.m. EST (4 a.m. GMT)

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first detected in June 2025 by the Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System (ATLAS) telescope in Chile. A few weeks later, its interstellar origin was confirmed due to its speed and trajectory. The comet is now set to pass Earth, offering a rare chance for observation.

Official statements

“This is a unique opportunity to study material from another star system,” said an astronomer involved with the ATLAS project.

Context

Prior to 3I/ATLAS, only two interstellar objects have been confirmed. 1I/ʻOumuamua made headlines in 2017 for its unusual shape and speed, while 2I/Borisov was identified in 2019 as a more typical comet. The study of interstellar objects helps scientists understand the formation and evolution of celestial bodies beyond our solar system.

What’s next

Following its closest approach, 3I/ATLAS will continue on its trajectory out of the solar system and will never return. Observations and data collected during this event will contribute to ongoing research in astronomy.
