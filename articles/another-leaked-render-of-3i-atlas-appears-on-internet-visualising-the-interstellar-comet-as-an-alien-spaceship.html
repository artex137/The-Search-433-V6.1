Another 'Leaked' Render Of 3I/ATLAS Appears On Internet, Visualising the Interstellar Comet As An Alien Spaceship

A new render of the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS has surfaced on social media, sparking interest among astronomy enthusiasts. The image was shared by a user on X, claiming it was released by the Japanese space agency JAXA, although the agency has not confirmed this, raising questions about its authenticity.

This incident highlights ongoing public fascination with 3I/ATLAS and the potential for misinformation surrounding astronomical discoveries. Authorities, including NASA, have reiterated that 3I/ATLAS poses no risk to Earth, as it maintains a safe distance during its passage through the solar system.

Key facts

  • 3I/ATLAS was discovered on July 1, 2025, by NASA’s ATLAS telescope in Rio Hurtado, Chile.
  • The comet's orbital path extends beyond our solar system, making it an interstellar object.
  • Chinese astronomer Qicheng Zhang captured images of the comet, showing a green glow, using a diatomic carbon filter.
  • The European Space Agency (ESA) captured images of 3I/ATLAS as it passed Mars on October 3, 2025.
  • NASA has confirmed that 3I/ATLAS is the third known interstellar visitor, following 1I/ʻOumuamua and 2I/Borisov.

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first discovered on July 1, 2025, by NASA’s ATLAS telescope in Chile. Indian astronomers photographed the comet using the Himalayan Chandra Telescope on July 3, 2025. On October 3, 2025, the ESA captured images of the comet as it flew past Mars. Recently, Qicheng Zhang reported capturing images showcasing the comet's distinctive green glow.

Official statements

“It’s sort of for the same reason that if we stay out in the sun too long without sunscreen, we get sunburnt,” said Qicheng Zhang, explaining the effects of ultraviolet light on the comet's molecules.

Context

Interstellar comets like 3I/ATLAS provide valuable insights into the formation of celestial bodies in other star systems. NASA has monitored 3I/ATLAS closely, confirming that it poses no threat to Earth.

What’s next

NASA and other space agencies will continue to study 3I/ATLAS as it travels through the solar system, focusing on its composition and the information it can provide about interstellar objects.
