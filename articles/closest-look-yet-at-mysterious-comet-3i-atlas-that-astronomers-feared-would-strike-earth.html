Closest look yet at mysterious comet 3I/ATLAS that astronomers feared would strike Earth

A European spacecraft has captured the most detailed images of comet 3I/ATLAS, which sparked concern among astronomers last year due to a small chance it could collide with Earth. The images were taken by the Jupiter Icy Moons Explorer (Juice) spacecraft on November 6, 2022, when it was approximately 66 million km from the comet.

These observations are significant as they provide insight into the comet's behavior and composition. The European Space Agency (ESA) is now analyzing the data collected during the mission to better understand this interstellar visitor.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS was observed by ESA's Juice spacecraft.
  • The images were taken on November 6, 2022.
  • Juice was about 66 million km away from the comet during the observations.
  • JANUS, MAJIS, SWI, PEP, and UVS instruments were used to collect data.
  • The spacecraft is scheduled to arrive at Jupiter in July 2031.

Timeline

Comet 3I/ATLAS was discovered last year, raising concerns about a potential impact with Earth. Further observations eased those fears. On November 6, 2022, Juice captured detailed images of the comet, revealing its dust and gas emissions. The spacecraft then transmitted the data back to Earth, which was received in late March 2023 after Juice moved to the opposite side of the Sun.

In total, more than 120 images were taken of 3I/ATLAS across various wavelengths. Teams are currently analyzing the data to understand the comet's composition and behavior.

Official statements

“The images show the object spewing dust and gas as it continues on its path,” said a spokesperson for the European Space Agency.
“The behavior of 3I/ATLAS is completely in line with what we expect from a normal comet,” added an ESA scientist involved in the mission.

Context

Prior to the observations, 3I/ATLAS had raised alarms among scientists due to its trajectory. However, further analysis indicated that the comet posed no threat to Earth. The ongoing study of such comets is crucial in understanding the dynamics of interstellar objects entering the Solar System.

What’s next

The ESA teams are working to analyze the data collected from the comet and will meet in late March to discuss their findings. The information gathered will help enhance our understanding of cometary behavior and composition.
