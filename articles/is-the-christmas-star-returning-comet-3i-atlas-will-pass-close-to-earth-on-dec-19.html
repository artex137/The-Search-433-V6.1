Published 2025-12-18 21:56 UTC

Comet 3I/ATLAS to Pass Close to Earth on December 19

Comet 3I/ATLAS will make its closest approach to Earth on December 19, 2023, according to astronomers. The comet, known for its bright appearance, is expected to be visible in the night sky during this event.

The approach of Comet 3I/ATLAS is significant for astronomers and stargazers alike. Observers anticipate a spectacular view, as the comet will pass within approximately 0.09 astronomical units (13.5 million kilometres) of Earth. Astronomical societies are planning events to educate the public about the comet and its features.

Key facts

  • Comet Name: 3I/ATLAS
  • Date of Closest Approach: December 19, 2023
  • Distance from Earth: Approximately 0.09 astronomical units (13.5 million kilometres)
  • Visibility: Expected to be visible in the night sky
  • Public Events: Organized by astronomical societies

Timeline

Comet 3I/ATLAS was first discovered in 2020. Since then, it has been observed as it travels through the solar system. As its approach to Earth has drawn closer, interest in the comet has increased among both scientists and the general public.

Leading up to December 19, various astronomical organizations have scheduled viewing events and educational sessions to help the public learn more about the comet and its significance.

Official statements

“The visibility of Comet 3I/ATLAS provides a unique opportunity for everyone to engage with astronomy,” said an official from a local astronomical society.

Context

The appearance of comets has historically sparked public interest and scientific study. Comet 3I/ATLAS is part of a larger trend of increased comet sightings in recent years, which has been attributed to advancements in observational technology.

Previous comets, such as Comet NEOWISE in 2020, have also garnered significant attention, leading to a resurgence of interest in amateur astronomy.

What’s next

As December 19 approaches, astronomers will continue to monitor Comet 3I/ATLAS. Observational data will be collected to study its composition and trajectory, contributing to ongoing research in the field of astronomy.
