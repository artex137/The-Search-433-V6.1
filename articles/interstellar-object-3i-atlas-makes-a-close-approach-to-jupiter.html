Interstellar Object 3I/ATLAS Makes A Close Approach To Jupiter

An interstellar object known as 3I/ATLAS has made a close approach to Jupiter, passing approximately 0.35832 astronomical units (AU) from the gas giant. The object, which was first detected last year by the Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System (ATLAS), has been under close observation by astronomers.

This close flyby is significant as researchers are analyzing the chemical composition of 3I/ATLAS to gather insights about its origins and the early activity of the Milky Way. The information may shed light on the conditions under which the object formed and its potential home system.

Key facts

  • Object: 3I/ATLAS
  • Detection: First detected in 2022 by ATLAS
  • Distance from Jupiter: Approximately 0.35832 AU
  • Estimated age: 10-12 billion years
  • Research team: Not disclosed
  • Study status: Not peer-reviewed
  • Publication: Posted on arXiv

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first detected by the Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System (ATLAS) in 2022. Since then, it has made close flybys of Mars, Earth, and most recently, Jupiter.

Following the flyby of Jupiter, researchers have begun analyzing the data collected to better understand the object's composition and origins. A new team has published a study that examines these aspects and aims to provide further insights.

Official statements

“Tracing the orbital trajectory back more than ∼ 10 Myr is difficult due to unpredictable (chaotic) gravitational interactions within the inhomogeneous Galactic environment,” the research team explained in their paper.
“Additional isotopic information will help illuminate 3I/ATLAS’s origin, leading to a better appreciation of how it can fit into, and help improve our understanding of, the processes of planet formation and Galactic chemical evolution,” the team added.

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third interstellar object detected in our Solar System. Its unique elemental composition has raised interest among astronomers, as it differs from any known Solar System body. The study of such objects is crucial for understanding the formation of planets and the chemical evolution of galaxies.

What’s next

Researchers plan to use spectral imaging observations from the James Webb Telescope to gain more definitive answers about the origins of 3I/ATLAS. Further analysis of isotopic information will also contribute to ongoing studies of the object.
