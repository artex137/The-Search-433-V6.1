Alien Theories Swirl As 3I ATLAS Baffles Astronomers

The astronomical community is buzzing over the arrival of comet 3I/ATLAS, which first entered the solar system in July 2025. This object is only the third known interstellar visitor, following Oumuamua in 2017 and Borisov in 2019, and has sparked speculation regarding its unusual features and potential artificial origin.

Scientists are closely monitoring 3I/ATLAS, which weighs approximately 33 billion tons and spans between 1.7 miles and 3.1 miles in diameter. The comet has displayed strange behaviors, including an anti-tail composed of carbon dioxide, water, cyanide, and a unique nickel alloy, prompting debate about whether it is a natural comet or an alien spacecraft. The International Asteroid Warning Network has added it to its list of monitored objects, calling it a significant opportunity for observation.

Key facts

  • 3I/ATLAS detected in July 2025.
  • Weighs approximately 33 billion tons.
  • Diameter estimated between 1.7 miles and 3.1 miles.
  • Closest approach to the Sun set for October 29, 2025.
  • Passed within 12 million miles of Mars in early October 2025.
  • Next visible on December 19, 2025, after being hidden by the Sun.

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first detected in early July 2025. By August 6, NASA’s James Webb Space Telescope captured images of the comet. The Hubble Space Telescope followed suit on July 21. In September, images from the Nordic Optical Telescope revealed the comet's unusual anti-tail. The International Asteroid Warning Network began monitoring the object in mid-October.

On October 26, 2025, 3I/ATLAS became obscured by the Sun and will remain unobservable from Earth for about a month. Scientists anticipate its reappearance on December 19, 2025, when they will analyze its trajectory for any signs of artificial alteration.

Official statements

“If the object is an alien spacecraft slowing down, and the anti-tail is braking thrust, then this change from anti-tail to tail would be entirely expected near perihelion,” said Avi Loeb, a Harvard astrophysicist.
“It is clearly a natural comet; suggestions to the contrary are laughed at by people who are actual comet experts,” stated Jonathan McDowell, an astronomer at the Harvard–Smithsonian Center for Astrophysics.

Context

3I/ATLAS has generated significant interest due to its peculiar characteristics, including its immense size and chemical composition. While most astronomers classify it as a natural comet, a minority, led by Avi Loeb, argue that its behavior could indicate an artificial origin. Previous interstellar objects, like Oumuamua and Borisov, did not provoke such intense speculation.

What’s next

Scientists will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS, particularly during its upcoming closest approach to the Sun and its flyby of Jupiter’s Juno spacecraft in March 2026. Researchers are also awaiting high-resolution images from NASA’s HiRISE camera, which have been delayed. The scientific community remains divided on the nature of this enigmatic visitor as it continues to be a subject of intense scrutiny.
