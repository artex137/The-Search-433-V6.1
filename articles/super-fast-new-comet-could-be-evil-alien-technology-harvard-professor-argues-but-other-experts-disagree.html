Super-Fast New Comet Could Be Evil 'Alien Technology,' Harvard Professor Argues — but Other Experts Disagree

A new comet named 3I/ATLAS, discovered speeding through our solar system, has sparked debate among scientists regarding its origins. The comet, first spotted on July 1, 2023, by a telescope in Chile, has been described in a recent research paper co-written by Harvard University physicist Avi Loeb as potentially being “alien technology” and possibly hostile. No injuries or fatalities have been reported in relation to the comet.

This discussion matters as it highlights differing views on the nature of celestial bodies and the implications for understanding extraterrestrial life. Authorities, particularly NASA, have clarified that 3I/ATLAS poses no threat to Earth and is simply an interstellar comet formed in another solar system.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS discovered on July 1, 2023, in Chile.
  • It is traveling at a speed of 137,000 miles per hour.
  • 3I/ATLAS is expected to reach its closest point to the Sun by the end of October 2023, remaining 130 million miles away.
  • It is the third known interstellar object discovered, following Oumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019.
  • NASA is currently investigating the comet's size and physical properties.

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first detected on July 1, 2023, by a telescope in Chile. Observations dating back to mid-June have also been recorded. The comet is predicted to reach its closest approach to the Sun at the end of October 2023.

Official statements

Avi Loeb, Harvard University physicist, stated, “We should put all possibilities on the table that it's a rock, a comet, or something else until we get the evidence, the data that will tell us what it is.”
Samantha Lawler, an astronomer at the University of Regina, commented, “All evidence points to this being an ordinary comet that was ejected from another solar system.”
Darryl Seligman from Michigan State University added, “There have been numerous telescopic observations of 3I/ATLAS demonstrating that it's displaying classical signatures of cometary activity.”

Context

The debate surrounding 3I/ATLAS mirrors previous discussions about other interstellar objects, such as Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov, which have also raised questions regarding their origins and implications for extraterrestrial life. The scientific community generally favors explanations based on observable data and established astronomical principles.

What’s next

Astronomers worldwide are continuing to investigate the physical properties of 3I/ATLAS as it approaches the Sun, with further observations expected in the coming weeks.
