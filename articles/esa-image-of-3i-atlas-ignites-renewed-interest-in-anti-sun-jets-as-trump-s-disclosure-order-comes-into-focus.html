ESA Image of 3I/ATLAS Ignites Renewed Interest In Anti-Sun Jets As Trump’s Disclosure Order Comes Into Focus

Published 2026-03-01 03:01 UTC

New Image of Comet 3I/ATLAS Sparks Scientific Interest Amid Disclosure Order

The European Space Agency (ESA) has released a new image of comet 3I/ATLAS, captured by the JANUS camera aboard the Jupiter Icy Moons Explorer (Juice) spacecraft, on November 6, 2025. The image shows jets emerging from the nucleus of the comet in an unusual orientation, raising questions among planetary scientists about its behavior following its closest approach to the Sun.

This release is significant as it comes shortly after a presidential directive from President Donald Trump, which calls for federal agencies to disclose information related to unidentified flying objects (UFOs) and extraterrestrial life. Scientists are now examining whether any classified data on 3I/ATLAS exists in government archives that could provide further insights into its unusual activity.

Key facts

  • Date of image capture: November 6, 2025
  • Distance from Earth: Approximately 66 million kilometers
  • Diameter of nucleus: Approximately 2.6 kilometers
  • Next significant pass: March 16, 2026, within 53.6 million kilometers of Jupiter
  • Instruments used: JANUS, MAJIS, SWI, PEP, and UVS aboard Juice

Timeline

On November 6, 2025, the Juice spacecraft captured over 120 images of 3I/ATLAS, revealing jets directed away from the Sun. This behavior contradicts standard cometary physics, which typically sees jets form towards the Sun. In late February 2026, President Trump issued a directive to federal agencies to release information related to UFOs and extraterrestrial life, potentially impacting the availability of classified data on 3I/ATLAS.

Official statements

Avi Loeb, a Harvard astrophysicist, stated, “The released frame shows jets directed anti-sunward, a surprising orientation because solar heating typically drives sublimation on the sun-facing side of icy bodies.”

Context

Historically, interstellar objects have been studied primarily through civilian astronomical networks. However, the U.S. Department of Defense operates space surveillance systems that track near-Earth objects with greater precision. There is currently no verified evidence to suggest that 3I/ATLAS is artificial, but the potential existence of classified military data remains an open question.

What’s next

The global scientific community is now focused on further observation of 3I/ATLAS, particularly during its upcoming pass near Jupiter, while investigations into the potential existence of classified data related to the comet continue.
