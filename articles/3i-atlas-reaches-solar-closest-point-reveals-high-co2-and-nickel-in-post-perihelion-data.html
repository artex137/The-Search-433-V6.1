3I/ATLAS Reaches Solar Closest Point, Reveals High CO2 and Nickel in Post-Perihelion Data

Comet 3I/ATLAS has reached its closest point to the Sun, revealing significant findings including high levels of carbon dioxide and nickel, according to data from various observatories. The closest approach occurred on October 30, 2025, as the comet continued its trajectory without any deviations, maintaining a speed of 68 kilometers per second.

This discovery is crucial for understanding the comet's composition and origins, with implications for models of exoplanet formation. Various space and ground-based observatories are actively monitoring the comet's behavior and chemical emissions.

Key facts

  • Closest approach to the Sun: October 30, 2025
  • Speed: 68 kilometers per second
  • Distance from the Sun: Not disclosed
  • CO2-to-water ratio: 8:1, exceeding solar system norms by six times
  • Nickel vapor detected beyond 6 AU
  • Closest Earth approach: December 19, 2025, at 1.8 AU (270 million kilometers)

Timeline

Observations of 3I/ATLAS have taken place over several months:

  • July 21, 2025: Hubble captures an image of the comet's droplet-shaped nucleus.
  • August 2025: James Webb Space Telescope confirms a carbon dioxide-dominated coma.
  • October 3, 2025: Mars orbiters MAVEN and ExoMars TGO gather data from a flyby.
  • October 30, 2025: The comet reaches its closest point to the Sun.

Official statements

“The high CO2 levels and the presence of nickel vapor provide valuable insights into the comet's formation and age,” said an astronomer involved in the study.

Context

3I/ATLAS has an eccentricity of about 6, ensuring it will exit the solar system unbound to the Sun. This behavior contrasts with other comets such as 2I/Borisov. The ongoing observations are part of a broader effort to understand interstellar material and its role in planetary formation.

What’s next

The International Asteroid Warning Network will conduct a special exercise from November 27, 2025, to January 27, 2026, focusing on comet astrometry and improving orbital measurements for 3I/ATLAS.
