Most sensitive radio observations to date find no evidence of technosignature from 3I/ATLAS

Researchers have reported no evidence of a technosignature from the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS following the most sensitive radio observations to date, conducted at the Green Bank Telescope in West Virginia on December 18, 2025. The findings were published on December 30, 2025, and come after 3I/ATLAS was discovered on July 1, 2025.

The significance of this research lies in the ongoing search for signs of alien life. The Breakthrough Listen program, which aims to detect potential signals from extraterrestrial civilizations, conducted the observations just before 3I/ATLAS's closest approach to Earth. Despite initial speculation surrounding the object, scientists have found no indications of advanced technology.

Key facts

  • Object: 3I/ATLAS
  • Discovery date: July 1, 2025
  • Observation date: December 18, 2025
  • Location of observations: Green Bank Telescope, West Virginia
  • Program: Breakthrough Listen
  • Publication date of findings: December 30, 2025

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was discovered on July 1, 2025, and quickly attracted attention due to its interstellar origin. On December 18, 2025, researchers made radio observations of the comet at the Green Bank Telescope, focusing on potential signs of alien technology. These observations were part of the Breakthrough Listen program, which aims to identify technosignatures. The results were published on December 30, 2025.

Official statements

“There is currently no evidence to suggest that interstellar objects are anything other than natural astrophysical objects,” the researchers stated in their findings.

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third interstellar object observed entering our solar system. The first, 1I/"Oumuamua, was initially classified as an asteroid before being reclassified as a comet. The second, 2I/Borisov, was confirmed as a comet. Both prior observations generated significant interest and speculation about the possibility of extraterrestrial life.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to monitor interstellar objects and analyze new data as it becomes available. The scientific community remains focused on understanding the nature of these objects and their implications for life beyond Earth.
