Interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS is leaking water in space, NASA says it’s unlike anything ever seen before

NASA has reported that the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS is releasing water vapor while positioned nearly three times farther from the Sun than Earth. This discovery has astonished astronomers and marks a rare cosmic event.

The release of water vapor from 3I/ATLAS, detected by scientists using NASA’s Neil Gehrels Swift Observatory, challenges existing knowledge of cometary behavior. Researchers have noted that this phenomenon occurs in a region typically too cold for ice to sublimate, making it a significant finding in the study of interstellar objects.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS is releasing approximately 40 kilograms of water every second.
  • It is emitting around 1.36 × 1022 molecules of water per second.
  • At least 8% of its surface appears to be active, compared to 3–5% in typical comets.
  • 3I/ATLAS is the third confirmed interstellar object, following ‘Oumuamua in 2017 and comet Borisov in 2019.
  • The comet’s activity was detected at a distance of about 3 astronomical units from the Sun.

Timeline

3I/ATLAS has been observed for its unique properties since its identification as an interstellar object. The detection of water vapor began when researchers utilized NASA's Neil Gehrels Swift Observatory.

As scientists studied the comet, they found that the water vapor was produced through a two-step outgassing process, which is rare in interstellar comets. This process allows microscopic ice grains to be released into space, where they warm and sublimate.

Official statements

“Every interstellar comet we study tells a different story,” said lead researcher Zexi Xing. “ATLAS’s distant water activity could rewrite existing cometary models.”

Context

The discovery of 3I/ATLAS contributes to a growing understanding of interstellar comets and their role in the solar system. Unlike its predecessors, which either lacked water or were rich in carbon, 3I/ATLAS is chemically distinct and highlights the diversity of materials that can form around different stars.

This finding provides insight into how the ingredients for life may be transported across the galaxy, suggesting that interstellar comets could deliver essential water and organic molecules to planetary systems.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to study 3I/ATLAS to gain further insights into its composition and behavior. These studies may help to illuminate the processes that shape planetary systems and the origins of life in the universe.
