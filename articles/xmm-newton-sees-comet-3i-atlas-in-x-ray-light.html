XMM-Newton sees comet 3I/ATLAS in X-ray light

The European Space Agency’s X-ray space observatory XMM-Newton has observed interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS for approximately 20 hours on 3 December. During this observation, the comet was located about 282–285 million kilometres from the spacecraft.

This observation is significant because it allows scientists to study the comet's composition through X-ray emissions, which are produced when gas molecules from the comet interact with the solar wind. These findings may enhance understanding of interstellar objects and their characteristics.

Key facts

  • Date of observation: 3 December
  • Distance from XMM-Newton: 282–285 million km
  • Duration of observation: Approximately 20 hours
  • Instrument used: European Photon Imaging Camera (EPIC)-pn

Timeline

On 3 December, XMM-Newton conducted a detailed observation of comet 3I/ATLAS. The observatory utilized its most sensitive X-ray camera, EPIC-pn, to capture the comet's emissions.

Astronomers anticipated detecting X-ray emissions due to the interaction between gas molecules from the comet and the solar wind. This interaction generates X-rays, which can provide insights into the gases present in the comet's atmosphere.

Official statements

“X-ray observations are a powerful tool. They allow scientists to detect and study gases that other instruments can’t easily spot,” said a representative from the European Space Agency.

Context

The first detected interstellar object, 1I/'Oumuamua, was identified in 2017. Scientists believe it may have contained exotic ice, such as nitrogen or hydrogen. While 1I/'Oumuamua is now too distant for observation, 3I/ATLAS offers a new chance to study interstellar material.

What’s next

Further observations of comet 3I/ATLAS will complement existing data from other telescopes, aiding scientists in determining its composition and characteristics.
