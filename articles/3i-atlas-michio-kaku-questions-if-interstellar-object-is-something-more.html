3I/ATLAS: Michio Kaku Questions If Interstellar Object Is Something More

The interstellar object 3I/ATLAS has become the focus of a scientific debate following its detection on 1 July 2025 by the ATLAS survey telescope in Chile. Renowned physicist Dr Michio Kaku and Harvard astrophysicist Professor Avi Loeb have called for greater transparency and analysis of the object's unusual characteristics.

This matter is significant as 3I/ATLAS is only the third confirmed interstellar visitor to our solar system, following 'Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov. NASA estimates the object's nucleus measures between 440 metres and 5.6 kilometres. Kaku has criticized NASA for not releasing high-resolution images and spectrographic data, arguing that this lack of transparency fuels speculation and hinders scientific understanding.

Key facts

  • Detected on 1 July 2025 by the ATLAS survey telescope in Chile.
  • Formally designated C/2025 N1 (ATLAS).
  • Only the third confirmed interstellar visitor to the solar system.
  • Nucleus estimated to measure between 440 metres and 5.6 kilometres.
  • Kaku emphasizes the need for public access to all relevant data.

Timeline

1 July 2025: The ATLAS survey telescope in Chile detects the interstellar object designated 3I/ATLAS.

Following its detection, Dr Michio Kaku raises concerns regarding the lack of available data from NASA.

Kaku also warns about the spread of AI-generated deepfake videos misrepresenting his views on the object.

Official statements

“This isn't something that needs to be classified,” Kaku said, emphasizing the importance of transparency in scientific inquiry.
“Speculation is fine. But you have to quantify it,” he added regarding the need for rigorous evaluation of the object.
“There could be natural reasons for the sudden change in speed ... that's not a smoking gun,” Kaku stated about the object's behaviour.

Context

The discussion surrounding 3I/ATLAS highlights ongoing challenges in studying interstellar visitors and the impact of misinformation fueled by advanced AI tools. Kaku and Loeb both underscore the need for open scientific inquiry into rare phenomena.

What’s next

Both Kaku and Loeb continue to advocate for the public release of data to facilitate thorough investigation and analysis of 3I/ATLAS's characteristics and behaviour.
