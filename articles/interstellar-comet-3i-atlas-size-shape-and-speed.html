Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS Sparks Debate Among Scientists

The interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS has been racing through our solar system at unprecedented speeds since its discovery on 1 July 2025 by the Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System (ATLAS) survey. Observations from NASA’s Hubble Space Telescope on 21 July 2025 have revealed its teardrop shape and a solid icy nucleus, measuring between 320 metres and 5.6 kilometres in diameter. As of now, there have been no reported injuries or fatalities related to this celestial event.

The comet's unusual size, shape, and trajectory have ignited discussions among scientists, researchers, and space enthusiasts regarding its true nature. Some experts speculate that 3I/ATLAS could be an artificial object rather than a natural comet. The ongoing analysis of its physical properties, including its record speed of 137,000 miles per hour (61 kilometres per second), has intensified interest in this cosmic visitor.

Key facts

  • Discovered: 1 July 2025 by ATLAS survey
  • Observation date: 21 July 2025
  • Maximum speed: 137,000 miles per hour (61 kilometres per second)
  • Closest approach to Mars: 29 million kilometres on 3 October 2025
  • Perihelion date: 30 October 2025, at 1.4 astronomical units from the Sun
  • Approaching Jupiter: 16 March 2026
  • Estimated mass: Over 33 billion tonnes (speculative)

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first discovered on 1 July 2025 by the ATLAS survey. Following this, NASA's Hubble Space Telescope captured observations of the comet on 21 July 2025, revealing its teardrop shape and icy nucleus. On 3 October 2025, the comet passed within 29 million kilometres of Mars. It is expected to reach perihelion on 30 October 2025, before approaching Jupiter on 16 March 2026.

Official statements

“We have found that none of the stars in the solar neighbourhood can explain the trajectory and high velocity of 3I/ATLAS,” said Xabier Pérez-Couto, lead author of a 2025 study tracing its origins.
“If the trajectory of 3I/ATLAS was designed by extraterrestrial intelligence, why is it inclined by about 5 degrees relative to the ecliptic plane?” questioned Harvard astrophysicist Avi Loeb.

Context

The debate surrounding 3I/ATLAS is not new. Previous interstellar objects, such as 'Oumuamua, have also prompted discussions regarding their origins and nature. While most scientists support a natural explanation for such celestial bodies, anomalies like those observed in 3I/ATLAS continue to fuel theories about potential extraterrestrial technology.

What’s next

As 3I/ATLAS continues its journey through the solar system, it remains a prime target for future studies by Mars and Jupiter space probes. Scientists will closely monitor its trajectory and physical properties as new data becomes available.
