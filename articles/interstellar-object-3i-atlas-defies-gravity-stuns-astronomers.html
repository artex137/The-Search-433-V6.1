Interstellar Object 3I/ATLAS Defies Gravity, Stuns Astronomers

Astronomers have expressed confusion over 3I/ATLAS (C/2025 N1), an interstellar object discovered on 1 July 2025 by the ATLAS telescope in Chile. The object has shown unexplained changes in its orbit and an unusual chemical composition. No injuries or fatalities have been reported.

The peculiar behaviour of 3I/ATLAS has raised significant questions about its nature and origin. NASA and the European Space Agency (ESA) have announced plans for further observations of the object following its perihelion, which will occur on 29 October 2025. These observations aim to gather more data on its structure and trajectory.

Key facts

  • Discovered: 1 July 2025, by ATLAS telescope in Chile
  • Perihelion date: 29 October 2025, at a distance of 203 million kilometres from the Sun
  • Rank on Loeb Scale: 4, indicating high orbital anomalies
  • Number of trajectory deviations: Eight
  • Expected observation period post-perihelion: Early November 2025

Timeline

3I/ATLAS has displayed eight distinct deviations from its expected trajectory, leading to its classification on the Loeb Scale. This scale categorises objects based on their motion anomalies, placing 3I/ATLAS above both ʻOumuamua and Borisov.

On 21 October 2025, 3I/ATLAS entered solar conjunction, positioning itself directly behind the Sun from Earth's perspective. This timing coincides with a theoretical opportunity for maximum-efficiency trajectory modification known as the Oberth manoeuvre.

Following its perihelion on 29 October, the object will begin its outbound journey, making it easier to observe in early November.

Official statements

“3I/ATLAS displays properties unlike typical solar-system comets, sparking questions about what forces might be acting upon it,” said a NASA spokesperson.
“While some online speculation has veered into sensational territory, 3I/ATLAS poses no threat to Earth,” emphasized an ESA official.

Context

The discovery of 3I/ATLAS adds to a growing list of interstellar objects that challenge current astronomical understanding. Similar anomalies were observed in ʻOumuamua, which also exhibited unexplained acceleration. The ongoing study of such objects is crucial in understanding their origins and compositions.

What’s next

NASA and ESA will release preliminary data in mid-November 2025, followed by a full spectral and trajectory report by the end of the year. Researchers aim to determine whether the unusual motion of 3I/ATLAS results from natural physical processes or represents a new class of interstellar behaviour.
