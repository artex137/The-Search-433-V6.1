Will 3I/ATLAS break up? Astronomers look for proof of Alien Mothership ‘Interstellar Visitor’ approaching

The interstellar object 3I/ATLAS is approaching its closest point to the Sun, known as perihelion, on October 29, 2025, coming within 203 million kilometers of our star. Astronomers are monitoring the object closely to determine if it will maintain its integrity or break into smaller fragments as it nears the Sun.

This observation is significant as it could reveal whether 3I/ATLAS is a natural comet or a more unusual object, possibly of technological origin. Scientists are utilizing various observational tools, including the European Space Agency's ExoMars Trace Gas Orbiter, to gather data and analyze the object's features and behaviors.

Key facts

  • 3I/ATLAS will reach perihelion on October 29, 2025.
  • The object will come within 203 million kilometers of the Sun.
  • No fragments or small objects have been definitively associated with 3I/ATLAS to date.
  • Recent images from the ExoMars Trace Gas Orbiter suggest faint features, likely noise artifacts.
  • Galileo Project is monitoring unidentified objects in Earth's atmosphere.
  • Studies indicate that comets can break apart due to solar heating, gravitational forces, or rotational stress.

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first detected in 2025 and has since been the subject of intense scientific scrutiny. As it approaches perihelion, astronomers are concerned about its structural integrity and potential fragmentation.

Recent observations have focused on identifying any small fragments or objects associated with 3I/ATLAS. Current data from the ExoMars Trace Gas Orbiter has not confirmed any definitive connections to smaller objects.

Official statements

Avi Loeb, a Harvard Professor, stated, “No small objects or fragments have been definitively associated with 3I/ATLAS.”
“Any new activity could provide critical clues about the nature of this interstellar object,” researchers noted.

Context

Previous studies of comets, such as 67P/Churyumov-Gerasimenko, show that these celestial bodies are often loosely bound structures. They are formed from smaller pieces and can easily fragment due to various stresses as they approach the Sun. Historical events, such as the breakup of comet Shoemaker-Levy 9 by Jupiter in 1994, illustrate how gravitational forces can impact comet integrity.

What’s next

After perihelion, 3I/ATLAS will be observed by ESA’s Jupiter Icy Moons Explorer in November, and terrestrial observatories will continue monitoring the object through December. Scientists aim to determine whether 3I/ATLAS behaves like a natural comet or exhibits signs of artificial deployment.
