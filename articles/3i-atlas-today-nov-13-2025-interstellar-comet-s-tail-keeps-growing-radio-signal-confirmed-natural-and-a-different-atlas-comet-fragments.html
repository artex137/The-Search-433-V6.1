3I/ATLAS Today (Nov. 13, 2025): Interstellar comet’s tail keeps growing, ‘radio signal’ confirmed natural, and a different ATLAS comet fragments

Fresh observations on November 13, 2025, have confirmed that 3I/ATLAS, the third confirmed interstellar object, remains a natural comet, while a different ATLAS-discovered comet, C/2025 K1 (ATLAS), is visibly fragmenting. No injuries or fatalities have been reported.

This discovery is significant as it helps clarify the nature of 3I/ATLAS and distinguishes it from other celestial bodies. Authorities, including NASA and various astronomers, continue to monitor both comets closely, providing updates on their trajectories and characteristics.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS is currently on a hyperbolic trajectory through the inner solar system.
  • 3I/ATLAS is located approximately 1.8 AU from Earth, posing no threat.
  • Images captured on Nov. 10-11 show the ion tail of 3I/ATLAS stretching roughly 0.7 degrees.
  • NASA and ESA have launched a wide campaign of observations, utilizing assets like Hubble and Webb.
  • C/2025 K1 (ATLAS) is showing clear signs of fragmentation, with multiple sub-components visible.

Timeline

On October 30, 2025, 3I/ATLAS reached perihelion, coming within approximately 1.4 AU of the Sun. Observations have shown that as it warmed, it began venting water, leading to increased outgassing.

On November 11, astronomers captured images of 3I/ATLAS which led to discussions about its integrity and potential unusual features. Following this, on November 13, new imagery confirmed the comet's tail has grown and a natural radio signal was detected.

Simultaneously, C/2025 K1 (ATLAS) exhibited significant fragmentation, with new images showing multiple pieces in its inner coma.

Official statements

“The radio detection confirms that the hydroxyl absorption is a result of natural processes, not technology,” NASA stated.
Harvard’s Avi Loeb commented, “Images suggest 3I/ATLAS remains a single body, but we need more observations to understand its peculiar features.”

Context

3I/ATLAS is only the third interstellar visitor studied by scientists, following 1I/‘Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov. Unlike its predecessors, 3I/ATLAS displays classic cometary features, providing a unique opportunity for comparison with Solar System comets.

C/2025 K1 (ATLAS) has been under observation since its discovery, with its recent fragmentation highlighting the volatile nature of comets from the Oort Cloud.

What’s next

NASA and ESA will continue to monitor both comets through ground- and space-based assets. Coordinated observations are planned throughout November 2025, with 3I/ATLAS expected to pass beyond Jupiter in March 2026.
