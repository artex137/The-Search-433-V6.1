Scientists Want to Catch 3I/ATLAS Before It Escapes the Solar System, But Mission Could Take 50 Years

Scientists have proposed a mission to intercept interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, which is currently moving away from the solar system at speeds exceeding 60 kilometres per second. The mission, which could last up to 50 years, aims to provide the first close-up study of materials from another star system, according to researchers from the Initiative for Interstellar Studies (i4is).

This mission is significant because it could yield valuable insights into planetary formation beyond our solar system. The team plans to launch a spacecraft in 2035, using a Solar Oberth manoeuvre to accelerate towards the comet, which is already on a trajectory out of the solar system.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS is moving at over 60 km/s relative to the Sun.
  • A launch is planned for 2035, optimally aligning Earth, Jupiter, and the Sun with the comet.
  • The mission could take around 50 years to complete.
  • This is the third interstellar object detected in our solar system, following 'Oumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019.

Timeline

The mission's timeline begins with the detection of comet 3I/ATLAS, which occurred after it had already passed Jupiter's orbit. As a result, researchers missed the optimal launch window for a direct approach. In response, scientists from i4is have developed an alternative strategy involving the Solar Oberth manoeuvre, which they plan to implement in 2035.

This manoeuvre will allow the spacecraft to use the Sun's gravity to gain speed and intercept the comet despite its high velocity. The long duration of the mission reflects the challenges of reaching an object that is already leaving the solar system.

Official statements

“The timing is crucial: it reduces the demands on the launch vehicle, limits the spacecraft's fuel requirements, and shortens the mission's flight time,” said Adam Hibberd, a software and research engineer with i4is.

Context

Interstellar comets like 3I/ATLAS present unique opportunities for scientific study. They carry materials from their home star systems, which could provide insights into planetary formation and the composition of distant planetary systems. Past missions have focused on comets originating within our solar system, making 3I/ATLAS a rare subject for study.

Current technology is sufficient for the proposed Solar Oberth approach, but alternatives such as directed-energy propulsion (DEP) remain years away from operational readiness.

What’s next

The i4is team will continue to refine their plans for the 2035 launch, focusing on the Solar Oberth manoeuvre to intercept 3I/ATLAS. Scientists are optimistic that the mission will yield groundbreaking data about the chemical makeup and structure of the comet, enhancing our understanding of planetary systems across the galaxy.
