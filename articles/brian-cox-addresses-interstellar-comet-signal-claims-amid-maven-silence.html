Brian Cox Addresses Interstellar Comet Signal Claims Amid MAVEN Silence

NASA's Mars Atmosphere and Volatile EvolutioN (MAVEN) spacecraft has remained silent since December 6, 2025, after losing contact during a routine orbital pass behind Mars. This communication lapse has coincided with public comments by Professor Brian Cox regarding the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS, which has sparked speculation about potential non-natural radio emissions.

The situation has raised concerns among scientists and space agencies about the implications of increasing terrestrial radio interference on deep-space research. Authorities are currently working to restore communication with MAVEN while clarifying the nature of the signals associated with 3I/ATLAS.

Key facts

  • Last successful telemetry from MAVEN was received on December 6, 2025.
  • Professor Brian Cox addressed the situation regarding 3I/ATLAS on December 29, 2025.
  • Nine candidate signals from 3I/ATLAS were identified as terrestrial radio interference.
  • MAVEN has been operational for over 10 years, studying Mars since its arrival in 2014.
  • NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL) plans to attempt a high-gain antenna reset during the next optimal alignment window.
  • If MAVEN does not respond by early January 2026, its operational status may be reclassified.

Timeline

On December 6, 2025, MAVEN lost communication during a standard orbital pass behind Mars. Following this, speculation arose about signals from the comet 3I/ATLAS. On December 29, 2025, Professor Brian Cox clarified that the signals were human-made interference, not emissions from the comet. NASA has since been working to restore telemetry with MAVEN.

Official statements

“The reality of space exploration is often found in the mundane challenges of physics and orbital mechanics,” Cox stated during a recent scientific address.
Cox emphasized that the “human noise” masking the comet’s signature is a direct result of the lack of stricter EPFD limits and sustainability metrics for commercial satellite constellations.

Context

The increasing density of low-Earth orbit radio traffic poses challenges for isolating high-value deep-space data. The identification of the signals as terrestrial interference illustrates the growing complexity of Space Traffic Management (STM) as more satellites enter orbit. MAVEN has been a critical asset in studying the Martian atmosphere and its interaction with solar phenomena since its mission began.

What’s next

NASA engineers will attempt a high-gain antenna reset during the next optimal alignment window. If there is no response from MAVEN by early January 2026, the agency may need to reassess the mission's operational status.
