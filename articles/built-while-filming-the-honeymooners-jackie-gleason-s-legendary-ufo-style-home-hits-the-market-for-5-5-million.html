Built While Filming 'The Honeymooners,' Jackie Gleason's Legendary UFO-Style Home Hits the Market for $5.5 Million

A unique home commissioned by actor Jackie Gleason has been listed for sale for $5.5 million in Cortlandt Manor, New York. The residence, completed in 1959 during the filming of "The Honeymooners," features a distinctive UFO-inspired design with no right angles.

This property, referred to as "The Mothership," is significant not only for its architectural style but also for its historical connections. It is the first time the home has been on the market in nearly 50 years. The sale has attracted attention due to its artistic features and celebrity history, as Gleason hosted notable guests there, including Frank Sinatra and Marilyn Monroe.

Key facts

  • Location: Cortlandt Manor, New York
  • Price: $5.5 million
  • Completion Year: 1959
  • Size: 7,000 square feet
  • Bedrooms: 5
  • Bathrooms: 4 full, 2 half
  • Property Size: 8.6 acres
  • Notable Guests: Frank Sinatra, Marilyn Monroe, Joe DiMaggio, President Richard Nixon

Timeline

Jackie Gleason commissioned the home in the early 1950s, during the filming of "The Honeymooners." The construction was completed in 1959. Gleason sold the property in a private sale to CBS, where he was working at the time. CBS later sold the house after Gleason moved to Florida. In 1976, it was sold to an orthodontist for $150,000. The home was listed for $12 million in 2018 but did not sell.

Official statements

"This Mid Century Modern architectural masterpiece is tucked away on 8.6 Hudson Valley acres, blending seamlessly with nature through floor-to-ceiling windows," said Heidi Henshaw of Corcoran, the listing agent.

Context

The home reflects the Mid Century Modern architectural style, designed by Robert Cika, a student of Frank Lloyd Wright. The unique features include curved walls and artistic metalwork, contributing to its recognition as a significant piece of art. The sale of the home marks a momentous occasion, as it has not changed ownership in nearly five decades.

What’s next

The property is now available for potential buyers, with hopes that it will find a new owner who appreciates its unique design and history.
