What to expect from alien files promised by Trump, and all that we already know

US President Donald Trump has promised to release all records related to alien life and Unidentified Anomalous Phenomena (UAPs) following a directive issued on February 19, 2026. The announcement has prompted renewed public interest and speculation about the existence of extraterrestrial life.

This initiative aims to enhance transparency regarding UAPs, which have been a topic of intrigue and speculation for decades. The Trump administration's directive challenges military agencies to disclose what they have kept secret, particularly regarding incidents like the 1947 Roswell crash. The All-Domain Anomaly Resolution Office (AARO) has been designated to lead this investigation.

Key facts

  • Date of announcement: February 19, 2026
  • Agency leading investigation: All-Domain Anomaly Resolution Office (AARO)
  • Active UAP reports: Over 2,000
  • Unresolved cases: Nearly 1,000
  • Historical context: 1947 Roswell crash

Timeline

On February 19, 2026, President Trump used his Truth Social platform to issue a direct order to the Department of War to release all records related to alien life and UAPs. This directive was partly influenced by comments made by former President Barack Obama regarding the reality of aliens.

Following the announcement, on February 25, 2026, Secretary of Defense Pete Hegseth confirmed that the Pentagon is prepared to comply with the Executive Order. The AARO is now tasked with processing a significant number of UAP reports, some of which remain unexplained.

Official statements

“We are moving away from tinfoil hats and entering an era where the government might finally have to admit what it has been hiding in the shadows,” Trump stated in his announcement.
“The military is ready to follow the upcoming Executive Order,” Hegseth confirmed, emphasizing the importance of transparency about UAPs.

Context

The Roswell incident in 1947 marked a pivotal moment in public interest regarding UFOs. Initially, the US military claimed to have recovered a flying disc, later retracting the statement to identify the object as a top-secret balloon. This incident has fueled decades of speculation regarding government cover-ups of extraterrestrial encounters.

As of now, the AARO has reported no verifiable evidence of aliens or off-world technology. Most UAP sightings have been attributed to mundane explanations such as weather balloons or drones. However, nearly 1,000 unresolved cases involve objects exhibiting flight patterns that defy known physics.

What’s next

The AARO is expected to begin the declassification process, which will aim to clarify public understanding of UAPs and potentially reveal new insights regarding unexplained aerial phenomena. The timeline for the release of these files has not been disclosed.
