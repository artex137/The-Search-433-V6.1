ESA's XMM-Newton Takes First-Ever X-Ray Photo of Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS

The European Space Agency's (ESA) XMM-Newton space observatory has captured the first-ever X-ray images of Comet 3I/ATLAS, an interstellar comet, during observations on December 3, 2025. The comet is currently on its closest approach to Earth, scheduled for December 19, 2025, at a distance of 1.79 astronomical units (AU).

This discovery is significant as it marks the first X-ray observation of an interstellar object in our Solar System. Researchers aim to understand how Comet 3I/ATLAS interacts with solar wind, which generates X-ray emissions that could offer insights into its chemical composition. ESA scientists have utilized the high-sensitivity capabilities of the XMM-Newton observatory to gather data that optical telescopes cannot provide.

Key facts

  • Observation Date: December 3, 2025
  • Closest Approach to Earth: December 19, 2025
  • Distance from Earth: 1.79 AU (approximately 282–285 million kilometers)
  • Observation Duration: Approximately 20 hours
  • X-ray Instrument Used: European Photon Imaging Camera (EPIC)-pn

Timeline

Comet 3I/ATLAS was first spotted by the ATLAS observatory in July 2025. ESA's XMM-Newton conducted its observations on December 3, 2025, focusing on the comet's X-ray emissions as it approached Earth. The closest approach is set for December 19, 2025.

Official statements

“These X-rays can come from the interaction of the solar wind with gases like water vapour, carbon dioxide, or carbon monoxide – which telescopes such as the NASA/ESA/CSA James Webb Space Telescope and NASA’s SPHEREx have already detected,” ESA stated.

Context

Comet 3I/ATLAS is the third interstellar object recorded in our Solar System. Previous interstellar objects, such as 1I/’Oumuamua, have raised questions about the nature of materials originating from other star systems. Observations like those of Comet 3I/ATLAS provide valuable data to help refine scientists' models of interstellar objects.

What’s next

Researchers will analyze the data collected from the X-ray observations to gain further insights into the comet's composition and origins. This information will complement other observations of interstellar objects and enhance understanding of their interactions with solar wind.
