Interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS reveals rare composition and mobilizes global scientific cooperation

A new interstellar comet, designated 3I/ATLAS, has drawn the attention of astronomers worldwide since its identification on July 1, 2025, by the ATLAS astronomical survey in Chile. The comet presents characteristics that challenge existing knowledge about the formation of celestial bodies, offering a unique opportunity to study material from another star system.

The unusual chemical composition of 3I/ATLAS, primarily frozen carbon dioxide rather than water ice, has significant implications for scientific understanding. Space agencies and observatories from various countries have collaborated to monitor and analyze the comet before it moves too far away for detailed observations.

Key facts

  • Identification date: July 1, 2025
  • Discovery location: Chile
  • Primary composition: Frozen carbon dioxide (CO2)
  • Confirmed origin: Interstellar
  • Significant data source: Telescópio Espacial James Webb (JWST)
  • Monitoring collaboration: International space agencies and observatories

Timeline

On July 1, 2025, ATLAS discovered comet 3I/ATLAS in Chile. Preliminary spectral analyses revealed its unusual composition, prompting a global scientific collaboration to study it. Observations by the JWST confirmed the predominance of carbon dioxide and uncovered additional components, including carbon monoxide. The comet’s hyperbolic trajectory confirmed its interstellar origin, making it only the third known interstellar comet.

Official statements

“The abundance of carbon dioxide in 3I/ATLAS challenges our understanding of comet formation and suggests a very different origin environment,” said a representative from the European Space Agency.

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third known interstellar comet, following ‘Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov. Its discovery highlights a growing trend in astronomy towards international collaboration, especially in light of budget constraints faced by NASA, which limits its direct involvement in the observation efforts.

What’s next

Scientists will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS to gather high-resolution spectra that may reveal more complex molecules. Understanding its rotation dynamics is also a priority, as it will provide insights into gas release from its surface and the structural integrity of its icy core.
