Interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS spills its secrets as scientists clock its water loss and radio silence

Scientists have measured water streaming from interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS following its closest approach to the Sun on December 27, 2025. The findings, released on January 2, 2026, in a report using data from the Solar and Heliospheric Observatory (SOHO), highlight the comet's significant water loss and lack of detectable radio signals.

This research is crucial as 3I/ATLAS is only the third confirmed object from interstellar space to enter the solar system. NASA has stated that the comet is on a hyperbolic trajectory, which means it will not return. The agency has confirmed that the comet poses no threat to Earth, maintaining a distance of approximately 1.8 astronomical units (AU) at its closest approach.

Key facts

  • Closest approach to the Sun occurred on December 27, 2025.
  • Peak water production rate was estimated at 3.17×1029 molecules per second.
  • Water production dropped to about 1–2×1028 molecules per second by December 8, 2025.
  • SOHO's SWAN instrument began observing the comet on November 6, 2025.
  • The comet is currently about 1.8 AU from Earth.
  • Breakthrough Listen program reported no credible narrowband radio signals from the comet.
  • 3I/ATLAS's brightness was around magnitude +15.6 on January 1, 2026.

Timeline

On November 6, 2025, SOHO's SWAN instrument began tracking the hydrogen coma of 3I/ATLAS. This observation occurred nine days after the comet's closest point to the Sun.

The peak water production rate was recorded shortly after perihelion, followed by a decline in water output as the comet moved away from the Sun. By December 8, the water production had significantly decreased.

On January 1, 2026, the Breakthrough Listen program reported that it had found no evidence of narrowband radio technosignatures originating from 3I/ATLAS.

Official statements

“We find no credible detections of narrowband radio technosignatures originating from 3I/ATLAS,” said lead author Ben Jacobson-Bell, as reported by space journalist Leonard David.

Context

3I/ATLAS is among a small number of interstellar objects that provide scientists with unique insights into material formed around other stars. The comet's high profile has spurred discussions about the need for a coordinated strategy for studying such rare interstellar arrivals as detection rates increase.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS as it recedes from the Sun, comparing its behavior to comets formed within the solar system. Further observations may help enhance understanding of interstellar objects and their characteristics.
