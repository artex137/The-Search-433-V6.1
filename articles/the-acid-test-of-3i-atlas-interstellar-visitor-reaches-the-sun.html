The ‘Acid Test’ of 3I/ATLAS: Interstellar Visitor Reaches the Sun

The interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS has reached its closest approach to the Sun, known as perihelion, on Wednesday, coming within about 126 million miles. There have been no reported injuries or fatalities associated with this astronomical event.

This moment marks the halfway point of 3I/ATLAS's journey through the Solar System and is significant for scientists studying its composition and origin. If the comet survives the intense solar radiation it faces at perihelion, it may challenge existing theories about comet formation and interstellar dynamics.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS reached perihelion on Wednesday.
  • It came within approximately 126 million miles of the Sun.
  • The comet was discovered in July 2025.
  • 3I/ATLAS is the third known interstellar object to enter our Solar System.
  • It will be visible again in late November or early December 2025.
  • Closest approach to Earth will occur on 19 December 2025 at a distance of about 165 million miles.

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was discovered in July 2025. Since late September 2025, it has been in solar conjunction, making it invisible to telescopes due to the Sun's glare. The comet reached its perihelion on Wednesday and will remain hidden until late November or early December 2025.

Official statements

“If the object emerges intact and continues on its own trajectory, we may be forced to rethink what we are looking at,” said Harvard physicist Avi Loeb regarding the comet's solar encounter.

Context

3I/ATLAS is only the third known interstellar object to enter our Solar System, following Oumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019. Its unusual chemical signature and carbon dioxide emissions suggest it may have originated from a colder region around another star, challenging existing models of comet formation.

What’s next

Astronomers will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS as it becomes visible again later this year. The data collected may reshape the conversation about interstellar visitors and their implications for understanding our universe.
