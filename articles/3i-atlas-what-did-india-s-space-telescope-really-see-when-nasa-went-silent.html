3I/ATLAS: What Did India’s Space Telescope Really See When NASA Went Silent

In July 2025, India's space telescope and other astronomers observed a mysterious interstellar object known as 3I/ATLAS while NASA maintained silence, presenting puzzling results that intrigued scientists worldwide.

This observation is significant as 3I/ATLAS is the third confirmed interstellar object to transit the solar system, following 'Oumuamua and Borisov. The behavior of 3I/ATLAS has raised questions among researchers, prompting calls for transparency from NASA and leading to a coordinated global effort to study this unusual visitor.

Key facts

  • Object: 3I/ATLAS
  • First detected: July 2025
  • Closest approach to the Sun: October 29-30, 2025
  • International collaboration: Supported by India's telescope and other global observatories
  • Notable chemical findings: Nickel vapor and nickel tetracarbonyl detected
  • Unusual observation: Anti-solar tail direction change

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first detected in July 2025 by the Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System, which initiated a global observational effort. As scientists studied the object, they noted its strange behavior, particularly the unexpected change in its tail direction.

Despite the unusual findings, NASA remained largely silent, leading to increased scrutiny and calls for transparency from the scientific community. Meanwhile, India's space telescope continued to observe 3I/ATLAS, contributing valuable data to the global effort.

The object made its closest approach to the Sun on October 29-30, 2025, marking a rare event expected to occur once every 400 years.

Official statements

“3I/ATLAS has the potential to change everything about our understanding of the Universe we live in,” said Avi Loeb from Harvard.

Context

3I/ATLAS follows two previous interstellar objects, 'Oumuamua and Borisov, which have also sparked significant scientific interest. The unusual characteristics of 3I/ATLAS, such as the presence of nickel compounds and the absence of iron, add to the growing intrigue regarding interstellar objects and their origins.

What’s next

The object will soon be lost in deep space for the foreseeable future, but telescopes worldwide, including those in India, will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS. Researchers will analyze the data and potential samples for years to come to uncover the secrets of the solar system's formation.
