Hyperbolic trajectory of comet 3I/Atlas is confirmed with a speed of 57 km/s in the solar system

Astronomers have confirmed the passage of comet 3I/Atlas, detected moving at a speed of 57 kilometers per second, as it travels through the solar system. The comet's hyperbolic trajectory indicates it is an interstellar object, and it is not expected to be captured by the Sun's gravity.

This discovery matters because it marks the third confirmed interstellar object to cross the solar system, following ‘Oumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019. Scientists are eager to study the materials of 3I/Atlas, which may provide insights into the composition and dynamics of other planetary systems.

Key facts

  • Object: 3I/Atlas
  • Speed: 57 km/s
  • Detection Date: January 19, 2026
  • Trajectory: Hyperbolic
  • Previous Interstellar Objects: ‘Oumuamua (2017), 2I/Borisov (2019)

Timeline

Comet 3I/Atlas was detected on January 19, 2026, moving at a speed that exceeds the local escape velocity of the solar system. Its hyperbolic trajectory confirms the object’s interstellar origin.

Continuous observations from a global network of telescopes are currently underway to refine the comet's orbit and predict its path as it exits the solar system.

Official statements

“The hyperbolic trajectory of 3I/Atlas is an unambiguous signature of its external origin,” said astronomers involved in the study.

Context

The study of interstellar comets is a relatively new field, with 3I/Atlas now adding to the small catalog of known objects from outside our solar system. The previous interstellar visitors, ‘Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov, have offered differing insights into the nature of such objects, with implications for our understanding of planetary formation.

What’s next

Scientists will continue to track 3I/Atlas to determine its closest approach to the Sun and analyze its chemical composition as it approaches, which may yield further insights into the origins of such interstellar bodies.
