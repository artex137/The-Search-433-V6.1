ESA releases new JUICE photo of interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS as fresh data reaches Earth

On March 5, 2026, the European Space Agency (ESA) released a new image of interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, captured by its JUICE spacecraft, from a distance of about 66 million km. The image was taken by the JANUS camera during a November 2025 observing run. There have been no reported injuries or fatalities associated with this event.

This image is significant as it provides new insights into the comet, which is only the third confirmed interstellar object observed in the solar system. Scientists are currently analyzing delayed data from multiple instruments aboard JUICE, with a joint review scheduled for late March 2026. This data will help researchers understand the composition of the comet, which carries material formed around another star.

Key facts

  • New image of 3I/ATLAS released by ESA on March 5, 2026.
  • Image captured from approximately 66 million km away on November 6, 2025.
  • Comet first spotted on July 1, 2025, by the ATLAS survey telescope in Río Hurtado, Chile.
  • 3I/ATLAS reached speeds of roughly 250,000 km/h at its closest approach to the Sun.
  • Nearest approach to Earth was about 270 million km.
  • JUICE spacecraft launched in 2023, designed to study Jupiter's moons.
  • Data from JUICE's instruments (JANUS, MAJIS, UVS, SWI, PEP) is being analyzed.

Timeline

On July 1, 2025, the ATLAS survey telescope first spotted 3I/ATLAS. The comet reached its closest approach to the Sun, traveling at around 250,000 km/h. On November 6, 2025, the JANUS camera aboard the JUICE spacecraft captured over 120 images of the comet. The spacecraft continued its journey, moving to the far side of the Sun from Earth.

In late February 2026, the bulk of the data from the observing run arrived on Earth. ESA has planned a joint review of the data for late March 2026.

Official statements

“The comet is around 10,000 to 100,000 times fainter than our usual target,” said Nick Thomas, principal investigator for ExoMars’ CaSSIS camera, regarding the challenges of obtaining clear data on 3I/ATLAS.

Context

3I/ATLAS is only the third confirmed interstellar object observed passing through the solar system, following 1I/‘Oumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019. The data collected from 3I/ATLAS is valuable as it may contain material from another star, providing insights into the formation of celestial bodies.

What’s next

ESA will continue to analyze the data from the JUICE instruments, with results expected to emerge following the joint review in late March 2026. Scientists will calibrate the data and assess the findings to distinguish between real structures and noise or artefacts.
