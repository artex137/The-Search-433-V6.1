Growing Tail of Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS Revealed in New Telescope Image

A recent image captured by the Gemini South telescope in Chile has unveiled the growing tail of the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS. The comet, approximately 7 miles (11 kilometers) wide, was first detected in early July and is currently traveling towards the sun from beyond the asteroid belt between Mars and Jupiter. No injuries or fatalities have been reported.

This discovery is significant for scientists, who are eager to learn more about the comet's origins and its implications for understanding the formation of star systems. Authorities, including astronomers from various observatories, are conducting ongoing observations to gather data on the comet's features and trajectory.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS measures approximately 7 miles (11 kilometers) in width.
  • First detected in early July 2023.
  • Current speed is over 130,000 mph (210,000 km/h).
  • Closest approach to the sun (perihelion) is on October 29, 2023.
  • Will approach Earth in December 2023, coming within 170 million miles (275 million km).
  • Observations include data from the James Webb Space Telescope and Hubble Space Telescope.

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first detected in early July 2023 as it entered the solar system. On August 27, astronomers captured a clear image of the comet's tail, marking a significant milestone in the study of this celestial object. As it approaches the sun, the comet's tail continues to grow due to solar radiation. The comet will reach its closest point to the sun on October 29, 2023, before making its nearest approach to Earth in December.

Official statements

"This latest image is both a scientific milestone and a captivating glimpse into the vast and dynamic galaxy," said Karen Meech, an astronomer at the University of Hawaii and a member of the Gemini observatories team.

Context

3I/ATLAS is recognized as the third and likely the largest interstellar object discovered to date, following 'Oumuamua in 2017 and Comet Borisov in 2019. Previous observations have shown that interstellar comets can provide valuable insights into the composition and evolution of celestial bodies beyond our solar system.

What’s next

Astronomers plan to conduct further research over the next year to enhance their understanding of 3I/ATLAS's origins and its role in the broader context of cosmic phenomena. Observations will continue as the comet approaches both Mars and Earth.
