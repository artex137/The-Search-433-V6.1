Mars Orbiters Capture Rare Images of Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS Zooming Past the Red Planet

Interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS has been photographed by Mars orbiters during its close flyby of Mars on October 3, 2025. The European Space Agency's (ESA) ExoMars Trace Gas Orbiter (TGO) and Mars Express spacecraft captured these rare images, providing a unique opportunity for scientific study.

This event is significant as it marks the first confirmed interstellar comet to pass through our solar system. The data collected from the comet's flyby will help researchers understand the composition and behavior of interstellar objects, enhancing our knowledge of the cosmos.

Key facts

  • Comet designation: 3I/ATLAS
  • Date of flyby: October 3, 2025
  • Agencies involved: European Space Agency (ESA)
  • Spacecraft: ExoMars Trace Gas Orbiter (TGO) and Mars Express
  • Significance: First confirmed interstellar comet to pass through the solar system

Timeline

On October 3, 2025, interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS made a close pass by Mars. The ESA's spacecraft, including the ExoMars TGO and Mars Express, successfully captured high-resolution images of the comet. This event allowed scientists to gather data on the comet's composition and behavior as it traveled through our solar system.

Official statements

“This interstellar comet’s flyby of Mars provides a unique opportunity for scientists to study an object from outside the solar system in unprecedented detail,” said an ESA spokesperson.

Context

3I/ATLAS is notable for being the first interstellar comet identified in our solar system. Its trajectory and composition provide insights into the materials that existed in the early universe, making its study essential for understanding cosmic evolution.

What’s next

After its flyby, 3I/ATLAS will continue on its trajectory out of our solar system, never to return. The data and images collected will be analyzed for years to come, contributing to ongoing research in interstellar exploration.
