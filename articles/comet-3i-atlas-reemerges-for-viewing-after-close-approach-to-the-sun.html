Comet 3I/ATLAS Reemerges for Viewing After Close Approach to the Sun

Comet 3I/ATLAS has reappeared for viewing after its close approach to the sun on October 30. The comet, discovered on July 1, is the third known interstellar object to pass through our solar system. It is currently visible to amateur astronomers, particularly in the constellation Virgo, just before dawn.

This reemergence is significant for astronomers and stargazers. Authorities and scientists are observing the comet's features, including its coma and tail, using both terrestrial and space-based instruments. With the comet now positioned away from the sun's glare, it can be more easily observed, especially in the early morning hours.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS was discovered on July 1.
  • It passed close to the sun on October 30.
  • The comet is currently visible in the constellation Virgo.
  • It can be observed just before dawn in early November.
  • Qicheng Zhang captured images of the comet using a 6-inch Ritchey–Chrétien reflector telescope.
  • Visibility is expected to improve over the coming days and weeks.

Timeline

On July 1, Comet 3I/ATLAS was discovered. The comet made a close approach to the sun on October 30, leading to a brief disappearance due to the sun's glare. Following this event, the comet has become visible again, particularly in early November.

Qicheng Zhang, a postdoctoral fellow at the Lowell Observatory in Arizona, has captured images of the comet, noting its current appearance as a fuzzy blob. He anticipates that visibility will improve in the coming weeks.

Official statements

“While the comet may currently appear as a fuzzy blob against the backdrop of stars, its visibility is expected to improve over the coming days and weeks,” said Qicheng Zhang.
“If the comet fades at a rate similar to its brightening, observers might see it reach its peak visibility in about one to two weeks,” Zhang added.

Context

Comet 3I/ATLAS is notable as the third known interstellar object to traverse the solar system. The study of such comets offers valuable insights into the origins and composition of materials from distant stars.

Previous interstellar objects, like 'Oumuamua and Comet 2I/Borisov, have also sparked significant interest in the scientific community, leading to extensive research and analysis.

What’s next

Amateur astronomers are encouraged to prepare for observations of Comet 3I/ATLAS. They can utilize various resources, including guides on capturing images of comets. Observers are invited to share their photos with the community, documenting the journey of this rare interstellar visitor.
