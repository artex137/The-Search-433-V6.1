ExoMars Trace Gas Orbiter observes comet 3I/ATLAS

On 3 October, ESA’s ExoMars Trace Gas Orbiter (TGO) observed interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS as it passed close to Mars. The TGO captured images of the comet from a distance of approximately 30 million km during its closest approach to the Red Planet. No injuries or fatalities have been reported.

This observation is significant as it showcases the capabilities of the ExoMars TGO, which is primarily designed for studying Mars' atmosphere. The successful imaging of 3I/ATLAS highlights the versatility of the spacecraft's instruments, particularly its Colour and Stereo Surface Imaging System (CaSSIS).

Key facts

  • Date of observation: 3 October
  • Distance from Mars: Approximately 30 million km
  • Instruments used: Colour and Stereo Surface Imaging System (CaSSIS)
  • Comet designation: 3I/ATLAS
  • Observation technique: Stacked multiple five-second exposures

Timeline

On 3 October, the ExoMars TGO directed its instruments towards comet 3I/ATLAS during the comet's close approach to Mars. Utilizing its CaSSIS, the orbiter captured a series of images despite the comet being significantly farther away than typical targets.

The images revealed the coma of gas and dust surrounding the comet’s nucleus, providing valuable data for scientists.

Official statements

“ExoMars TGO had the closest view of the comet of all ESA spacecraft,” said an ESA representative.

Context

Comet 3I/ATLAS is an interstellar comet, a rare type of celestial object that originates from outside our solar system. Previous observations of interstellar comets have provided insights into the composition of these distant objects.

What’s next

Scientists will analyze the data collected from the ExoMars TGO's observations to better understand the characteristics of 3I/ATLAS and its potential implications for future studies of interstellar objects.
