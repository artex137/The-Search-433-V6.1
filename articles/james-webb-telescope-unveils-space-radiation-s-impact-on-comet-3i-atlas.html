James Webb Telescope Unveils Space Radiation’s Impact on Comet 3I/ATLAS

New research utilizing the James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) has revealed the significant impact of space radiation on Comet 3I/ATLAS. The study indicates that the comet has experienced billions of years of cosmic ray exposure, resulting in a unique and deeply irradiated surface.

This research is important as it enhances understanding of the effects of cosmic radiation on celestial bodies. The findings may provide insights into the composition and evolution of interstellar comets and their interactions with the surrounding environment.

Key facts

  • Comet Name: 3I/ATLAS
  • Perihelion Date: October 29
  • Research Lead: Romain Maggiolo, Royal Belgian Institute for Space Aeronomy
  • Speed: Nearly 130,000 mph (210,000 km/h)
  • Depth of Irradiation: Approximately 15 to 20 meters (50 to 65 feet)
  • Cosmic Ray Exposure: Estimated 7 billion years

Timeline

Key events related to Comet 3I/ATLAS include:

  • July: Researchers discovered Comet 3I/ATLAS and began observations using various telescopes.
  • August: JWST captured the first images of the comet.
  • October 29: Comet reached perihelion, closest point to the sun, causing surface heating and sublimation of ices into gas.

Official statements

“The research underscores the necessity of considering the aging effects of cosmic radiation when analyzing such celestial bodies,” said Romain Maggiolo.

Context

The findings regarding Comet 3I/ATLAS highlight a previously documented enrichment of carbon dioxide (CO2) attributed to cosmic radiation. In contrast to the protective heliosphere of our solar system, the comet has been unshielded in interstellar space, exposing it to extensive cosmic rays.

What’s next

Researchers anticipate that solar erosion may unearth pristine materials from the comet’s nucleus following its perihelion, providing valuable comparative data between observations made before and after this event.
