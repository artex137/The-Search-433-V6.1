Nickel Discovery in Comet 3I/ATLAS Reveals Interstellar Secrets

A team of international scientists has discovered glowing nickel vapor in the atmosphere of the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, first identified on July 1, 2025, during a routine sky survey by the Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System (ATLAS). There have been no reported injuries or fatalities associated with this discovery.

This finding is significant as it provides new insights into the chemical composition of interstellar objects. Researchers from various institutions, including the Instituto de Astrofísica-Pontificia Universidad Católica de Chile and Michigan State University, are closely monitoring 3I/ATLAS as it approaches the Sun, leading to enhanced understanding of its chemical evolution.

Key facts

  • Discovery Date: July 1, 2025
  • Nickel Detection: July 20, 2025, at 3.88 astronomical units (AU) from the Sun
  • Mid-August Detection: Cyanogen gas at around 3.07 AU from the Sun
  • Closest Approach to the Sun: October 29, 2025
  • Key Observatories: Very Large Telescope (VLT), James Webb Space Telescope (JWST)

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first identified on July 1, 2025, during an ATLAS survey. The first detection of nickel vapor occurred on July 20, 2025, at a distance of 3.88 AU from the Sun. By mid-August, researchers identified cyanogen gas at approximately 3.07 AU from the Sun. The intensity of nickel emissions increased as the comet continued its journey toward the Sun.

As 3I/ATLAS approaches its closest point to the Sun on October 29, 2025, scientists are anticipating further increases in activity and the potential emergence of new chemical species.

Official statements

“The presence of nickel vapor at such cold distances challenges existing theories about cometary composition,” said Rohan Rahatgaonkar from the Instituto de Astrofísica-Pontificia Universidad Católica de Chile.
“This discovery not only enhances our understanding of 3I/ATLAS but also raises intriguing questions about the origins of interstellar comets,” noted Darryl Z. Seligman from Michigan State University.

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third confirmed interstellar object to be observed, following ‘Oumuamua and comet Borisov. Unlike these predecessors, 3I/ATLAS was detected early in its solar system transit, allowing for real-time study of its chemical evolution. The findings about nickel vapor and other gases suggest that the processes involved in the formation of interstellar comets may differ from those in solar system objects.

What’s next

As 3I/ATLAS continues its approach to the Sun, scientists will conduct ongoing monitoring to capture any new chemical activity. This research aims to deepen understanding of the chemical diversity of planetary systems across the galaxy.
