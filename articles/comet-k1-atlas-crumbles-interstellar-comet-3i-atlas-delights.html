Comet K1/ATLAS Crumbles, Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS Delights

Comet C/2025 K1 (ATLAS) has begun to disintegrate while interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS shines unexpectedly bright in the sky, as reported by astronomers on November 10, 2025. Observers have noted that K1/ATLAS, discovered in May 2025, is breaking apart into at least three visible fragments.

This event is significant as it provides a rare opportunity for astronomers and enthusiasts to witness the disintegration of a comet. The ongoing observations allow for a better understanding of comet behavior and dynamics as they approach the Sun.

Key facts

  • Comet K1/ATLAS is currently a 10th-magnitude object visible from 2 a.m. to dawn local time.
  • The comet's perihelion passage occurred on October 8, 2025, at a distance of 49 million kilometers (31 million miles) from the Sun.
  • Observers report that K1/ATLAS has produced two brightness outbursts between October 31 and November 4, 2025.
  • Comet 3I/ATLAS has been observed at a brightness of magnitude 9.8, making it visible in a 6-inch telescope.
  • 3I/ATLAS is currently passing through the eastern sky in Virgo, close to the star Gamma (γ) Virginis.

Timeline

On October 8, 2025, Comet K1/ATLAS reached its closest approach to the Sun. Following this, the comet exhibited two bright outbursts between October 31 and November 4, prompting further observation.

On November 10, astronomers noted that K1/ATLAS had split into multiple fragments. The same day, observations confirmed that interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS was brighter than initially expected, leading to excitement among skywatchers.

Official statements

“Observers can use this unique opportunity to watch the process unfold,” said astronomers tracking K1/ATLAS.
“Comet 3I/ATLAS has exceeded early brightness predictions, which is a delightful surprise for astronomers,” a local astronomer noted.

Context

Comets are fragile bodies made of ice and dust that can disintegrate as they approach the Sun due to heat and gravitational forces. The recent disintegration of K1/ATLAS highlights the volatile nature of comets, similar to what occurred with C/2024 G3 (ATLAS), which also lost its nucleus.

Interstellar comets like 3I/ATLAS are rare visitors from outside our solar system, providing unique insights into the materials and conditions of other parts of the galaxy.

What’s next

Observers will continue to monitor both comets as K1/ATLAS potentially disintegrates further and 3I/ATLAS remains visible in the morning sky until at least November 21, 2025. Astronomers will track the evolution of K1/ATLAS and its fragments in the coming weeks.
