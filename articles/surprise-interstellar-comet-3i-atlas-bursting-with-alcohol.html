Surprise! Interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS bursting with alcohol

Comet 3I/ATLAS has gained attention for its unusually high levels of methanol, a type of alcohol, according to findings from astronomers using the ALMA telescope in Chile. The research, published on March 6, 2026, reveals that 3I/ATLAS contains more methanol than almost any comet in our solar system.

This discovery is significant as it provides insights into the chemical composition of interstellar objects and the conditions under which they form. Nathan Roth, lead author and professor at American University, stated that observing 3I/ATLAS is akin to taking a fingerprint from another solar system, helping scientists understand how objects might be created in different planetary systems.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS observed using ALMA in late 2025.
  • Research reveals high levels of methanol compared to hydrogen cyanide.
  • Methanol-HCN ratios measured at about 70 and 120.
  • 3I/ATLAS is the third confirmed interstellar object observed in our solar system.

Timeline

In late 2025, astronomers used the Atacama Large Millimeter/submillimeter Array (ALMA) to observe comet 3I/ATLAS as it approached the sun. As sunlight warmed its icy surface, the comet released gas and dust, forming a glowing halo, or coma, around its core. Scientists analyzed this coma to study its chemical composition.

On two separate observing dates, the team measured the ratios of methanol to hydrogen cyanide, revealing an unexpectedly high concentration of methanol. These findings indicate that the icy material from 3I/ATLAS formed under conditions different from those that shape most comets in our solar system.

Official statements

Nathan Roth, lead author and professor at American University, said, "Observing 3I/ATLAS is like taking a fingerprint from another solar system. The details reveal what it’s made of, and it’s bursting with methanol in a way we just don’t usually see in comets in our own solar system."

Context

Comet 3I/ATLAS is only the third confirmed interstellar object observed in our solar system, following 1I/‘Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov. Previous studies have indicated that these interstellar objects often exhibit unusual properties, contributing to an evolving understanding of planet formation in other planetary systems.

What’s next

Further observations and studies of 3I/ATLAS will continue as astronomers seek to understand more about the chemical composition and formation conditions of interstellar objects.
