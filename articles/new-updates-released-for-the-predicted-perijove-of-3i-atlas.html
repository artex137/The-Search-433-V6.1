New updates released for the predicted perijove of 3I ATLAS

The predicted perijove of the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS has been updated, according to Harvard Professor Avi Loeb, following revisions to its non-gravitational acceleration. The updates were released on November 24, 2025, indicating a change in the radial acceleration component from 1.6x10-6 au per day squared to 4x10-7 au per day squared. The object is expected to encounter Jupiter on March 16, 2026, at a minimum perijove distance of 53.445 (+/- 0.06) million kilometers.

This information is significant for astronomers and researchers monitoring 3I/ATLAS, as the updated perijove distance closely aligns with Jupiter’s Hill radius at that time, which is 53.502 million kilometers. Loeb emphasized the importance of this measurement, stating that within this radius, Jupiter's gravity dominates over the Sun's tidal forces.

Key facts

  • Initial radial acceleration component reported as 1.6x10-6 au per day squared on October 30, 2025.
  • Revised radial acceleration component is now 4x10-7 au per day squared as of November 24, 2025.
  • Minimum perijove distance forecasted at 53.445 (+/- 0.06) million kilometers.
  • Jupiter’s Hill radius at the time of encounter is 53.502 million kilometers.
  • Predicted perijove encounter date is March 16, 2026.
  • Statistical likelihood of perijove distance matching Hill radius is smaller than 0.00004.

Timeline

On October 30, 2025, the radial acceleration component for 3I/ATLAS was initially reported as 1.6x10-6 au per day squared. Subsequently, on November 24, 2025, this value was revised to 4x10-7 au per day squared.

As 3I/ATLAS approaches its perijove on March 16, 2026, new measurements and observations will be made, particularly from spacecraft such as Juno, Juice, or Psyche.

Official statements

“Interior to that radius, Jupiter’s gravity dominates over the Sun’s tide,” said Loeb, emphasizing the significance of the measurement.
“We will know whether the perijove distance agrees with the Hill radius in the coming months thanks to data collected as 3I/ATLAS approaches its perijove,” Loeb stated.

Context

Loeb explained that the revision to the radial acceleration model accounts for sublimation of carbon dioxide ice, replacing a previous model based on water ice. This adaptation reflects changes in the object’s behavior as it approaches the Sun.

Prior reports suggested a luminosity profile of 1/r7.5 inside 2 au, indicating significant mass loss in the coma of 3I/ATLAS as it nears perihelion.

What’s next

Upcoming observations from various spacecraft are expected to provide more precise measurements as 3I/ATLAS nears its perijove. The final verification of the perijove distance will be posted on the JPL Horizons website.
