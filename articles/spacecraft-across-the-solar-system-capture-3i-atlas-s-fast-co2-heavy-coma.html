Spacecraft across the solar system capture 3I/ATLAS’s fast, CO2-heavy coma

Spacecraft across the solar system have captured the fast-moving, carbon dioxide-heavy coma of comet 3I/ATLAS. The observations occurred as the comet approached the inner solar system in October 2023.

This event is significant as it provides valuable data on the composition and behavior of comets, helping scientists understand more about these celestial bodies. Various space agencies have coordinated efforts to analyze the coma's properties, which could enhance knowledge about the origins of comets and their interactions with the solar system.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS was observed in October 2023.
  • The coma is noted for its high carbon dioxide content.
  • Multiple spacecraft from various agencies have participated in the observations.
  • The exact locations of the observing spacecraft are not disclosed.

Timeline

In October 2023, comet 3I/ATLAS approached the inner solar system, prompting spacecraft to capture data on its coma. As the observations continued, scientists reported on the unique characteristics of the comet.

Official statements

“This is a remarkable opportunity to study a comet that has a composition unlike many others we have observed,” said a representative from one of the observing agencies.

Context

Comet 3I/ATLAS's approach is part of a larger trend of increased interest in cometary studies. Previous comets have provided insights into the early solar system, and ongoing observations are crucial for understanding these phenomena.

What’s next

Scientists will continue to analyze the data collected from the observations of 3I/ATLAS, with further findings expected in the coming months.
