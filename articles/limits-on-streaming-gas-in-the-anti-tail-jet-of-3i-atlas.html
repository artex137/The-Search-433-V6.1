Limits on streaming gas in the anti-tail jet of 3I/ATLAS

Observations of the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS have revealed an anti-tail jet extending sunward for several hundred thousand kilometers following perihelion, according to Harvard Professor Avi Loeb’s blog posts on Medium in December 2025. There have been no reports of injuries or fatalities related to this astronomical phenomenon.

This discovery is significant as it provides insights into the behavior of gas and dust emitted from the comet's nucleus. Authorities are now focusing on verifying the characteristics of the anti-tail and its implications for understanding interstellar objects.

Key facts

  • Object: 3I/ATLAS
  • Distance of gas streaming: Approximately 5,000 kilometers from the nucleus
  • Mass loss rate: Estimated at 500 kg/s
  • Gas speed: Approximately 0.2 km/s
  • Solar wind density: Approximately 3×10−24 g/cm³ at ~2 AU
  • Observational tools: Hubble Space Telescope, ground-based telescopes in Belgium, Keck, VLT, ALMA, SPHEREx, and Webb
  • Estimated number of similar interstellar objects: Around 10²³ within the Milky Way

Timeline

In December 2025, Professor Avi Loeb published findings regarding the anti-tail jet of 3I/ATLAS on his blog. The data suggests that the streaming gas is limited to about 5,000 kilometers from the comet's nucleus, influenced by the solar wind's ram pressure. Observations from various telescopes confirm the presence of a glowing halo surrounding the nucleus, consistent with the calculated gas stopping distance.

The mass loss rates were derived from data collected by the Webb telescope, indicating the dynamics of gas flow and the interaction with the solar wind.

Official statements

“For a natural comet, the anti-tail jet of 3I/ATLAS is expected to carry gas only up to approximately 5,000 kilometers from the nucleus, with larger dust particles extending further,” Loeb stated in his blog.

Context

3I/ATLAS is part of a larger population of interstellar objects, which are characterized by their high velocities and escape from the solar system. The findings related to 3I/ATLAS contribute to a broader understanding of these objects and their behaviors, as similar interstellar bodies are estimated to number in the order of 10²³ within the Milky Way.

Previous observations have shown that gas dynamics in these objects can vary significantly depending on their composition and the forces acting upon them.

What’s next

Future observational strategies will focus on confirming the physical limits of the anti-tail jet through molecular tracing and dust imaging. Researchers plan to utilize advanced telescopes and potential interceptor missions to analyze materials from other interstellar objects.
