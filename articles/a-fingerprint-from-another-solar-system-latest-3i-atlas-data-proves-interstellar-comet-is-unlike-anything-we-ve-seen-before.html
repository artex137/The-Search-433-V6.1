'A fingerprint from another solar system': Latest 3I/ATLAS data proves interstellar comet is unlike anything we've seen before

Comet 3I/ATLAS has been found to contain an unusually large amount of methanol, an organic molecule that could be considered a precursor for life, according to a study conducted using the Atacama Large Millimeter/submillimeter Array (ALMA) in Chile. The study reveals that the chemical structure of 3I/ATLAS is distinct from the majority of comets found within our Solar System. This discovery may provide insights into the conditions of the star system from which the comet originated, located in a far corner of our Galaxy.

This research is significant as it enhances our understanding of interstellar comets, which are rare. Authorities, including astronomers and scientists from various observatories, are continuing to study 3I/ATLAS as it passes through our Solar System, with hopes of gleaning further information about its origins and composition.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS was discovered on 1 July 2025.
  • It is one of only three known interstellar comets.
  • ALMA has revealed that 3I/ATLAS contains more methanol than most known comets in our Solar System.
  • The study used ALMA’s Atacama Compact Array on multiple dates in late 2025.
  • 3I/ATLAS is currently passing through the Solar System and will soon be out of view.

Timeline

Comet 3I/ATLAS was discovered on 1 July 2025. Following its discovery, astronomers began studying it with various telescopes, including the Hubble Space Telescope and the James Webb Space Telescope. In late 2025, researchers used ALMA to observe the comet as it approached the Sun, leading to the discovery of its unique chemical composition.

The comet's close approach to the Sun resulted in a glowing halo, or coma, forming around it. The ALMA observations focused on the comet's outgassing, revealing significant amounts of methanol compared to hydrogen cyanide, which is typical for comets in our Solar System.

Official statements

“Observing 3I/ATLAS is like taking a fingerprint from another solar system,” said Nathan Roth, lead author of the study and a professor with American University. “The details reveal what it’s made of, and it’s bursting with methanol in a way we just don’t usually see in comets in our own Solar System.”

Context

Prior to the discovery of 3I/ATLAS, only two other interstellar comets had been identified. These comets provide valuable data about the formation and evolution of celestial bodies beyond our Solar System. The study of 3I/ATLAS could lead to a deeper understanding of the chemical processes occurring in distant star systems.

What’s next

Astronomers plan to continue monitoring 3I/ATLAS until it exits the Solar System. As more powerful observatories, such as the Vera Rubin Observatory, come online, researchers anticipate discovering more interstellar comets, enhancing our understanding of cosmic complexities.
