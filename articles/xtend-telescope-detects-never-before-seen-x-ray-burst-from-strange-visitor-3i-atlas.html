Xtend Telescope Detects Never-Before-Seen X-Ray Burst From Strange Visitor 3I/ATLAS

Astronomers have detected a faint X-ray halo surrounding comet 3I/ATLAS using the X-ray Imaging and Spectroscopy Mission (XRISM), marking the first observation of high-energy emissions from a confirmed interstellar comet. The detection occurred recently, as scientists continue to study this unique celestial visitor.

This discovery enhances the scientific understanding of 3I/ATLAS, revealing a dynamic interaction between the comet's gaseous coma and the solar wind. Researchers from the Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency (JAXA) and NASA are analyzing the data to glean insights about the comet's composition and origins.

Key facts

  • Detection of X-ray emissions from comet 3I/ATLAS reported by XRISM.
  • First time X-rays have been observed from an interstellar comet.
  • X-ray halo spans approximately 400,000 km around the comet's nucleus.
  • 3I/ATLAS is the third confirmed interstellar object observed in the Solar System.
  • Previous interstellar objects include 1I/ʻOumuamua and 2I/Borisov.
  • Data analysis indicates presence of carbon, nitrogen, and oxygen in the comet's coma.

Timeline

Since July 2025, astronomers have mobilized numerous ground and space-based telescopes to gather data on 3I/ATLAS. The XRISM team announced the detection of X-ray emissions, transforming the understanding of the comet's interactions with solar particles.

Observations with the Neil Gehrels Swift Observatory confirmed water activity in the comet, indicating active outgassing at a heliocentric distance of 3.51 AU. Additionally, polarimetric observations from the Very Large Telescope (VLT) revealed unique properties in the coma.

Official statements

“Our early data hint at a faint X-ray glow spreading hundreds of thousands of kilometers across its coma — a first glimpse of this rare visitor from beyond the Solar System,” said a representative from the XRISM mission team.

Context

Prior to this detection, astronomers had observed X-ray emissions from comets within the Solar System, but never from an object originating beyond it. The study of comet 3I/ATLAS offers new opportunities to understand the physical and chemical processes occurring in interstellar comets.

This discovery suggests that other interstellar visitors may also produce detectable X-rays, indicating the potential for X-ray astronomy to become a valuable tool in studying such objects.

What’s next

The XRISM team plans to conduct a more rigorous analysis of the data to confirm the origins of the X-ray emissions. As 3I/ATLAS continues its outbound journey, scientists will continue to monitor and study this unique comet for further insights.
