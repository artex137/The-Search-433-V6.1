Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS Today: Latest Dec. 17, 2025 Updates Ahead of Its Dec. 19 Earth Flyby

Interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS has approached within 166.9 million miles (268.6 million km) of Earth as of December 17, 2025, just two days before its closest flyby on December 19. The comet will not pose any threat during this event, as it will remain approximately 1.8 astronomical units from Earth, according to NASA.

This flyby presents a significant opportunity for astronomers to study an interstellar object that formed around another star and entered our solar system. Observations are crucial for understanding the composition and behavior of such comets, which differ from those originating in our solar system. NASA has mobilized various missions to observe 3I/ATLAS before it departs for interstellar space.

Key facts

  • Date of closest approach: December 19, 2025
  • Distance from Earth: Approximately 1.8 AU (170 million miles / 270 million kilometers)
  • Current distance: 166.9 million miles (268.6 million km) as of December 17, 2025
  • Object designation: 3I/ATLAS (also known as C/2025 N1)
  • Previous interstellar comets: 1I/ʻOumuamua (2017) and 2I/Borisov (2019)
  • Origin of discovery: NASA-funded ATLAS survey telescope in Río Hurtado, Chile

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first reported in early July 2025 after the ATLAS survey telescope in Chile detected its trajectory. Scientists quickly identified it as an interstellar object due to its path not being gravitationally bound to the Sun. As it approaches Earth, various space missions have initiated observation campaigns to gather data on the comet's characteristics and behavior.

As of December 3, 2025, the European Space Agency's XMM-Newton observatory captured low-energy X-rays emitted by the comet. On December 17, multiple guides from science outlets have emphasized that 3I/ATLAS is a telescope target rather than a naked-eye object, with its current position near the constellation Leo.

Official statements

“It looks and behaves like a comet, and all evidence points to it being a comet,” said NASA Associate Administrator Amit Kshatriya, responding to speculation regarding the nature of 3I/ATLAS.

Context

3I/ATLAS is only the third confirmed interstellar object ever observed in our solar system. Its unique origin provides valuable insights into how comets form and behave in different stellar environments. The comet’s flyby is an opportunity to compare its measurements with those of comets originating from our solar system.

What’s next

NASA and other space agencies will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS as it approaches its closest point to Earth. Observations will focus on gathering data on gas composition, dust behavior, and the comet's activity as it moves away from the Sun after perihelion.
