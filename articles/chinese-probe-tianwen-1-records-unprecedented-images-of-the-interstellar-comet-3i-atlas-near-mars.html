Chinese probe Tianwen-1 records unprecedented images of the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS near Mars

The Chinese probe Tianwen-1 has captured high-resolution images of the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS while operating in orbit around Mars, marking a significant achievement in planetary science, according to the China National Space Administration (CNSA). The images were taken at a distance of approximately 30 million kilometers from the comet in October 2025.

This event represents a milestone in the study of objects outside our solar system. The successful capture of the images demonstrates advanced technologies and methods of deep space observation. The data collected will allow scientists to analyze the composition and trajectory of interstellar visitors more thoroughly.

Key facts

  • Object: Comet 3I/ATLAS
  • Distance from Mars: Approximately 30 million kilometers
  • Speed: 58 kilometers per second
  • Diameter: About 5.6 kilometers
  • Time of image capture: October 2025
  • Agencies involved: CNSA, ESA, NASA

Timeline

In July 2020, the Tianwen-1 mission was launched, which included an orbiter, a lander, and the Zhurong rover. The probe entered Martian orbit in February 2021. In May 2021, the Zhurong rover successfully landed on Mars and began its operations.

In October 2025, Tianwen-1 successfully captured images of comet 3I/ATLAS, marking the first time an interstellar object has been photographed in such detail from the orbit of another planet.

Official statements

“The success of this operation validates new technologies and methods of deep space observation,” said a CNSA spokesperson.
“These images provide crucial data for the global scientific community,” the spokesperson added, emphasizing the importance of international cooperation in space exploration.

Context

Comet 3I/ATLAS is the third confirmed interstellar object, following the detections of ‘Oumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019. Its trajectory indicates that it is not bound to our solar system, traveling on a hyperbolic orbit that allows it to escape the Sun's gravitational pull.

Previous observations of interstellar objects have provided valuable insights into the formation of planetary systems and the diversity of chemical compositions in the universe.

What’s next

The data collected from the images will undergo analysis by the global scientific community. Further studies will focus on understanding the materials formed in the protoplanetary disk of 3I/ATLAS's home star system, providing insights into the formation of other planetary systems in the Milky Way.
