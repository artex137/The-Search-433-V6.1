Comet 3I/ATLAS sends out radio waves and triggers NASA’s planetary defense team

Scientists have detected an unusual radio signal from the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, first identified on July 1, 2025, by the ATLAS system in Río Hurtado, Chile. The comet follows a hyperbolic trajectory at approximately 100,000 kilometers per hour relative to the Sun. There have been no reported injuries or fatalities related to this event.

This discovery has prompted NASA to mobilize its planetary defense team for intensive monitoring. The agency is following strict protocols for celestial objects of unknown origin that exhibit unusual characteristics. The goal is to gather data and evaluate any potential implications of the comet's behavior.

Key facts

  • Date of detection: July 1, 2025
  • Location: Río Hurtado, Chile
  • Radio signal detected: October 24, 2025
  • Frequency: 1.6 GHz
  • Closest approach to Earth: December 19, 2025, at a distance of 270 million kilometers
  • Diameter of comet's nucleus: Between 320 meters and 5.6 kilometers
  • Classification: Third interstellar object observed

Timeline

The following events have occurred regarding comet 3I/ATLAS:

  • July 1, 2025: 3I/ATLAS is first detected by the ATLAS system in Chile.
  • October 24, 2025: The MeerKAT radio telescope in South Africa captures a radio signal from the comet.
  • December 19, 2025: The comet will make its closest approach to Earth.

Official statements

“The emission of radio waves, combined with atypical behavior, has led to the mobilization of NASA’s planetary defense team,” a NASA spokesperson stated.

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third interstellar object identified, following ‘Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov. Its classification as an interstellar comet reinforces the importance of studying such celestial bodies to enhance understanding of stellar systems beyond our own.

What’s next

Nasa plans to continue monitoring 3I/ATLAS as it approaches Earth and will analyze its composition and behavior using various telescopes. The data collected will contribute to knowledge about planetary formation in distant stellar environments.
