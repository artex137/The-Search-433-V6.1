WATCH LIVE: Interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS moves away from the Sun in free livestream tonight

Comet 3I/ATLAS has re-emerged from behind the Sun and will be visible in a livestream starting at 5:15 a.m. CET (11:15 p.m. ET) tonight from Manciano, Italy. This comet is the third known interstellar object to enter our solar system, and astronomers have closely followed its trajectory over the past few weeks.

The livestream, hosted by the Virtual Telescope Project, offers a rare opportunity for the public to observe the comet as it moves through the stars of Virgo. Scientists have been analyzing whether 3I/ATLAS has survived its close solar approach, which has sparked excitement and speculation in the scientific community and among the general public.

Key facts

  • Event: Comet 3I/ATLAS livestream
  • Date: Tonight
  • Start Time: 5:15 a.m. CET (11:15 p.m. ET)
  • Location: Manciano, Italy
  • Magnitude: +10.9
  • Livestream Source: Virtual Telescope Project

Timeline

Comet 3I/ATLAS entered our solar system four months ago, confirming its interstellar origin shortly after its discovery. It recently made a close approach to the Sun, prompting extensive observation and analysis by astronomers.

Recent images suggest that the comet has remained intact following its solar flyby, which has led to renewed discussions about its origins and stability.

Official statements

Avi Loeb, a Harvard astrophysicist, stated that the unusual stability of the comet could merit consideration of non-natural explanations. Other experts have expressed skepticism, attributing its condition to standard cometary physics.

Context

The comet's journey has generated significant interest, particularly due to its path near Jupiter, Venus, and Mars, which has fueled discussions on social media. NASA is expected to release high-resolution images of the comet soon, captured by the Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter's HiRISE camera.

What’s next

As the livestream provides an opportunity for public observation, NASA's upcoming release of high-resolution images will likely further inform discussions about 3I/ATLAS and its origins.
