The Challenge of Measuring the Mass of 3I/ATLAS

The interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS has raised significant questions about its mass and size, with observations made by the SPHEREx space observatory from August 8 to 12, 2025, suggesting a nucleus diameter of 46 kilometers. There have been no reported injuries or fatalities related to this object.

This matter is vital for astronomers as understanding the mass of 3I/ATLAS could provide insights into its composition and origin. Authorities are exploring various methods to measure the mass, including using data from the Webb telescope and potential gravitational interactions with Mars in October 2025.

Key facts

  • Observation Dates: August 8-12, 2025
  • Estimated Nucleus Diameter: 46 kilometers
  • Mass Loss Rate: 129 kilograms per second
  • Outflow Speed: 0.44 kilometers per second
  • Closest Approach to Mars: October 3, 2025, at 29 million kilometers
  • Minimum Orbit Intersection Distance (MOID): 0.018 AU (2.7 million kilometers)

Timeline

From August 8 to 12, 2025, the SPHEREx space observatory detected flux from 3I/ATLAS, indicating a possible nucleus size.

On October 3, 2025, 3I/ATLAS will pass within 29 million kilometers of Mars, potentially affecting the planet's trajectory.

On March 16, 2026, 3I/ATLAS will come within 34 million kilometers of Jupiter, presenting another opportunity to gather data.

Official statements

“The mass loss rate scales with area and the non-gravitational acceleration scales inversely with volume, making the rocket equation a good approach for measuring the mass of small objects,” said Avi Loeb, head of the Galileo Project.

Context

3I/ATLAS is notable for its proximity to Mars and its potential as a technological object. Previous interstellar comets, such as 2I/Borisov, have provided different mass estimates, highlighting the importance of precise measurements for understanding interstellar bodies.

What’s next

In the coming months, scientists will continue to analyze data from various telescopes and prepare for the close encounters with Mars and Jupiter to better understand the mass and characteristics of 3I/ATLAS.
