Mars Probe Captures Rare Images of Interstellar Comet Debunking Alien Spaceship Theories

An interstellar comet, known as 3I/ATLAS, has made a significant passage near Mars on October 3, 2023, coming within 30 million kilometers of the red planet. No injuries or fatalities have been reported.

This event matters as it provides a unique opportunity for scientists to study a comet that originated from another solar system. Two European Space Agency (ESA) spacecraft, ExoMars and Mars Express, captured images of 3I/ATLAS, which may be the oldest comet discovered. Further analysis is expected after the comet’s closest approach to the sun on October 30, 2023.

Key facts

  • Comet Name: 3I/ATLAS
  • Date of Passage: October 3, 2023
  • Distance from Mars: 30 million kilometers
  • ESA Spacecraft Involved: ExoMars and Mars Express
  • Next Closest Approach to the Sun: October 30, 2023

Timeline

On October 3, 2023, 3I/ATLAS passed close to Mars, allowing for unprecedented imaging by ESA's ExoMars and Mars Express. The comet is believed to have traveled through space for billions of years before being drawn into our solar system.

Following its closest approach to the sun on October 30, scientists plan to conduct further analysis of the comet’s composition, particularly its icy materials.

Official statements

“Current scientific assessments indicate that there are no signs of 3I/ATLAS being an alien spacecraft,” an ESA spokesperson said. “It does not reflect light like a metallic object nor does it emit any signals.”

Context

This sighting marks the third observation of interstellar objects passing through our solar system, following 1I/Oumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019. Each detection has generated speculation about extraterrestrial life, though current assessments consistently debunk these theories.

What’s next

The European Space Agency plans to launch the Comet Interceptor mission in 2029, which aims to target unknown interstellar visitors for closer examination.
