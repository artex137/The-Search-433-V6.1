Alcohol ‘Extremely Abundant’ in Interstellar Visitor Comet 3I/ATLAS

Astronomers have detected unusually large amounts of methanol in interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, according to findings from the Atacama Large Millimeter/submillimeter Array (ALMA) in Chile. The observations, made in late 2025, reveal that the levels of methanol exceed those found in almost all known comets from our solar system.

This discovery is significant as it provides a rare chemical snapshot of how planetary systems beyond our own form and evolve. The research team, led by Nathan Roth, a professor at American University, emphasizes that the findings could reshape our understanding of comet composition and the diversity of interstellar objects.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS observed in late 2025.
  • Methanol detected at ratios of roughly 70 and 120 compared to hydrogen cyanide.
  • 3I/ATLAS is the third confirmed interstellar object to enter our solar system.
  • ALMA is an international observatory in Chile, with the U.S. National Science Foundation as a partner.

Timeline

In late 2025, astronomers used ALMA to observe comet 3I/ATLAS as it approached the Sun. The comet's icy surface warmed, releasing gas and dust, which formed a surrounding cloud known as a coma. Researchers focused on the chemical makeup of the comet, specifically measuring the levels of methanol and hydrogen cyanide.

The team identified exceptionally high amounts of methanol compared to hydrogen cyanide, marking 3I/ATLAS as one of the most methanol-rich comets studied to date. This finding suggests the comet formed under different chemical conditions than most solar system comets.

Official statements

“Observing 3I/ATLAS is like taking a fingerprint from another solar system,” Nathan Roth said in a statement. “The details reveal what it’s made of, and it’s bursting with methanol in a way we just don’t usually see in comets in our own solar system.”

Context

Comet 3I/ATLAS follows in the footsteps of 1I/‘Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov, the two previous interstellar visitors that also exhibited unexpected properties. These findings raise questions about the chemical diversity of objects formed around other stars and how they compare to those in our solar system.

What’s next

Further investigations are expected to explore the implications of the findings, particularly regarding the formation of comets and planetary systems beyond our solar system.
