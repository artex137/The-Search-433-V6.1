Interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS: NASA tracks fastest object to enter solar system

NASA has begun tracking an interstellar comet, 3I/ATLAS, which is entering the solar system at a speed of approximately 245,000 km/h. The comet, also known as C/2025 N1 (ATLAS) and initially named A11pl3Z, is the third confirmed interstellar object detected, following 'Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov. The detection occurred in June 2025, and the comet is expected to reach its closest point to the Sun around October 30, 2025.

This discovery matters because it offers a unique opportunity for astronomers to study material from another star system. According to NASA, 3I/ATLAS presents clues about the chemistry and conditions of distant stellar neighbourhoods. The object has been classified as an interstellar comet due to its signs of a coma and tail. NASA has confirmed that 3I/ATLAS poses no threat to Earth as it will pass at a safe distance.

Key facts

  • Object: 3I/ATLAS (C/2025 N1)
  • Speed: Approximately 245,000 km/h (152,000 mph)
  • First detected: June 2025
  • Closest approach to the Sun: October 30, 2025
  • Path: Hyperbolic, not gravitationally bound to the Sun
  • Classification: Interstellar comet
  • Threat level: No threat to Earth

Timeline

NASA detected 3I/ATLAS in June 2025. The comet is travelling toward its perihelion, expected around October 30, 2025, when it will come just inside the orbit of Mars.

The object has shown tentative signs of a coma and tail, leading to its classification as an interstellar comet. Observations have revealed its high speed and unusual trajectory.

Official statements

“3I/ATLAS presents a rare and exciting opportunity for astronomers to study material originating from another star system,” said a NASA spokesperson.
“The evidence points to it being a natural, though unusual, comet,” the spokesperson added, addressing speculation about its origin.

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third interstellar object confirmed by astronomers, following 'Oumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019. The detection of such objects is significant for understanding the dynamics of our solar system and the interstellar medium.

Previous interstellar objects have sparked interest and debate regarding their origins and compositions, leading to ongoing research in astrophysics.

What’s next

NASA will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS as it approaches the Sun, providing further data to astronomers to enhance understanding of interstellar objects.
