NASA astronomers have discovered a third interstellar object: what is known about comet C/2025 N1

Astronomers have detected a new interstellar object, designated as 3I/Atlas, on July 1 at an observatory in Chile. The object is currently over 670 million kilometers away from the Sun and moving at a speed of 60 km/s on a hyperbolic orbit.

This discovery is significant as 3I/Atlas is likely the third known interstellar body to traverse the Solar System. Researchers have observed cometary activity from the object, including a faint coma and a short tail, leading to its additional designation as C/2025 N1. Scientists have noted that 3I/Atlas poses no threat to Earth.

Key facts

  • Object detected: July 1, 2023
  • Location of detection: Atlas telescope, Chile
  • Current distance from the Sun: over 670 million kilometers
  • Speed: 60 km/s
  • Closest approach to the Sun: October 30, 2023, at a distance of 130 million kilometers
  • Estimated size: up to 20 km in diameter
  • Visibility: Expected to be observable through amateur telescopes in late 2025 - early 2026

Timeline

On July 1, 2023, astronomers detected the interstellar object 3I/Atlas using the Atlas telescope in Chile. Following its discovery, researchers noted cometary activity, including a faint coma and a short tail. The object is currently moving towards its closest approach to the Sun, expected on October 30, 2023.

Official statements

“Currently, it is not expected that the comet will be visible to the naked eye, but it can be seen through an amateur telescope in late 2025 - early 2026. As it is studied over the coming weeks, we will have a better understanding of how visible it will actually be,” said Jake Foster from the Royal Observatory in Greenwich.

Context

The discovery of 3I/Atlas adds to the growing list of known interstellar objects. Previous interstellar bodies, such as 'Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov, have also garnered significant interest from the scientific community. This trend highlights the increasing ability of astronomers to detect and study objects from outside the Solar System.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to monitor 3I/Atlas as it approaches the Sun. Further studies will provide more insights into its visibility and characteristics as it moves through the Solar System.
