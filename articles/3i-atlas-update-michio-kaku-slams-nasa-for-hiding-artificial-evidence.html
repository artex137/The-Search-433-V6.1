3I/ATLAS Update: Michio Kaku Slams NASA for Hiding 'Artificial' Evidence

The interstellar object 3I/ATLAS, first detected on 1 July 2025 by the Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System, has sparked controversy due to its unexpected behaviour and alleged lack of transparency from NASA. The object, only the third interstellar visitor identified, has lost its tail after passing close to the Sun, a phenomenon that has raised questions among astronomers.

This situation matters as it has led to significant speculation about the nature and origin of 3I/ATLAS. Dr. Michio Kaku, a prominent theoretical physicist, has criticized NASA for withholding information, claiming it undermines public understanding and trust in scientific transparency.

Key facts

  • Object designation: 3I/ATLAS
  • Date detected: 1 July 2025
  • Detection method: Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System
  • Behaviour: Lost its tail post-perihelion
  • Estimated age: 7 billion years
  • Notable composition: Unusually high nickel content

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first identified on 1 July 2025. Following its detection, it passed close to the Sun, leading to observations that indicated it had lost its tail. This behaviour is atypical for natural comets, prompting further investigation and discussion among scientists.

Dr. Michio Kaku has since criticized NASA for not releasing the latest images of 3I/ATLAS, raising concerns about transparency. He has also highlighted delays in releasing data from the Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter, suggesting a pattern of unnecessary secrecy.

Official statements

"This isn't something that needs to be classified," said Dr. Kaku, criticizing NASA's handling of the situation.

Context

The controversy surrounding 3I/ATLAS is not isolated. Dr. Kaku's remarks reflect a broader concern about NASA's transparency regarding interstellar objects and space exploration data. Previous incidents have raised questions about information control and public trust in scientific institutions.

What’s next

As the investigation into 3I/ATLAS continues, the demand for transparency from NASA is likely to grow. The public and independent researchers await further information to understand the true nature of this interstellar object.
