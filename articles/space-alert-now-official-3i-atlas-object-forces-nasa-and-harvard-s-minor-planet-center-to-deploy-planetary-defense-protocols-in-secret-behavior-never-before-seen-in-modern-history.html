Space alert now official - 3I/ATLAS object forces NASA and Harvard's Minor Planet Center to deploy planetary defense protocols in secret

NASA and Harvard's Minor Planet Center have activated planetary defense protocols for comet 3I/ATLAS, a fast-moving interstellar object currently traveling through our solar system. This action took place on October 21, 2025, at 21:08 UT, as part of a closed event involving international space agencies. There are no reported injuries or fatalities associated with this event.

This activation marks a significant moment in space safety, as it is the first deployment of this nature in modern history. According to the International Asteroid Warning Network (IAWN), the focus on 3I/ATLAS will continue until January 27, 2026. The scientific community is particularly interested in the comet's unusual behavior, which they have described as "inexplicable."

Key facts

  • Event date: October 21, 2025
  • Time of activation: 21:08 UT
  • Object: Comet 3I/ATLAS (C/2025 N1)
  • Closest approach to the Sun: October 30, 2025, at a distance of 210 million kilometers
  • Size of comet's core: Approximately 5 kilometers in diameter
  • Speed: 209,000 km/h
  • Minimum distance from Earth: 270 million kilometers

Timeline

On October 21, 2025, NASA and the Minor Planet Center issued a technical notice known as the MPEC bulletin (2025-U142), marking the official activation of planetary defense protocols for comet 3I/ATLAS. The comet, traveling at an extraordinary speed, passed closest to the Sun on October 30, 2025. Observations and studies will continue until the end of January 2026.

Official statements

“This is the first time we have had to activate such protocols for an interstellar object,” said a representative from NASA. “The behavior of 3I/ATLAS is unlike anything we have seen before.”

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third interstellar object detected in our solar system, having been ejected from another star system long ago. The comet's unique characteristics, including its hyperbolic trajectory and anti-tail phenomenon, present new challenges for tracking and studying celestial objects. Previous instances of planetary defense protocols have typically focused on objects that posed a threat to Earth.

What’s next

NASA and international space agencies will continue to monitor comet 3I/ATLAS closely until January 27, 2026, using this opportunity to gather data and prepare for future encounters with stellar objects.
