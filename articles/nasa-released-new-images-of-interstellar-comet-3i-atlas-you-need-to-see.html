Nasa Releases New Images Of Interstellar Comet 3I/ATLAS

Nasa has released new images of interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS, which was first discovered by the NASA-funded Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System (ATLAS) telescope in Chile on July 1, 2025. The latest batch of images was captured between late September and mid-October 2025, showcasing some of the clearest and most detailed views of the comet to date.

The release of these images marks a significant advancement in understanding the comet's characteristics and trajectory. NASA has utilized multiple satellites to capture these images, including the Solar and Heliospheric Observatory and the Perseverance Rover on Mars. Scientists anticipate that 3I/ATLAS will be visible to ground-based telescopes and the James Webb Space Telescope when it flies by Earth on December 19, 2025.

Key facts

  • Discovery Date: July 1, 2025
  • Image Capture Period: Late September to mid-October 2025
  • Upcoming Flyby: December 19, 2025
  • Distance from Earth: Expected to be 170 million miles away
  • Agencies Involved: NASA, European Space Agency, Russia's Federal Space Agency

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was first discovered on July 1, 2025, by the ATLAS telescope in Chile. Since its discovery, 12 NASA cameras have captured imagery of the comet. The latest images, taken between late September and mid-October, have provided enhanced details of the comet's appearance. It is expected to make its closest approach to Earth on December 19, 2025.

NASA's sun-observing PUNCH satellites and other missions have contributed to the imaging of 3I/ATLAS, revealing it primarily as an illuminated dot with a faint tail. As it approaches Earth, it is anticipated that more observations will yield insights into its chemical composition and potential origins.

Official statements

Nicky Fox, associate administrator of NASA's Science Mission Directorate, stated, "We certainly haven't seen any techno signatures or anything from it that would lead us to believe it was anything other than a comet."
Fox also remarked, "It could be from something that existed before our own solar system. That is so cool."

Context

Comet 3I/ATLAS is the third confirmed interstellar object to enter our solar system, following 1I/'Oumuamua in 2017 and comet 2I/Borisov in 2019. The previous objects have contributed to NASA's understanding of dark comets and the dynamics of interstellar bodies.

The interest in 3I/ATLAS has been heightened by speculation regarding its origins, with some theorizing it could be a form of alien technology. However, experts have dismissed these claims, emphasizing that the comet is likely a natural space rock.

What’s next

In the coming weeks, scientists aim to gather more data on 3I/ATLAS's chemical makeup, speed, and potential place of origin as it approaches Earth. Observations during its flyby will provide critical insights into its characteristics.
