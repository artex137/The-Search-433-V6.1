Alien attack in November? Mysterious object has scientists on alert

A newly confirmed interstellar object named 3I/ATLAS is racing through the inner solar system, intriguing astronomers as it approaches the Sun. The object is traveling at over 130,000 mph and is expected to make its closest pass to the Sun around October 30, 2025, with no threat to Earth anticipated during its approach in mid-December.

This discovery matters because it raises questions about the object's composition and trajectory. While 3I/ATLAS is the third interstellar object ever discovered, following ʻOumuamua and comet 2I/Borisov, some researchers, including Harvard professor Avi Loeb, have speculated that it may not be a simple space rock. However, most scientists believe it is a natural comet.

Key facts

  • Object Name: 3I/ATLAS
  • Speed: Over 130,000 mph (209,000 km/h)
  • Closest Approach to the Sun: October 30, 2025
  • Closest Approach to Earth: Mid-December 2025, approximately 1.8 AU (about 170 million miles)
  • Estimated Size: Approximately 3.5 miles (≈5.6 km) across
  • Discovery Order: Third interstellar object, after ʻOumuamua and 2I/Borisov

Timeline

3I/ATLAS was identified as an interstellar visitor, prompting astronomers to monitor its trajectory closely. It is currently developing a classic cometary coma, which is typical behavior for comets as they approach the Sun. NASA's Hubble Space Telescope has captured images showing a dust-and-gas coma, indicating active cometary behavior.

As the object continues its path, researchers are conducting spectroscopy and imaging to gather more data. They aim to confirm its nature and trajectory, focusing on whether it has a natural or artificial origin.

Official statements

Avi Loeb has noted, "The approach geometry and timing could make observation harder from Earth, though the odds favor a natural origin."
Many researchers contend that 3I/ATLAS fits the profile of a natural comet, supported by observations from Hubble and other observatories.

Context

Prior interstellar objects, such as ʻOumuamua and 2I/Borisov, have garnered significant attention, but 3I/ATLAS is unique due to its high speed and trajectory. While theories about its potential artificial nature have emerged, the scientific consensus leans toward it being a natural comet.

Current data indicates that 3I/ATLAS is active and brightening as it approaches the Sun, which aligns with typical comet behavior. Researchers emphasize the need for continued observation and data comparison to draw accurate conclusions.

What’s next

Researchers will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS as it nears the Sun, focusing on spectroscopy and imaging to clarify its nature. The priority remains on gathering data to ascertain whether it is a comet or something else entirely, with significant findings expected in late October and December.
