What Happened When 3I/Atlas Was Scanned For Aliens? - Results Are Finally Here

Scientists have confirmed that 3I/ATLAS, an interstellar comet that entered the solar system in July 2025, has no evidence of alien technology following extensive observations. The findings were announced after a concentrated search for technosignatures conducted on 18 December 2025, when the comet made its closest approach to Earth.

This discovery is significant as 3I/ATLAS is only the third confirmed object known to have originated from outside our solar system. The extensive public interest and speculation surrounding the comet have prompted scientific agencies to conduct thorough investigations into its true nature. The Breakthrough Listen project led the search for potential extraterrestrial signals.

Key facts

  • 3I/ATLAS entered the solar system in July 2025.
  • The comet was discovered by the Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System (ATLAS) telescope in Chile.
  • A concentrated search for technosignatures took place on 18 December 2025.
  • The Robert C. Byrd Green Bank Telescope in the United States was used for the search.
  • Over 471,000 initial signals were detected, but only a handful were considered after filtering.
  • No strong artificial radio transmissions were found exceeding 0.1 watts.
  • NASA imagery and other observations confirmed 3I/ATLAS behaves like a comet.

Timeline

3I/ATLAS entered the solar system in July 2025, sparking global interest and speculation about its origins. The comet was discovered by the ATLAS telescope in Chile, leading to various theories about its nature, including the possibility of it being an alien probe.

On 18 December 2025, astronomers conducted a focused search for technosignatures using the Robert C. Byrd Green Bank Telescope. This search aimed to detect any artificial signals as the comet approached Earth. After analyzing over 471,000 signals, researchers confirmed that all remaining candidates were attributable to known noise or interference.

Official statements

“Importantly, no strong artificial radio transmissions were found at a strength exceeding about 0.1 watts,” said a representative from the Breakthrough Listen project. “This means that even low power emitters would have been detected if they were broadcasting from the object's location.”

Context

3I/ATLAS is the third interstellar object confirmed to have entered the solar system, following 1I/'Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov. The unusual characteristics of these objects have led to significant public interest and speculation about extraterrestrial life. The involvement of prominent astrophysicists in the discussions has further fueled these theories.

Previous investigations into interstellar objects have prompted debates about their origins and the potential for detecting signs of alien technology. The ongoing interest in 3I/ATLAS reflects a broader fascination with the possibility of life beyond Earth.

What’s next

Following the findings, researchers will continue to monitor 3I/ATLAS and other interstellar objects to gather more data. Future studies may focus on understanding the composition and behaviour of these celestial bodies as they travel through the solar system.
