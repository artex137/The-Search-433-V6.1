Mystery of 'alien spaceship' 3I/Atlas could 'finally be solved' in new x-ray view

Published 2025-12-12 19:55 UTC

Mystery of Comet 3I/ATLAS Deepens with New X-ray Observations

Comet 3I/ATLAS is approaching Earth and is expected to reach its closest point next week, while the European Space Agency (ESA) has reported significant new findings from its x-ray camera. The comet has raised concerns about potential alien technology due to its unusual appearance and behaviour, but recent observations may help clarify its true nature.

This discovery is important as it could provide insights into the composition of the interstellar object, which some experts speculate might display signs of alien technology. The ESA has conducted extensive observations to enhance understanding of 3I/ATLAS and its interactions with the solar wind.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS is expected to reach its closest point to Earth next week.
  • The ESA's XMM-Newton observed the comet on 3 December for approximately 20 hours.
  • The comet was about 282–285 million km from the spacecraft during the observation.
  • The XMM-Newton used its European Photon Imaging Camera (EPIC)-pn, its most sensitive x-ray camera.
  • X-ray observations may reveal details about the gases emitted by the comet.
  • Recent planetary defence drills have been conducted globally in response to concerns about the comet's behaviour.

Timeline

On 3 December, the ESA's XMM-Newton spacecraft tracked Comet 3I/ATLAS for about 20 hours. The observations revealed the comet glowing in low-energy x-rays, which astronomers had anticipated due to interactions between gas molecules and the solar wind.

As the comet approaches Earth, experts have raised questions regarding its composition and potential threats. Recent planetary defence drills have heightened awareness of the comet's unpredictable behaviour.

Official statements

“X-ray observations are a powerful tool for scientists to detect and study gases that other instruments can’t easily spot,” said the European Space Agency.
Astrophysicist Avi Loeb suggested that the comet's sunward anti-tail could indicate a swarm of compact objects travelling alongside 3I/ATLAS, rather than merely a gas plume.

Context

Comet 3I/ATLAS is the second interstellar object observed since 1I/'Oumuamua was discovered in 2017. The latter was speculated to be made of exotic ice, but its distance has made further study impossible. The current observations of 3I/ATLAS provide a rare opportunity to examine another interstellar object in detail.

What’s next

As 3I/ATLAS gets closer to Earth, researchers will continue to analyze the x-ray data to determine the comet’s composition. Ongoing planetary defence preparations will also likely adapt to address the potential complexities posed by the comet's behaviour.
